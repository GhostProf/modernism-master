“Primeval mud impenetrable dark”: Towards an Annotation of Comment c’est/ How It Is

C. J.Ackerley

A working premise: that when Beckett writes first in English, and that text is later put into French (by himself, or by another), then what ensues is a translation. This is the case with Murphy and Watt, Beckett’s two first published novels, the translations of which he was closely involved with; but it is equally true of Dream of Fair to Middling Women and More Pricks Than Kicks, the translations of which he did not approve during his lifetime.1 However, when Beckett writes first in French, then rewrites in English—his native language, whatever his reasons might have been for having chosen another—then what emerges is not a translation in the usual sense of that word, but a re-writing, a re-creation. Works such as Molloy or En attendant Godot/Waiting for Godot thus have a dual ontology, neither entirely English nor entirely French, yet grounded in both. This distinction, I suggest, applies equally to the works of Beckett’s middle period, such as All That Fall, of which Tous ceux qui tombent is a translation, and broadly to the later works, such as Words and Music, where the French Paroles et musique cannot do justice to the echoes of Yeats; or Worstward Ho, which Beckett considered to be untranslatable.2 The issue may be more complex with respect to late texts conceived and/or written in French and English either simultaneously or very nearly (Cascando, Catastrophe); but the distinction applies to Comment c’est, written first, with considerable pains, in French; then reworked, with comparable agonies, into English.

The problem may be simply put: if a text has a dual or bilingual ontology, then, in this case, notes in English may not properly respect the French original; conversely, a French commentary may be unfair to its English re-creation. Furthermore, any would-be annotator is unlikely to be equally versed in the two languages and cultural codes. To speak personally: as a professional annotator, I have long been immersed in the minutiae of Beckett’s writings in English; but I cannot say this of those in French. I have written book-length annotations of Murphy and Watt, and smaller treatments of Beckett’s poems and the later plays for television, but always with reference to their English texts. The latter afforded a challenge: given the diminuendo al niente effect of so many echoes, and the attenuated nature of so many allusions, the problem was to derive a way to annotate the barely visible or audible: the ill-seen, the ill-heard. My solution was to affirm that an echo, however faint or diminished, is nevertheless an echo of something; and an allusion, however attenuated, is equally an allusion to something. The problem was thus pragmatically redefined, for any given effect, as that of identifying the often distant source and then determining the appropriate range and resonance of voice and echo.3 This emphasis is not incompatible with what Anthony Cordingley, following others, has termed Beckett’s “poetics of ignorance,” which “neutralizes the content of his inevitably learned language by employing references in a private way, such that they no longer affirm their source meaning but are rather the raw material for his own creation (poetics)”; but it first foregrounds the referent rather than the process of reference.4 That experience has affinities with the immediate problem: having to annotate the largely invisible and almost inaudible forced me to accommodate my practice to a different set of conditions and develop new strategies. Beckett’s bilingual texts challenge me as an annotator to develop a pragmatic strategy that will do justice to both versions. A major problem arises: however competent and versatile I might be as an annotator of Beckett’s English texts, I lack that same competence and versatility in French. I can read the French texts, including those in manuscript, and refer to these within an English annotation; but I do not have the intimate awareness of the linguistic and cultural codes that might allow me to annotate the French texts with the same authority as I could the English texts. To approach a bilingual text from an English perspective would be an act of cultural arrogance, treating a derivative text as primary; yet to privilege the French text and consider the English as simply a “translation” would be equally misguided, overlooking the unique qualities of that re-creation. To illustrate this point: in Part 2 of Molloy, as Moran is about to venture on his journey, he smells a stew cooking in the kitchen. Fearing that his servant, Martha, may have held back on the onions, in the Minuit text he goes “à la recherche des oignons,” and in so doing generates a Proustian echo with magnificently absurdist overtones.5 The English text does not even try to duplicate the effect:

The stew was a great disappointment. Where are the onions? I cried. Gone to nothing, 791 replied Martha. I rushed into the kitchen, to look for the onions I suspected her of having removed from the pot, because she knew how much I liked them.6

Proust, so obvious in the French, has, like the onions, “gone to nothing,” a phrase, incidentally, which lacks the concision of the French réduit, even as the Minuit “l’Irish Stew” (FM, 133) is rather more lumpy than the “Irish stew” of the English (M, 101). Yet “Martha” works well in both texts, as the name of one who serves, for the Mary/ Martha syndrome is part of the Christian heritage shared by both societies and so part of a common code. An annotation of this name, in principle, should be identical for both texts. However, a little later in the English text, when Martha serves up left-over shepherd’s pie (M, 121), the French “le plat de berger” (FM, 159), a kind of lamb stew, does not readily convey the ironical intimations of the Good Shepherd (le Bon Pasteur).7 Even though that figure is common to both traditions, the linguistic and culinary coding differs.

This interlude offers a miniature paradigm of the problem of annotating the bilingual work. Both texts depict the same situation and unfold broadly within a shared cultural and biblical tradition (Irish stew, onions, Martha, the Good Shepherd); but neither can accommodate the full range of effects implicit in the ideal “text.” Each monolingual version is differently enriched or impoverished, with the flavor of the Proustian onions lost in translation, and that of the shepherd’s pie in French. How, then, might a would-be annotator set about this problem, when lacking an equal command of the requisite cultural codes?

A conceit: the annotator as a “monster of the solitudes.”8 He or she would be a denizen of the purgatorial realm, caught between the hard rock of the text and the critical light above, seeking illumination yet enmeshed in the “primeval mud impenetrable dark” (HII, 7). More prosaically: the goal of annotation is to offer pertinent information that initiates better readings. Some time ago I was asked to contribute to a volume on Beckett a chapter on the phenomenology of annotation.9 This proved unexpectedly daunting. I addressed the simple question that had arisen from the television plays: that when one annotates something, one presumably annotates some thing—but what is that thing? My response, in brief, was to invoke the influential study by E. D. Hirsch, Jr., Validity in Interpretation, which adopts a phenomenological framework to affirm a principle of validity that is, for my purposes, essential to the art of annotation.

There are problems in Hirsch’s understanding of validity, notably his attempt to identify textual meaning with authorial intention, but his insistence on the sharability of meaning lies at the heart of his formulation. This leads to his notion of consensus: the firmly grounded agreement that one set of conclusions is more probable than others.10 Hirsch offers as his working assumption the principle that “each interpretive problem requires its own distinct context of relevant knowledge” (V, vii). This constitutes, equally, my axiom for validity in annotation. The annotator’s aim is thus two-fold: to identify the “interpretive problem,” and to determine the “distinct context of relevant knowledge” relevant to it. In my earlier chapter on the phenomenology of annotation I

792 discussed in detail the assumptions that these assertions entail—the nature of consensus, meaning as a condition of consciousness, the hermeneutic circle thus conjured—but essentially, I endorsed Hirsch’s principle of validity and related it to that of scientific method, specifically the claim that “validity” does not entail “truth” or “certainty” but is rather the affirmation of the best possible hypothesis, to be replaced when another of greater elegance or explanatory power is advanced.11 Nor does it restrict polysemy to multivalent Mormons: a valid hypothesis might well identify ambiguity or indeterminacy as essential to its formulation. Nevertheless, an insistence on validity in annotation, in my understanding, tempers the Cartesian arrogance of assertion with a Geulincxian sense of humilitas, in that any affirmation must be at best partial and provisional. Two examples are in order. One may be familiar from Hugh Kenner’s The Pound Era, namely the “Song” from Shakespeare’s Cymbeline: “Golden lads and girls all must / As chimney-sweepers come to dust.”12 Many have delighted in the contrast of youth and age, the pun on “come to dust”, gold (braving time) offsetting dust (the wages of mortality), and the chill of closure as “must” rhymes with “dust” as if to clench the inevitability of Beckett’s favorite biblical verse: “for dust thou art, and unto dust shalt thou return” (Gen. 3:19). But Kenner tells of the Warwickshire rustic blowing the grey head off a dandelion: “We call these golden boys chimney-sweepers when they go to seed.”13 And all is clear: dandelions, shaped like a chimney-sweeper’s brush, wilt in the sun and cannot endure the winter’s rages. It is an image both radically new yet also familiar: death as the blowing of a common flower. So a new hypothesis emerges: something, previously unperceived, is now a presence, precipitating consciousness, generating an interpretation of greater elegance and explanatory power, and thus offering a better reading. A less dramatic instance demonstrates the same principle. A few pages into How It Is, the narrator throws away empty tins “with the gesture of one dealing cards and also to be observed among certain sowers of seed” (HII, 7). These are clear visual images, but perhaps little more. Nor is the French text obviously different: “d’un geste de donneur de cartes et qu’on peut voir aussi chez certains semeurs de grain.”14 Yet a French reader, more easily than an English one, might see in “semeurs de grain”—despite the masculine plural form—the image of a commonplace French postage stamp (1903 to 1932, with occasional issues thereafter), depicting “La Semeuse.” The iconography of the French text, I suggest, at this point differs subtly from that of the English, and the proposed annotations should reflect that subtle difference. (See Fig. 1.) To summarize, annotation assumes a Joycean omniscience, but the annotator works with a Beckettian impotence and ignorance. Annotation, thus defined, implies the identification of a textual or critical crux, and the determination of the appropriate “context of relevant information” pertinent to that crux. Yet there cannot be, in either principle or practice, any guarantee that the problem has been correctly defined or that any explanation advanced is valid. For the understanding of the problem may change, the terms that constitute its apparent resolution may be invalidated as conditions vary, or it may be replaced by others of greater explanatory power. The art of annotation, by this definition, is an aesthetic of failure, and the only plausible outcome is the resolution to fail better.

This means thrusting through the primeval mud and impenetrable dark; breaking into the hermeneutic circle of part and whole by identifying, however provisionally, interpretive challenges before proceeding, however tentatively, to determine the information relevant to that problem. As this aspires to the light of omniscience, it invites sardonic comparison of the annotator with Faust’s servant, Wagner, seeking illumination from his master’s books:

Mit Eifer hab’ ich mich der Studien beflissen; Zwar weiß ich viel, doch möcht’ ich alles wissen.

[I have applied myself zealously to learning; I know a great deal, for sure, surely I can know everything.]15

Such gestures are melodramatic, for we crash through hermeneutical hoops quite cheerfully all the time. As practical critics, and in this respect like M. Jourdain, we have been making prosaic hypotheses all our lives. The art and science of annotation is to encourage better, which is to say more valid, readings. As Hirsch says, also sardonically, the circle is rather “less mysterious and paradoxical than many in the German hermeneutic tradition have made it out to be” (V, 77).

To reach for another metaphor, the zealous annotator seeks to strike a rich vein, but gleams of pay-dirt must be sifted from irrelevant dust or flecks of iron sulphite. Biographical digging locates lodes of intentionality, and once these are identified the tailings may leave traces of meaning. James Knowlson tells how the original French draft, like the English version some years later, was written at Ussy, which he had de

794 scribed to Alan Schneider as his “hole in the Marne mud.”16 The very history of that place (like a mole in Beckett’s lawn, narrator as Mole, now there’s a thought) burrows into the trenches of the First World War (tin-opener as bayonet? Surely not?), much as the early drafts of Fin de partie had done.17 The work may have been “slow and obnoxious,” but Beckett’s life left many traces in the text.18 Knowlson identifies the biographical circumstances behind the image of the child in a nightshirt at prayer, which appears in How It Is (10), and he notes Beckett’s visit to a friend in hospital, bearing a gift of marguerites, details that shape the Pam Prim interlude of Part 2.19 More could be made of these matters: a copy of the photograph of the child at prayer, taken by Dorothy Elvery, a Foxrock neighbor, is held at the Harry Ransom Humanities Center, Austin, having survived being torn up by O in Film (CSB, 195, 260).20 Knowlson does not identify the hospitalized friend, although he refers to the hospital and the approximate date of the visit, which Beckett had described to Lawrence Harvey. Nor does he pay more than passing homage to the motif of marguerites, which blend with the pretiosa margarita of Thomas à Kempis and other pearls (CSB, 574). Even a comment as casual as that by Anthony Cronin, to the effect that Beckett at Ussy was virtually living off cans of soup—principally Heinz minestrone—may say something about tins and tin-openers.21 The annotator of the bilingual text, naturally, must differentiate between biographical details specific to the original French text, those pertinent to the English version, and those common to both. Thus, Beckett’s letter to Barbara Bray of 11 March 1959, in which he tells of struggling along with the new moan, “trying to find the rhythm and syntax of extreme weakness,” relates immediately to Comment c’est, even though that syntax would condition the English writing to follow, deepening, or perhaps “shallowing,” its exploration of impotence and ignorance.22 Archival materials, likewise, testify to the etiology and genesis of the text. The notebooks and early drafts (manuscript and typescripts) of Comment c’est are either at the Harry Ransom Humanities Center, Austin, or at the Beckett International Foundation, Reading.23 These comprise notes, an autograph manuscript in six notebooks, and several typescripts, most of which are heavily revised. Both archives possess versions of small sections of the text; some drafts, but others segments destined for journals, such as “L’Image” and “Découverte de Pim,” which appeared before the publication proper of Comment c’est.24 Likewise, some English extracts were pre-published, often with significant differences from the later text.25 With this exception, archival evidence for How It Is is less extensive, consisting essentially of an untitled original manuscript, held in Reading. This signifies a major difference of emphasis between the two texts. The drafts and variants of Comment c’est testify to the care and pain that went into the creation of what became, ultimately, a short roman, for as well as evidence of changed intention there are elaborate calculations—“j’ai toujours aimé l’arithmétique” (Cc, 45)—paradigms, tables, notes, doodles, and diagrams.26 The French text, created ex nihilo (as it were), thus has an archival orientation that the English text lacks, since its French semblable is its acknowledged template. Archival material offers the annotator empirical evidence not only of the genesis of a text but of relationships between its parts; links that might not otherwise be ap

parent, such as a pause during the process of composition that translates into a textual 795 hiatus: “tant pour le moment” (Cc, 11). This phenomenon is of considerable interest with respect to Trois romans, where changes of date, place, ink, and attitude in the HRHRC manuscript notebooks are directly implicated within the narrative action of the novels, but it may also be pertinent to the compositional structure of the French text. A Critical-Genetic Edition/Une édition critico-génétique makes some material available; but an electronic genetic edition of Comment c’est/How It Is of the kind now planned would present archival sources comprehensively, so that draft materials from the various French and English versions can be hyperlinked and/or cross-referenced within an annotation.27 Indications of new paradigms that reflect incommensurabilities between the two cultural traditions would be of particular interest, such as the use of metric or imperial units. Linear measurement offered Beckett little difficulty; when crawling through the mud the difference between a meter and a yard is insignificant, and the geometrical calculations drawn up in the notebooks were unaffected by this change of base, which, nevertheless, may explain Beckett’s preference for yards over feet as the basic unit of extension. Units of weight are not so easily transposed. Despite the useful homonymy, the “sacs” of Comment c’est weigh “cinquante kilos” (Cc, 10), about 112 pounds, whereas the “sacks” of How It Is are “five stone six stone” (HII, 4), some 70 or 84 pounds. But household coal in England and Ireland was not delivered in stones. It arrived either in sacks, each a hundredweight—112 pounds, the official measure—or in “sugarbags,” much lighter. Both were made of jute, and were equally “horsy” (HII, 37). Given the obvious commensurability of “cinquante kilos” and “hundredweight,” I cannot explain the change, and can make only a feeble witticism about “lessening effects” in the English text. But given the care that Beckett took with the physical laws of his kosmoi, and the “massive” difference this makes to the burden of the respective narrators, an explanation is warranted.

Equally intriguing is the analysis of the residua that remain as a consequence of changed intention. As Cordingley has noted, one original link between the L’Innommable and Comment c’est, which takes as its theme the need to “commence” from where the earlier text could not go on, was the story of St Anthony and the tropes of desert asceticism (BI, 147). The setting of “Pim” (as Comment c’est was entitled in its six notebooks) was to be the desert, rather than the mud of Leopardi. The narrator would be an ascetic, a “monstre des solitudes” visited by “explorateurs” who “s’enfuient dans un endroit désert” and bring him skins (Cc, 18).28 This episode was further reduced in each rewrite, the English rendition finally offering only the sense of “life then without callers present formulation” (HII, 8) and the unforgettable image of the monster of the solitudes who, trusting man whom he sees for the first time, does not flee: “explorers bring back home his skin among their trophies” (HII, 8). A detailed annotation would reflect this fascinating narrative of textual evolution.

The French and English versions of a bilingual text reflect the different literary and cultural traditions to which each relates. Perhaps the trickiest problem faced by the bilingual annotator is to retain a sense of total textual unity in the presence of such diversity. For example, the French text has many references to Pascal, whom Beckett

796 was reading intently during the composition process. As Cordingley notes, Beckett “saw in Pascal a figure onto whom he could project his perception of the absurdity of his own exploitation of ‘ignorance’” (BI, 131). But however intimate Pascalian references to asceticism and grace might be in the French text, their subtlety is lost in English. This is not to say that some elements of Pascal do not survive translation: the iterated “business” at the end of How It Is has a resonant sense of Pascal’s “distractions” that save us from the abyss (CSB, 262). It is arguable, too, that having rejected St Antony’s asceticism and deciding to align the latter half of Part I of Comment c’est with Dante, Beckett, writing in France, may have drawn on French traditions of Dante scholarship. Yet when he began his English version the theme was redefined in terms of the struggle of incipient form to emerge from existential formlessness (CSB, 259). Equally, the Dante from whom Beckett drew his chief images was, among others, that of Cary’s translation of Inferno VII, 121–26: “Fix’d in the slime . . . but word distinct can utter none” (CSB, 259–60), a return to Leopardi’s “E fango è il mondo” which, many years previously, he had used as epithet for his study of Proust (CSB, 316, 460). This is a complex detail, and only one of many like it, but it suggests that when Beckett engaged with the English version of the text he reverted, to a greater extent than is usually admitted, to his native English traditions. Although this is an hypothesis I wish to test rather than assert, yet it has a corollary. Subsequent rewritings of Comment c’est reflect clearly a “lessening” effect, and How It Is in many respects is much sparser than its French counterpart, and yet the authorial process of deliberate elimination meets an equal and opposite principle of perhaps unconscious resistance. That is, the English text tends to be more deeply allusive than its original. Few would dispute that the text of Comment c’est/How It Is has undergone from the first drafts to the English version a process of deliberate impoverishment. Punctuation is réduit or gone to nothing: capital letters, commas, and full-stops are eliminated to simulate breathless pace, and syntactic ambiguity is consequent on this reduction. Similarly, the movement away from the imperfect tense in early French drafts towards the “mythological present” of the English text is evidenced in consecutive variants as a conscious exercise in diminution. And subjectivity is reduced when the “je me disais” (Cc, 12) or “je prends la corde” (Cc, 14) of the French text is decollated to “saying to myself” (HII, 5) or “take the cord” (HII, 7). Yet this pattern of diminution may not apply to the language of the English text. For instance, the eyes of the small boy on the verandah in Comment c’est are upcast to the sky, “d’où nous vient le secours” (Cc, 19). Although this is explicitly Biblical (Ps. CXXI:1), the language of La Sainte Bible lacks the archaic intimacy of the King James equivalent, “whence cometh my help.” To be sure, one might counter that “moïse” in Comment c’est (12), with its dual sense of Moses and wicker cradle—or in classic argot, être dans la moïse, to be totally broke, or “dans la merde”—has similar emotional power. But such dispute would be counterproductive, for as I have shown, the very nature of the bilingual text is the dialogue between enrichment and impoverishment, with effects possible in some languages but not in others.

And yet, and yet . . . nothing in the French original quite approximates the crescendo 797 of “yes” and “yes” at the end of the English text, which has unmistakable echoes of the end of Ulysses, and which challenges Joyce’s wish to end on a positive note with the only affirmation finally possible for Beckett, that it is “all balls, yes” (HII, 127). Nor does Beckett, in Comment c’est, refer to many of his earlier writings, with the curious exception of “ma seule saison” (Cc, 30), which echoes the short poem “vive morte ma seule saison” with its images of flowers and nests ground into the mud. The echo is implicit but inaudible in English: “my only season” (HII, 24–25). In How It Is, however, Beckett replaces “parfaitement” (Cc, 19) with “yes I assure you” (HII, 10), which assumes increasingly the force of a leitmotif as it echoes insistently the last line of “Enueg II”: “doch doch I assure thee” (CSB, 180); the usual French equivalent elsewhere is simply “mais oui.” Likewise, the “colonne brisée” of the defenestrated Pam Prim (or the equally misfortunate Skom Skum) lacks in French the echo of Robert Burton’s Anatomy of Melancholy, his epithet for a faithful wife that Beckett had recorded in the Dream Notebook and used in “Echo’s Bones” and Dream of Fair to Middling Women (CSB, 77).

Instances could be multiplied, because the text, for all its brevity, is replete with curious conundrums. Here are a few teasers, not unlike those that Moran puts to himself (M, 174–6; MF, 226–28), each needing its precise context of relevant information (but with no attempt here to provide that). Does “un voix ancienne” (Cc, 9) differ from “an ancient voice” (HII, 3)? Why does the German word “Blütezeit” (“flowering time”) appear in the French text (Cc, 12), but not in the English? Does the English ear hear in “quaqua” (Cc, 24) an echo of “caca”? Is “greenhorn” (HII, 17) too contrived an equivalent of “un bleu” (Cc, 27)? Am I alone (sole elect) in hearing in the “those awful syllables first asparagus” (HII, 20) a hint of the linguistic triviality that reputes “asparagus” to be the longest morpheme in English? Does the “champs de course” (Cc, 35), with its white rails and old rose, suggest for the French reader as readily as for the Irish one Leopardstown racecourse? Does “aussitôt” (Cc, 72) have the quasi-legal resonance of “instanter” (HII, 54; CSB, 276)? If “défenestré” (Cc, 81) in French is as arcane as “defenestrated” (Prague, 1618) in English, then why is it that the latter “falls out of the window” (HII, 56)? Can the English light “above” (73) assume anything of the irony of Diderot’s “là-haut” (Cc, 88)? Does “Kram Neuf” (101), greatest of them all, honor Beethoven or Schubert?

These examples have been chosen, not quite at random, to accentuate the point that the bilingual text is embedded within two linguistic and literary cultures, neither of which (concubine or consort) can be embraced without irritating the other. Their effect is to suggest the implausibility of any overarching theoretical framework, and hence to defend the “present formulation,” namely, acknowledging the demented particularity of each interpretive problem, and determining the relevant details accordingly. Yet these examples also hint at something that is more difficult to explicate or even to illustrate, yet must be a crucial part of this activity: a recognition of the semiotic function of the phonic and prosodic features of language, phonemes, suprasegmentals, tone, and stress; in a word, rhythm; in two, auditory imagination. Rather than list further

798 examples, I simply affirm that this auditory element is of the utmost importance in the annotative response to the bilingual text, in that a dual reading requires sensitivity to the phonic elements of both languages. These matters are often ignored, yet at every point the auditory experience comes into play: the narrator’s hand reaches through the mud in Comment c’est, to find “au lieu de la fange familière une fesse” (23). The English text, with its “instead of the familiar slime an arse” captures the basic sense, and even the rhythm, but fails to fashion phonic felicity (HII, 13). Again, the approach must be pragmatic. The annotator cannot endorse any one mode of analysis but must emphasize the auditory dimension as one of the most important aspects of discourse to be taken into account when defining the appropriate context of relevant knowledge. Which is why, perhaps, annotation is finally more an art than a science: there are rules (principles determining validity, humilitas, no sin except stupidity), but in the end awareness is all. That awareness, moreover, must include the audible, the emotional, the ironic, and the aesthetic as much as the visible and the rational.29 Enough has been said to substantiate the initial proposition that the texts of Comment c’est and How It Is, though intimately related, are nevertheless different, each with its own idiosyncrasies that, cumulatively, distinguish it from the other, to which it is nevertheless bound. How, then, to annotate this bilingual text, so as to do justice to both the French and English components? My answer is the obvious one: just as an edition of a bilingual text will normally present the two versions as mirror images on facing pages, in Beckett’s works, with the original French on the versos and the later English on facing rectos, so might their annotations be presented bilingually. One would be set in French, relating primarily to Comment c’est, to the left; and another, an equivalent set in English, to the right. The two are the same in that they reflect an identical structure and argument, yet they are different since each bears a primary allegiance to the text of its own language. Each anticipates elements that work with other notes in that language; Diderot’s “là-haut” in the French, for example; or the movement towards the “mythological present” in the English. In principle, it ought not to matter which set of annotations, the French 799 or the English, might be written first, for each should mirror the other faithfully, save in the respects where their emphasis must differ.30 Either might form a template for the other. In practice, given the unlikelihood of any one commentator with the requisite mastery of both languages and cultures, it means deploying one annotator, with an intimate knowledge of the French text, and a reasonable sense of the English, to work closely with another, one with an intimate knowledge of the English text, and a reasonable awareness of the French. Each annotator would respond to the other and modify his/her notes for reciprocal improvement. That way, two sets of annotations should ensue, each with its own integrity but closely integrated with the other. This outcome, suitably, would be not a translation but another bilingual re-creation.
