An Unexpected Beginning:
Sex, Race, and History in T. S. Eliot's Columbo and Bolo Poems

Gabrielle McIntire

[res]

In the period from 1909—1922 when T.S. Eliot was writing and publishing poems such as "The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock," "Portrait of a Lady," "Preludes," and The Waste Land—poems that firmly established his reputation as one of the major poets of the century—he was simultaneously composing a long cycle of intensely sexual, bawdy, pornotropic, and satirical verse that has only recently come to light. Centered on the seafaring adventures of a colonial explorer named "Columbo" (Eliot uses a pseudo-Italian version of Christopher Columbus's name) and his encounters with two native inhabitants of Cuba, "King Bolo and his Big Black Bassturd Kween," these poems comically and obscenely portray the history of early European expansion as an orgy of uncontrollable desire and deviant sexuality. Columbo's voyages and his first contacts with the King and Queen of Cuba take place by, through, and for sex, and they re sodomy, masturbation, miscegenation, scatological rituals, and rape as the modus operandiof colonial expansion. Eliot continued to write this extensive cycle throughout his life, and portions of it he shared privately with a homosocially arranged coterie of male writers, including Conrad Aiken, Clive Bell, Bonamy Dobrée, James Joyce, Wyndham Lewis, and Ezra Pound.Taken as a corpus, the poems (untitled by Eliot) allegorize Eliot's concerns with his nascent reputation as a poet, his anxious desire for publicity, his exile from the United States, and his uneasy relation to issues of race, sex, and colonialism. They explode reigning myths about Eliot's asexuality, and they demand radical rereadings of the place of sex, race, history, and desire in Eliot's poetic and critical oeuvre.

Shockingly different in form, kind, and content from Eliot's canonical poems, the Columbo and Bolo stanzas, with sing-song, adolescent rhythms and rhyme schemes that seem more appropriate to schoolboy doggerel than to the poetry of a major literary re, would hardly have done Eliot's literary reputation much good on the London literary scene of the 1910s and early 1920s. Nevertheless, as we will see, Eliot actively tried to get them published. In what appears to be two of the early stanzas—in terms of the New World voyage of discovery that Eliot charts—Columbo is preparing to leave Spain under the patronage of Queen Isabella and King Ferdinand, and the following scene takes place:

One day the king & queen of Spain
They gave a royal banquet
Columbo having passed away
Was brought in on a blanket
The queen she took an oyster fork
And pricked Columbo's navel
Columbo hoisted up his ass
And shat upon the table.
Columbo and his merry men
They set sail from Genoa
Queen Isabella was aboard
That famous Spanish whore. 1

The penetrating "prick" of the queen's "oyster fork" is apparently enough to resurrect Columbo from the dead: prodding him—indeed, marking him with the threat of rape—with her phallic utensil, as she mixes sex and appetite to awaken him to his scatological act. Columbo returns the queen's "prick" with a contrary but commensurate response, marking his immanent departure of discovery with a gesture of anal excrement. Evidently we are meant to read her "prick" as phallic, since it repeats a phallic moment just seven lines earlier where Eliot writes anti-Semitically that when Columbo went to see a doctor in Spain, "Where doctors are not many / The only doctor in his town / Was a bastard jew named Benny," who "filled Columbo's prick / With Muriatic Acid" (IOTMH, 315). Eliot's rendition of the colonial historical imaginary begins with a mocking critique of its origins, which are now located in the performance of infantile and anal phantasies involving a non-verbal exchange between patron and subject, one whose signification operates as both erotic tribute and offensive rebuke.

Indeed, anal excrement will be the great, expendable form of currency and expression in these poems: it is alternately deposited at formal social occasions, thrown, used as a sexual turn-on, depicted as the goods Columbo brings back to Europe from the New World, and proposed as the substance of a meal. Later on, within the social structure Columbo finds in Cuba, "King Bolo's swarthy bodyguard . . . A wild and hardy set of blacks" will echo and repeat Columbo's gesture of anal excrement in front of Queen Isabella: while Bolo is lying "down in the shade / His royal breast uncovering / They mounted in a banyan tree / And shat upon their sovereign" (IOTMH,316). Part insult, part sexual exhibitionism and even sexual flattery, anal excrement is omnipresent in these poems, and asks us to rethink the relation between colonialism and waste, pillage and bodily excess.

In the above stanza, Eliot is also already playing with the queer valences of "Queen"—as he will do elsewhere in the Bolo poems—to suggest not only a hyperbolically sexual character of colonialism, but also to propose that it thrives on deviant sexualities. Manly women who "prick" prompt a shocking admixture of resurrection and excrement. Eliot is at once homophobic in his rendering and homoerotically fascinated, and, as will be shown, the homoerotic content of these poems was central to the impetus for their creation and circulation among an all-male group of peers. Queen Isabella, Christopher Columbus's Spanish-Catholic patroness who favored his voyage while others looked more skeptically on his prospects, is red as both an ambiguously female and potentially queer "queen," while she is also the radically and misogynistically (though less dangerous) feminine "whore" who acts as the envoy for Columbus's early trans-Atlantic voyages. Her support of Columbus allowed him to become the major Atlantic explorer for Spanish royalty and "discoverer" of the "New World." Queen Isabella, who lived contemporaneously with the great British "virgin," Queen Elizabeth—another major female re of rule who had to negotiate alternating cultural representations of her as whore and virgin—has been sexualized in more than one account, as it has been difficult for interpreters of colonial history to imagine a powerful queen without also imagining her sexual availability.

In terms remarkably similar to those found in Eliot's better-known poems, the categories of history and sex, and memory and desire, make themselves felt together and inseparably as Eliot proposes that to write a history of New World discovery is also, inevitably, to write a sexuality. In "Gerontion" , he res history both as intensely feminine and corporeal:

        Think now
History has many cunning passages, contrived corridors
And issues, deceives with whispering ambitions,
Guides us by vanities. Think now
She gives when our attention is distracted
And what she gives, gives with such supple confusions
That the giving famishes the craving.

(IOTMH, 32-38)

Although it has rarely been read as such, this passage refers rather explicitly to female genitalia as a metaphor for the permutations of history, and the Columbo and Bolo poems corroborate such an interpretation. In The Waste Land , "mixing memory and desire" is claimed initially as April's particular cruelty, but memory is thereafter unable to operate without always already intimating desire—whether for the language of Shakespeare, and the family romance his words invoke ("I remember / Those are pearls that were his eyes"), for the memory of Richmond, where "I raised my knees, / Supine on the floor of a narrow canoe," or for the homoerotic tribute to the drowned Phoenician sailor, to cite only three moments in the poem's complicated network of memory and desire. 2 Likewise, the Columbo and Bolo poems negotiate the intersections of cultural memory and erotic desire to propose, in a more sophomoric vein, that colonial discovery was about sexual conquest, and that to remember the origins of colonialism we would do well to laugh at its physical excesses.

To date, these Columbo and Bolo poems have received almost no critical attention and have been largely unexamined since their respective dates of (still partial) publication in 1988, 1990, and 1996. At present there is only one essay on the poems, apart from a few published reviews treating Ricks's 1996 Inventions of the March Hare (in which the bulk of the stanzas first appeared), and the occasional indications in biographies and critical guides that Eliot wrote some "bawdy" verses about Christopher Columbus. 3 It is difficult to know where to begin with these bawdy verses, since they are as offensive as they are fascinating, as dangerous as they are subversive. They play recklessly on prohibitions, offering a satirical poetics of desire and memory whose comic edge is always in danger of collapsing into the outright racism, homophobia, and misogyny that they ventriloquize, repeat, and critique. Yet to read them without addressing their comic-book humor would be to oversimplify them radically, and would be to refuse the playful interruption they offer to the still-dominant but all-too-narrow reading of T.S. Eliot as a conservative representative of high literary modernism and its critical correlative, the New Criticism.

Given Eliot's desire for their publication, the extended period of their composition, and the range of issues that they tropologically invoke, we cannot simply dismiss them as "mere" juvenilia. The Columbo and Bolo stanzas are not only an occasional literary preoccupation: their composition spans Eliot's entire adult lifetime and the body of work they represent is substantial, totaling at least seventy-five stanzas in all. So far, twenty-nine of these have been published—including nearly ten in Valerie Eliot's 1988 The Letters of T.S. Eliot: Volume One, 1898-1922, seventeen in Christopher Ricks's 1996 Inventions of the March Hare, two in The Faber Book of Blue Verse—and dozens more sit unpublished in archives. 4

In his 1998 review of Inventions of the March Hare, Alex Zwerdling argues that "the cultural dispensation from which Eliot emerged demanded virtual silence on the subject of erotic life. Though he would free himself from it in part, the unpublished poems show that his imagination constantly brooded on the forbidden subject in ways that would have shocked his original readers". 5 Zwerdling also notes that in the sexually explicit sections of the draft for "The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock"—"Prufrock's Pervigilium," which appears in Inventions of the March Hare for the first time—one "finds not only 'lonely men in shirtsleeves, leaning out of windows,' but 'women, spilling out of corsets,' standing in the entries of 'evil houses leaning all together' and 'pointing a ribald finger at me in the darkness.'" 6 Zwerdling's remarks might equally apply to the more shocking Columbo and Bolo verses. In these, Eliot goes much further. Instead of synecdoche ("spilling out of corsets" for female body parts) Eliot offers an exaggerated and explicit bawdiness, in which the poems' res are capable of extraordinary cartoon-like feats, and exist without fear of death or repercussions of any kind. Consider these cartoon-like images: "A bullet came along the road / And up Columbo's asshole;" when he is "sick-o" he "filled the pump with argyrol / And rammed it up his prick-o;" Columbo "spun his balls around his head / And cried 'Hooray for whores;'" and following the dictates of navigation, the crew "turned [Columbo's] asshole S.S.W." (IOTMH, 318, 319). As we discover when reading the Columbo and Bolo verses, Eliot was hardly a passive victim of sexually suggestive finger pointing: he himself shakes quite a "ribald" finger at his original and later readers in these verses.

Eliot not only mocks historical and quasi-historical res of the early colonial era, but he does so while simultaneously parodying poetic form and expression. As in his other poems, Eliot pushes traditional generic boundaries, in this case by mixing aspects of several forms of traditional light bawdy verse, including the limerick and the clerihew, to offer a peculiarly modernist, self-conscious form of pornotropic doggerel. He called his Columbo and Bolo poems "ballads," and imagined setting them to music one day. Jonathan Gill argues that the verses reveal an "affiliation with songs found in any number of popular anthologies of Negro song," and claims that they borrow conventions from minstrelsy song by featuring "phonetic spelling, improper syntax, and highly charged and malleable rhythms," as well as a "double tone". 7 As Francis Teague writes of the clerihew, we might also say that this verse provides "a cockneyed look at the great"—of historical conquest, in this example. The clerihew "differs from other light verse forms by its roughness: while other light verse is polished, the clerihew is deliberately clumsy, and might be said to burlesque a form such as the limerick." 8 In effect, the form thus mirrors the poems' content, burlesquing traditional literary genres at the same time that the poems parodyreceived narrative histories about the legend of Columbus.

1 Public Desires and Private Circulations:
Eliot's Coterie

Who were Eliot's intended readers? Eliot had been writing the Columbo and Bolo poems at least since he was an undergraduate at Harvard (1906-1909), and it is likely that he shared them aloud with several of his male friends from that time onward. 9 The earliest record we have of their circulation appears in July 1914, immediately before the beginning of the First World War, when Eliot included them in several letters to Conrad Aiken, a slightly younger friend with whom he had worked on The Harvard Advocate. At the time, Eliot was studying German at a summer-language program in Marburg that would soon be cut short by Germany's declaration of war against Russia on 1 August, 1914. Eliot, in other words, began sharing his parody of early European colonialism with others at precisely the moment when Europe was entering the First World War. The Columbo and Bolo poems thus gesture backward to relatively distant historical "origins" exactly when a new era of European national and international politics was unfolding. That is, they look backward while reflecting the preoccupations of the new, using a historical rendition to critique the continued operations of colonialism within the modernist moment whose avant-garde demanded formal and thematic innovation.

Eliot persisted in writing these verses throughout his lifetime, and he gradually expanded the circle of friends with whom he shared them. Although at present we do not have access to the very latest poems, we know from Aiken that Eliot was still writing them until close to his death. Aiken wrote a letter to Eliot and his wife Valerie in December 1964, less than a month before Eliot died on 4 January 1965, expressing regret that this year they would not have their usual exchange of Columbo and Bolo poems: "But o dear we shall miss our annual meeting in New York and the exchange of Bolos and lime rickeys at the River Club or Vanderbilt." 10 Astonishingly, the Bolo verses were the ones Eliot most unceasingly returned to and produced.

Their circulation seems to have been initially prompted by conditions of both literary and personal history—in part by the arrival of Wyndham Lewis's first issue of Blast on the London literary scene in June 1914, and in part by Eliot's growing disillusionment with the academic establishment of which, in 1914, while still a Ph.D. student in philosophy at Harvard, he believed he was destined to be a part. Their circulation thus stems from overlapping public and private desires: evidence of his wish to participate in the public moment of the appearance of Blast's second issue, tokens of a personal desire to parody and distance himself from the pedantic milieu that he was soon to leave.

The circulation of the Columbo and Bolo poems begins, importantly, with mimicry—a mode they will continue to reflect as they alternate between parody, satire, and outright repetition of racist codes and topoi. Deliberately echoing the sort of humor found in Lewis's new journal, in which Lewis alternately mocks ("Blasts") and praises ("Blesses") public res according to satirical moral judgments of them, Eliot begins his letter to Aiken with the following heading:

BLESS
                COLUMBO
BOLO BLUBUNG CUDJO
THE CHAPLAIN BRUTUS SQUIRTY PANSY
BLAST
THE BOSUN COUSIN HUGH THE COOK
PROF. DR. KRAPP

A jocular letter ensues that pokes fun at academics and includes one of Eliot's accomplished comic-like pen drawings ( 1). The drawing shows Bolo as a rather large, bald man of ambiguous race, smoking a cigar, decked out with a monocle and a polka-dotted bowtie. Beneath him the caption reads "VIVA BOLO!!" (LOTSE, 43). Bolo seems rather pleased with himself, and appears to be ogling something or someone outside the frame of the drawing. Behind him is a mountain which places him in the rocky landscape of contemporary Germany. (Eliot depicts this again a week later in a letter to his American cousin, Eleanor Hinkley [LOTSE, 48]). Bolo is thus curiously distant, temporally speaking, and present at the same time: he is simultaneously the imaginary King of Cuba from Columbus's day, and a symbolic, timeless re with infinitely changeable historical locations—a gesture that asks, in another way, that we read Eliot's fascination with Bolo as reflective of his own contemporaneity.

Most of those whom Eliot chooses to "Bless" or "Blast" are historical or stock res that he parodies elsewhere in the Bolo cycle. While Christopher Columbus is the most explicitly historical re, King Bolo may also be modeled on a historical re named King Shamba Bolongongo who died in 1628, "ruler of the Kuba tribes, legendary for the number of widows and children he left." 11 Christopher Ricks suggests that Eliot was possibly aware of some early comedic ballads by W. S. Gilbert (of later Gilbert and Sullivan fame), whose poem, "King Borria Bungalee Boo," depicts a "man-eating African swell" with a small tribe of all-male "subjects." 12 Gilbert's ballads, which are disturbingly racist without showing the least evidence that they are aware of being so, recount how King Burria's tribesmen wage war against a tribe of Amazon women ruled by "Queen Loo" in order to satiate their cannibalistic urges. While the Amazonian women decide that they will try to win over the all-male tribe peacefully through their coquettish ways, their sexual advances do not persuade King Burria's men, and eventually the king himself eats Queen Loo while his tribesmen feast on her subjects. As in Eliot's Columbo and Bolo poems, appetite and sex are commensurate with each other, and the two ends of the spectrum of war and peace turn out to be a choice between two forms of incorporation: cannibalism or sex, both involved with Eros and Thanatos.

The other res Eliot chooses to "Bless" or "Blast" are again either historical or representatives of a type. The redundantly titled "Prof. Dr. Krapp" is aptly named for the "Krapp" he produces, and is one of several fictional professors whom Eliot mocks at some length in the same letter, and then in his 1920s correspondence with Bonamy Dobrée. "Squirty Pansy" invokes the playfulness of slang to praise a sexually ejaculative homosexual male. (The OED cites 1929 as the first time "pansy" takes on a queer valence in English, defining it as "effeminate; homosexual; affected;" Eliot's use of the term precedes the OED's example by fourteen years. 13) The fact that Eliot "Bless[es]" "Squirty Pansy" should alert us again to the homoerotic content of the Bolo poems, and to a recurring investment Eliot had at this time with the queer. We might also note the preponderance of the alliterative "B's" and "C's" in Eliot's "Bless" and "Blast" tribute to Lewis: with the exception of "Squirty Pansy" all of his res begin with either a "B" or a hard "C" sound. Taking alliteration to ridiculous limits, Eliot joins in with Lewis's version of cultural critique and adopts Blast's idiom in order to express his own scatological and anti-academic satire of imperialism, one which is also ambivalent towards institutional religion.

Although Eliot "Bless[es]" the Chaplain, his attitude to religion in these stanzas is as vexed as his attitude to colonialism. Sundays in particular seem to be good days for masturbation and rape in the cycle, and they represent the only day of the week that is ever named. The chaplain himself is hardly a re of sexual purity, and he eagerly participates in the "spirit" of sex aboard the ship by whistling at King Bolo's Kween and buggering himself while at sea: "K.B.b.b.b.k. / Was awf'ly sweet and pure / She said 'I don't know what you mean!' / When the chaplain whistled to her" (LOTSE, 126). Eliot's register of buffoonery about the religious may strike us as rather surprising, given his conversion to Anglo-Catholicism in 1927. These poems show us an altogether different side of Eliot, a re more divided in his literary and religious convictions than we might now think, especially in the context of what Ronald Bush has articulated as "the reductive force of his most recent metamorphosis," whereby critics have insisted on reading him as a representative of formalism and orthodoxy. 14 As Michael Levenson argues about The Waste Land, "it has been too tempting for too long to assimilate the poem to the events that came later in the decade, even shortly later, to see it as safely predicting the dispositions of the mid and late twenties: the poem as proto-Anglican, crypto-classical, or neo-monarchist." 15

Immediately after the imitative tribute to Lewis, Eliot appends a Columbo and Bolo stanza to his letter:

Now while Columbo and his men
Were drinking ice cream soda
In burst King Bolo's big black queen
That famous old breech l(oader).
Just then they rang the bell for lunch
And served up—Fried Hyenas;
And Columbo said "Will you take tail?
Or just a bit of p(enis)?"

(LOTSE, 42)

The first stanza Eliot sends to Aiken is both disgusting and humorous, adolescent while it ventriloquizes racist and sexist stereotypes. Columbo and his all-male crew are evidently enjoying a dessert-like treat when suddenly "King Bolo's big black queen" "burst" upon the scene. This is apparently the queen's preferred mode of entrance, and in another stanza she arrives on board in the same fashion: "King Bolo's big black kukquheen / Was fresh as ocean breezes. / She burst aboard Columbo's ship / With a cry of gentle Jesus" (LOTSE,205). A disruptive element, the queen is immediately sexualized and propositioned by Columbo, as he wonders if she might "take" the strange (evidently native) food he offers. (It is nothing less than parts of a hyena, an animal known for its uncontrollable laughter, which in its own way emphasizes the hysteria underlying the encounter between Europeans and natives.) Eliot here participates in a racist imaginary that res blacks, and especially black women, as inviting sex and its attendant discourses, at once using and mocking a phantasmatic perception that had been well in place from the time of early colonialism.

Not only have black bodies been red in the white imagination as signifying an excess of sexuality, but, through their ostensible uncontrollability, they have also been situated on the border ground with homosexuality. bell hooks quotes Sander Gilman to propose that "'by the eighteenth century, the sexuality of the black, male and female, becomes an icon for deviant sexuality.'" 16 This date seems rather late, and Jonathan Goldberg, for one, has pointed out that part of the rhetoric of the very first European explorers and colonists involved classifying the native people of color as dangerously sexual, even sodomitical. "'They are all sodomites;' so reads the dispatch sent from Vera Cruz, July 10, 1519, in the first so-called letter of Cortéz." 17 Goldberg then argues that "once made, few reports failed to repeat the charge [of sodomy]." 18 Eliot was apparently well aware of these kinds of homophobic anxieties about New World discovery, and his Columbo and Bolo verses recall these associations only to redeploy them as modernist humor.

Like so many other modernists on both sides of the Atlantic during this period—including James Joyce, Ezra Pound, William Faulkner, and Ernest Hemingway—Eliot was fascinated with ventriloquizing racism. Here Eliot displays the racism of his culture, evidently, taking pleasure in compulsively reiterating the moral abjection of prejudicial discourse, while he repeats it with a palpable, self-conscious mockery that exposes it at its most attenuated and outlandish limits. This is prohibited language which marks the extremity of what can properly be spoken. "King Bolo's Big Black Bassturd Kween"—the most potent moment of naming in the cycle—disturbs because of the element of racial hatred it speaks, while it already troubles that racism by pointing to its own absurdity, turning the critical glance back upon itself to disclose more about the speaker than about the queen. Ventriloquizing as such is a kind of mimicry that takes pleasure in its model but revolts against it through the modes of mockery and parody, correcting it while exploiting it.

This, of course, does not get Eliot "off the hook" of racism. Yet by troping away from the culturally popular racism of the 1910s and 1920s to critique it while he participates in it, his stance toward racial difference appears more complicated. While Eliot evokes stock racist stereotypes that represent the colonies as filled with "savages" and "cannibals," he also inverts and mocks these stereotypes. In the extensive anthropological commentary on Bolo and Bolovian customs he concocts for Bonamy Dobrée, Bolo is hardly a "savage." He is the emblem of all that Eliot deems good about "civilization." Meanwhile, in the stanza above, "Bolo's big black queen" offers the disruptive element to the dessert party, but the obscene sexual advance comes from Columbo's words—as the representative of European politics and language—rather than from her body. Columbo needs no prompting for his lewdness: he is sexually out of control, and his mission centers on sex whether he is on board his ship or in the newly discovered colonies. The white colonial party carries and (quite literally) disseminates the mark of hyperbolic sexuality, and it is precisely their sexual ardor that functions as their most potent motivation for mixing with the native inhabitants. Yet the miscegenation of Columbo and the Big Black Queen operates less as that worried marker of the meeting and mixing of races in the last century than it does as a simple, albeit titillating, historical fact. The stanzas thus offer at least as much judgment on Columbo as they do on the native queen. That is to say that, if there is a moral stance to be taken, the obscenity in this instance lies with Columbo's perception of the queen, and not with the queen herself.

As Eliot expanded the group of friends to whom he sent the Columbo and Bolo material, his correspondents responded in turn by affirming the poems' masturbatory qualities. When Eliot told Wyndham Lewis early in 1915 that he would send him some of his ribald verses, Lewis in turn wrote to Pound: "He told me he had written a lot of filthy sexual verse, which, if he sends it, I shall hang in the W.C. He described it as Verlainesque, damn his shifty little eyes. Well, well." 19 Pound thought of them this way too: in 1922 Eliot announced he was going to send some of his Columbo and Bolo poems to Joyce. Pound retorted, "You can forward the Bolo to Joyce if you think it wont unhinge his somewhat sabbatarian mind. On the hole he might be saved the shock, shaved the sock" [sic] (LOTSE, 505).

Eliot's willingness to circulate these poems only to male writers, while simultaneously parodying the queerness of colonial history, participates in the epistemological pairings of "secrecy/disclosure" and "public/private" that Eve Kosofsky Sedgwick has argued are constitutive of societal structures of gay closeting. 20 Eliot was both very much in the closet about his queer fascinations, and halfway out of the closet because of his willingness to play with homoerotic titillation among the members of his coterie. Columbo and his men are preoccupied with anal eroticism, and regularly sodomize both each other and King Bolo's campily named "Kween." After a graphic scene of masturbation, when Columbo had grown "rooty" from "three weeks at sea," Columbo "took his cock in both his hands / And swore it was a beauty" before pouncing on the cabin boy: "Columbo grasped him by the balls / And buggered him in the ass-o" (IOTMH,317). In another stanza, "Columbo slid along the deck / And raped the smoke-room steward." 21

Eliot, in fact, seems to have arranged his little coterie much as coteries had been arranged in literary circles for centuries. As Wendy Wall writes in her discussion of Renaissance coteries, "the coterie used the circulation of texts to consolidate the bonds formed among an elite and primarily male group." 22 J.W. Saunders, in turn, argues that the Renaissance coterie functioned as:

. . . a finishing school where members polished each other's art, which, like the taste for clothes, or the ear for a compliment, or the aptitude for dancing or fencing or riding, was very much a matter of doing the right things in the right way, in a game where every man tried to dazzle and out vie his competitors. More seriously a group restricted, ideally at least, to those who were equals or near-equals in social status . . . [they] cooperated in the formulation of critical principles, a sense of values. 23

Although Eliot did not explicitly seek criticism from the members of his coterie for his Columbo and Bolo poems, he certainly sought to "dazzle and out vie his competitors" with the shocking content of his verses. Indeed, their circulation participates in and offers a pornotropic extension of the collaborative effort Eliot made with Pound while crafting The Waste Land, a collaboration that Wayne Koestenbaum and others have read as emphatically homoerotic. 24 Their "dazzle" involves what I want to call epistological titillation: the poems evoke the pornographic comic strip to try to shock and impress his male peers with the language of obscenity. There does not, however, appear to have been a commensurate response from all of his male friends, who, apart from Pound and Dobrée, did not reciprocate epistologically in the game of shock and titillation.

As I have suggested, Columbo's principal crewmembers are just as "obscene" as Columbo himself, including the cabin boy, the bosun, and the chaplain (a version of Charlie Chaplin in an early letter to Aiken, LOTSE, 126). Eliot's rendition of colonial exploration thus imagines a pronounced sexual affiliation among sailors that extends the tradition of homoerotic "comradeship" found in sailor fiction. For the most part, the Cuban natives also share the same propensity to anal and excessive eroticism as the colonists, and Columbo's crew thus find uncannily queer mirrors of themselves in the colonies. Offering an inverted version of Bhabhan mimicry, in which the colonized subject responds "through the repetition of partial presence" to "the narcissistic demand of colonial authority," King Bolo and his Queen seem to belonga priori to the same confraternity of anal and scatological eroticism. 25 The stanzas thus level difference by writing the colonial encounter as one in which a radical commensurability and sameness of desires—the game of sex—overrides dissimilarities of race, language, custom, history, region, and religion.

Yet the poems never once offer explicit judgment on their homosexual practices, and Eliot simply constructs queerness as intrinsic to the cycle. 26 The poems are a version of Eliot's sexual discovery, charting the evolution of a queer poetics through an allegory of New World discovery. In only one instance does the speaker self-reflexively comment on the obscenity of the explorers, and this happens when the cabin boy Orlandino is accused by the narrator of "obscene" language:

A child of upright character
But his language was obscene-o.
'Fuck Spiders' was his chief remark
In accents mild and dulcet.
They asked him what there was for lunch
And he simply answered 'Bullshit.'

(IOTMH,316)

Interestingly, the censorious comment does not cite the violently sexual or scatological acts, but notes the obscene words of one of the younger characters. Eliot is establishing a dichotomous and discontinuous set of values between the imagined narrator of these verses and the res who inhabit them: the speaker complains that Orlandino's language is obscene even though the stanzas themselves contain language much more hyperbolically so, thereby creating a mise en abîme that testifies to the almost endless play of possible allegories which these stanzas offer. Meanwhile, Orlandino's character is considered "upright" despite the fact that he is one of the more overtly queer characters in the poems—in a stanza Eliot sent to Aiken in September 1914, "the cabin boy was sav'd alive / and bugger'd, in the sphincter" (LOTSE,59). This valence continues, especially if we take into account the unpublished stanzas at Yale, which are even more expressly homoerotic than those published so far.

In his first Columbo and Bolo letter to Aiken, cited above, Eliot also mocks the projects of anthropology, history, and the academic establishment in general. He glosses his Columbo and Bolo poem to write:

The bracketed portions we owe to the restorations of the editor, Prof. Dr. Hasenpfeffer (Halle), with the assistance of his two inseparable friends, Dr. Hans Frigger (the celebrated poet) and Herr Schnitzel (aus Wien). How much we owe to the hardwon intuition of this truly great scholar! The editor also justly observes: 'There seems to be a double entendre about the last two lines, but the fine flavour of the jest has not survived the centuries.'—Yet we hope that such genius as his may penetrate even this enigma. Was it really the custom to drink ice-cream soda just before lunch? Prof. Dr. Hasenpfeffer insists that it was. Prof. Dr. Krapp (Jena) believes that the phrase is euphemistic (LOTSE,42).

The "double-entendre" refers, of course, to homosexuality, and Eliot mocks the phony earnestness that can attend academic labor. Eliot's disillusionment with the scholarly life began in full force while he was in Germany, and his stock German professorial res function as yet another caricature in this collection of poems. Academia, he suggests, is a dirty pursuit of the parenthetical, the excised, and the obscure. He writes a remarkable body of pornotropic poetry, then laughs at us for considering it.

2 Desiring Publicity:
Eliot as the Kween of Golder's Green

Given the strikingly bawdy character of these poems, it is also remarkable that Eliot actively tried to get the Bolo poems published. As it turns out, they are, prima faccia, the first face that Eliot wanted to show the world. After having participated epistologically in a teasing mimicry of Blast—desiring inclusion within its moment—Eliot sent several Columbo and Bolo stanzas to Wyndham Lewis early in 1915 for the second (and, what would be the last) issue of Blast. Lewis, however, rejected them, along with some of Eliot's other racy poems, "The Triumph of Bullshit," and "Ballade pour la grosse Lulu," which did not end up seeing publication until Ricks's Inventions of the March Hare in 1996. Lewis wrote to Pound to justify his decision: "Eliot has sent me Bullshit and the Ballad for Big Louise. They are excellent bits of scholarly ribaldry. I am trying to print them in Blast, but stick to my naif determination to have no 'words ending in -Uck, -Unt, and -Ugger.'" 27

Eliot reacted right away by writing a letter of complaint to Pound in February 1915:

I have corresponded with Lewis, but his puritanical principles seem to bar my way to Publicity. I fear that King Bolo and his Big Black Kween will never burst into print. I understand that Priapism, Narcissism etc. are not approved of, and even so innocent a rhyme as
    . . . pulled her stockings off
    With a frightful cry of 'Hauptbahnhof!!'
is considered decadent.

(LOTSE,86)

Eliot transforms Lewis's queasiness about offensive language into a teasing charge that Lewis's "puritanical principles" would keep Eliot from finding the "Publicity" he desires. Lewis did, however,publish some of Eliot's other verse ("Preludes" and "Rhapsody of a Windy Night" [sic]) in Blast. 28 Did Eliot consider "Preludes" and "Rhapsody" insufficient for the "Publicity" he hoped to achieve? Or did he find out after his complaining letter to Pound that Lewis would include these two poems? Was he hoping that obscenity would do the "publicity-work" for him?

The young Eliot was hoping for a bit of capital "P" "Publicity" from the Columbo and Bolo poems, and he imagined them as his first "burst" onto the London literary scene. His desire for publication at this time was "keen," and he was willing to risk being dangerously flamboyant to find the publicity he wanted. Importantly, in his complaint to Pound, Eliot gives an exact echo of the language he uses in the Columbo and Bolo verses when he describes the queen's mode of presenting herself; he suggests that his poems cannot "burst" upon the literary scene with the same ease that Bolo's "big black queen" "burst" onboard or "burst" upon the party of colonists "drinking ice cream soda." As elsewhere in his letters, especially to Pound, Eliot proposes that if writing is a sexual act, then publishing is its ejaculative fulfillment. Indeed, Eliot seemed to enjoy the valences of "burst[ing]" into print a great deal, and the term recurs in 1922 when he is about to publish The Waste Land in the first issue of his new journal, The Criterion. In this instance the tables are turned, and he gives Lewis a back seat, writing to Pound: "I think that if the Waste Land [sic] bursts out in the first number and you contribute to the second, that Lewis must remain behind the scenes until the third" (LOTSE, 553).

After he was rejected by Lewis, Eliot persisted in seeking publication for the Bolo poems for a number of years. Even as late as May 1921 Eliot was still hoping that the Bolo poems would find public circulation. Although he did not know Joyce well, he offers him a sample of the Columbo and Bolo poems in a letter of May, 1921. He enviously writes to Joyce after having had a "taste" of the Ulysses manuscript for which he claims to have "nothing but admiration," particularly for the bawdy sections, confessing (as did others), "I wish, for my own sake, that I had not read it" (LOTSE, 455). He compliments Joyce on the news that "even a limited and very expensive edition is to appear," asking "has it been properly circularised in England?" (LOTSE, 455). He admits that he has been having difficulty himself finding the publication and publicity he desired for his own "limited edition":

I wish that Miss Beach would bring out a limited edition of my epic ballad on the life of Christopher Columbus and his friend King Bolo, but
Bolo's big black bastard queen
Was so obscene
She shocked the folk of Golder's Green.

(LOTS0E, 455)

Eliot thus draws an analogy between Ulysses and the Columbo and Bolo cycle. He had already asserted a serious admiration for the scandalous sections of Ulysses in 1919, writing to John Quinn, friend, New York lawyer and patron of the arts, "I should like to do everything I can about it over here. The part of Ulysses in question struck me as almost the finest I have read: I have lived on it ever since I read it" (LOTSE, 314). The ribaldry of the scandalous, masturbatory homoerotic sections of Ulysses are not unlike that of Eliot's verse, and part of Eliot's frustrated admiration for Joyce could be due to the fact that Eliot met the censor before publication and "publicity," while Joyce met the censor after both publication and the immense publicity that is scandal.

As he will do elsewhere, in Eliot's letter to Joyce he blurs the distinction between his "epic ballad" and "Bolo's big black bastard queen:" both it (his poem) and she (the queen) are "so obscene" that they "shocked the folk of Golder's Green." Eliot thus makes Bolo's big black queen perform the allegorical work of obscenity; that is, she functions as a fictional objective correlative for his own desires for publicity. In his letter to Joyce Eliot draws on the shock-value of race and sex as markers of disruption in the precious atmosphere of suburban London and applies them to his unpublishable poetry. In effect, he constructs a tautologous narrative about his inability to publish: the disruptive elements of color and sex are too obscene for the "folk of Golder's Green;" because these elements are in the poems, no one will publish them; therefore both the poems and he himself are as obscene as the "big black queen."

In a letter to Conrad Aiken of 1916, Eliot included two more Columbo and Bolo stanzas as a gloss on his current situation. Here, as elsewhere, he explicitly uses the Columbo and Bolo poems to allegorize details of his own biography:

The news is that I am to be at Highgate School, near town, next term, that I am starting to rewrite my thesis, that my wife has been very ill, that I have been taken up with the worries of finance and Vivien's health, that my friend Jean Verdenal [to whom he dedicated the Prufrock volume in 1917] has been killed, that nothing has been seen of Armstrong who is now a captain in Kitchener's army, that compulsion is coming in, that my putative publisher will probably be conscripted, that we are very blue about the war, that living is going up, and that
King Bolo's big black bassturd kween
  That airy fairy hairy un
She led the dance on Golder's Green
  With Cardinal Bessarian.

(LOTSE, 125)

King Bolo's queen has by this time moved from Cuba to London, to the fashionable, Jewish neighborhood of Golder's Green, and she is on display as a dancer, paired with a powerful Catholic religious representative. But what work is this queen doing for Eliot? Her status as a foreigner of color with no proper parentage would mark her as a "bassturd" in the strictures of British society of the time, while Eliot again implies that the queen is not female at all, but is in fact a male "queen" or "fairy" in drag. Reminiscent of the women in "The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock," whose arms are "downed with light brown hair," (Lines 62-63), Bolo's queen is even more hirsute, lacking subtlety but still warranting the dialogue between erotic attraction and physical repulsion that we find in "Prufrock"—suggesting an androgyny verging on transgenderism which the queerness of Eliot's cycle would support.

In the same letter to Aiken, Eliot continues by claiming, "I am keen about rhymes ending in -een," and he attaches the following stanza:

King Bolo's big black bassturd kween
  Her taste was kalm and klassic
And as for anything obscene
  She said it made her ass sick.

(LOTSE, 125)

Eliot here takes a linguistic step to incorporate himself into his poems, playing with sound and meaning by alluding to his feeling of being "keen" for the "een" sound, and thereby effectively, performatively, enfolding himself into their rhyme scheme, and into the poems themselves. It is as if he himself is an extension of the "-een" rhymes in his cycle, or as if he were just as queer as his verses—as if he just might be the "kween of Golder's Green." Invoking sodomitical metaphors to insist on the queen's distaste for obscenity—"anything obscene" "made her ass sick"—he again proposes that it is not, in fact, the queen who is obscene, but the cultural dispensation that deems the fact of her large, queer, blackness obscene, and it is the "folk of Golder's Green" who are the markers and makers of obscenity. 29

The black queen is simultaneously a racist portrayal, a point of titillation, and a satire on racist, sexist, and homophobic formulations. She functions as the mark of difference in the middle-class culture of Golder's Green at a moment when even foreignness was akin to obscenity, and the Jewish suburb did not necessarily give shelter to other displaced groups. Eliot himself felt an unwelcome foreigner in London—he was classless in British society, and practically parentless at this time as well, since he had "fled" family when he left America straining relations with his father. In his letters, Eliot was preoccupied with "foreignness," and already in 1914 when studying in Germany, wrote to Conrad Aiken, "I think that this will be a very pleasant exile on the whole,—though I cannot look upon a summer in Germany as anything but an exile" (LOTSE, 41). This attitude changes very little during the first years in England, and he writes to his cousin Eleanor Hinkley in the fall of 1914, "I don't think that I should ever feel at home in England, as I do for instance in France . . . I should always, I think, be aware of a certain sense of confinement in England, and repression" (LOTSE, 61). In a letter to Mary Hutchinson of 1919 he writes, "but remember that I am a metic—a foreigner, and that I want to understand you, and all the background and tradition of you" (LOTSE, 318).

Instead of finding the publicity he desired for them, Eliot shared the Bolo poems privately over the years, usually as a satiric way to negotiate his dealings with the public world of publishing and notoriety, and the very private world of his identity as an émigré. Instead of the Bolo poems, Eliot's first publications, all in 1915, were "The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock," "The Boston Evening Transcript," "Aunt Helen," and "Cousin Nancy" in Poetry, "Preludes" and "Rhapsody on a Windy Night" in Blast, and "Portrait of a Lady" in Others. At the beginning of 1915 he was a young struggling poet, lucky to be befriended by the more established Ezra Pound. At the end of the year he was on his way to becoming the T. S. Eliot, the author who would be canonized by the Dial prize in 1922, the subject of intense critical attention in magazines and reviews of the 1910s and early 1920s, and then the canonical re constructed by the New Criticism of the late 1920s and 1930s. These became the early basis of Eliot's reputation, and began to shape the character of English modernism.

3 Eliot's Parergon

How can we negotiate the fact of these poems alongside the body of Eliot's published material? We might think of the Columbo and Bolo poems as a pornotropic parergon for Eliot's work. The term is borrowed from Conrad Aiken's allusion to the Bolo poems in a short essay called "King Bolo and Others" that he wrote to celebrate the occasion of Eliot's sixtieth birthday for a volume of tributes collected in 1948. Aiken's piece actually gives scant attention to the Bolo poems, but refers to them as a knowing insider, participating in the closeting of Eliot's verses that had by now been going on through his career. It is unlikely that any other contributor to the collection, with the exception of Clive Bell, knew the content of these verses.

Although he does not elaborate with a sample from the Columbo and Bolo verses, Aiken does give a rather interesting brief interpretation of the import of the Bolo poems to Eliot's oeuvre. He reminisces about their days together at Harvard:

What did we talk about? or what didn't we? It was the first "great" era of the comic strip, of Krazy Kat, and Mutt and Jeff, and Rube Goldberg's elaborate lunacies: it was also perhaps the most creative period of American slang, and in both these departments of invention he took enormous pleasure. How delighted we were with the word "dinge" for negro! This rich native creativeness was to be reflected, of course, in his poetry, notably in Prufrock . . . 30

Eliding the boundaries between the humor of the comic strip and racist "slang," Aiken brings racism and humor together under the single banner of undergraduate delight. But where did Aiken get this slang from? Why were he and Eliot so excited to borrow racist humor and terminology? As Hortense Spiller proposes, they would not have known many blacks, but "what they did know was that 'dinge' named a limit, a neologism and, therefore, an unnamable, beyond representability." 31 These poems also offer the mnemonic trace of Eliot's American identity. Turning to an allegory of New World discovery precisely at the moment he was choosing the Old World over the New himself, these poems worry the limits of Eliot's self-representation as a racial other, and his genealogical history as an American.

Aiken continues his tribute by asserting that Eliot's delight in slang and comic-book characters "gave rise to the series of hilariously naughty parerga which was devoted spasmodically to that singular and sterling character known as King Bolo, not to mention King Bolo's Queen, 'that airy fairy hairy—'un, who led the dance on Golder's Green with Cardinal Bessarion'" (TSEAS, 22). Quoting from Eliot's 1916 letter, Aiken over thirty years later again effaces race and obscenity from the equation to portray the "queen" simply as "Bolo's Queen" rather than his "big black queen." He thus teases his fellow contributors by offering what could only seem a silly, sophomoric-sounding rhyme without context—evacuating accountability and precluding the possibility that his audience might decipher the meaning of these allusions.

Aiken's notion that the poems operate as parerga is, though, a fitting metaphor: the poems are precisely "an extra ornament in art" (OED) insofar as the parergon is always that which can surprise us and tell us more about what we deem to be central to the text (whether literary or otherwise) than the center itself. Derrida articulates it in this way:

A parergon comes against, beside, and in addition to the ergon, the work done [fait], the fact [lefait], the work, but it does not fall to one side, it touches and cooperates within the operation, from a certain outside. Neither simply outside nor simply inside. Like an accessory that one is obliged to welcome on the border, on board [aubord, à bord]. It is first of all the on (the) bo(a)rd(er) [Il est d'abord l'à-bord]. 32

Eliot's Columbo and Bolo poems situate themselves for us on the "border" or the boundary of his other canonical poems, touching them by illuminating the limits of interpretation; neither entirely "outside nor simply inside," they "inscribe[] something which comes as an extra, exterior to the proper field . . . but whose transcendent exteriority comes to play, abut onto, brush against, rub, press against the limit itself and intervene in the inside only to the extent that the inside is lacking." 33 The pornotropic poems surprise us, illuminating and foregrounding Eliot's obsessions with sexuality, race, and the corporeal, revealing a complicated sexual imagination. In his birthday tribute, Aiken insists on the Bolo poems' value as social and academic critique: "These admirable stanzas, notable at times for their penetrating social criticisms were to continue for years as a sort of cynical counterpoint to the study of Sanskrit and the treatise on epistemology. Their influence on the development of a Style will no doubt come in due course to the attention of Herr Dr Krapp of Wien" (TSEAS, 22). Again, Aiken's audience of fellow contributors—not to mention his readers—would have had no idea what he was talking about: who might "Herr Dr Krapp" be exactly? As it turns out, he is us: academic scholars who are only now uncovering these poems to give them critical attention. Eliot writes a bawdy parergon to his poems, and then laughs at us for considering it, embracing us into the fold of humor and titillation as its newest objects of satire.
