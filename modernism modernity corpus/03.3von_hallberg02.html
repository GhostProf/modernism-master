Literature and History: Neat Fits

Robert von Hallberg

Our America and Nativist Modernism:
A Panel

I want to return to Marjorie Perloff's point about Michaels's terminology and periodization, in order to confirm her claim that the scope of Our America is less comprehensive than its subtitle suggests. The term in dispute is of course modernism, which is conventionally distinguished from a good deal of writing, painting, and music produced by twentieth-century artists. Modernism indicates some affiliation by resemblance or avowal across the generic boundaries of music, painting, and literature, or even intellectual disciplines like physics, philosophy, psychology, and sociology between the years--in Anglo-American academic literary history--1909, taking the first Futurist manifesto as the starting point, and maybe 1930 or a little later.

Michaels's nativist modernism is much more nativist than modernist: the connections of his authors to international trends in other arts or areas of expression are faint (William Carlos Williams and Langston Hughes are the exceptions). One reason this matters is that terms like modernism and romanticism are reserved for especially comprehensive historical trends, such as seem in motion when artists or thinkers in diverse media begin to collaborate. Michaels's subject is a distinct range of modern writing more than modernism. His period begins with the end of World War I and runs through the 1920s, which in literary-historical terms was a time of fruition more than inception. Modernism in the English language peaked in 1922, when The Waste Land and Ulysses appeared, and 1923, when William Carlos Williams's Spring and All was brought out at his own expense, and Ezra Pound wrote the Malatesta Cantos. A good fourteen years of intellectual and especially artistic agitation and a world war led to the peak of those years.

Michaels's nativist modernism is fearful of contact with the new immigrants and even more of African Americans. The attraction to incest in William Faulkner, and other authors, as Michaels shows, expresses just this phobia. Michaels presents a strong case for a close fit between literary and political history--Willa Cather's The Professor's House, for instance, and the Immigration Act and the Indian Citizenship Act of 1924 (OA, 30)--that serves our short-term professional interests well. Do literary historians engage the issues that determine the future of our America? Yes, certainly: the texts we teach address just the issues that govern debate about political and social policy. The long-term problem is exactly this reassuring fit. If literary history fits social and political history too snugly, it becomes redundant, after one has examined the history of legislation, say. Fear of immigrants in Congress, the courts, and then--small surprise--in novels and poems too.

Literary history is better justified just where it records some deviance from social, political, and institutional history. The deviance I have in mind is visible when writers dream of a different country than the one to which they are born. Michaels loves to expose the nativist-modernist folly--articulated by Calvin Coolidge--of becoming what you already are. But I think of Ernst Bloch's courageous advocacy of French surrealism in Heritage of Our Times just when Germany was enforcing Nazi conformity; 1 his claim, taken up by Fredric Jameson in The Political Unconscious, is that literary texts rightly desire a different world. Bloch wrote in 1918 that the "spirit of utopia exists in the ultimate predicate of all great expressions." 2 That is the revolutionary dimension of artistic expression. Michaels excavates a spirit of realism and conformity instead, or a neat fit between political history and artistic expression.

My point can be felt more concretely in the case of William Carlos Williams. Michaels rightly shows how Williams enthusiastically expresses Coolidge's impulse to become more American, but Williams's deviant imagination of rape and miscegenation complicates this picture. Spring and All is about violation, though significantly not incestuous violation. The core myth there is that of the abduction of Persephone, the crossing of virginal purity with hellish alterity. Remember too that Père Rasles, the male hero of In the American Grain , is distinguished by his will "TO MARRY . . . to hybridize, to crosspollenize" with the North American continent and its Indian inhabitants; Jacataqua, a female hero of the book, is distinguished as a leader by "her mixed French and Indian blood." 3 Her power and articulateness comes not from being one of a tribe of Indians (the rest of her tribe seems not to speak or act), but from being both European and Indian. Hybridity meant the invention of new subjects, of new life forms. Michaels's account of the many white writers who romanticized simply becoming Indians is only one part of the imaginative life of the time.

Williams, Marianne Moore (as David Kadlec has shown), and Pound were all fascinated by exogamy, hybridity, crossings. 4 Pound fondly remembered the brilliance of Henri Gaudier-Brzeska's conversation in prewar London; "It might be exogamy," Pound said of the topics, "or the habits of primitive tribes, . . . or aesthetics." 5 Pound's account of the merits of literary translation is red in just these terms: one literature needs the "cross" with its contrary, or other; this is the life of literature. He himself set out from a suburb of Philadelphia, where his parents did missionary social work with Italian immigrants at the beginning of the century, to begin his literary career in Venice. When he got to London he spent years hammering at British insularity, insisting above all that the English recognize their need for southern Mediterranean literary culture. When he returned to America in 1912, uncertain whether his literary future lay in the States or Europe, this is what he described:

I see . . . a sign in the surging crowd on Seventh Avenue (New York). A crowd pagan as ever imperial Rome was, eager, careless, with an animal vigour unlike that of any European crowd that I have ever looked at. There is none of the melancholy, the sullenness, the unhealth of the London mass, none of the worn vivacity of Paris. I do not believe it is the temper of Vienna.

One returns from Europe and one takes note of the size and vigour of this new strange people. They are not Anglo-Saxon; their gods are not the gods whom one was reared to reverence. And one wonders what they have to do with lyric measures and the nature of "quantity."

One knows that they are the dominant people and that they are against all delicate things. 6

It would require only a little archaeology on the first paragraph to show that Pound's vision of the urban crowd derives from the fin-de-siècle European race discourse about degeneration. This new strange people is a mongrel mass, as Pound sees it, and he admires it, even while fearing its rude vitality. This is the prose of the Progressive era, not Michaels's nativist-modernist period, and Michaels identifies a greater flexibility about race among Progressive writers than among their slightly later colleagues. Pound had only irony then for the American interest "in being itself." 7 He was, as Keith Tuma has shown, very influenced by Progressive policy, but was he to just that extent not a modernist? 8 Are we to think of the poet-critic who developed an immensely influential program for literary translation, related closely to this passage from Patria Mia, as simply working out the consequences in the 1920s of Progressive thought?

This is the moment to question the aptness of literary history positing a divide at 1918, as American political historians do, rather than at 1914, as American and European cultural historians commonly do. The difference in periodization here is short, in historical terms, but significant. Michaels's nativist modernism begins after World War I, which is to say after intellectuals had been forced to abandon dreams of a comprehensive European culture based on a confederation of European states--another Enlightenment. The surrender of that hope is just what enables Perloff and many cultural historians to distinguish the enthusiasm and radicalism of the avant guerre from the grimmer years that interest Michaels.

Michaels argues cogently that "America would mean something different in 1925 from what it had meant at, say, the turn of the century; indeed, the very idea of national identity would be altered" (OA, 2). His further claim is that international modernists such as Pound and T. S. Eliot were more of the old world than of the new, because they never saw the work that the concept of race would come to perform. My point then is that stay-at-home modernists like Williams and Moore, as well as international modernists like Pound and Eliot, saw the need to become not just what you already are but in fact something entirely new: namely, hybrids. How wonderful, really, that just when legislators were promoting exclusive immigration policies, some writers were exploiting not just that impulse, as Michaels shows, but its contrary too. That, I think, begins to look like a literary history that recovers some of the richness of an intellectual life worth living.

The issue of literary evaluation arises when one examines an understanding of American modernism that does not accommodate Pound, Eliot, or Moore. Michaels was recently asked by a student why we should admire or even care about his nativist-modernist authors who apparently excelled at constructing rationalizations for Negrophobic racism. Our America suggests that Faulkner, F. Scott Fitzgerald, Cather, and others are considerable authors exactly because they engaged meaningfully with the ideas and crises of their moment. This is an evaluative argument altogether familiar to readers of nineteenth-century criticism and social thought: literature is considerable because it takes up the live thought of its moment. Authors who labor with the senescent thought of their predecessors are by this account less considerable. For example, Henry James's preoccupation with class difference just when race was displacing class as a principle of social differentiation left him seeming passé or, in Michaels's words, "insufficiently alert to racism's potential as a technology of social organization" (OA, 56-57). James missed an opportunity to reinvent the social thought of his time. When Michaels speaks of the dissociation of race and culture, he is analyzing a step in the development of political thought or culture in America. His writers shaped that thought. However racist this thought was, it was not stupid, as he shows; Michaels's method so vigorously explicates the reasons behind this or that statement--

the reason for that is . . . [OA, 40]
the point here is . . . [OA, 41]
That's why . . . [OA, 54]
This is what it means for . . . [OA, 66]
it is for this reason that . . . [OA, 78]
Which is to say . . . [OA, 92]
The truth is that . . . [OA, 132]

--that it is not surprising that he implicitly esteems logic and effectively arrayed reasons, even when they entail ugly consequences.

Those authors whose texts were not efficiently governed by reasons and rationales--Gertrude Stein and Moore, but poets generally, come to mind--cannot fare well by this evaluative criterion. Another way of putting this is to say that Michaels's method of analysis depends on quite familiar modes of representation. The account of modernism that he intends to displace--viz., that modernism derived from a crisis in representation--cannot be fully met by Michaels's method of paraphrase and logical analysis. Stein's Tender Buttons , for instance, is not adequately engaged (nor does Michaels try) by analyzing this or that premise about national identity that may surface occasionally; nor can the author of Kora in Hell and Spring and All be adequately engaged by a critical method that deals comfortably with the pseudophilosophy of the posthumous Embodiment of Knowledge but rather avoids the complicating illogic and excess of his poems and expressionist prose. There is an important connection between Williams's imaginative attraction to hybridity and extreme exogamy--as in rape by numerous strangers 9 --and his belief that new poetic forms are "additions to nature." 10 Michaels's method does not attend closely to poetic form, let alone allow for it as a source of knowledge or innovation.

What, then, is a just assessment of Michaels's nativist modernism? Well, the more cosmopolitan modernism of James, Pound, Eliot, and Moore seems actually reinvigorated by this acute examination of their successors who made a too compliant peace with the ideas and even the laws of their time. Nativist modernism, 1918-29, appears a distinct and dishonorable (if not unique) chapter in the history of the Anglo-American and European international movement in the arts known as modernism. Michaels has fully demonstrated the need to subdivide the historical category of American modernism in order to accommodate his nativist modernism, but it will not do to let his term substitute for the more comprehensive term, or to overlook the dialectic within his own period between the writers drawn to utopian exogamy and those who imagined instead the purity of endogamy. This dialectic is familiar to scholars of international modernism--Marinetti's swarthy nurse and Mafarka's male parthenogenesis 11 --and it is part of American literature too. The endogamous impulse of Michaels's nativist modernists, such as Faulkner, should be understood in relation to the exogamous impulse of "earlier" writers, such as Pound; the exogamous impulses of other writers of the same period, such as Moore; and the exogamous impulses of these same nativist modernists, because such a writer as Williams seemed to feel the pull in both directions. The writers who did not see the work to be done by the dissociation of race and culture seem to Michaels to have fallen out of step with the onward march of mind. They held instead to another vestige of the Enlightenment: the notion that making oneself a citizen of the world or at least a European was an ambitious and better project than making one what one already was, i. e., American. Given Michaels's persuasive critique of 1990s identitarian cultural politics, it is puzzling that he underestimates the utopian interest in exogamy and miscegenation in the larger modernist era. He seems to esteem more highly the engagé project that he explicates so brilliantly but also condemns.
