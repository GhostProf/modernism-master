Appreciation, Depreciation: Modernism's Speculative Bubble

Jennifer Wicke

What follows is a position paper, although it contains a number of modernist specimen cases upon which, as if they were park benches, it comes momentarily to rest. In the early pages of Ludwig Wittgenstein's Philosophical Investigations, itself a modernist test case of sorts, Wittgenstein proposes a language game consisting of a single word--"slab"--shouted out as bricklayers build a foundation and a house of language. 1 Think of this essay as akin to the slab game, a somewhat deliberately crude attempt to set up a way of talking about the larger foundation of our enterprise as critics of modernism and modernity, instead of an elegant perambulation within modernist textual spaces. Skirting the perimeter of modernism's walls, my polemic addresses the critical superstructure surrounding modernism's revival, probing its brickwork and sounding its slabs. These slabs, serving as the park benches I invoked above, are different in kind; one is a material conceit coming from the ground literally, while the second offers a critical concept taken from Ernst Bloch as a metonymic bench. Interspersed are leaves drawn from several modernist artifacts, while the entire essay rests on economic metaphors from speculative finance. Bubbles and slabs are antithetical, yet both can refer to critical discourse, in the first case as a speculative investment in value, in the second (slabs) as an antifoundationalist foundation for critical practice.

I'll trace out a conceit through much of the argument, borrowing an analogy from another cultural arena also subject to speculative bubbles. "I speak," to cite Miss Prism in TheImportance of Being Earnest, "horticulturally." Consider the following remark, on its face yet another frenzied, fin-de-siécle denunciation of alterity: "It is impossible to deny that the blending of foreign blood has seriously impaired the value of the pure strains. Many of the continental sorts lack cleanliness and when they crop up amongst their purer sisters their appearance at once betrays them." We imagine we know the discourse of empire, with its binaries of contamination and purity, self and other, so well we could gloss this in our sleep. Yet the excoriation of sordid miscegenation with pure native stock emerges not from imperial apologetics, nor from the popular press, but from a troubled report put out by the beleaguered British tulip growers association, circa 1900. The once majestic Royal National Tulip Society, representing the aesthetic and taxonomic elite of the tulip industry, a society which was down to two members when it tried to confer its varietal prizes in 1926, makes an eerie parallel to the fates and fortunes of the modernist critical industry. 2 The Royal National Tulip Society--which waned altogether in 1936--had been the arbiter of excellence in all things tulip for centuries, at the forefront of aesthetic and horticultural innovation in the United Kingdom; and had an eclectic membership made up of hobbyists, botanists, aristocratic gardeners, and garden-variety tulip fanciers (TT, 247). The creations of the Society, given the name "English Florist's tulips," sprang up as one-of-a-kind marvels in country estate gardens as well as in humble plots cultivated by the side of the railways. Again for centuries, English Florist's tulips were judged semiannually in well-attended meetings held by convention in public houses. There individual tulips, among them the feathered Bizarre, "George Hayward," the soft, yellow "Mrs. Moon," the resplendent "Bronze King," and the Bybloemen tulip, "Princess Charlotte's Cenotaph," went head-to-head for prizes and approbation, their decapitated petaled tresses uniformly inserted into three-inch-tall brown beer bottles.

What had interfered with this long reign of horticultural glory, not to say purity? In a sense mass culture was the villain, the same foe slinking down the pike and gunning for modern(ist) literature and the arts, at least in commonly-held versions of modernism. The first insidious notion capable of shaking the foundation of the Royal National Tulip Society's authority came, not surprisingly, from the United States, that bastion of sleazy mass culture. In the early-nineteenth century an American gardener had planted and raised tulips en masse as bedding plants; the discovery failed to catch on commercially at that juncture, but at the turn of the century Dutch tulip entrepreneurs--who had a long history of making guilders off the backs of tulips--systematized and mass-marketed the American tulip bed invention. The resulting bed or plot of tulips became not only mass culture, but mass spectacle. The pivotal World Exposition held in Paris in 1889, itself a design monument to modernity, where the Eiffel Tower was erected as sheer public spectacle, introduced the first world exhibition of massed tulips as spectacular eye candy. The great Dutch tulipiere, E. H. Krelage, filled the beds of the Trocadero Palace during the fair with what a current newspaper editorial disparagingly called the "bang in the eye" of thousands of planted tulips (TT, 223). Tulips en masse surrounded the base of the iconic apex of modern urbanism, adding a horizontal, floral modernism to the Eiffel Tower's vertical modernist spectacle.

The mass cultural sea of tulips Krelage had planted was comprised of no ordinary or anonymous tulip legions. As another first, these were brand-name tulips, the "Darwin" brand to be exact. Krelage had the creative and marketing genius to buy the rights to the name from Charles Darwin's son, Francis (TT, 259). "Darwin" was an apt new brand-name for a diverse collection of pink and purple tulip bulbs he had bought at a vast tulip close out sale in Flanders, as that region ceased growing tulips commercially (TT, 262). The individual botanical and varietal names of the motley collection were excised; Darwin tulips were collectivist, a brand instead of a taxonomic variety or florist's name for a unique type. Once branded "Darwin," tulips became modern commodities--not because they were being sold and traded on the market, which they had been for centuries before--but because they now were cultivated to be planted en masse, and given a brand name accordingly: tulips became a modern, or even modernist, floral genre--tulips had truly evolved.

Subsequent to Krelage's breakthrough on the tulip branding front, tulips also entered modernity as a pivot of the modernizing tourist industry. In order to replenish consumer wonderment at the spectacle of tulip fields in bloom, thousands of bulbs were given away as municipal marks of esteem, as awards for heroism, and even in exchange for arms at the outset of World Wars I and II (TT, 268). Tulips shared another feature of modernism in their internationalism and cosmopolitanism. An international floral currency, the tulip began to be grown commercially in far-flung places, such as Japan in the 1920s. The Japanese deplored tulips as unrefined and vulgar, but overcame these aesthetic objections since they had the ideal climatic conditions for their cultivation and export to less-refined, tulip-hungry Western nations (TT, 268). Tulips acted as the thin edge of the economic wedge in complex colonial situations, at times valuable as a raw material for "extraction," at other moments important as a symbolic show of imperial floral might. Hardball economic battles waged by Britain over its possession and control of colonial tulip bulbs echoed the violent, and ultimately successful, mid-nineteenth-century struggles of the British empire to wipe out the indigenous Indian textile industry. British women's clothing transmuted the often bloody suppression of Indian weaving into flower-strewn calico cloth, its innocuous floral prints masking a secret history; so too did brand-name tulips flower on the foreign, colonial soil whose native tulip bulbs had been confiscated. 3

Perhaps not surprisingly, it was a celebrated Irish gardener, William Robinson, who proposed an anticolonialist twist in modern tulip display. In The Wild Garden, Robinson argues that tulips should be set out in masses, but not in delineated beds (TT, 259). Instead they should be planted in nooks, on hillsides, or across a lawn, where they will appear to have sprung up randomly in the wild. Robinson articulates an anti-Plantation explosion of tulips beyond the pale of the garden plot, and transforms the most cultivated, artificial, and man-made flower in European history into a simulacrum of wildness. Only the modern fashion for a Gustave Le Bon-like crowd of tulips--that is, the brand-name cultivars Krelage and others had taught the masses to see and revel in en masse--makes it possible for a subversive gardener to achieve its antithesis, the aesthetic effect of rogue tulips springing up wildly yet beautifully over a "natural" landscape. Robinson poeticized the crowd of tulips by embracing their previously suppressed wild side, in an act of colonial (Irish) mimicry of the English Florist's tulip so strategic, his tulips could be called Bhabha hybrids avant la lettre. By dint of a speculative bubble afloat on the material froth of history, making a nearly circumferential global orbit, and scattered by economic, aesthetic, and political revolutions alike, the tulip had traced its way back to a faux sauvage fashion identity.

Modernist tulips also parallel modernism by providing a material canvas for extensive aesthetic experimentation--or enough of a parallel to keep this conceit in play. The departure from Kantian formalism in the aesthetic evaluation of tulips was decisive by the end of the nineteenth century. Heretofore, the criteria for excellence in tulips were largely formalist. Dr. W. G. Hardy had written the seminal treatise, "On Perfection of Form in the Tulip," in 1847, wherein he took up the gardening cudgels against those who disagreed that the half-sphere was the perfect form of the tulip blossom (TT, 232-7). Complex mathematical equations subtended the raging arguments regarding the beauty of tulips--whether their beauty inhered most in the straight-on frontal view, or when seen from above looking down into the cup of the tulip, required alternative calculations of sublime mathematical precision; petal depth in relation to diameter offered a Platonic calculus of tulip Form. Modernist tulip genres abandoned all such aesthetic numerology and the formalism beneath it. The great English gardener Gertrude Jekyll began suggesting, as of 1899, that avant-garde gardens contain beds of multiple tulips chosen so that their colors modulated in finely-tuned colorscapes--for example, "the blackish-purple Faust tulip" shifting to a lighter and redder variety, bronzes shading into purple-tipped copper, and flame reds coexisting with crimson in a fauvist riot. 4 With such a colorist's eye, mass tulips could still be differentiated as spectacle; Jekyll's Saussurean langue of tulips insisted that their value was always diacritical, a matter of their relation to the other tulips in the bed. Jekyll and other modern greats of the English garden established a modernist garden practice, eschewing essentialism and "the old stable ego of [tulipine] characters" in favor of fluid, nonlinear, and self-reflexive gardening techniques. 5 Gardens were to be shaped with verbal and visual allusiveness: Jekyll's tulips entered into a modernist compact in which their most prized attributes, their individual characteristics, were set afloat in a collage-iste sea of quotation, reference, and countermemory.

On the other hand, the beauty of modern tulips was also a social construction, contingent on the material context of their viewing. The gleaming radiance of the 30,000 Silver Hawk white tulips displayed in the Sheffield Botanical Garden dazzled spectators thanks to the coal mining strike then in progress; their unbesmirched whiteness was the direct result of lowered smoke in the city's atmosphere, and depending on the political point of view, veritably radiated governmental perfidy or the strength of the labor cause (TT, 271).

The swerve toward massification in outdoor displays of tulips was accompanied by its opposite: the very modernist vogue for tulips displayed indoors as cut flowers, destined for vases and bouquets. Oscar Wilde was among the first writers to bring the cut tulip into his textual mise-en-scène; in The Picture of Dorian Gray, tulips in the drawing room hang their heads over the rim of blue and white porcelain vases from Japan, the visual trope of decadent modernity. 6 The tulip had rarely been contemplated before in its decapitated state, sundered from its invisible bulb, until its bizarreries suddenly made the indoor tulip visible, and au courant. It is almost frightening on this score to search out floral references in Virginia Woolf's Mrs. Dalloway, so profuse and pervasive do they prove to be. 7 Almost every page is rampant with blooms. To inventory them with an eye to tulips requires sharp critical pruning. In film theory a distinction is made when discussing the sound of a film between diegetic sound and the film's score or sound track. Diegetic sound refers to sound or music emanating from something in the scene, such as a song being played on a car radio, while the score or sound track comes from "outside" the scene or narrative, superimposed upon the visual images. Woolf's writing is rife with "diegetic" flowers--characters fill vases, sniff bouquets, peer into garden blossoms, purchase flowers, and sell them--yet flowers, petals, blossoms, gardens are the green fuse that drives her language: sinuous vines and proliferating blossoms are Woolf's primary metonymies. Mrs. Dalloway hypostatizes flowers, and almost obsessively specifies them. Among those mentioned diegetically or as glimmering metonyms are roses of all colors and kinds, including hallucinated red roses on Septimus's wallpaper, dahlias, sweet pea, violets, hollyhocks, lilies, syringa, blue hydrangeas, orchids, pampas grass, purple heather, moss, carnations, lilacs, delphiniums, iris--and one and only one mention of tulips, as follows: "It was her nature to enjoy. . . . She enjoyed practically everything. If you walked with her in Hyde Park now it was a bed of tulips, now a child in a perambulator, now some absurd little drama she made up on the spur of the moment." 8 So Peter Walsh muses about Clarissa's character. The sight of the tulip bed triggers her enjoyment, but provides an occasion for her to illuminate the world for someone else. The tulip bed she gestures towards is a shared visual spectacle that the massified modern tulip synecdochizes perfectly. Even these almost throwaway lines about the tulip bed are significant in a novel so bombarded with the language of flowers, if one pauses for a bit of textual spadework around the flower's edge. One common critical notion about spectacle follows Guy Debord's determinist analysis in The Society of the Spectacle: that is, spectators are in thrall to spectacle, and their thralldom is commodified as an act of consumption. 9 But Mrs. Dalloway as a whole will have none of this fixed economy of the spectacle, which Clarissa and others cut against repeatedly. Clarissa's expressed delight over the sight of the tulips nudges Peter Walsh into a moment's shared reverie. The modernist tulip bed is a commodified spectacle that dissolves and deliquesces with the nudge of an elbow. Like the coin Clarissa throws into the Serpentine in an act of expenditure, so too do tulips work their tiny magic as a feature of her gift economy. Clarissa is too old-fashioned perhaps to have cut tulips in her home, but she tiptoes through the modernist tulips to dapple the sight of them with the wonder of her enthusiasm. Rather than providing an occasion of "shock," which the novel so amply limns as a corollary modern experience, the tulip bed spectacle provides a distraction, in Walter Benjamin's terms, one that in Mrs. Dalloway's gift economy is only a proper distraction when disproportionately shared. 10

Seventeenth-century Tulipomania and the South Sea Bubble affair of the eighteenth century are retrospective instances of speculative frenzy reaching the level of the sublime, or at least the sublimely hysterical; the modernist tulip market I speak of doesn't have the gossamer sublimity of those bubbles, but instead the gritty, embedded materiality of the smutty foam that delineates a port's edge. Tulips no longer waxed and waned in value overnight, nor were single bulbs hoarded as species of gold. Financial speculation is an iridescent bubble in much modern socioeconomic theory, whereby the airy liquidity of intangible speculative value is privileged for its very vulnerability to the surface tensions that cause it to pop--yet one more demonstration of the mysteries of commodity form, and the contradictions of capitalism. 11 Modernism as a cultural icon, or bubble, or iconic bubble, to oxymoronize, does not resemble the feeding frenzy of past Bubbles or manias, when capitalism was in doubt as a world system, nor does it bob up and down in the waters of a singular "market." Bursting modernism's bubble becomes necessary, all the more so when our own frantic critical splashing tries to make one large bubble come to the surface, even if it be a cluster of bubbles with the adjective "new" on its label. That is to say, we as critics and scholars of modernism have to recognize our own resemblance to the Royal National Tulip Society, insofar as we also attempt to keep hold of modernism and retain the right to award retroactive prizes to sportive modernist hybrids. The Royal National Tulip Society clung to its role as arbiter of tulip excellence, as it disavowed massified tulips, brand-name tulips, national and global tulip economies, modern spectacle, and the commercialization of tulip tourism. The parallel disavowals we make in our self-professed roles as arbiters of the new modernisms include purging modernist orthodoxy of formalism, tradition, hierarchies, social Darwinism, commercialism, bourgeois modernism, neo-imperialism, and totalization of all kinds, without seeing how much we rely on the modernist brand.

Modernism is a brand name. Coming from me, I should stress, that's a compliment. Brand names have all the complexity of the Hegelian hieroglyph, and they offer cultural space for the hierophantic, the necrophantic, and ideological ghosts of all kinds. A brand name is not prima facie a shill, a facade covering an empty sameness, a con game designed to persuade gullible consumers that identical items differ in kind due to their brand name. Brand names carve out a locus for social recognition or attention, yet also defy linear logic or cause and effect reasoning. Brand names are exquisitely sensitive diacritical registers; they are more than the sum of their parts, if those parts are taken to mean products, the ads for them, production, distribution, and consumption. Brands can die as persons die, yet unlike the latter, be raised from the dead in their own lifetime. Brands are aesthetic, onto-politico-material agglomerates that live because, as with Tinkerbell, we have applauded them into reanimation. Brand names also can live a crepuscular existence, since twilight sleep or suspended animation is often preferable to expending a brand's waning spirit in a waste of shame--or killing it off. Modernism's brand name, as we know, lingered for decades in such a boring twilight sleep, etherized upon a dissecting table, or slumbering in a jar. Efforts such as the Modernist Studies Association, the "New Modernisms" conferences, and even a provocative journal devoted to , share credit for reawakening a slumbering critical and scholarly and aesthetic spirit--modernism's brand. Each has something to do with resurrecting and refurbishing a brand in advanced moribundity--at least in some parts of the world. And as with so many commercial brands, the modernist brand can only be resurrected by ironizing or implicitly denying its original cultural status. This is glorious and salutary in some ways, but should not be mistaken as gestures of pure cultural exhumation or backdated exoneration. Brand names are revived so that they can still be of use, can still move the goods. In marketing there is a name for this: rebranding. A return to modernism with a difference, and with a plural vengeance, as in "new modernisms," legitimately opens key debates, stages productive re-readings, and enacts agonistic language games of genuine import and ferocity. We cannot ignore, however, our own investments in modernism's speculative bubble. As revivers of its brand, a brand we cannot seem to do without, we re-brand ourselves as critics and theorists just as we rebrand modernist others.

Because these comments can easily be misconstrued as a tired version of the self-critical lament that "we're all complicit," or even worse in my book, that any mode of criticism, interpretation, or theory inevitably "appropriates" or "co-opts" something that doesn't belong to it, I'll harp perhaps unnecessarily on what I don't mean to say, by way of the now highly attenuated tulip analogy. Tulip growers of the late-nineteenth and early-twentieth centuries were not "complicit" with a grubby market in tulips worldwide, nor did they co-opt or appropriate authentic or indigenous qualities of tulips by forcing bulbs, hybridizing them, and finally creating tulip brands. The fierce competition between and among tulip fanciers, tulip state apparatuses, and imperializing tulip stratagems doesn't boil down to hegemonic violence, or not exclusively so. Within a churning roil of bubbles, among them such contingent and overdetermined elements as color intensity, royal patronage systems, the rise of the natural history museum, botany hobby kits, the predilection for British railway supervisors to dabble in passionate gardening, nationalism, internationalism, cosmopolitanism, little Englandism, the transformation of public space in the modern city, Dutch mercantilism, tourism, feminism, diplomacy, imperial competition, the brutal exigencies of colonialism and slave labor, beauty, the Eiffel Tower, and ad infinitum, the agglomerate bubble of the modern tulip market burbles to the surface.

As go tulips, so goes modernism. That is, its meaning is inseparable from its uses and its overdeterminations. A relatively common stance amongst those who would like to "make modernism new" leans away from the so-called "high modernist canon," and tilts toward lesser known or even unknown modernist artists to escape what appears to be the cul-de-sac of high modernism. Exorcising the modernist canon posthumously, these critical acts of purification disregard the modernist branding that, like Sherwin Williams paint, covers everything, even criticism and critique. Those nouveau modernist critics who wish to enlarge the modernist canon, so to speak--and already the terms "modernist" and "canon" and "enlarge" beg too many questions to address here--had better hope that the illusion of a modernist or high modernist canon stays in place, to permit rebranding along the edges of a very big tent.

Another and to me better frame for the reappraisal of modernism I pluck from the resonant, splendid critical opus of Ernst Bloch, resurrecting a term he himself had borrowed, in both cases of borrowing to infuse it anew. In the essays collected together as The Utopian Function of Artand Literature, 12 Bloch cites "colportage," a real word that historically referred to the practice of travelling booksellers who crisscrossed Europe and elsewhere from the sixteenth to the early-twentieth centuries, selling novels, medical tracts, romances, scientific pamphlets, calendars, fairy tales, political documents, sermons, housewifery manuals, adventure tales, pornography, and prayer books at market fairs, from wagons, on the street, or house to house. Colporteurs, then--and the phrase unfortunately has nothing to do whatsoever with Cole Porter--in their meandering commercial travels gathered up, collected, and retailed the chaff and the wheat, the flotsam and the jetsam, the cream and the skim milk of published material of their moment. By means of colportage books and publications that belonged to completely different discourses, market niches, and even ontological levels were thrown together; in their array constituting more than the sum of their inventory. Cheek by jowl, their presence a matter of contingency as much as supply and demand, the colportage stocks taken as a whole offered, via commercial exchanges, an experience of "the Novum, the startlingly and unexpectedly new," (UF, xxxvii). The Novum, to follow through on Bloch's densely mystical yet materialist concept, is a concrete manifestation which orients people, even by way of monetary transactions, toward the wild horizon line of pre-illumination, or Vorschein (UF, 41, 73-7). In Bloch's thought colportage becomes a philosophical, political, and aesthetic category simultaneously, and its place on the plane of analysis is similarly multiple--colportage is a process yet also a realization. Capable of referring to the plenum of genres and imaginative documents--including the ideologically dubious--set forth into the world by colportage, that is, colportage as both a verb and a noun, colportage is also a metacategory, a manner of regarding representation in its social, political, and aesthetic dimensions all at once. There is much that is reminiscent of Benjamin's notion of "profane illumination" in colportage, but Bloch applies no ideological litmus tests to the contents of colportage. The more Brechtian Benjamin of "The Work of Art in an Age of Mechanical Reproduction," for instance, evaluates the products of mass culture ideologically, and finds them wanting in all but future potential. 13 Colportage in a Blochian sense occupies or engenders a site of anticipatory meaning. This vestibule of pre-illumination is neither the endlessly displaced abyss or black hole of meaning, as more recent post-structuralist thought would have it, nor, although it is dialectically informed, is colportage a form of negative dialectics. Theodor Adorno's crystalline rejection of shabby and impure genres, tainted with commerce, whereby true art must exist as the dark side of the cultural moon, makes him virtually the anticolporteur. 14

I am inching toward an assertion that colportage, like the modernist tulip market but less facetiously, is a remarkably helpful way to envision the ductile "thing" we cart around from place to place, put on exhibit, preach to and preach from, closing the doors upon certain display cases, rearranging the merchandise within, colporteurs all. Modernism is as baggy and capacious and eclectic as those motley wares of colportage, gathered together because of historical contingency, circulated, and sold. It is far more modernism's link to the Vorschein, or anticipatory illumination, that makes the term apropos. Bloch would probably have none of this conceit, as it runs the risk of including "false illusion" or kitsch along with the colportage; as he wrote, "the dream of colportage is: never again to be trapped by the routine of daily life," (UF, 83). On the other hand, colportage gives me a vivid conceptualization of and for modernism, especially as it is put to use in cultural and academic circulation. Bloch's insistence on the relevance of qualitative judgments within the precincts of colportage and its often disdained genres, as in art generally, is an aspect of his critical thought I am not willing to do without vis-à-vis modernism. In many instances, the return to modernism as a site of study and reconceptualization entails critical theories and assumptions where value not only plays no role, but cannot be countenanced as anything other than an invidious "distinction of taste," in Pierre Bourdieu's phrase. 15 By other lights value smacks of a thinly veiled plot to reinstantiate hierarchy, elitism, or transhistorical, universal values. Bloch's thought, like Benjamin's and that of numerous others, points us to the fire escape exit from those egregious simplifications of "value" and aesthetics, the bonfire of the vanities. Bloch reorients criticism to values in time and of time. His privileged use of the word "now" (Jetzt) reveals the stakes of discriminating value as both "new" and "now":

The now [Jetzt] moves and propels itself through each day, whenever. It knocks in all that happens, with its shortest time span, and it knocks on the door. But not every present opens up for it. The actual impulses, the socially driving pulses, do not knock in each present fresh and vital. Not every time opens up for the now and the next now that stands exactly at that moment in front of the door and that has never "entered" before. It has not unloaded its true contents with which and toward which it is on its way. [UF, 215]

Discriminations or judgments of value are being made all the time, and are operative in every sphere, every genre, and at every level. Judgments of value that spring from a colporteur's intellectual valise arise from a mental economy of abundance, not scarcity, from a sense of the possible, not measurement, and with an absolute lack of shame about expressions of pleasure, emotion, and wonder. Bloch's colportage is predicated on the unpredictable knock of the present on the door. More than that, though, colportage contains a critical ethos of appreciation we can best appreciate in textual performance. Colportage is thematized and indeed enacted in Joyce's Ulysses, as in his other works, with Finnegans Wake perhaps the most sustained spiritual act of colportage ever attempted on behalf of a Europe plunging into war. Leaving that to one side, one need only look at the encounters Joyce stages for his characters within Dublin colportage circa 1904. 16 Leopold Bloom is the very re to whom Bloch might be referring in saying, "the age-old pleasure of people, in no way simple and no way decadent, is preserved in the fair, wanders within it and without," (UF, 182). Bloom is surely a re for the modern consumer--as is the entire cast of Ulysses and its readers too--yet his immersion in the artificially protracted colportage of Dublin's colonially-depressed economy transforms him into a species of colporteur. He shores its fragments not against his ruins, but as a zone of fierce intellectual inquiry, passionate political imaginings, and intense, pleasure-laden evaluation of the most utopian sort. Colportage is bricolage to the third power: no mere act of magpie borrowing and collection, colportage is materially invested, rife with judgments of value that hinge on hope and transformation. Above all, colportage is a site of the Novum. "Make it new" is a familiar formalist credo to be found on modernist colportage's collapsible shelves. The Novum or newness lying immanent in colportage, however, is transformational, affective, collective, and interactive illumination. It emanates not only from the work or artifact made new, but from the rich possibilities for newness, for Vorschein, to be found in the pages of its disparate volumes, many of them old, tattered, and shop-worn. Bloom strolls into the bookstall to find himself pricked by this sense of newness; the Novum knocks on his door.

"Mr. Bloom turned over idly pages of The Awful Disclosures of Maria Monk, then of Aristotle's Masterpiece. . . . He laid both books aside and glanced at the third: Tales of the Ghetto by Leopold von Sacher-Masoch.

--That I had, he said, pushing it by.

The shopman let two volumes fall on the counter.

--Them are two good ones, he said. . . . He bent to make a bundle of the other books, hugged them against his unbuttoned waistcoat and bore them off behind the dingy curtain. [U, 193]

When the shopman returns, replete with "oniony breath, phlegmy cough, rawskinned bald spot" and all, Bloom has made his choice and holds it up, numinous, before him. The shopman lifts rheumy eyes and intones: "--Sweets of Sin, he said, tapping on it. That's a good one" (U, 194).

And in some odd way they are all "good ones." Modernism inevitably contains its trash masterpieces, as it does its "classics." Bernard Shaw made a similarly colportagist point when he insisted that people require a steady diet of "trash" along with their masterpieces. The colportage stall of modernism doesn't privilege a canon--for canons are, after all, the illusory aftereffects of inclusion, not exclusion, where canonicity produces more works, rather than fewer--but it certainly makes distinctions, ever shifting, about which works it makes sense to envision in relation to one another, which works give off sparks of elective affinity, which works look different given what rests on the shelf above or below them. The high/low distinction crumbles away, and with it the need to tidy the wagon or purge the bookstall. This is a modernism in and of the market, but clearly colportage as a historical social practice involves buying and selling within a complex niche or facet of "the" market, a niche sedimented with earlier practices such as barter, coffeehouse information exchange, and clandestine or "black market" elements built in. If possible, imagine modernism's brand name as if it were a plaque or blazon or handpainted sign on a colportage wagon, booth, or stall, a brand reprinted on all the contents of this unclassified and unclassifiable collection.

Yet the harrowing, dystopian exigencies of colportage are on display in Ulysses. As much as the colportage bookstall is capable of cracking open the golden bowl of the now, so too the fissures of class and gender and race and colonial status striate its space. Stephen and his sister, Dilly Dedalus, chance to meet at a bookcart, where each has headed for a private quaff of colportering. Stephen's eye passes over assorted titles: "Tattered pages. The Irish Beekeeper.Lifeand Miracles of the Cure of Ars.Pocket Guide to Killarney." Too mixed and motley for Stephen's interest to be piqued--although as the titles of real books, they are colportage squared by virtue of their hidden significance to the text in progress--and too reminiscent of the three masters he must disavow, his sister Dilly's book choice, a "one-pence Chardenal's French primer," is for her the Novum incarnate. "Is it any good?" she poignantly asks the brother who is newly returned from a Paris she is unlikely ever to see (U, 199-200).

In the whirling dervish of a chapter, "Circe," where all that has passed is recapitulated before a reader's eyes, Bloom is temporarily elevated to the highest position of civil and spiritual authority. After he declares "the new Bloomusalem in the Nova Hibernia of the future," his bodyguards practice colportage as miracle by distributing not only the obligatory free loaves and fishes, but also "cheap reprints of the World's Twelve Worst Books" (U, 395), each of which echoes a chapter of the book: "Froggy & Fritz (politic), Care of the Baby (Infantilic), 50 Meals for 7 & 6 (culinic), Was Jesus a Sun Myth? (historic), Expel that Pain (medic), Infant's Compendium of the Universe (cosmic), Let's All Chortle (hilaric), Canvasser's Vade Mecum (journalic), Loveletters of Mother Assistant (erotic), Who's Who in Space (astric), Songs that Reached our Heart (melodic), Pennywise's Way to Wealth (parsimonic)" (U, 396).

The colportage modernism I am trying to articulate puts a premium on both intention and reception--on value as an affective and a cognitive judgment intertwined with pleasure, hope, and politics--and on the diacritical relation between and among artifacts of modernism, in their moment and ours. A few years back I could never have written the word "intention" as anything but a critical epithet, and even now I invoke it neither Hirschianly nor in Benn Michaels-ese, that is, in the voices of two critics who made intentionality a platform of antitheoretical textual analysis. 17 Intention in my critical playbook doesn't refer to a mythical inner intention on the part of an author to give a specific meaning to a work overall, or any of its parts, a meaning affixed there like authorial crazy-glue. Instead, I'm trying to offer intention as a dialectical relationship, a way of capturing conceptually the agency involved in tilting a work or an artifact or a critical text toward the social, political, aesthetic, affective, cognitive, and cultural context of its reception. For example, by this definition of intention Joyce intends that Ulysses offer an analogous version of the bookstall placed within its chapters, so that as readers hold the book in their hands they are inevitably repeating the distracted colportage encountered in the text, sometimes literalized as in Stephen and Dilly and Bloom's bookstall moments, sometimes traceable only in the colportage whiffs given off by Ulysses's language. Its collection of styles, quotations, allusions, data, historical fact, and transgressions animate the Novum. Bloom's daily sojourns in the bookstall stage the possible, premonitory appearance of the new--a hope held out by the collision of his curiosity, yearning, and intense enthusiasm for change with an ideal constellation of books for sale that day, capable of allowing the present to knock upon the door. Modernist writers such as Joyce don't intend a single meaning or message, but do intend for their work to be received and consumed in dialectical relation to surrounding works, discourses, events, experiences, and so on--they intend the slant to the message, if you will. Resistance or transgression--our critical bête blanches these days--do not then lie in subtexts or hidden messages under the surface, nor even in the self-unraveling of language languaging: it's less a question of subverting a putatively hegemonic language, than of exposing more of the material surface of the new in order for the now to knock on the door. Modernism is colportage, then, but not all modernists are colporteurs, not by a long shot.

The sounds of gunfire attend the final critical example I add to the string of modernist allegories and conceits. Insurgent modernism is a shelf of the modernist colportagere, to coin a word, an intent within a work to push the Novum of awareness it entails to the foreground. I've echoed John Reed's classic book, Insurgent Mexico, in proposing yet another metonymy for modernism; the final re for exploration of modernism's bubble then and now arises from a relatively unknown modernist cinema, created along the U.S./Mexican border during the Mexican revolution against Porfirio Díaz and the U.S. military who aided his regime. 18 This cinema was produced in a meteoric arc by one Romaine Fielding, an actor and film director who as a star was associated with the Lubin Film Company, headed by Sigmund "Pop" Lubin, in Philadelphia. There was nothing obscure about either Lubin Films or Romaine Fielding in the silent picture era of 1910-5, prior to Hollywood dominance, when film companies roamed from Astoria, Queens to Philadelphia, to Arizona and the New Mexico territory and on to California; even D. W. Griffith's Biograph Film company made two films in a one-week stay in Albuquerque, New Mexico in 1911. 19 Romaine Fielding, whose self-authored biography claimed an exotic Corsican birth and a link to Napoleon, was actually William Blandin of Riceville, Iowa. His huge popularity in the U.S., Europe, and Latin America and Mexico--for early film genuinely represented the international modernism of Raymond Williams's analysis--was demonstrated by his being chosen as the "world's most popular player" in the 1911 Motion PictureStory Magazine. 20 He won by several million votes over such still-resonant names as Mary Pickford, Blanche Sweet, Pearl White, and Francis X. Bushman (NMH, 7). A consummate actor, Fielding was a tyro of writing and direction as well, and left the Lubin company temporarily to set up a film studio in Silver City, New Mexico. His intent was to make films in express sympathy with the Mexican revolution and its key res, Pancho Villa and Emiliano Zapáta, as well as pro-labor films supporting unionization of mine workers, both involving the unprecedented use of mixed Mexican, Chicano, and Anglo-American casts (NMH, 9). It now seems inconceivable that a film star and director of such widespread popularity in the U.S. and abroad could flaunt these pro-labor and antimilitarist stances in his insurgent modernist cinema, and moreover gain popularity as a result. Understanding the Fielding moment in modernism entails moving beyond a high/low split mentality, acknowledging intent on the part of its auteur, recognizing the popularity, pleasure, and spectacle inherent in this everyday modernism, whose achievement could not have occurred outside a branded, mass modernism without a shred of aesthetic autonomy.

The excitement about Fielding and his enterprise ran so high that the Albuquerque Journal wrote hundreds of adulatory pieces covering his work in Silver City, where he essentially commandeered the town, titling one such dispatch, "Romaine Fielding: A Revolution of Human Energy and Endurance" (NMH, 6). Among his oeuvre are films made in Santa Rita, New Mexico at the Chino Copper Mine, exposing the grotesque labor exploitation there, and inventing and celebrating a celluloid strike which became real later in the year when the actual copper miners followed cinematic suit. The audiences for these regionally-filmed and highly insurrectionary pictures were by no means merely local, or Southwestern--the films were circulated up and down the East Coast, and played to transfixed audiences in Sweden, Belgium, Ireland, and Spain, among other countries and colonies. Fielding titled one film centering on the struggle of the Mexican peasantry Los Insurrectos; he brought Mexican and Mexican-American actors and extras by the hundreds to small-town or ghost-town sites for vividly recreated skirmishes bursting with fire, explosions, and horses galore. The city fathers in Albuquerque, New Mexico's largest city, were so far from scandalized over this affront to U.S. government policy vis-à-vis Mexico and the labor unions--the cavalry and big capital were both called out--that they begged Fielding to relocate his fledgling studio there. But this radical municipal glory was not to be (NMH, 8). When Fielding left the Lubin Company to found Cactus Film Company, his fame faded and his company folded within a few years. On the whole, like W. C. Fields, he'd rather have been in Philadelphia.

Fielding's work endures, as does the historical record of its reception. His intention for these dazzlingly innovative movies--filming "on location," inventing special demolition effects--was to show what he called the "romance" of revolution and insurgency, to take the "Cowboy and Indian picture" and make it newly political (NMH, 12). To reach this goal, Fielding relied on a realism more exacting than his contemporaries were able to command: to enable his insurgent cinema vérité he was lavished with abandoned homes to blow up, sought after by eager extras and would-be desert starlets, gifted with derelict mine shafts and weary horses by a starstruck citizenry. Some may call this realism, not modernism, but I see it as modernist colportage, that is, the melding of genres and styles--documentary, reportage, romance, melodrama, westerns, adventure, political propaganda, etc.--to arouse emotional and intellectual intensity, and above all pleasure and wonder, in the spectacle of political transformation. Romaine Fielding of Riceville, Iowa is a vanished colporteur in modernism's colportage legacy. To recognize the existence of a vibrant and popular modernist cinema with an ideological and aesthetic twist that doesn't fit the dimensions of modernism's straitjacket is to recognize the limitations of our grasp of modernism and its possibilities, in its own time and ours.

We can rebrand modernism, or various modernisms, but we cannot recapture the Novum of particular texts, whether visual or verbal or auditory or choric, nor of politics, performances, movements, manifestos, and events, in the absence of modernist appreciation. Appreciation (read: valuing) has even less critical cachet and credibility than does intention, and for good reason--any return to literary appreciation or impressionist criticism or, God help us, to belletrism, would be a critical and theoretical disaster. But without passionate appreciation in the market sense critics and scholars cannot make the necessary rebranding of modernism anything more than an exercise. We have more than a little in common collectively with the Royal National Tulip Society; we are closer to the rheumy-eyed, onion-breathed bookstall colporteur, muttering "that's a good one," than we may wish to admit; we stand in lieu of Clarissa Dalloway directing others to "look" at modernist tulip beds; like Romaine Fielding, even our demolition jobs must be done with passionate intensity and spectacular flamboyance. Finding value prompts appreciation--that sentence can be read in an economic way and in a literary sense, yet is rarely taken as a speech act, an exhortation to express enthusiasm. "Enthusiasm," William Blake enjoins us, "is the all-in-all." Such enthusiastic appreciations of modernism can be (should be) rigorous, historical, aesthetically incisive, and politically aware all at once, yet without enthusiasm (even critique requires exuberance), the critical acts that float modernism's speculative bubble in the twenty-first century cannot permit us to experience modernism as a "tiger's leap into the past." 21 That is Benjamin's famous definition of fashion, yet it combines with Bloch's colportage to suggest something about leaps of critical enthusiasm: the tiger's leap is the same thing as opening the door upon hearing the knock of the now. We know that modernism and its myriad works did not redeem the world in the ways we would wish, and the impulse to reach back into the past of modernism and imagine that a re-branding act of criticism can change that is understandable, yet deluded. Like an eccentric sect that must rather embarrassingly go on about its daily business after its leader has misjudged the End of Days, we have to recognize that things are as they are. This is tautological, and yet also extraordinary.

Did modernism, of so many tulip stripes and shades, then fail? Is the critical act of floating modernism's bubble a doomed or tainted enterprise? No, and no, yet not in thunder. Echoing Wittgenstein as this essay also began, "the world is everything that is the case." 22 Modernism is and was the case in the world experiment, which in Bloch's critical humanism we are always embarked upon, the "world experiment" being the shared need to first recognize, and then change, the world. The modernist bubble of our speculations, set aloft by our appreciations as by our depreciations alike, enlarges the surface of what has been and might be the case, allowing modernism into our moment's world experiment, making modernism anew. In retailing modernism this way, we don't risk selling out--the only danger lies in failing to appreciate and give value, and thereby selling modernism short.
