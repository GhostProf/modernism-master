The Music of Joyce's Vernacular Voices
Margot Norris

"[M]aybe the most beautiful extant recording of an unaccompanied speaking voice." That is how Douglas Wolk in a January 21, 2004 article in The Seattle Weekly describes the famous 1929 recording that James Joyce made of a section of "Anna Livia Plurabelle." He goes on to say, "For eight minutes, a liquid-voiced Joyce impersonates the sound of the world talking to itself: Two washerwomen chat on the banks of a river as night falls, and they turn into a tree and a stone. His Irish accent, in fact, is so thick that it's hard for an American, 75 years later, to hear the crackling recording as language, rather than as water and music."1 This description of Joyce's prose language as sonic, as simulating the sound of music, has, of course, inspired a huge number of composers to transform it into musical compositions. Scott Klein in a superb essay on "James Joyce and Avant-garde Music" points out that Joyce's influence, as an author, on the world of modern music may be unprecedented.2 The early works, especially Joyce's poems, stimulated tonal and Romantic arrangements, and Klein cites Myra Russell as counting 140 composers who have set the poems to music. Samuel Barber not only set such poems as "I Hear an Army" to music, but in 1947, eight years after the publication of Finnegans Wake, he produced Nuvoletta, inspired by the sounds of the Wake. However, as Klein discusses at length, Finnegans Wake chiefly influenced avant-garde composers, and John Cage's Roaratorio, An Irish Circus on 'Finnegans Wake' produced in 1979, is clearly the most famous of these productions. But I would like to point to a different feature of that beautiful Finnegans Wake passage Joyce recorded in his thick Irish accent in 1929, and relate its aural qualities to an entirely different set of early twentieth-century poetic practices and aesthetic concerns. Like Molly Bloom's famous soliloquy at the end of Ulysses, the dialogue of the washerwomen in "Anna Livia Plurabelle" underlines the aesthetic qualities of ordinary conversational speech, that is, the beauty of vernacular language. And in its heavily accented Irish tones, this female speech is inflected with a working class idiom and with Irish ethnicity. This semantic aspect of the sonic quality of the vernacular speech in Joyce's later work may be fruitfully related to a poetic consciousness and a poetic practice occurring across the Atlantic in Joyce's day, in the poetry of the Harlem Renaissance.

Modernist studies in the last decade or two have begun to attend to the importance of looking at high modernism in relation to vernacular discourses. A 2004 University of Pennsylvania dissertation by Matthew Hart titled "Synthetic Vernacular Poetry and Transatlantic Modernism 1922–2002" treats twentieth-century vernacular poetry with its attention to race, region, and nation as a response to the privileging of the internationalist current in high modernism.3 And Michael North's 1994 study The Dialectic of Modernism: Race, Language, & Twentieth Century Literature draws attention to the "linguistic mimicry and racial masquerade" already in practice in the discourses of high modernists such as Eliot, Pound, Stein, and William Carlos Williams.4 These studies complicate T. S. Eliot's problematic positioning of vernacular speech—and particularly of its class markers—in the famous pub passage in The Waste Land. "When Lil's husband got demobbed, I said—/I didn't mince my words, I said to her myself [. . . .] Now Albert's coming back, make yourself a bit smart./ He'll want to know what you done with that money he gave you/ To get yourself some teeth" . Although the passage is generally thought to evoke Cockney, it reproduces little of the sonic quality of a Cockney accent and marks its class chiefly in marred syntax—"what you done" rather than "what you did." The degradation of vernacular implied in this passage is reflected both in form—particularly the speaker's "un-minced" or un-sculpted words—and in its content of debased fertility and sexuality. Michael North, however, finds in high modernism traces of formal address to issues of race and vernacular culture, noting the possible influence that Joyce may have exerted on some of the Harlem Renaissance writers. Claude McKay, for example, found "a possible model in Joyce , who had transformed Irish linguistic ! disposse ssion into a comprehensive refashioning of English fiction" (North, 117). And in his 1999 study, Reading 1922: A Return to the Scene of the Modern, North also unearths the extent to which Ulysses was criticized by modernist purists like Clive Bell for producing a species of jazz that turned Joyce into a kind of "ragtime" artist.5 .

To what extent does Joyce's prose present vernacular language as a form of musical poetry that offers an address to class, gender, and regionality in his work? And to what extent are these markings produced by sounds? The expression of regionality may seem irrelevant to a contemporary non-Irish audience, but it serves to dispel any notion of Joyce's Ireland as a monolithic culture by introducing nuances of internal diversity and specificity into Irish speech. Some of these nuances have a political significance that is capable of sonic expression in the form of the accent. As early as A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man, Stephen Dedalus derides in his mind an Ulster classmate whose Belfast accent causes him "to pronounce the word science as a monosyllable."6 And the washerwomen in the ALP chapter of the Wake make fun of the protean quality of HCE's pretensions and the vernacular forms they take. "And the cut of him! And the strut of him! How he used to hold his head as high as a howeth, the famous eld duke alien, with a hump of grandeur on him like a walking wiesel rat. And his derry's own drawl and his corksown blather and his doubling stutter and his gullaway swank."7 (197.1). This recognition of regional inscription in speech on the part of the washerwomen raises the question of their own accents. Terence Killeen comments on the extent to which Joyce's mimicking of their voices in his 1929 recording either positions, or fails to position, the washerwomen regionally. According to Killeen, Joyce's accent clearly places the women in the country rather than in the city, marking them as rural but otherwise regionally ambiguous. He writes, "The accents are in fact impossible to pin down to a particular region—they are not distinctively Dublin, Cork, or Kerry, for instance. They are generalised Irish ru! ral acce nts, the intonation of Galway being perhaps the closest to the voice on the recording."8 Considering that the resolutely urban character of Joyce's fictional geography set him apart from many of his contemporaries in the Celtic revival, the accents of the washerwomen may have moved Joyce's last work closer to their poetic project of regional and national language recovery. John Millington Synge discovered his Irish voice by listening to "the nuances of Aran speech" during his summers on the Aran Islands at the turn of the century, as Richard Ellmann reports.9 But although Joyce initially disparaged Synge's work, he later cast his wife Nora in Synge's Riders to the Sea and encouraged the other actors to imitate "the Aran speech rhythms" in her voice (Ellmann, 440).

But if the Irish revival attended to the music of Ireland's diverse regions, Joyce in addition listened to the sounds of gender and class in language. The melody of Molly Bloom's thoughts in "Penelope" convey the inflections of a specifically female idiom, but one with only vaguely middle-class markings. In Finnegans Wake, in contrast, Joyce also inflects female speech with the markings of both class and labor. Much of the washerwomen's conversation concerns their work, exhorting each other in an alliterative idiom to do a good job—"Wallop it well with your battle and clean it" (FW 196.16)—and complaining about its cost to their bodies: "My wrists are wrusty rubbing the mouldaw stains" (FW 196.17). In this respect, "Anna Livia Plurabelle" borrows from the genre known as the "working song," a type of expressive vernacular composition that foregrounds the music of working class speech. This genre plays a conspicuous role in African American folk literature, and Sterling Brown in his essay on "Negro Folk Expression: Spirituals, Seculars, Ballads, and Work Songs" traces its origin to African American work songs in the field or in work on the rails.10 But the genre is also indigenous to Irish culture whose folk traditions include a specifically female variant of the working song as Nóirín Ní Riain discusses in her essay "The Female Song in the Irish Tradition."11 Like Langston Hughes, who published "Song to a Negro Wash-woman" in The Crisis in 1925, Joyce too celebrates this humble variant of female domestic labor, the washing of dirty linen. But unlike Hughes, who has the poet see the washerwoman singing without hearing her song,12 Joyce has his res give voice to their gestures of spreading the washed linens out to dry. "Will we spread them here no! w? Ay, w e will. Flip! Spread on your bank and I'll spread mine on mine. Flep! It's what I'm doing. Spread!" (FW 213.21). Not only the women's conversation, sprinkled with alliteration and repetitions, but the sound of the sheets being flipped and spread is made audible in this passage. The repetition of the word "spread" spreads itself throughout their discourse.

The washerwomen's work song is also a song of misfortune and complaint. "Amn't I up since the damp dawn, marthared mary allacook, with Corrigan's pulse and varicoarse veins, my pramaxle smashed, Alice Jane in decline and my oneeyed mongrel twice run over, soaking and bleaching boiler rags, and sweating cold, a widow like me, for to deck my tennis champion son, the laundryman with the lavandier flannels?" (FW 214.22).13 This comic plaintive recital of troubles and exploitation is again marked by alliteration—"damp dawn," "varicoarse veins," "bleaching boiler rags," "the laundryman with the lavandier flannels," and so on. But in his book, The Sound of 'Finnegans Wake,' Peter Myers finds additional poetic strategies at work in passages such as this. "Perhaps the strongest single musical characteristic of 'Anna Livia' is its interplay of the interrogative, indicative and imperative moods," he writes.14 The question and answer format, even when the question is rhetorical as in the above sentence, creates its own tonal alterations, variations, and repetitions. At the same time, the washer-women's recitation of woes also groups itself into reiterated cadences—"my pramaxle smashed, Alice Jane in decline." Peter Myers points out the poetic consequence of this tendency of the discourse to divide itself into two parts joined by what he calls a "pivot." "If we read through it we shall notice that there is no simple metrical account which can explain why we should group them together. In metrical terms the phrases differ considerably; yet they create the effect of one musical motif" . At the end of Finnegans Wake, however, Joyce goes still further to invest the vernacular of the female complaint with a performative quality. At the end of the book, an old woman is out walking and talking to her silent husband about the hardships of her life. As she speaks, her! speech becomes increasingly labored with the effort, as she appears out of breath and continues only with difficulty:

I've lapped so long. As you said. It fair takes. If I lose my breath for a minute or two don't speak, remember! Once it happened, so it may again. Why I'm all these years within years in soffran, allbeleaved. To hide away the tear, the parted. It's thinking of all. The brave that gave their. The fair that wore. All them that's gunne. I'll begin again in a jiffey. The nik of a nad. . . I only hope whole the heavens sees us. For I feel I could near to faint away. Into the deeps. Annamores leep. Let me lean, just a lea, if you le, bowldstrong bigtider. Allgearls is wea.

(FW 625.27)

Joyce here conjures up the weariness of old age in the broken sound of the woman's voice, unable to finish its sentences. "Let me lean, just a lea, if you le," she says, too weak to pronounce the word weak itself—"Allgearls is wea." Yet if this is vernacular speech, performing its sense through its sound, it nonetheless manages to produce poetic tonality in the repetition of consonant and vowel sounds. "Let me lean, just a lea, if you le" picked up again in "Allgearls is wea." As Peter Myers points out, "Every auditory detail, and in fact every form of repetition, whether of sound, image or anything else, can reinforce a rhythm" .

I would like to end by conceding a problem dramatized when any of us read Joyce's ALP speeches out loud. My own poor reading of these Joyce passages—in a female, non-Irish accent—clearly violates the sonic quality that the words are intended to convey. This difficulty helps to foreground a final principle attached to the gender, class, and regional valences of Joyce's vernacular as sonic music—and that is their ultimately and paradoxically nonmaterial quality. Terence Killeen's point about the ambiguous regional inflection of the washerwomen's voices is an inference drawn from Joyce's reproduction of their sound, rather than an inference drawn from the graphological representation of written accent on the page.15 Insofar as Joyce's voice is male, not-withstanding his attempted mimicry of a female voice, it necessarily fails to do sonic justice to his written vernacular text. But if even the seemingly authoritative exercise of James Joyce's own reading necessarily falsifies the imagined sound of "Anne Livia Plurabelle," then is the sound of his vernacular writing audible at all? James Joyce may give the regionality of the dialogue expression but not its gender, whereas my American female voice can produce neither the general Irish accent evoked by the piece, let alone its indeterminate rural variant. Even SiobhAn McKenna's recording of the dialogue reproduces an Irish female voice but arguably without the rural inflection of the Joyce recording. Perhaps Joyce's vernacular embeds such a layering of minority inflections into his English text that the language produces no more than a construct of speech both familiar and irrevocably marked by the difference of otherness. The elusive materiality of its sonic quality marks the barrier to our desire to imagine ourselves into the lives of these fictional creations. We can imagine the trials and pains of the washerwomen's lives as conveyed in their speech, but our empathy is reduced to a! crude mimicry when we try to embody it with our voice. In the end the music of Joyce's vernacular voices reminds us that throughout Finnegans Wake, what we hear is the voice of the other.

Margot Norris  

Margot Norris is Chancellor's Professor of English and Comparative Literature at the University of California, Irvine. She is the author of six books including four on the work of James Joyce: The Decentered Universe of 'Finnegans Wake' , Joyce's Web: The Social Unraveling of Modernism , Suspicious Readings of Joyce's 'Dubliners' , and a study of the Strick film of Ulysses .
