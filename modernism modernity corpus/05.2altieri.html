The Concept of Force as Modernist Response to the Authority of Science

Charles Altieri

An organization of forms expresses a confluence of forces. . . . For example, if you clap a strong magnet beneath a plateful of iron filings, the energies in the magnet will proceed to organize form. It is only by applying a particular and subtle force that you can bring order and vitality and thence beauty into a plate of iron filings, which are otherwise as ugly as anything under heaven.

Ezra Pound

This is what Suprematism means to me--the dawn of an era in which the nucleus will move as a single force of anatomized energy and will expand within new, orbiting spatial systems. . . . I think that freedom can be attained only after our ideas about the organization of solids have been completely smashed. . . . There is only energy. Therefore everything is linked and at the same time separate in its own motion. . . . Expressing this dynamic functioning is the primary purpose of consciousness.

Kasimir Malevich 1

Recently I attended an extraordinary conference, "From Energy to Information," at the University of Texas at Austin. Scholars there did superb ground-breaking work showing how deeply modernist art was engaged in the issues and the themes developed by the sciences. But the very power of these presentations made me sufficiently anxious to want to address the dangers involved in this historical enterprise. On the most fundamental level, establishing scientific contexts for the arts raises the problem of just how we state the relationship between what seems to be the artist'ss intention and what we can say about the frameworks within which that intention is articulated? And that question requires our clearly addressing two closely related but importantly distinct issues: how such contexutalizing will change our interpretations of these writers and artists as historical agents, and how such a stance will modify our own perspectives as we try to evaluate what claims their work has on our present?

My fear on the first issue is that, simply because we can find analogies or because the artists and writers make vague claims about matters like the fourth dimension, we will go on to use our knowledge of the science to force strong thematic analogies on to the work. If we rely too directly on these invocations of science, we run the risk of underplaying the considerable uneasiness most of the modernists had about such dependencies. Were they fully to embrace science, they might have to accept both the impersonal subject positions cultivated by the sciences and the ideals of impersonal verification fundamental to their sense of community. But such commitments were not easy to correlate with the seductive appeal of the alienation-postures still underlying much modernist work or with the emphases on feeling and on constructive will that the artists and writers hoped might counter or dignify that alienation.

However liberating science's version of impersonal dehumanization might prove, artists almost always had to restore some aspects of the romantic values they were ostensively denying, as in Eliot's claim that only those who knew what it meant to suffer from personality would appreciate the impersonality he was calling for. And however exalting science's models of universalizing abstraction and its promise of progress, the arts had to restore the rative and exemplifying power of individual compositions as their instruments for realizing how nature's deep laws took effect. 2 So on the most general level I think we have to find a perspective for dealing with responses to science that float warily between two quite different positive appeals--the opportunity for specific thematic allegories drawn from ideas like "the fourth dimension" and the promise of an experimental method that would replace allegorizing entirely by giving access to realities that are immediately present in their abstract elemental force and hence do not depend at all on speculative interpretation.

One way the modernists handled their ambivalence in relation to such appeals was to explore possible allegiances with with more mystical versions of science, versions that Bruce Clarke shows were dangerously close to a scientism in which experimental method yields to allegorical procedures. 3 Therefore it is reasonable to examine just how deeply into their work such explorations went. But these invitations also bring with them a double problem. Not only do we have to be careful to connect the interest in science to the modernists' other basic concerns (especially with formal innovation), but we also have to worry about whether pursuing the scientific analogies will help us capture what to other artists and writers has seemed the most imaginatively vital features of their work. If we are not careful on this matter, the effort to contextualize the art within the parameters of modern science will end up reducing modernism to charming mystification. We will stress what was the least intensely invested and is the least intellectually challenging dimension of the art, in the process losing both what seemed most innovative at the time and what has the richest possibility for engaging contemporary imaginations.

In order to avoid these problems, even while learning from the efforts creating them, I will explore a way of acknowledging what the artists sought in their forays into science without forcing the art within the straitjacket imposed by the specific, historically dated scientific doctrines the modernists invoked. And I hope to develop a more general theoretical account of how the effort to find thematic analogies deeply misconceives the overall ambitions driving the artists' versions of formal experiment. By turning from the specific details of modern science to what seems the basic desire underlying the artists' use of such details, I hope we can keep foregrounded those aspects of their art that do not go gently into the dump that history keeps in wait for them. My model for specifying the terms of this desire will be Hegel's work on the concept of force. Using this model will not entail claiming that the modernists read Hegel, although Hegelian ideas are basic to some of the spiritualisms that fascinated them. Nor will I claim that the artists and writers had a coherent and articulate concept of force. I will use the concept of "force" to characterize how a substantial body of modernist art makes compelling and still vital imaginative structures out of their interests in quasi-spiritual claims about latent dynamism that we find in thinkers ranging from Bergson to Eddington. A short catalogue would point to Mondrian on "dymamic equilibrium," Malevich on the fourth and fifth dimensions as aspects of non-objective force, and the literary metaphors developed to adapt to the plastic tensions made popular by Cubism. All of these emphases share with the sciences a commitment to the most elemental dynamic building blocks of contemporary life, but they also insist on representing that dynamic dimension in ways more directly connected to the life of the mind than all but the most mystical and problematic of the prevailing scientific perspectives allowed.

Hence Hegel. Hegel on force enables us to address both problems I have been discussing--what modernists seek to make articulate and how we can best develop value languages for that work. The concept of force helps us get close to the artists' intentions because it keeps attention focussed on dynamic physical dimensions of affective life and because it demands a notion of the psyche in deep sympathetic vibration with the modernists' intense suspicions of humanist ideals. It is not a large leap from Hegel on force to the following sentiment by Nietzsche:

It is not all necessary to get rid of the "soul" . . . and thus to renounce of the most ancient and venerable hypotheses--as happens frequently to clumsy naturalists who can hardly touch on the "soul" without immediately losing it. But the way is open for new versions and refinements of the soul-hypothesis; and such conceptions as "mortal soul" and "soul as subjective multiplicity," and "soul as social structure of the drives and affects," want henceforth to have citizen's rights in science. 4

Even more important, relying on Hegel on force provides an overall methodological contrast to work straightforwardly elaborating analogues between modernist art and modern science. Rather than fit the art to science's explanatory schemas, we can explore the ways in which the concern for the dynamic actually modifies how works are constructed and relations to audiences envisioned. We cannot deal with force simply as an idea. We have to see how it is embodied, or how it affects ideals of embodiment. And that means treating the arts in roughly the same phenomenological fashion that Hegel demonstrates is necessary to talk about force at all in so far as force is engaged as an actual dimension of our experience. So critical work in this spirit has to shift its focus away from which ideas link the arts with science and toward the distinctive uses which the art works make of science in their specific modes of unfolding before audiences. Finally this connection to phenomenology will enable us to clarify how treating the works as appearances establishes an imaginative realm within which the artists' work can retain currency long after the specific sciences they allude to are at best remote footnotes in textbooks.

2.

Hegel's basic treatment of "force" takes place early in his Phenomenology of Spirit as his means of establishing the inadequacy of any pure perception-based empiricism; then later in the Phenomenology he uses the same basic logic to criticize Enlightenment science's version of covering laws. But in order to show how his account will matter for us, I want first to set up an example that I will then keep central in my exposition. The example is Kasimir Malevich's Red Square Black Square--in part because the painting so dramatically sets something like force against something like the conditions of extensional empiricist description, and in part because we will have a strong example of how artists like Malevich melded idealist thinking into the quasi-scientific vocabulary that abstract painting was eager to appropriate.

In my Painterly Abstraction I make three basic points about Malevich's painting that bear repeating here. 5 First, it calls attention to the dialectic established by the painting's structure. The tilt of the smaller red square refuses subordination to the spatial frameworks imposed by the black square and by the frame. That negation then becomes a demand that the viewer bring to bear a second system of coordinates in which the tilt itself determines alignments within the painting. Attending to these coordinates then turns the surrounding white space into an active field helping to constitute an intricate balancing of both organizational principles in relation to now dynamic color, texture, and size contrasts. What could have been simple flat surfaces echoing each other becomes instead a dynamic field reconciling potential motion in several dimensions with an overall compositional harmony.

My other two points involve the thematic possibilities that these visual relations elicit. For example it is difficult not to treat Malevich's tilt as also a means of foregrounding the intricate ontology that his abstraction affords. On one level the world of sensation is reduced entirely to purely extensional flat colored surfaces. There is no illusion at all, except perhaps for the hints of depth set up by the relation of the two squares. Yet this reduction to pure visual matter also seems to sustain a powerful transcendental movement. It may be precisely as pure extensional surface without illusion that the painting produces the dynamic intensive balances that I will elaborate as a thematics of the tilt. Here abstraction literally marries the possibility of meaning to pure sensation and hence opens a realm of metaphoric possibilities based not on what the painting represents but on what its elements do.

My third claim offers specific ways to read those metaphoric possibilities. Minimally, Malevich is playing on the relation of art to life and mind to matter. This painting is so elemental that it could not be a found object; only mind could make it with such delicate balances of shape and color. These flat shapes are and are not simply objects, since within the painting, within the frames provided by art conventions, the shapes work as relationships and the relationships open metaphoric implications that do not displace but pervade the material elements. It seems as if intelligence literally inhabited these otherwise purely visual elements. In fact the material tilt here becomes a powerful emblem for the relation of art to life and of art to the representational traditions underlying this play of intelligence. In coming alive as a work of art, this conration literally enacts a tilt, both acknowledging and providing alternatives to the coordinates established by purely extensional seeing. Malevich's painting invites us to participate in a metaphorical dwelling within what it has reduced to purely extensional marks, so that painterly intelligence becomes simply the possibility of maintaining just such an imaginative space, reducing representation to marks in order to free intelligence from the confines of description and dramatic interpretation. Finally, because that intelligence makes its own place, the painting ultimately offers itself as a self-reflexive embodiment of all those features of our lives where the only difference between pure extensional matter and what we have called spirit is a simple tilt producing an irreducibly "non-objective" dynamics. What else makes my body and world into a subject's? And what else can define an ontology for the mysteries of loving and caring that seem very difficult to account for within standard Enlightenment principles. Tilting is a purposive but not rationalized model for how we inhabit extensional objects.

3.

It should be easy now to see why it was so tempting for my Painterly Abstraction to treat the self-reflexive participation that such paintings invite in terms of thematics of agency. I thought this could capture the dynamic features of Malevich's ontology, but now that I have read Hegel I have a measure of how my own metaphoric register was severely limited by its anthropomorphic investments. Hegel's chapter on force in his Phenomenology of Spirit allows us to stay at a threshold where the human remains intricately caught up in what cannot be humanized. He accomplishes this by locating the non-objective in a very elemental but irreducible supplement to those extensional features that can be adequately handled by empirical description. 6 Hegel's preceding chapter had been devoted to the certainties of sense, and hence provided a picture of what his extensional knowledge could be. He turns to force in order to establish dialectical transitions between the world of pure perception and one in which awareness of the world proves inseparable from the intricacies of self-consciousness. Once that link is established the rest of the Phenomenology can go on to elaborate "what consciousness knows in knowing itself" (PS, 103). But Hegel's chapter on force does not quite yield to this faith in ultimate absolute knowledge. Instead Hegel focusses not on how consciousness knows itself but on how consciousness is solicited into attempting to situate itself within the world of appearances that its own activity renders incomplete and problematic. So on this level of Hegel's story there need be no talk of absolutes or even of the dialectical interplay of substance and spirit. Here Hegel offers only an account of those demands and embarrassments that both torment and fascinate consciousness as it seeks to articulate its fundamental relations to the sensual world.

Let me then take a few moments to trace the basic movements in Hegel's discussion of force so that the case can create intellectual resonances for Malevich's painting, while the painting provides a concrete image grounding the philosophy's abstract arguments. 7 For Hegel, our most elemental experience of force is as something whose effects are visible in or upon the objects of perception but which cannot be the simple object of our attention, since we cannot refer to the force within the same pictorial model we use to refer to the objects. As examples one might think of electricity or gravity or, more metaphorically, of desires and emotions. We can characterize force and even regulate it, but we cannot represent it as force except through abstract formulae. Therefore any description that wants to preserve the qualities that lead us to speak about force, without reducing these to static pictures, will have to vacillate between phenomenological and explanatory perspectives. Because force both invites and repels description, Hegel can use it as his means of shifting from the static unities that consciousness establishes to the constant differing from itself which is characteristic of self-consciousness. 8

Hegel begins his account of force by claiming that it becomes manifest as active resistance to the plenitude of universals put in place by perception. Perception depends on universals because it has to work within established categories, has to see something as only a positive identity (PS, 80). We perceive a light bulb as on or off. But suppose we begin to ask what produces that difference (or ask what makes us ask about that difference). We cannot answer such questions simply by observing. They move us from a world of objects perceived to one where perceptions are only appearances, only phenomena whose shape and effects depend on something that cannot be located entirely in the object. Awareness of force, then, makes it impossible simply to treat the object as an isolated, self-sufficient particular. Force acts upon the object, rendering it passive in relation to the being of some other reality. And in so doing it also opens the possibility that this object has kinship with other apparently independent objects. Once we grasp electricity as a force, even without understanding how that force works, we see that all light bulbs seem to work in the same way by responding to some general feature we now have to add to our sense of what counts as real.

Hegel insists on providing an ontology for these relational properties. Thus force is characterized as gathering "otherness itself, into itself" because it literally gets its identity as the substance of its differences from the universalizing thought perception relies on. Most philosophers would avoid the expression "otherness itself, into itself," because that gives a positive existence to what Hegel's account presents primarily as negative. But Hegel takes that risk because he wants to develop parallels between the force that we observe and the forces that are involved in our observing. If we try to understand what underlies the coming on and off of light bulbs at various moments, we have to see ourselves both negating and sublating any model of thinking bound only to the particular object. We have to hypothesize some supplemental factors, within and without. Yet our new account still has to preserve our direct awareness of the specific light bulb. Similarly, we find our relations to ourselves closely paralleling the relation we posit between object and force: we are driven by something we cannot picture, yet it is awareness of our subjection to that "something" which ultimately allows us to bring into one framework the differences our activity generates. We, in effect, are aspects of the turned on lights that have to recognize how through our observing we differ from the simple states from which we began.

Once the psyche enters, Hegel's account gets even more interesting, since there are at least three concrete ways in which the distinctions he generates to handle questions about force in the object world also clarify principles we need for talking about the psyche. First, once consciousness concerns itself with force, what had been objects that simply constitute a world become appearances beneath which some other factors are responsible for the manifest effects. Second, when appearances can no longer be treated adequately by the languages we apply to objects, there emerge questions about expression. What do the appearances make visible that is not suited to purely descriptive languages, and what then is the specific relation between the being that appears and these underlying factors now also part of the scene for consciousness? And third, trying to understand that relation between appearance and what underlies it creates often unresolveable concerns about which aspects of the expression are active, that is controlled by the mode of appearing, and which aspects show how the being is acted upon by some ancillary conditions. Active forces reveal the being for itself, eagerly "soliciting" what might complete it, while what seems passive requires our asking how one enters the role of being "solicited" as a being for another whose controlling presence we then have to speculate upon. 9 For example, if our light bulb were to dim, we might have to explain that dimming as caused by the specific condition of the light bulb, or its part in the home's specific electrical structures, or a symptom of some system-wide failure. Human dimming and brightening allows richer possibilities for talk of activity, but also requires more complex stories suspicion about the actual impact of the soliciting forces exercising their influence from without.

At this point in Hegel's exposition the interplay of active and passive becomes far too complex for me to track. But although we probably already have enough background to understand what Malevich is making of his nineteenth-century sources, I want to provide at least some of the flavor of that dialectic. If the light here dims too much, please just skip the next three paragraphs.

We have just seen that what seems active as force in relation to an object can also seem passive as an effect of some more comprehensive system. But, Hegel insists, there cannot be any simple one way relation between what he calls the comprehended and the comprehensive aspects of force. For we have to posit a force working through objects and a force working through mind, and we have to see that while mind organizes physical relations, it is these relations that both solicit and influence how the mind develops those organizations. In Hegel's terms, the notion of force "becomes actual through its duplication into two forces" (PS, 85)--one the grounding element and the other the manifest medium of the effect of that ground. Both phsyical and mental activities function as ground and as medium.

We can see the mind determined by the body or the mind giving purpose to what otherwise might give purpose to it. This duplicity then creates for each locus of activity the danger that it will seem to lose its particularity as force because its activity vanishes into the concepts we use to fix it or into the objects we use to trace its diminishment (such as spirit becoming only body). At the opposite pole each locus has the corresponding possibility of actively standing over against concepts or objects by managing to define its own expressive capacities. We can lose the phenomenology of electricity to purely general analyses or to specific accounts of what happens in particular objects; and we can lose the phenomenology of self-consciousness to the satisfactions of dwelling simply within the generalizations that are developed to explain how electricity works. Conversely there remains the capacity for expression to break through repression--either in sudden glimpses of why we are fascinated by electricity or why we are fascinated by our own processes of thinking on the topic. 10

Hegel's chapter's richest concrete speculations on this interplay of active and passive, phenomenological and structural, take place when he turns to discussing how attention to force leads necessarily to the positing of laws. Laws offer a mode of universalization quite different from those of perception, since a law is not simply a category for particular objects but a way of giving objectivity to relations among objects. Law is "the stable image of unstable appearance" (PS, 90). By postulating laws we produce sameness out of the play of difference. Even when the eye sees only differences, the mind can register underlying identities because of how the differences participate in the same structural necessities. Law, then, regularizes flux into deeper structures, and it creates the possibility that knowledge itself extends beyond appearances.

Law makes force one thing rather than many. But this unity is at best an incomplete one, because the concept of law is not sufficiently attuned to the variety that it organizes. 11 So the very unity law brings opens the way to further division. Spirit finds itself having to account not only for how law relates to its objects but also how it both creates and is created by subjects. Law seems the within of appearances, but its distance from what it claims to regularize also makes it an appearance through which we seek some other within. That within turns out to be another manifestation of force, since whatever seems to underly our attributions of lawfulness has the same dynamic and ungraspable relation to universals that force does in its simpler manifestations.

Now Hegel is ready to show what will be involved in consciousness becoming conscious of itself. There is one law that seems as if it can actually capture necessity because it makes the Kantian shift from what underlies objects to what underlies our experience of making sense of those objects. This law takes form as a generalizing of the very process of self-consciousness that is emerging--namely, "that like becomes unlike and unlike becomes like" (PS, 96). Whatever consciousness grasps it inverts, turning force into concept and law and casting those universals back into the play of forces that constantly solicit them. Such self-consciousness can have certainty of itself because of its capacity to provide form for force, and, through that, to feel its powers to make attributions about a "supersensible." Yet this "supersensible" need not be trapped in the same static world as Kant's transcendental reasoning because whatever force consciousness feels must remain tightly wound into the structure of appearance. Any other version of force called up by consciousness will soon be revealed as empty, or merely mystical because it will not be situated within what actually solicits consciousness. Reflecting on the relation between force and self-consciousness then leads us to realize that the supersensible is entirely immanent--not the other of appearance but an other within appearance that allows us to formulate the concept of "appearance qua appearance" (PS, 89). Rather than dwelling beneath appearances, the supersensible consists in the mind's simultaneous sense of its bondage to appearance and its freedom to bring our values and causes within a world that now on one level exists for consciousness, hence as appearance. 12

Hegels qua, in other words, is inseparable from Malevich's tilt. Consciousness feels itself as the point of transfer between appearance and appearance seen as appearance, and hence as involving complex relational dependencies on what makes appearance different from perception. And self-consciousness emerges as the awareness of the interplay of negative and reconstructive powers that enables us to appreciate those relational dependencies and treat them as complex balances. In one dimension the tilt is a simple occurence, almost a whimsical variation manifesting the simple force of change. But in the very process of registering that effect, the painting also establishes a supremely confident moment of self-consciousness both defining its capacity to engage new coordinates and coming to appreciate the overall relational structure that this gesture creates. Solicited by the shift from object to appearance, self-consciousness can explore its own soliciting powers, until they become their own opposite, mainfesting the painting's sense of being solicited by something that seems more capacious and structural within appearance than individual self-consciousness. What emerges as agency now is inseparable from a mediating process that such consciousness can no longer pretend to control, even though it cannot stop trying.

4.

Providing an intellectual context for reading one Malevich painting does not a theory make. But it does offer one example of how we can interpret modernist work so as to acknowledge its interest in the spirit of science without turning it into a thematic analogue for specific scientific doctrines. Malevich uses a scientific atmosphere as a frame for an experience whose specific unfolding requires a quite different vocabulary and a quite different interpretive stance. I have tried to clarify the vocabulary; now I will turn to developing a few theoretical consequences that follow from emphasizing those dynamic aspects of the art works foregrounded by the concept of force. First, though, a brief caveat. Shifting to theory here will demand walking a delicate line between claiming that the concept of force can make a substantial difference in our discourses about the arts in general and isolating values distinctive to modernist experiments in the arts. But there is a reward for facing this problem, since this balancing act is one we will share with the modernists. They felt the need both to break from their heritage and to use that break as a means of revitalizing their heritage in the arts (or, as Hegel would have put it, to extend their heritage by negating it in ways that make articulate latent forces that in fact keep the heritage unified).

Let me, then, keep modernist abstract painting as my specific focus, but I will concentrate on issues sufficiently theoretical to suggest an overall perspective quite different from both classical and Kantian aesthetics and quite resistant to the thematizing of analogies, scientific or moral or social. Using the lens of what Hegel on force dramatizes in modernist abstraction, I hope to clarify how we locate expressivity in art, how art works can engage emotions in ways that complicate standard notions of subject-object relations, and, finally, how we understand the role of theory in these inquiries. But I cannot do this without presupposing my conclusions on the last of these topics. I will proceed under the assumption that one need not treat theory in the arts as high-level explanation but can instead approach it as oriented towards the phenomenological thickening of appearance so that we ultimately find the qua of appearance charged with intellectual resonance. Rather than place particulars within categories, this perspective uses generalization to suffuse those particulars with meanings that give self-consciousness both peace and substance.

My first claim is my most general, potentially least contentious, and probably most important. Hegel on force makes clear why any work of art, perhaps any particular we come to value, cannot be treated entirely in descriptive or pictorial terms. One must capture the tension between force and objects of perception, and consequently one must preserve the dynamic relation between appearance and some constitutive other to the object that emerges when it becomes seen as appearance. To do that theory has to introduce concerns for expressiveness, concerns which require attending to relations among what is hidden and what manifest, to the play of soliciting and solicited features, to the work of postulating universals or laws and the recognition of their limitations, and to the intricate ways that these various aspects fold into and out of one another.

Such concerns are basic to any theory of expressiveness. Hegel on force makes possible a distinctive theory because he deals with such elemental features of expressivity that we need not rely on specific psychological hypotheses. Perhaps more important, this focus on elements links Hegel to Levinasian concerns for an otherness fundamental to eliciting human responses (without requiring Levinasian conclusions). Expressivity is not simply a matter of how agency is deployed or emotions made manifest: expressivity remains a relational structure between something not stateable and necessarily incomplete efforts to frame what appearance manifests.

The best way to bring out the value of basing the analysis of expressivity on Hegel's treatment of force is to develop a contrast with the far more influential Kantian analysis of the immanence basic to the appearance (Schein) constituting aesthetic objects. For Kant art works matter because they are radically particular and yet give pleasure in a mode that can be called judgment because the pleasure appeals for universal assent. That radical particularity stems from "purposiveness without purpose," that is from marks of a shaping intelligence that cannot be successfully subsumed under any concepts, and hence that manifest a genius giving the rule to art. From Kant on, then, positive accounts of immanence in art works have emphasized the work that psychological or formal or interpretive intelligences do in giving shape to inchoate worlds. Such assumptions have bound theory to a fairly narrow range of evaluative principles like those stressing complexity or intricate unity, in part because the stress on formative intelligence makes it extremely difficult to talk directly about emotions or about how audiences are positioned and acted upon. Analogously, the Kantian line of argument entails treating all struggle that matters in a great work as part of the author's plan, rather than resistances from the material or from contradictions the author refuses to face.

There have been strong objections to this aspect of Kant, of course, but for the most part they derive from psychoanalytic criticism (that has trouble dealing with art as art) or from a rhetoric of common sense or from oppositional claims to expose the underlying contradictions. And all of these critiques continue to rely on the binaries Kant set up. Those binaries in turn make it difficult for theory to account for the emotional component of aesthetic experience because they force us to treat everything we cannot locate as specifiable formal properties within the object as necessarily elements attributable to entirely private subjective response. Similarly, when Kant's stress on authorial intelligence is challenged, it is done by reducing art works to semiotic weavings that critics fit into various allegories subordinating internal energies within the work to the critic's theoretical schema.

Concentrating on Hegel on force allows us a very different view of expressivity and the structure of emotions that expressivity elicits. From a Hegelian perspective, specific works can be seen as embodying an interplay between force and appearance that does not reduce force to intelligence, nature to either heteronomy or lawfulness, or response to already constituted empirical subjects. Instead expressiveness gets located in the processes that emerge as forces working within and against appearance. These processes retain their concreteness while taking on a suggestiveness that seems generalizable yet resistant to concepts. Such concreteness obviously can produce a wide variety of effects. But two effects seem to me especially worthy of notice because they have substantial implications for how we value the arts. Noam Gabo neatly summarized the first when he characterized the "absolute shapes" of abstract art as manifesting "all the properties of a real force having a positive and a negative direction." 13 Kantian aesthetics treats the object only as a positive structure, generating aesthetic value primarily by internal relations. Gabo's modernism, on the other hand, emphasizes the reality of the object as object so that we will attend not only what it asserts but to how that assertion is positioned in relation to what it rejects or sublates. Unlike concepts of form, those of force treat objects as embodying transitions between states, and hence as exemplifying actual struggle. Expression is also resistance; construction also vital negation working to justify negative processes like the act of abstraction itself.

Heidegger then adds a second aspect to this sense of how art works are active within the world. His entire aesthetic is based on Hegel's ideal of having appearance emerge qua appearance. Art works can have compelling immanence because "the work opens up a world and keeps it abidingly in force." 14 In so far as the art manifests its powers as work, it establishes a "world," a "forever non-objective" concrete situation in which things "gain their lingering and hastening, their remoteness and nearness, their scope and their limits." "A work by being a work makes space for that spaciousness" by which "the world worlds" (PS, 45). What remains in excess of appearance does not become law but instead becomes a condition of activity inseparable from what the work makes manifest. Consequently it cannot suffice to "interpret" works of art. Interpretation must be kept in continual tension with an awareness of how the work maintains its "appearance," engaging through that an interplay of soliciting and solicited forces that refuse to settle into any one determinate entity for the imagination.

In an essay related to the present one, I try to build from this analysis a specific theory of expressivity in the arts. 15 But here I can only posit as my second basic claim a brief case that this approach to expressivity emphasizes both the affective life within the work and the possibility of our recasting the psychology by which we characterize that life. Let me use Pound's vorticist poem "Heather" as my example:

The black panther treads at my side,
And above my fingers
There float the petal-like flames
The milk-white girls
Unbend from the holly-trees,
And their snow white leopard
Watches to follow our trace. 16

First, if we concentrate on how art objects intensify Hegelian versions of force, we can see as part of their power their capacity to keep us fascinated by what remains an "other" to discursive intelligence, an "other" keeping intelligence dialectically aware of its own limitations and its own imperatives. Ultimately Malevich's painting remains an arrangement of shapes and colors that enter into relations evoking thought and even exemplifying certain powers of mind, yet these relations lose their vitality if they lose their concreteness or prove mere instances of some general law or perspective. And in Pound's poem almost all the effect depends on its keeping the ground of the contrast between the black panther and the white leopard just beyond the intellect, so that the sexual trace claimed by the end of the poem extends into strange necessities that in turn suggest entrance into disturbing psychological sites.

This otherness then has at least two important consequences. Minimally, such art does not allow self-sufficiency to any knowledge position satisfied by description and by subsuming particulars under generalizations. Responsiveness to art is also recognition that these knowledge positions must also be seen as themselves positioned by factors that cannot be entirely transparent to consciousness. On that basis, I think, we can then add the stronger claim that even within the processes of establishing knowledge claims we find an active negative, a non-objectivity that is not simply an aporia but an immediacy and silence which point beyond our practical sense of ourselves as agents. We experience intentionality as situated at a margin of sense where it is composed as much as it composes, and hence where we appreciate the imperative within art to break human images down into elemental factors not quite intelligible within the selves we compose for direct action. In this regard abstraction is the progenitor of Picasso's monster-artists and Surrealism's reconrations of agency in terms of body-parts.

My second claim about affect simply extends this sense of irreducible otherness to the domain of emotions evoked and constructed within the work of art. It is a commonplace to say that modernist emotions resist narrative organization, but we rarely get good accounts of why the quest for alternatives to narrative seemed so pressing a goal. If we focus on how force is established as resistance to appearance, we can explain the rejection of narrative as a refusal to treat powerful emotions as if they were something we could understand simply by placing them in developmental contexts. Emotions must be allowed their strangeness, their capacity to make demands on the intellect that the intellect cannot satisfy. And, more important, an adequate artistic rendering of the force of emotions must evoke different dimensions of self-consciousness than those that narrative calls upon. When we are confronted with emotional demands that do not easily contextualize, we are invited to appreciate the degree to which the life of the passions is not congruent with the grammars that arrange experience in terms of intellectual categories, especially categories based on sharp boundaries and on clear distinctions between active and passive or subject and object. Such force restores instead the complex grammar of "feel," with its intricate interrelations of active and passive elements.

Think again of Pound's trace or of Malevich's dynamic tilt. In one sense the feelings elicited are aspects of hyper-conscious attention to how particular elements are related. Yet this very intensity of consciousness is not easily attributed entirely to active agency--for either artist or audience--because of the degree to which we find ourselves literally "moved" by what partially comes to determine how our individual investments in the world get deployed. The force in art works sets up an intricate flow of traces and possible sources positioning the psyche as simultaneously deep in its absorption and transparent in its openness and manipulability. Even that understanding we do manage for representing how we are moved seems very close to an illusion or evasion that we create, as if we arrived at what we could contain in our representation because of forces impelling us to just this form of containment. The tear in appearances penetrates the psyche, so that emotion too has its fullness only by taking form within the work as emotion qua emotion. And that makes it dizzyingly difficult to say just who is it who responds to and as the locus of those emotions. Is it really my empirical ego that responds to the tilt or that feels itself deployed in the expression "our trace" that ends Pound's poem?

Finally, we arrive at my third and last general claim. This aesthetics of force changes our model for what is at stake in how art crosses other domains of life, and hence for the preferred roles interpretive theory can play. Here I ask you to recall the central passage of Pound's Canto II. 17 There Pound stages as "a breathing out of nothing" a theophany that embodies a pagan incarnation--not the word become flesh but the world become metamorphosed into the presence of the god. Then he turns to the effect this incarnation has on the one witness not caught up in violent blindness toward that god. All that character says is "I worship,/ I have seen what I have seen." Yet that is all he need say: what matters is not how he interprets the event but how his openness to the event modifies him. Force is not something added to details; rather it is the sense of demand built into the details, so that the effect is not something we reason to but something that provides a basis for the giving of reasons. Force is the dimension of experience where all the ladders start.

Correspondingly, theory seems most useful not for telling us what the witness should have seen but for fleshing out terms for what might be seen and how that seeing might have direct effects on further interpretations. Historically, such experiences require a Nietzschean hermeneutics and provide one measure for the importance of Nietzschean psychology, especially in relation to the issue of how modernism's relation to science might be interpreted. Using Pound as my example, I suggest that modernism in the arts gives considerable urgency to at least the negative side of Nietzsche's powerful contrast between the will to truth and the will to power. And in so doing such work makes clear what we lose if we ally the arts too closely to even what is best in the sciences. For each involves at bottom a quite different version of the will to power and consequently of what constitutes interpretive community.

Let me be schematic, even though careful distinctions are necessary. Nietzsche saw the dominant tendencies of his culture as manifestations of a will mired in epistemic commitments. Truth seemed the highest state that persons could achieve, and nobility involved making the sacrifices necessary to bind oneself to one's capacity for rational analysis, whether that be Kant's deontology or Bentham's empiricism. But when truth is so idealized the personal becomes its antagonist. All the most intense states of the individual person must be suppressed or repressed in order to align the individual to the normative power of rationalizing disciplines. Yet while these disciplines offered the power to deny such personal states, they lacked the power to comprehend them. The personal would always be the marginal, the disturbing, and the disruptive. Science itself cannot adequately explain the personal ambitions for power of its protagonists nor adequately direct public ambitions for the use of such power, at least insofar as the methods of science proved difficult to correlate with needs shaped by local traditions.

Recognizing the force of Nietzsche's criticisms here does not warrant embracing his own alternative. On the one hand, the same criticisms can be used simply to weaken what counts as "truth" so that we can use pragmatist stances that bring the will to power under social control. And one might also cite vague ideals of resistance or sentimental calls for response criticisms and other forms of subjectivism that complement but do not really change the normative structure of the will to truth. On the other hand, there is the constant danger of taking Nietzsche's own alternative too literally, so that the arts become emblems for a will to power that takes on the right to decide in our public practices what will count as "truth." Pound made that mistake. But the terms of his own epic vision need not lead in that direction. They require only that we recognize how desire sets the stage for what counts as truth, and hence that we devote our theorizing about the arts to speculations that flesh out what is involved in different kinds of loyalties. At stake is not so much how we decide among those loyalties as how fully we let those loyalties themselves make claims upon us that we can only express in the mode of witnessing.

Such witnessing involves a testimonial model of criticism. But such testimony is not based on identity politics or on any claims organized in terms of how our expressions might originate in specific experiences or conditions of victimage. The object of testimony is not who we are as empirical agents but who we become when we find ourselves moved to intensities that have no explicit grounding separable from the direct expression of loyalty--I have seen what I have seen, and I worship until some more powerful god comes along. Of course for an entire polity to live in accord with such intensities we have to keep art's way of being public from taking over recieved measures for deciding on rights and on justice. But we are only faithful to the most intense forces in the arts when we recognize with pain how thoroughly those concessions contradict the imaginative attitudes cultivated by the arts. And now science reenters my story--as the basic cause of that pain because for it witnessing only makes sense within a rationalizing discipline. Confronted by that demand, art's silences become socially ineffective, and historians rush into the opening void.
