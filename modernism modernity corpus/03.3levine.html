Science and Citizenship: Karl Pearson and the Ethics of Epistemology

George Levine

Science and Modernism:
A Panel

When I was a boy the New York Daily Mirror ran on its masthead the injunction from John 8:32: "Ye shall know the truth and the truth shall make ye free." I was never too young to miss the irony of that statement on a Hearst newspaper, but I felt it as an irony that didn't force me to question the ideal the masthead affirmed. I didn't, however, know then that the apostle John and William Hearst were affirming a fundamental tenet of late-nineteenth-century positivism. The great Enlightenment ideal, the commitment to knowledge and rationality as a means to progressive improvement of the human condition, found one of its last important outlets in positivism. Let me red-facedly admit that--despite a lifetime of rebuffs--I have not given up that commitment.

Karl Pearson participated vigorously in it. In his 1892 Grammar of Science, he virtually begins, in a section called "Science and Citizenship," by asserting that the scientific "frame of mind seems to me an essential of good citizenship." 1 If this looks suspiciously like science as the opiate of the masses, it may also be taken as a good-faith commitment to the view that epistemology should be driven by human needs. Put that way, it sounds not like scientific disingenuousness but pragmatist revisionism. Surely, Pearson's explanation has some contemporary resonance: "Every modern citizen," he says, "is thrust into an appalling maze of social and educational problems." And every citizen is "called upon to form a judgment apart, if it possibly may be, from his own feelings and emotions--a judgment in what he conceives to be the interests of society at large" (GS, 10). Here an Arnoldian ideal of disinterest joins with positivist idealizing of knowledge.

The Grammar of Science, the most important summary of scientific epistemology, method, and possibility since John Herschel's Preliminary Discourse on the Study of Natural Philosophy of 1830, embraced both the political and epistemological ideals of a new vision for science and society. "The goal of science is clear," Pearson concludes; "it is nothing short of the complete interpretation of the universe" (GS, 18).

Against this scientific imperialism it would be easy now to succumb to the temptation to push once again the argument that scientific thought--ostensibly disinterested and objective--disguises self-interest and ideological complicity. Pearson was a propagandist for strong pre-Vienna Circle positivist theory and at the same time one of the most active and important of eugenicists. The connection makes itself. There can be no mistaking that his major scientific initiatives were ideologically driven by a passion for social reform, and a reform based on a eugenic program of racial improvement.

I want to resist that temptation. I am interested in shaking up what may be a current critical complacency latent in the idea that all knowledge is political. Of course. And then what? In Pearson's case, for example, there is the fact of "statistics." Statistics as a scientific discipline given shape and direction by Pearson is an indispensable tool of modern life. J. B. S. Haldane once remarked that Pearson's "theory of heredity was incorrect in some fundamental respects. So was Columbus's theory of geography. He set out for China, and discovered America." 2 It is partly because of this apparently odd result--the growth of a valuable science from an obviously ideologically driven enterprise--that I want to think about the implications of his work for our own relations to knowledge and science and politics. In addition, Pearson is a particularly interesting subject because his theories raise echoes of contemporary nonpositivist epistemological enterprises, and because his politics are far more complicated--he was both socialist and feminist--than the connection with eugenics first suggests. Pearson is, then, an ideal test case for my tentative exploration of the possibility of recuperating some aspects of the positivist enterprise, including certain elements of the cognitive and something like an imagination of uncontaminated knowledge.

I don't propose, with Pearsonian hubris, to manage such a recuperation. At best, I expect to complicate the picture: Pearson's kind of positivism opens onto possibilities that many of us have been embracing and may continue to embrace, both intellectually and politically.

I want, then, to go over some familiar ground about the positivist epistemology that undergirded Pearson's idea of science, and to watch his progress from citizenship to epistemic skepticism, to the possibility of knowing. In doing so, I will be emphasizing aspects of his thought close to current humanist critiques. And I do so because I believe that we have tended to surrender too easily the value of some sort of rational and cognitive consensus to the politically and intellectually conservative. Positivism, certainly in the hands of Pearson, was an enterprise for human betterment. Like all such enterprises, it was therefore dangerous, but no more so perhaps than our own anti-positivistic enterprises. It's worth seeing where we connect with it and why it ended up in places that have led to its dismissal.

Let me then recall that positivism, popularly conceived as dogmatic, realistic, and fact-oriented, shares with the Nietzsche of Pearson's own day a deep-rooted skepticism, and a perspectivist belief in the inevitable human element buried in every "fact." It is initiated as much as a theory of strong disbelief as of strong belief. "The improver of natural knowledge," Huxley famously said, "absolutely refuses to acknowledge authority as such. For him, scepticism is the highest of duties." 3 The condition of strong belief is a preliminary rejection of virtually everything one has believed. The word "positivism" itself implies something of the Cartesian enterprise--the clearing away of the debris of knowledge, all that is muddy and doubtable, not "clear and distinct," for whatever foundational certainty one might find. 4

But Pearson sought ways to resist the instability he believed in. The movement through negations to aggressively affirmative modes of positivism is a tricky one, based on fine description of the methods of science. Not Cartesian in that he begins with a total commitment to empiricism, Pearson replays Descartes's hunt for what it might be possible and necessary to believe. He sustains at once a fundamental incredulity almost as complete as Descartes's Great Doubt, and uses it to build a confident (if stringently limited) way of knowing. Part of Pearson's program was the absolute dismissal of traditional religion and metaphysics from the sphere of knowledge; the other, "positive," part was the definition of a valid mode of knowing.

Pearson tries to argue both that everything is in principle knowable and that anything that lies outside sense impression is unknowable. This is a particularly difficult maneuver, initiating the positivist strategy of dismissing empirically indeterminable questions as nonsense, yet allowing that maybe, yes, someday, we might know about reality. But we are too ignorant to know whether we can know it. He finds the positive modes of knowledge where we might expect, in science. "Wherever there is the slightest possibility for the human mind to know, there is a legitimate problem of science" (GS, 23). But, finally, to make science valid on his terms and sweep up any metaphysical residue, he rejects all claims that science is about reality.

Pearson's world is familiar to those who know Nietzsche's transformation of knowledge into fiction. Kant's "thing in itself" is as much fiction to Pearson as to Nietzsche; science is a mode of interpreting rather than a representation of the real. Pearson argues that we know only our own consciousnesses, that the world is in effect a construct of our consciousness (where have we heard this before?). Strictly speaking, the scientist discovers nothing: he is an inventor. The laws of nature owe their existence to the creative power of man's intellect: "There is," says Pearson, "more meaning in the statement that man gives laws to Nature than in its converse that Nature gives law to man" (GS, 77). Pearson's kind of positivism probably provided much of the intellectual ground for the paradoxes of Oscar Wilde, who was unusually knowledgeable about science, and a close reader of the naturalists, T. H. Huxley, W. K. Clifford, and John Tyndall. 5 Here, in effect, Pearson says, as Wilde would, that nature imitates art, not vice versa.

Pearson retreats from all large knowledge claims to one fundamental recognition--not so much a cogito as a "sentio": I experience, therefore a certain "routine of perception" (GS, 111, passim) exists. Routine of perception is Pearson's antimetaphysically chilling formula for what others might call reality, the regularity of impressions that allows the mind to invent laws of nature. Scientists simply describe accurately and economically the routine of those perceptions, through which it becomes possible to infer past and future routines of perception. Such empiricist orthodoxy threatens the very stability it seeks and entails further recuperative moves. There are no "laws of nature," only descriptions of routines of perception. This purging of reality and metaphysics leaves positivism hovering over an abyss of nonknowing. (Note that the pragmatist tradition shares intimately with this positivist one the refusal to allow for science or language as "representation," particularly as representation of some "reality" that lies outside of anyone's consciousness.) For Pearson as for the pragmatists, we are all bound by the egocentric predicament.

"Science," Pearson argues, "is in reality a classification and analysis of the contents of the mind: and the scientific method consists in drawing just comparisons and inferences from the stored impresses of past sense impressions, and from the conceptions based on them" (GS, 49). Only by making all knowledge claims pass through the narrow funnel of individual consciousness can Pearson make the world safe from the incursions of metaphysics. He thus moves himself, as so many of his contemporaries moved, inside Pater's "thick wall . . . through which no real voice has ever pierced on its way to us, or from us to that which we can only conjecture to be without." 6

Yet Pearson's socialism entailed the belief that all individuals are bound to and subsumed under the community; his epistemology made the same requirement, for both moral and intellectual reasons. How, then, negotiate an empiricist subjectivity that makes it impossible to get beyond the self? Pearson, like many others at the time, including Wilde, Tyndall, and even Nietzsche, adopts a strategy characteristic of strayed foundationalists, the biologistic appeal to evolution.

Pearson replaces foundations with "normality." "The universal validity of science depends," he says, "upon the similarity of the perceptive and reasoning faculties in normal civilized men" (GS, 45); and for "a conception to have scientific validity [it] must be deducible from the perceptions of the normal human being" (GS, 50). Scientific universality "has only meaning insofar as we refer to a certain type of perceptive faculty, namely, that of a normal human being" (GS, 75).

The Foucauldian critique waits in the wings, but I defer it to get clear how Pearson makes his epistemological maneuvers. Minds, he says, are machines, and if they don't act adequately in response to stimulation, "we say that the mind is disordered, the reason wanting, the person mad" (GS, 88-89). Normality is, then, a power of evolutionary survival. In an Erewhonian way, error becomes illness, and Pearson's epistemology reaches out beyond mind to the body that seems to house it. For the mad person, "The fitting exertion would not follow the sense impression, and any such man would have small chance of surviving among men whose perceptive and reasoning faculties were attuned" (GS, 91). Interestingly, in Wilde's notebooks there is an entry under the heading "Survival of Fittest in Thought" that reads: "Nature kills off all those who do not believe in the Uniformity of Nature and the Law of Causation." 7 Thus, with no access to other people's minds, Pearson believes he can, with the concept of normality, validate knowledge claims.

But another epistemological step, some antimetaphysical tool for knowing, is required. The crucial instrument Pearson finds, learned of course from Francis Galton, is "correlation." Things remain unknown, but a "sequence" of impressions can be known. We will never find out what causes the impressions, never get behind the screen of consciousness, and yet what we find out will be enough to increase our chances of survival. Statistics, in effect, becomes the basis of an antimetaphysical science in that it allows the invention of laws to describe in the most economical possible way the sequences of events--woops, impressions. If I experience this, I am also likely to experience that. Such language replaces the idea of cause, a word Pearson is willing to use only in a revised sense. Probability contains the idea of "provability," as Pearson himself notes. Taking into account the underdetermination of pure empirical evidence, Pearson finds that statistics provides a convincing description of the world, that is, of the routine. The "clear and distinct" of Descartes gives way to "probability." Science moves from representation of reality to statistical correlation. "Henceforward," he wrote, "the philosophical view of the universe was to be that of a correlated system of variates, approaching but by no means reaching perfect correlation, i. e., absolute causality" (GS, 188). In The Grammar of Science the metaphysics of causality becomes the science of correlation.

All of this is very clever. Yet, res don't lie, but liars re, and we know that Pearson measured things for which he had totally inadequate information; that he posited entities like normal mental acuity, for which he had no justification; that he rejected Mendel's results in a closed-minded and rather unscientific way. Even on the grounds of conventional science, these can be detected as errors. But note in how many different ways the idea of correlation and statistics satisfies epistemological needs. Correlation makes no absolute claims about relationship; it only registers that a relationship can be inferred. The numbers used to establish correlation are comfortably free from the personal, and the trick is only (a) to make sure you are counting the right things, (b) to make sure you are asking the right questions in the right way, and (c) to make sure that the mathematics is correct. Correlative statistics seems to provide an algorithm for action and, as William James would have seen it critically, precludes the one real necessity, the need and responsibility to choose. But James's critique does not preclude the necessity for shareable knowledge. And what I want to emphasize here is Pearson's brilliant effort to construct the shareable, rather than to insist on its adequacy.

When I first read Pearson I didn't know of his connections with eugenics, and I followed the circuits of his argument without trying to resist. Pearson's social goals were allied to his science because he believed that true knowledge was attainable, and that once attained it would carry sufficient weight to guide political and moral action. Current strategies of the critique of science manifest similar attempts to demonstrate a set of facts (the incoherence of the narrative of ideal science, for example) in order to produce social goals. Donna Haraway, for example, imagines a science that would make "a no-nonsense commitment to faithful accounts of a 'real' world, one that can be partially shared and friendly to earth-wide projects of finite freedom, adequate material abundance, modest meaning in suffering, and limited happiness." 8 I'm not trying to equate Haraway and Pearson, but to call attention to a fundamental similarity in their projects: science tries to provide the best possible description of its subject in order to produce a social ideal. This is not the normal way philosophers and scientists themselves talk about science these days, yet the roll call of those who connect the project of science with social goals is impressive--Francis Bacon, John Herschel, Huxley, Pearson, Karl Popper, Bruno Latour, Haraway.

The usual idea that the intrusion of social and personal ambitions into the working of science disrupts the possibility of disinterest and therefore of true knowledge might well, as in Pearson's case, have it backwards. Since there is no escaping the pressure of the subjective in the act of knowing, the moral recognition of the primary need to confirm knowledge with others and to sustain the larger community in conditions of equity and fairness could in fact lead to the sorts of disciplined restraint of the personal that could lend credibility to assertion.

The attempt to make statistics a perfect epistemological machine that allows impersonality in a system otherwise inevitably biased was an important enterprise in its effort--perhaps excessive--to get beyond the personal, to discipline the desires, to penetrate the disguises of ideology. That Pearson developed various statistical strategies through his biometric work has not invalidated those strategies. That the ingenuities of science have been touched by the pressures of money, ambition, and social hierarchy has not diminished the importance of its modes of knowing. Pearson's epistemology, the positivist ecstasy (or effort to stand outside the self) and social critiques of science each tend to set their sights far too high. They each assume that the only way to achieve rational authority in knowledge is to distinguish radically the knower from the act of knowing, and absolutely and austerely to refuse to affirm any knowledge that lacks the authority of valid evidence, however that may be defined. This is veritably a Cartesian standard of doubt, and it exemplifies what Rom Harré has called "the fallacy of 'high redefinition.'" 9

It is not clear to me that although beginning with several assumptions not available to Pearson--that the eugenic project is an evil one, that science has been driven in part at least by strong nonrational forces, and that it has not been an unequivocal force for good--current strategies of negotiating with skepticism and with the impossibility of ideal self-repression are clearly an improvement on Pearson's. Ironically, positivism and its enemies negotiate the same difficulties with the same end: an ideal of valid, shareable, humanly usable knowledge. The excesses of resistance to the inescapability of the partial in the projects of knowledge and good citizenship do not negate the projects. It's not that science and citizenship are oxymoronic, only that our definitions of these terms need modification in the light of ideas like Pearson's and in the light of his failures.

Current strategies for negotiating with skepticism and with the impossibility of self-repression are not self-evidently an improvement on Pearson's. The ideal of disinterest still has work to do in our society. The ideal of modest truth claims, connected to disinterest, has yet more work to do.

For Pearson science, broadly, was rigorous thinking that makes the minimal possible claims based on the fullest possible experience in order to improve the quality of human life. I am not arguing for this epistemology, but for a recognition of our continuing investment in his kind of epistemology, for the importance of attempting to reaffirm the possibility of human connection through rational understanding and knowledge, and our responsibility for it.

He is author of Darwin and the Novelists and, most recently, of a book of birding memoirs, Lifebirds .
