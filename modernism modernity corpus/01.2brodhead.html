Strangers on a Train: The Double Dream of Italy in the American Gilded Age *

Richard H. Brodhead

In late 1887 Bernard Berenson left Boston for his first tour of Europe. This gifted young man--the child of Lithuanian Jewish parents who had immigrated to America after his birth--had had his way paid to Harvard by Boston Brahmin families impressed by his potential for cultivation. After his graduation, wealthy patrons supplied him with the means for the appropriate next step in a career of self-civilization: the European Wanderjahre, sequel to the old-fashioned Grand Tour. Having already travelled to France, to England, and to Germany, Berenson in the summer of 1888 descended into what his biographer Ernest Samuels calls "the fabled art cities of Italy," where his travel took on a sharper focus. Lengthening out his stay, Berenson made a rapturous tour of Italian artistic monuments both on and off the beaten path. (Samuels's phrase for his swing through Pesaro, Urbino, Gubbio, Città di Castello, San Sepolcro, and back to Florence--"a strenuous and highly methodical campaign and the prelude to many similar bone-wearying tours in quest of paintings and architecture"--will suggest the blend of ardor and discipline that drove these travels.) In the course of his researches he met the principal art historians of contemporary Italy--Giovanni Cavalcaselle and Giovanni Morelli, chief exponents of the new, more scientific form of art historical study. He also developed, under the weight of his accumulating art-historical exper-tise, an increasing mental distance from his adopted home: to a patron from the self-appointed culture capital Boston he wrote that Boston now appeared provincial to him, not the metropolis but that culturally impoverished opposite of the center called "the colonies." By Berenson's account, these new alienations and devotions came to a head in 1890, when, sitting in a Bergamo café with his Italian-Peruvian friend Enrico Costa, he found his life-structuring mission:

Nobody before us has dedicated his entire activity, his entire life to connoisseur-ship.... We shall give ourselves up to learning to distinguish between the authentic works of an Italian painter of the fifteenth and sixteenth century, and those commonly ascribed to him. We must not stop until we are sure that every Lotto is a Lotto, every Cariani a Cariani, every Santacroce a Santacroce. 1

A second emblematic incident occurs within a year of Berenson's finding of his vocation but has a very different cast. In 1891 the superintendent of police in New Orleans (in John Higham's words) "was murdered under conditions which pointed to the local Sicilian population." Egged on by both city officials and the media, the police rounded up a number of Italian suspects, who were quickly put on trial. "But when some of the accused were tried," Higham continues, "the jury (which may have been suborned) stunned the city by refusing to convict. While officials stood idly by, a mob proceeded to 'remedy the failure of justice' by lynching eleven Italian suspects." In the wake of this nationally publicized event the United States had an Italian-based version of the Big Red Scare. Italy withdrew its ambassador from Washington in protest over the lynchings; an impending attack by the Italian fleet became a subject of popular rumor and fear; and workers in places remote from New Orleans went on strike, demanding that their employers rid the workforce of a hated Italian presence. 2

The topic of this essay is the image of Italy in American culture on the verge of modernity. I start with these two episodes because it seems to me that the main point to be made is that there were not one but two images of Italy in late nine-teenth-century America, for which my initial examples can stand as synecdoches. The decisive fact about Italian American relations in the postbellum period is that contact between these cultures became greatly extended at this time, but in two starkly different modalities. On the one hand, this is the time of the birth of modern tourism, so that many more Americans "saw Italy" in the last decades of the nineteenth century than had ever done so before. (The touristic influx of Americans to Italy, still a comparative trickle in the days of Margaret Fuller or Hawthorne in the 1840s and 1850s, had taken on the dimensions of an organized mass migration by the 1890s, when Henry James said of afternoon life in Venice, "the bark of Chicago disturbs the siesta.") 3 On the other hand, the same decades that saw the institutionalization of European tourism also saw the beginning of mass immigration from Italy to the United States, so that Americans at this time also began in new measure to encounter an Italy in their own home.

As a result, American relations with Italy in the Gilded Age took the form of a rapidly developing traffic with two phases at once weirdly symmetrical and sharply antithetical: the movement, in one set of boats, of American pilgrims to the shrines of artistic culture, located in the land that another set of pilgrims was at that same moment leaving, in another set of boats, in search of a better life in the first group's land. But the traffic with Italy at this time did not entail physical relocations alone. The drama of the American encounter with Italy in the Gilded Age consisted partly in a growing mutual contact, but partly too in the elab-oration of the cultural conceptions of "Italy" through which this contact was imaginatively managed, mediated, and mastered. Berenson saw Italy through one American idea of "Italy" fashioned by the culture of his time. The New Orleans lynchers saw Italians through another contemporaneously constructed American idea of "the Italian." To understand America's Italy in the Gilded Age is to understand how these experience-organizing conceptions were brought to social life.

The cultural frame through which Berenson selects and composes his Italy might for convenience be labeled the touristic-aesthetic conception. This conception's founding tenet is that a high, possibly even supreme, experiential value is lodged in works of art, more especially in that special rank of works called the classic. Italy, a place where such works are found in thick abundance, becomes virtually synonymous with its artistic heritage in this conception, a quasi-sacred ground of art, and "visiting Italy" is then defined as the act of travelling from monument to aesthetic monument to make visual and spiritual contact with the value they contain.

As Goethe's wanderings might remind us, the aesthetic-touristic conception of Italy did not originate in the second half of the nineteenth century. But in America, it acquired a greatly augmented cultural dissemination and power at that time, when its rise has everything to do with the emergence of a new-model gentry class. 4 To summarize this history very quickly: The period after the mid-1850s saw the coming together of a new kind of upper order in America, composed of members of the older local social elites, the owners of new industrial enterprises, and the elements of the mid-nineteenth-century middle class now moving into new professional and managerial positions. These groups were all in different ways beneficiaries of the new wealth generated in the United States through capitalist and industrial development. But the same transformations that brought these groups new fortunes also destabilized the older grounds and markers of American social hierarchy, so that after mid-century such groups were linked not just by their incomes but by their shared need to confirm their superior status in the unsettled social world American development helped bring into being. As has long been obvious, as it came to social coherence in the Gilded Age, this new upper class identified itself in an especially strong way with its new body of leisure practices, and it deployed its new recreations--serviceable evidences, in Thorstein Veblen's phrase, of its wealth and freedom from the need to work 5 --as chief weapons in its struggle to establish social distinction. The expensive new sports of the later nineteenth century--golf, yachting, tennis--embody one of the status-bearing leisure practices of this new class. Its expensive new vacation customs--visible in its summer houses and vacation colonies--are another such classifying habit. And this group's devotion to Culture--the nonproductive activity par excellence--formed another and no less central part of its work of social definition.

Devotion to and cultivation of the high arts had, of course, an association with an aristocratic class ethos long before the American Gilded Age. At the same time, Culture in this sense became an especially useful means for creating the social distinction sought by the upper orders in these years. In the nineteenth-century United States, to have physical access to high-order artistic creation--and still more, to have had the means to acquire the trained abilities needed to appreciate such art--signalled an enjoyment of social privileges and a freedom from material constraint reserved for the few. The possession of Culture had the further beauty of seeming to lift its owners out of the material orders in which it simultaneously advertised their preeminence in, marking them as devotees of "higher things" and superior to such vulgar concerns. For reasons both of sincere devotion to such culture and of its peculiar strategic value in securing social distinction, the American social elites of the later nineteenth century placed a special premium on their association with high art, investing their wealth, their civic energy, and their creativity especially heavily in this cause. This investment is witnessed in such upper-class social phenomena of the late nineteenth century as the unparalleled purchasing of classical art and the endowment of great social institutions to house it; the reformation of premier American universities that made the initiation of the children of the upper orders into the mysteries of the high arts one of their main educational functions (English and Art History departments in the modern academic sense have their birth in this development); the conspicuous deflection of gentry children of both sexes into work in the arts and arts management; and so on. 6

This excursion may seem like a digression from the question of Italy, but it is not; in the nineteenth century Italy took on one of its dominant American meanings through the history I have laid out. Touristic-aesthetic Italy--Italy rered as the Land of Art where the well-to-do can travel at leisure and cultivate aesthetic consciousness--is another projection of the upper-class American culture of the postbellum decades, in which one real social world is recast in the image of the interests of another. In the later nineteenth century and after, Italy primarily meant Art considered as a civilization-inducing (and distinction-conferring) value in those parts of American culture the new upper class succeeded in infusing with its sense of the world. Berenson's Wanderjahre have this social origin written all over them. Transplanted to Boston, spiritual capital of upper-class high-cultural piety in the United States in the 1870s and 1880s, and reaccul-turated there in the great high-culture-bearing institutions of the upper orders (the Harvard of Charles Eliot Norton and the Episcopal Church of Phillips Brooks), this one-time European returns to a Europe he now thinks of almost exclusively as a set of artistic treasures, the perfectly acquired knowledge of which will give purpose to his life. This is Italy: an Italy encountered in Europe but whose motivating social meaning has been made in America.

The second American Italy of the postbellum decades, the cultural frame that organized the responses of the New Orleans mob, might for symmetry be called alien-intruder Italy. This conception, a retouched version of a cliché of "dark Italy" in circulation at least since the Renaissance, drew its particular social energies from the late nineteenth-century history of immigration, a history apparently remote from, but at a deeper level entwined with, that of late nine-teenth-century elite class-formation. After all, the same economic transformation that produced the new wealth of America's best-off social segments also pro-duced the new positions that opened up at the lower end of the American economic ladder at this time; so that the immigrants who came in mounting waves to enlarge a new class of urban poor and low-wage workers were living at the other end of the same world of opportunity exploited by the contemporaneous leisure class. Italians arrived in large numbers in the United States through the operations of this history. There had of course been Italian immi-grants before these years, but Italian immigration notably accelerated in the mid-to-late 1880s, resuming again after the chill of the depression of 1893-94. This newly conspicuous group presence--most of it southern Italian 7 --reinforced the new idea of Italy that was growing in American minds, but again this idea did not only register real features of actual Italians.

Instead, Italians like the other immigrants of this time had complex meanings projected onto them from within the culture of the native-born, and were recruited into a symbolic cultural function among their other labors. 8 Economic transformations that had been underway at least since the 1830s had produced, by the mid-1880s, a sense of widespread crisis in America, a sense that something had gone deeply wrong with the American civic order. In the charged field of this sense of crisis, endlessly reinvigorated by the recurrent experiences of violent labor strife and menacing urban poverty, the new, more "foreign" immigrant of the 1880s could easily be read as the cause of the painful changes of the present, the foreign matter that had made the social body sick with troubling "modern" symptoms. The Italian immigrants of the late nineteenth century were largely read through this generalized image of the foreigner as agent of contemporary instability--as in the New Orleans lynching episode, where Italians are clearly being responded to as local targets of more general phobias. The proximity of this event to the Haymarket riot and anarchist trial of 1886-87 suggests that it replays a highly portable late-century scenario in which infiltrating strangers can be held accountable for American domestic woes. (The New Orleans mob adopted Italians from among the list of functionally interchangeable suspect ethnicities.) Lynching, the punishment mechanism in question, similarly shows Italians being assimilated into more general schemes for the ritual control of internal Others, since lynching was the main parajudicial rite for managing that chief "menace" of the American South, the black male.

In American thought of this time "the Italian" is often known as the dark or swarthy one, ethnic difference being in this way amalgamated with racial difference; "the Italian" is envisioned as the untidy, non-self-improving one, natural resident of the slum and natural doer of the most unskilled labor, and so is made a synecdoche for the lower classes; and "the Italian" is imagined as the organized murderer, and thus as a "cause" of violence in the world at large. 9 But these enduring prejudices only give minimally individuating "Italian" features to a core image of scarcely differentiated xenophobia. The Italian like other new arrivers in late nineteenth-century America is at root imagined simply as Foreigner, the American as Unamerican and Destroyer of Our Thing.

It needs to be emphasized that the antithetical conceptions of Italy as High Cosmopolitan Civilization and Low Domestic Barbarism were not just pieced together at almost the same time but coexisted in the same minds, supporting each other, paradoxically, through the structure of their opposition. In particular, the Undesirable Alien image frequently formed part of the same upper-class mind set that valorized cosmopolitan high culture in the late nineteenth century: so that one branch of the Bostonian Warren family sent Bernard Berenson to Italy while another branch was organizing the Immigrant Restriction League; and the same New York gentry who supplied an audience for Italian opera--rendered an upper-class, high cultural art form in the American organization of culture--also preferred that it be performed in German, to cleanse it of "low" ethnic associations. 10 Never before or since has American writing been so absorbed with the Italian as it was during the Gilded Age. I want to suggest that American literature was infused with Italy at this time in part because Gilded Age literary activity was so tightly bound to the social segment that had the Italian thus doubly on its mind. Its conceptions largely frame the "Italy" imagined in American writing, which imbibes them together with its surrounding culture of letters; and working through the images of Italy designed in this social niche can be said to be one function of American writing in the Gilded Age.

William Dean Howells won the status of principal living author or repre-sentative man of letters in the "serious" literary culture of his time, and his knowledge of Italy contributed directly to this success. Howells's first exposure to Italy came about by happy accident: he won the patronage post of United States Consul to Venice as the reward for authoring the 1860 campaign biography of his fellow midwesterner Abraham Lincoln. But if he came to Italy through the well-managed luck of the political draw, Howells grasped early on how he could use his Italian sojourn to help establish an American career. As has been shown elsewhere, Howells was sharply aware that a distinct high literary culture was being institutionalized in the United States in the early 1860s, and Howells spent the Civil War years in Venice filled with ambition to get into this new sphere. 11 In Italy he intuited the value that this emerging American cultural order attached to Italy, and so saw how he could exploit his knowledge of "Italy" as a career-founding resource. The event he later designated the turning point of his life, James Russell Lowell's 1863 acceptance of his essay "Recent Italian Comedy" for printing in the North American Review, shows Howells's canny guess that playing up his European artistic initiations would be the way to win acceptance into the cosmopolitan, art-valuing literary and social circles coalescing at this time. Howells did not unambivalently like living in Italy, but he threw himself undividedly into the task of writing Italy up for American audiences, and by this means made his way into the cultural world to which he aspired. The American publication of his Venetian Life led more or less directly to his hiring as assistant editor of the Atlantic Monthly, chief organ of the newly self-demarcated quality zone of literary production, and to his entrée into the social world of the Bostonian gentry. The early travel novels he soon serialized in the Atlantic--books like A Foregone Conclusion and The Lady of the Aroostook--similarly displayed knowledge of Italy in order to consolidate a high-cultural literary and an upper-class social status at home.

In the early 1870s, before he turned full-time to fiction writing, Howells had the bright idea that his first genre--touristic descriptive prose--could be wittily redirected from foreign subjects to the quotidian, contemporary home scene. His work of tourism-in-one's-own-home, Suburban Sketches , shows how early the other Italy of immigration enters his writing. This book's remarkable chapter "Doorstep Acquaintances" records, as a characteristic everyday phenomenon of the new urbanism, the experience of knowing people in passing, through casual encounters in anonymous public space. (These acquaintances are from across the doorstep, or from outside the home.) In this essay, the people one "knows" in Boston's suburban streets are largely Italian: a little tambourine-boy; the widow of a "Vesuvian lunatic" trying to collect money for her return home; a chestnut roaster; a religious image dealer who comes "da Lucca"; a Garabaldi-partisan scissors grinder; "a little old Genoese lady [who] comes to sell us pins, needles, thread, tape, and the like roba"; "dark-mooded, sad-faced" Neapolitan street musicians, including one who is "swarthiest of the organ-grinding tribe." 12

Finding that a competence acquired through cosmopolitan travel can have an unexpected second usefulness at home, Howells repeatedly speaks to these people in his excellent Italian, activating the pleasing fantasy that by his use of their language "we had made a little Italy together" (SS, 38). But the ironic awareness that there may be only a quite fictitious community between Italian touristic initiates and actual Italian immigrants--the awareness that speaking their language verbally may only conceal how little Howells "speaks their language" in any other sense--vaguely haunts this essay's self-satisfied exercises in multiculturalism; and at the end this awareness comes close to being fully faced. The last doorstep acquaintance gets brought within doors, where Howells offers him odd jobs to do. In this closer contact, the person speaks of his actual human needs, needs not at all shared by Howells: "What I want is a home." In response to this breach of decorum, Howells amazingly writes:

A sudden and unreasonable disgust for the character which had given me so much entertainment succeeded to my past delight....and I said to him: "Do you know now, I shouldn't care if I never saw you again?" (SS, 59)

Doorstep acquaintance here implicitly recognizes that it is a relation predicated on a nonrelation: that the pleasurable pseudo-intimacies of upper-class home-owners with the "different" people they meet in the streets have been based on the secure knowledge that they do not share a world in any real sense, and that these relations can be summarily terminated should such sharing actually threaten to develop.

When the Italy of cosmopolitanism meets the Italy of immigration next in Howells's work, Howells lodges his own experience in Suburban Sketches in a fictional character, where he can study its more general social meanings. Bromfield Corey, the patrician father in The Rise of Silas Lapham , has spent his young manhood in Italy, not (like Howells) through political fortune but through the standard operations of a class acculturation system. The son of a great Boston merchant, he is a moneyed person of a second generation, in which aptitude dissevers itself from a commercial activity no longer economically requisite and seeks the "higher," nonmaterial goods of cosmopolitan cultivation. In particular, Corey goes to Italy, an Italy envisioned as the sum of its famous art (while in Rome Corey has acquired a "theory of Titian's method"), 13 where he obtains the eminence-securing equipment suited to his generation of the American elite: the sense of the aesthetic derived from Italy, a sense that was highly expert yet carefully amateurish in its social manifestation, and that marks him as a member of the upper orders.

After Corey visits the place of business of the noncosmopolitan, ungentrified Silas Lapham, the industrialist paint magnate his son has gone to work for, he strolls

at his leisurely pace up through the streets on which the prosperity of his native city was founded, [where] hardly any re could have looked more alien to its life. He glanced up and down the facades and through the crooked vistas like a stranger, and the swarthy fruiterer of whom he bought an apple, apparently for the pleasure of holding it in his hand, was not surprised that the purchase should have been transacted in his own tongue. (RSL, 133)

In this vignette the language-sharing scene of Suburban Sketches gets replayed in a more satiric way, a way that underlines the social gaps thus verbally papered over and exposes the gesture's social origins. Speaking Italian to an Italian is here recognized as an unwitting expression of class: an expression of the leisure ("lei-surely pace") and aesthetic self-cultivation ("apparently for the pleasure") that differentiate Corey's social position both from the immigrant's lower-class one and from the equally "alien" commercial classes in whose district he wanders.

What is widely regarded as the climactic effort of Howellsian realism, the novel A Hazard of New Fortunes , is separated from The Rise of Silas Lapham by the span of years that saw the beginning of heavy southern Italian immigration to the United States. This historical shift is marked in literary terms by the fact that within this book old Bromfield Corey-like habits of cosmopolitan aestheticization get applied to scenes that reflect new mass immigration. Basil March, the center of consciousness of this novel, is not a patrician but a member of something like the professional-managerial upper middle class (he is an insurance executive when the book begins), another social fragment, the book notes, that identifies itself with its superior possession of culture and cultivation. ("They had traveled and seen much of the world, and they had formed tastes which they had not always been able to indulge, but of which they felt that the possession reflected distinction on them," Howells writes. "Their house had some good pictures, which her aunt had brought home from Europe in more affluent days, and it abounded in books on which he spent more than he ought.") 14 When the Marches move to New York, they are able to organize the spectacle of the city of foreigners largely through the class-specific schema of touristic-aesthetic cosmopolitanism that they share with Corey, as when "the rear of the tenement houses showed [March] the picturesqueness of clotheslines fluttering far aloft, as in Florence," or when, in Washington Square, "they met the familiar picturesque raggedness of southern Europe with the old kindly illusion that somehow it existed for their appreciation and that it found adequate compensation for poverty in this" (HNF, 259). (Here Howells's own earlier gesture of solidarity with the Italian is seen with a harshness that goes far beyond Corey's apple buying: "March thought he sufficiently expressed his tacit sympathy in sitting down on one of the iron benches with his wife, and letting a little Neapolitan put a superfluous shine on his boots" [HNF, 48].)

By this phase in his career Howells has succeeded in bringing to full con-sciousness the two of contemporary American life and the way a frame of perception learned through one of these culturally organized experiences can be used to master the other. This process is exposed most fully in Howells's description of Basil March's mental process as he rides the new New York mass transit system toward Lindau's home on the Lower East Side:

He went over to Third Avenue and took the elevated down to Chatham Square. He found the variety of people on the car as unfailingly entertaining as ever. He rather preferred the East Side to the West Side lines, because they offered more nationalities, conditions, and characters for his inspection. They drew not only from the uptown American region, but from all the vast hive of populations swarming between them and the East River. He had found that, according to the hour, American husbands going to and from business, and American wives going to and from shopping, prevailed on the Sixth Avenue road, and that the most picturesque admixture to these familiar aspects of human nature were the brilliant eyes and complexions of the American Hebrews, who otherwise contributed to the effect of well-clad comfort and citizen self-satisfaction of the crowd. Now and then he had found himself in a car mostly filled with Neapolitans from the constructions far up the line, where he had read that they are worked and housed like beasts; and listening to the jargon of their unintelligible dialect, he had occasion for pensive question within himself as to what notion these poor animals formed of a free republic from their experience of life under its conditions; and whether they found them practically very different from those of the immemorial brigandage and enforced complicity with rapine under which they had been born. But, after all, this was an infrequent effect, however massive, of travel on the West Side, whereas the East Side offered him continual entertainment in like sort. The sort was never quite so squalid. For short distances the lowest poverty, the hardest pressed labor must walk; but March never entered a car without encountering some interesting shape of shabby adversity, which was almost always adversity of foreign birth. New York is still popularly supposed to be in control of the Irish, but March noticed in these East Side travels of his what must strike every observer returning to the city after a prolonged absence: the numerical subordination of the dominant race. If they do not outvote them, the people of Germanic, of Slavonic, of Pelasgic, of Mongolian stock outnumber the prepotent Celts; and March seldom found his speculation centered upon one of these. The small eyes, the high cheeks, the broad noses, the puff lips, the bare, cue-filleted skulls, of Russians, Poles, Czechs, Chinese; the furtive glitter of Italians; the blond dullness of Germans; the cold quiet of Scandinavians--fire under ice--were aspects that he identified and that gave him abundant suggestion for the personal histories he constructed, and for the public-spirited reveries in which he dealt with the future of our heterogeneous commonwealth. It must be owned that he did not take much trouble about this: what these poor people were thinking, hoping, fearing, enjoying, suffering; just where and how they lived; who and what they individually were. These were the matter of his waking dreams as he stared hard at them, while the train raced further into the gay ugliness--the shapeless, graceless, reckless picturesqueness of the Bowery. (HNF, 158-59)

The New York of this passage is one that Howells was one of the first to register, the polyglot or radically multicultural New York produced by the new immigration of the late 1880s, and Howells's street Italians have changed accordingly from solitary strolling musicians or fruit vendors to a mass of manual laborers (Neapolitan construction workers) partly differentiated from, yet partly also amalgamated with, a yet larger mass of sheer foreignness. Basil March's way of confronting this world embodies a new way of deploying a long-acquired cultural equipment. He sees this scene through a habit of perception rooted in the practice of tourism: he conceives what surrounds him as scenery or visual tableau, makes the world a "sight" he can stand outside of and subject to his connoisseurial appreciative powers. Jews, thus, become a kind of pigment producing an unusual and interesting "effect" for March, and immigrant poverty a tasty visual treat--an "interesting shape of shabby adversity." (Note how the rough-and-ready visual identifiers of different ethnicities--high cheeks, furtive glitters--take on the pleasurable strangeness of ethnic cuisines for the eye.) But March does not just aestheticize the scene in a static way. Rather--and I take this to be Howells's really brilliant achievement in the passage--March in effect constructs the scene as a visual display he can mentally adjust the resolution of, a screen on which--while being sure that he will have no real relation to the strangers he sees--he can now humanize them a little, now return them to an anonymous mass; now enter a little into their real, individual experiences, now blur them out again. Southern Italians...construction workers; workers whose labor is building the very apparatus I am travelling on.... These people are indecently housed, the newspapers report!...What could their life be like, how could America look through their eyes? it occurs to March to wonder, but the thought just as quickly fades from his mind. Then it comes up again: Small eyes? Must be a Russian. High cheeks? Must be a Pole.... What could the inner lives be like of the people with these visual exteriors? March asks this question, but does not wait for an answer.

The habit of mind Basil March plays over his doorstep acquaintances--now the much more anonymous and disindividuated mob of mass transit--receives an unprecedentedly harsh censure in A Hazard of New Fortunes. "It must be owned that he did not take much trouble about this: what these poor people were thinking, hoping, fearing, enjoying, suffering; just where and how they lived; who and what they individually were," the suddenly stern narrator says in judgment of March's reveries. When the Marches visit Castle Garden (predecessor of Ellis Island) to watch new immigrants being processed, this bite enters Howells's voice again: "It warmed their hearts, so easily moved to any cheap sympathy" (HNF, 263). Yet if the book treats this mental process as reprehensibly self-serving, self-congratulatory, and self-centered, it simultaneously registers it not as a moral deficiency but as a social reality: something urban contemporaries will know, a historically new way of having experience. This, I take it, is where the matter of Italy eventuates in Howells's work--not in a literary realism of Italian immigrant experience ("who and what they individually were" finally occupies Howells as little as it occupies March); and not only in the satire of upper-class cosmopolitan aestheticism as a way to master the foreign in our midst; but in the study of this realizing yet derealizing, outward-reaching yet mentally self-enclosing way of having relations with strangers in transit in anonymous public spaces--a modern mode of community that arises, Howells suggests, when the touristic-aesthetic mindset and foreign immigration come into sufficiently close cultural conjunction.

Henry James could be presented as an even more advanced critic of a class-based contemporary "practice" of Italy. Gilbert Osmund, the suave expatriate of The Portrait of a Lady, is James's version of the cultural type Howells red in Corey, and Berenson in himself: the professional amateur, the one who has brushed aside chances for worldly success to become a connoisseur of aesthetic effects. (Osmund says of Italy, "There were advantages in living in the country which contained the greatest sum of beauty. Certain impressions you could get only there. Others, favorable to life, you never got, and you got some that were very bad. But from time to time you got one of a quality that made up for everything.") 15 James's point is that Osmund's pose of disinterested connois-seurship is his tool for projecting a social superiority that he cares for after all, and this baring of a status-seeking dimension to Italophile aestheticism makes for powerful satire. But it would be wrong to infer that James himself is simply detached from the res of Italy in circulation in his world. A larger survey would suggest that he is deeply invested in the culturally constructed Italys of his time in their most orthodox forms--and that, like Howells, James moves toward the greater complications of his later, more evidently "modern" outlooks not by transcending such cultural frames but by working his way forward through them in a changing social terrain.

In what became virtually a mandatory first step in the American literary Künstlerlaufbahn of the Gilded Age, the equivalent of a later generation's stay in Paris, James too made a long sojourn in Italy in his twenties, in 1869-70 and 1872-73. The difference of this eventual expatriate and echt cosmopolitan's experience from Howells's can be said to be that he responded to Italy more ardently. More accurately, we could say that he induced in himself a more ardent realization of the idea of "Italy" then being normalized in American high culture. James unlike Howells came to Italy en touriste, saw the Italy laid out by the touristic-aesthetic itinerary (he had his Ruskin in one hand and his Murray in the other), and had the reactions and "experiences" prescribed by this map. Arriving in Rome in 1869, James registers the responses of the Tourist Ecstatic or what might be called the High Achiever in the touristic mode: "At last--for the first time--I live!...I went reeling and moaning thro' the streets in a fever of enjoyment." 16 There is no reason to doubt the sincerity of this hyperbolic account, but even James is occasionally aware, amidst his gaping and moaning, that he is having an intensely personal version of an experience that has been culturally framed. In an 1870 letter to Charles Eliot Norton, his early patron and the chief American exponent of the high-cultural conception of Europe and the aesthetic, James makes clear that the Italy he is learning about derives its shape and value from a Norton-affiliated American conception: "I find myself tending more and more to become interested in the things for which you have said so much--art and the history of art and multifarious Italian matters" (L, 262). That James--like the young Howells--also used his Italian initiations to improve his American cultural position goes without saying. In the letter to this editor and cultural arbiter he admires "Our friend Tintoret" (L, 260): our shared love of Italian painters, he tells Norton, creates a bond between you and me.

I have argued elsewhere that in his highly elaborate construction of his authorial career, James laid the foundations for the artistic identity he erected by taking in, and taking yet more seriously, a number of the creedal notions of con-temporary high culture: the idea of value-graded aesthetic hierarchy, the idea of the Master, the idea of the classic, and so on. 17 Here it could be added that James made Italy, taken at its official estimate as Home of Art or terrestrial Seat of the Beautiful, into another career-supporting article of personal faith. Already in his 1872 review of Howells's Italian Journeys James has enlisted in the church mili-tant of the contemporary religion of Italy:

We go to Italy to gaze upon certain of the highest achievements of human power,--achievements, moreover, which from their visible and tangible nature, are particularly well adapted to represent to the imagination the maximum of man's creative force. So wide is the interval between the great Italian monuments and the works of the colder genius of neighboring nations, that we find ourselves willing to look upon the former as the ideal and perfection of human effort, and to invest the country of their birth with a sort of half-sacred character. 18

The Italy of late nineteenth-century American high culture receives what may be its single most eloquent definition in this passage, where it is made to embody the height of human creativity or what might be called the cultural sublime.

When James put his Italian travel essays together into the volume Italian Hours in 1909 he wrote a late, self-revisionary update to an 1873 piece he had done on Siena that gives an idea of the continuing authority of this conception of Italy in his career. Writing in implicit criticism of an "earlier poor judgment" now discerned to have "fallen short," James notably fails to criticize the most obvious omissions of the earlier piece. Operating within the coordinates of the touristic-aesthetic conception, James had written of Siena as if Italy were its classical past, a "sweetly-stale grain from the wondrous mill of history," not a still living society with a history still forming in the present. He had also written as if Siena were a set of sights to "look well" at, not a social world of living people. (James's main human contact in 1873--what better proof of the touristic nature of his involvement?--is a waiter he chats up.) But these are not the deficiencies he identifies in 1909. Instead the inadequacies he measures are ones marked as lacks within the touristic-aesthetic conception itself: he did not on his first visit attend the Palio, and he did not then take the "extra credit" side trip to "the pictured convent of Monte Oliveto" (the place where Berenson had converted to Catholicism) to see "those extraordinarily strong and brave frescoes of Luca Signorelli and Sodoma"--works that again exemplify the secular mystery of High Art: "the great sheltered sun-flares and colour-patches of scenic composition and design where a couple of hands centuries ago turned to dust had so wrought the defiant miracle of life and beauty that the effect is of a garden blooming among ruins." 19

The touristic-aesthetic frame does not appear without self-criticism in James's œuvre; but it is important to discriminate the form this self-criticism takes. The 1877 essay "Italy Revisited" is perhaps James's most brilliant exercise in distancing himself from the preconceptions I have been describing. Noting the disjunction between Italy's unaesthetic "modern crudity" (to touristic eyes) and the richly aesthetic "relics of the great period," James here chooses against "thinking of Italy as historical and artistic," preferring "to think of her, for a while, as modern, an idea supposed (as a general thing correctly) to be fatally at variance with the Byronic, the Ruskinian, the artistic, poetic, aesthetic manner of considering this fascinating peninsula." 20 James's labor here is to recognize the different, actual, contemporary life effaced by touristic expectation, in order both to gain some sense of its human reality and to expose the touristic-aesthetic schema as a fiction of perception, a "manner of considering." In a characteristic bit of self-mortification, he thus recalls encountering in the countryside near Genoa a handsome young Italian who sings as he walks. By habit, he reads this sight aesthetically, apprehending the youth like "a cavalier in an opera" and musing that "such a re had been exactly what was wanted to set off the landscape." But entering into conversation with him, James learns that this picturesque re is in fact a "brooding young radical and communist,...an unhappy, underfed, unemployed young man, who took a hard, grim view of everything, and was operatic only quite in spite of himself." So known, the re becomes a harsh rebuke to the outlook that had negated his human reality:

This made it very absurd for me to have looked at him simply as a graceful ornament to the prospect, an harmonious little re in the middle distance. "Damn the prospect, damn the middle distance!" would have been all his philosophy. Yet, but for the accident of my having a little talk with him, I should have made him do service, in memory, as an example of sensuous optimism! ("IR," 52-53)

"Our observation in any foreign land is extremely superficial, and our remarks are happily not addressed to the inhabitants themselves, who would be sure to exclaim upon the impudence of the fancy-picture" ("IR," 51). This is the sweeping condemnation James builds the case for in "Italy Revisited"; but we should beware of concluding that James has therefore escaped from the touristic-aesthetic point of view. After all, the unhappy young man is known only fleetingly in this scene, certainly not in the deep, sustained, interactive form we could label "intimacy." In any case his rhetorical function in the essay is not to become interesting in his own right but to permit a self-exposure of the aestheticizing approach: so far from leading truly outside the touristic-aesthetic mindset, he appears only with respect to that mindset, which continues to organize the possibilities for perception even when that perception takes the form of self-critique. (The similarity of this passage to Howells's critique of Basil March's obliviousness to real others suggests that this form of self-criticism is not neces-sarily a break from but perhaps a regular by-product of the touristic-aesthetic frame, a self-consciousness this habit of vision regularly generates.) The chief confirmation that this framework of perception has been complicated but not eluded in such a passage lies in the rest of the essay, which, after bouts of self-censure and feints toward potential interest in Italian industrialism, proceeds to readopt the touristic-aesthetic approach: "the river-front of Florence" is thus, seen as a "delightful composition" ("IR," 58); the (unknown) inhabitants of villas "disposed upon the neighbouring hills" are seen as "res in an ancient, noble landscape" ("IR," 60); and James resumes a charmed wandering "from one corner of Florence to another, paying one's respects again to remembered masterpieces" ("IR," 69). 21

Except for a few glancing references, for instance the mention of "the children of the disappointed" in Central Park in The Bostonians, 22 James's writing largely fails to register the immigrants who came to America during the years of his expatriation. When he does confront this presence, notably in his report on his 1904-1905 exile's return, The American Scene, it is striking how much his response is organized through the foreigner-as-intruder image also taken in from American gentry culture: an image he heightens and hyperbolizes just as he did the same culture's ideal of high art. Ross Posnock, the critic who has written most discerningly on this subject, has argued that James shows surprising curiosity about the immigrants he finds in his old home, and an equally surprising willingness to question the native-born's right to "own" the world they now share with others. 23 Posnock may be right about where James's musings eventuate (especially his musings on Jews); but it would seem worth adding that if James puts a certain distance between himself and gentry-class prejudices, this distance is effected after a strong initial investment in something very similar to those prejudices, and carries its weight as a swerve from an initially adopted line of thought. When this real-life Basil March takes to the streets, he sees around him "the ubiquitous alien," "the inconceivable alien," "gross aliens," "gross little aliens," "millions of little transformed strangers" caught in "the first grossness of their alienism." 24 His baroque or even byzantine flights of ration link this alien, with an almost zany extremity of imaginative enforcement, to highly familiar terms of prejudicial slur. The immigrant thus appears in The American Scene as physically indecent, a human equivalent of the greasy spoon: "[in seeing New York] do we not feel ourselves feeding, half the time, from the ladle, as greasy as he chooses to leave it for us, that he holds out?" (AS, 118); as the product of vomit-in-reverse: "it is a drama that goes on, without a pause, day by day and year by year, this visible act of ingurgitation on the part of our body politic and social" (AS, 84); as linguistic monstrosity or destroyer of intelligibility: "it is as if the syllables were too numerous to make a legible word. The illegible word, accordingly, the great inscrutable answer to questions, hangs in the vast American sky, to his imagination, as something fantastic and abracadabrant, belonging to no known language" (AS, 121-22); as uncanny dispossessor: "a ghost in [my] supposedly safe old house" (AS, 85).

When Italians appear in The American Scene, as they do with some frequency, they are focused through imagery of the sort just described. When James revisits the Boston Common, he thus finds Italian immigrants to be its chief occupants, and so the chief usurpers of "'my' small homogeneous Boston": "the people before me were gross aliens to a man, and they were in serene and triumphant possession" (AS, 231). When he revisits Salem and seeks directions to the great nativist mythological landmark the House of the Seven Gables, uncompre-hending aliens--for a change here called "flagrant foreigners"--turn out to have taken over this world too. "At Salem, when, wandering perhaps astray, I asked my way to the House of the Seven Gables, the young man I had overtaken was true to his nature; he stared at me as a remorseless Italian" (AS, 265). This foreigner, who not only does not know who Hawthorne was but cannot even understand James's question, embodies "the other, the smoky modernism" that has displaced "the small original Hawthornesque world" (AS, 270): he is the Italian as bad modernity, the Italian as air pollution and eroder of old forms. Elsewhere the Italian appears just as the irremediable other. Visiting a "large new rural resi-dence" being thrown up on the Jersey shore, a part of the conspicuous con-sumption of the turn-of-the-century leisure class, James finds the soon-to-be-hidden labor of construction being done by "groups of diggers and ditchers," in short by a manual working class. These workers are "Italians, of superlatively Southern type," and what happens when James and his wealthy host meet them is simply nothing. They are so estranged that they can only stand in "staring silence" as if understanding that contact between them "were out of the question" (AS, 118-19).

As in A Hazard of New Fortunes, James's mental interrogation of the Italian immigrant reaches a kind of climax in a scene set in mass transit. In "the electric cars," James like Basil March finds himself reduced to a minority status, surrounded by an "alienism unmistakable, alienism undisguised and unashamed" that leaves "the observer" to "gasp with the sense of [his] isolation." Before the "face after face" of these foreigners paradoxically so much "at home" (AS, 125), James begins to muse on the process of their assimilation, the process by which immigrants begin to lose the socializations of their culture of origin and to take on the new lives of a new land. James continues:

There are categories of foreigners, truly, meanwhile, of whom we are moved to say that only a mechanism working with scientific force could have performed this feat of making them colourless. The Italians, who, over the whole land, strike us, I am afraid, as, after the Negro and the Chinaman, the human value most easily produced, the Italians meet us, at every turn, only to make us ask what has become of that element of the agreeable address in them which has, from far back, so enhanced for the stranger the interest and pleasure of a visit to their beautiful country. They shed it utterly, I couldn't but observe, on their advent, after a deep inhalation or two of the clear native air; shed it with a conscientious completeness which leaves one looking for any faint trace of it. "Colour," of that pleasant sort, was what they had appeared, among the races of the European family, most to have; so that the effect I speak of, the rapid action of the ambient air, is like that of the tub of hot water that reduces a piece of bright-hued stuff, on immersion, to the proved state of not "washing": the only fault of my image indeed being that if the stuff loses its brightness the water of the tub at least is more or less agreeably dyed with it. That is doubtless not the case for the ambient air operating after the fashion I here note--since we surely fail to observe that the property washed out of the new subject begins to tint with its pink or its azure his fellow-soakers in the terrible tank. (AS, 128-29)

Just as the 1909 hymn to Monte Oliveto marks the climax of his involvement with touristic-aesthetic Italy, this passage can be taken to represent the culmination of James's involvement with the Italy of immigration, and its ingredients need to be specified fairly subtly. Present with an almost obscene lack of embarrassment here are some mental derivatives from the coarsest strains of contemporary prejudice: does the notion of Italians as "the human value most easily produced" mean that they are shamelessly prolific, or "only" that their life isn't worth much whatever their numbers? Present too is the expectation that these people should be more appealing or agreeable, an expectation bred by touristic experience of "a visit to their beautiful country." At the same time, without fully freeing itself from these preconceptions, the passage also dramatizes an authentic movement of curiosity in the direction of the immigrants' lives--but a movement (like Basil March's) that, by its nature, never crosses out of self-involved speculation. Where has the homeland manner of these immigrants gone, James wonders? How exactly has their reacclimatization changed them? The question expresses James's powerful imaginative sympathy with those he sees, a real will to grasp the operative conditions of their lives, but his answer stays shut up in the playful patterns of his mind. Italians are like noncolorfast garments only within the conceit of the hot washtub (witty variation on the melting pot), and the further thought that bleached-out ethnic colors do not "run" onto other fabrics in the "terrible tank" is generated through a further turn of the observer's verbal re, not a further knowing of the men and women before him. We are back where all the most protracted literary interactions with Italy lead in the American Gilded Age--to a spectatorial act of mind that appreciates the visual drama of con-temporaneity in complex ways on the understanding that no interaction with real others is about to take place.

Considered in a longer temporal prospect, Howells's and James's mass-transit scenes point forward to many similar scenes in the writing of the early twentieth century: moments in the metro in Pound, Crane, and T. S. Eliot; encounters with Italian immigrant aliens in Dos Passos's U.S.A. and Faulkner's The Sound and the Fury; and so on. More largely, Howells and James give a review of that inten-sified yet contradictory disposition toward the foreign--the conjunction of cosmopolitanist Europhile attraction with various shades of anti-immigrant repulsion (anti-Semitism is one)--that recurs in American modernist writing. In this context, the Gilded Age forebears do more than supply a frisson of structures of sentiment yet to come. They suggest that this doubleness in its specific early twentieth-century American forms came out of a certain social-historical set-ting--the high-cultural upper-class habitus the American moderns were either born to or educated in. And they suggest that a certain way of looking at the foreign was formed, there, through the interaction of elements made neighbors in that milieu: love of the foreign regarded as aesthetic; dread of the foreign as insurgent native presence; and a habit of pictorializing, derealizing "appreciation" highly useful at home but first practiced in touristic initiations.
