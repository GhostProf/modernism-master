Girls Gone Wild in Weimar Germany: Siegfried Kracauer on Girlkultur and the Un-Kultur of Americanism

Michael Ermarth

This essay explicates Siegfried Kracauer’s vehemently critical views on the merging Weimar cultural waves of so-called “Girlkultur” and “Amerikanismus.” By way of frenetic media-stoked hyperbole, these two confluent waves took on extravagant, “wild” meanings during the brief phase of relative stabilization in Weimar Germany between 1924 and 1930, when Kracauer was cultural editor of the Frankfurter Zeitung. In his various polymath roles as journalist, novelist-litterateur, architectural expert, philosophically-oriented culture critic, and sociological theorist, Kracauer was well-positioned to follow both the contours of these highly visible surface waves and their deeper undercurrents and subsurface channels. After 1926 he was increasingly swayed by what coalesced into Frankfurt School critical Marxism, which remained sharply critical of the three preeminent ideological positions of the age—centrist liberal-democratic capitalism, radical rightist fascism, and radical leftist Stalinist communism. Each of these major ideological postures was considered by Frankfurt School adherents to be totalitarian in tendency, albeit with discernibly different accents. After 1930, during the Great Depression until the inception of Nazi Germany in 1933, Kracauer continued as cultural editor of the Frankfurter Zeitung in Berlin, working alongside Ernst Bloch and Walter Benjamin as well as maintaining close contact with Theodor Adorno and Leo Löwenthal, whose various heterodox Marxian views he often echoed, accented with his own singular modulations.2

By way of such audacious, ulterior Besserverstehen, the personal “she” of the Girl is recognized, shockingly, as having been totally transformed into an objectivized “it” of rationalized reification. This bold interpretive claim of Besserverstehen trumped the more customary subject-centered and personalist approach of empathic understanding (Verstehen) of “I-Thou,” conceived as the re-experiencing or “re-living” of other life, meaning, expression, or lived experience.

The more traditional view of interpretive Verstehen— as empathic re-living or “coconsciousness” with an other—was embodied during the Weimar period in major currents of existential thinking, phenomenology, philosophical anthropology, personalism, and neo-idealist historicism. These contemporary intellectual currents were monitored by Kracauer with the same combination of close involvement (Verstehen) and “extraterritorial” critical detachment (Besserverstehen) that he bestowed on mass popular culture. Indeed, both traditional German high philosophical culture and “Americanized” mass popular culture were, on his reading, reciprocally embroiled in the same dark modern destiny of total pan-rationalization: evolving from different levels, high culture and mass culture were headed toward the same desolate final end; this gloomy convergence was deemed irrevocable, despite Kracauer’s own remarkable capacity to use the one level of culture to shed “interim” points of light on the shadow-bound other.3

Although his views shifted somewhat with circumstance and locale, Kracauer never varied in his conviction that human beings of the modern age, starkly typified as homo americanus and even more luridly as The Girl, were becoming far less than—or quite “other” than—fully and genuinely human. For all his textural nuances, his interpretive stance impelled him toward the pitfall of overgeneralized abstractions about totalizing systems and their visible (in-) human types, thereby undercutting his own humane aims and, more tellingly, his own critique of modern rationalizing abstraction as a semiconscious, fateful dead-end for humanity. Kracauer wound up overreaching his own observational framework towards vast—but as yet still veiled—surplus meanings, which he insisted were at once both hyper-rationalized and hyper-mythified. His indictment of Girl-culture and Americanism as advanced forms of pan-rationalization-cum-hypermythification converging into the moloch of a totalizing “system” gone amok reflected his own reductive overinterpretation, which in some ways played into the hostile hands of his right-wing and Nazi enemies, as it shared some common grounds of radical ulterior “extraterrritoriality” toward liberal-capitalist, democratic mass modernity. Simply put, Kracauer’s systemic and holistic stance, grounded on the audacious supposition of Besserverstehen, and imputing a momentous degree of historical significance to unintended meanings and unconscious outcomes, wound up inadvertently subverting his own higher humane aims.

The Girls in the System and the System in the Girls: The Inverted World of the Robotic “Little Machinelettes”

In “Twilight of the Girl” (1928), Kracauer wanly congratulated the American actress Phyllis Hayes for embodying completely “the Platonic idea of Girldom (Girltum), the hollow, ordinary little machinelette (Maschinchen), which is reproduced in every magazine and everwhere else.”4 On conspicuous display in the showy spectacles of current stage-revues, Girl-culture and its feverish aura of high-pressure Americanism were for Kracauer the latest forms of advanced, rationalized “late” capitalism. Both were compounded of de-mythifying and re-mythifying impulses, operating at the same oblivious impasse in history. Just as traditional German high culture carried the fateful suicidal tendency to degrade into what Nietzsche called hollow cultural philistinism (Kulturphilistinismus), Kracauer extrapolated this evident debasement of upper bourgeois elite culture to convey the yet more “massive” danger of mass popular culture, as the new Kulturindustrie was rapidly devolving into a manipultative cult of distraction and mass ornament. Whereas refined high Kultur had evolved slowly and painfully over eons out of primitive archaic Kult, the burgeoning new Kulturindustrie was disposed to lapse (both back and forward simultaneously) into another kind of sheer automatic Kult—a mechanical mystique of rote distraction disguising the ugly truth of inhuman rationalization. It was automatic-mechanical, instrumental-instinctive ratio not autonomous-humane reflective Vernunft that was coming to rule the modern and “newest” new world of masses, machines, and money. And it was the automatic ratio of the system, not their own reasoning or thinking, that drove the entire rote spectacle of the Americanizing Girls.5

Only a year prior to glimpsing an imminent fading or “twilight” for the American Girl figure, Kracauer had in 1927 vehemently reinforced his linear equation of Americanism, the Girl-type, and the new modern masses in the most emphatic “mathematical” terms. Kracauer’s grand equation made them identifiably American in origin yet also globally planetary in scope and dimensionality, as the latest of modern “mass” life-forms. “Mass” was equatable, quite literally, with “math”—and in no uncertain terms:

These products of American distraction factories are no longer individual girls, but indissoluble girl-clusters whose movements are sheer demonstrations of mathematics. As they condense into figures in the revues, performances of the same geometrical exactitude are taking place in what is always the same packed stadium, be it in Australia or India, not to mention America. […] It is the mass that is appearing here. Only as parts of a mass, not as individuals who believe themselves to be formed from within, do people become fractional parts of a figure. […] The mass movements of the girls take place in a vacuum; they are a linear system that no longer has any erotic meaning but at best points to the locus of the erotic. […] Rather the girl-units do their drill in order to produce an immense number of parallel lines, the goal being to train the broadest mass of people in order to create a massive pattern of undreamed-of dimensions. The end result is the mass ornament, whose closure is brought about by emptying all the substantial things of the world of their real contents.6

Incarnate in the de-humanized, de-eroticized Girl figure, the relentless monorail modern evolution toward “emptying-of-substance” into “mathematicized mass” was merely a restatement of the main argument of Kracauer’s 1921 work Sociology as Science: An Epistemological Inquiry, in which he insisted even more boldly than had his mentors Max Weber and Georg Simmel that the modern world of rationalizing science, technology, and social organization had stripped all things (and persons) of inherent or intrinsic meaning, leaving a lonely and bereft individual “subject” facing a shifting array of instrumentally construed “objects,” organized methodically into a hollow, even vacuous, yet ironclad “system” devoid of any binding meaning, whether inherent or inherited. Kracauer seemed to echo Pascal’s grief at a totally Cartesian universe of mutually inhuman subjects and objects: “Catapulted out into the cold endlessness of empty space and empty time, the modern subject finds itself faced with sheer matter which has been stripped of all significance. The subject must process and form this matter in accordance with the ideas it possesses within itself (and which it has rescued from the earlier epoch of meaning).”7 The modern mind as post-Cartesian “subject” must thereby fail to encounter a true Other (as Thou or Self) but rather comes to regard Otherness not as meaning-laden but only as objective It-ness, that is, more and more as a sheer abstract entity like itself. The modern “subject” thereby transforms even other subjects—other persons more and more like itself—into mass-mathematical objects of calculation, advantage, and domination. Meaning (including language, art, and culture) has been steadily de-meaned to become merely means-to-manipulate.

In Kracauer’s dour trajectory of massified/mathified mono-modernity, it was above all the shocking trauma of the Great Depression that conjured up the imminent prospect of a twilight of the Girl-figure and her undergirding mass-modern system. In a May 1931 article “Girls and Crisis,” which recalled almost wistfully the frivolous vogue of Girl-revues of the recent past prior to the economic crash of 1929, Kracauer looked back to the blithe, head-over-heels naiveté of that bygone “golden” era, now sadly forsaken and buried in bitter disillusionment. In a real historical sense, the vertiginous crisis of capitalist civilization appeared to fulfill his previous culture-critical prognosis of an impending “twilight” of the whole supporting infrastructure. Kracauer purported to see right through the elaborate if scanty costuming of the type-cast Girl to the entire automatic system beneath her fanciful facade—and then to foresee her templated figure high-kicking towards the unexpected final systemic end:

In that postwar era, in which prosperity appeared limitless and which could scarcely conceive of unemployment, the Girls were artificially manufactured in the USA and exported to Europe by the dozens. Not only were they American products; they demonstrated at the same time the huge magnitude of American production as such. …When they formed an undulating snake, they radiantly illustrated the virtues of the conveyor belt; when they tapped their feet in fast tempo, it sounded like business, business [sic]; when they kicked their legs with mathematical precision, they joyously affirmed the progress of rationalization; and when they kept repeating the same movements without interrupting their routine, one envisioned an uninterrupted chain of autos gliding from the factories into the world, and believed that the blessings of prosperity had no end.8

The Girls had operated on the world’s stage like a locomotive, a sawmill, or an auto assembly line, but with the added unctuous lubricant of a happy, put-on smile; indeed, this winning human trait was itself fully rote and functional, only perfunctorily human: “a smile drips a steady supply of oil onto the joints so that the cogs do not suddenly fail.” The whole moloch-mechanical spectacle, when it stops on signal, is, in Kracauer’s revealing formula, a “dead whole.” The Girls’ coy, cosmetic eros has been calculated so as to remain flirtatious but not really erotic: deep instinctual, carnal, or fleshly attraction has been magically yet rationally transmuted toward superficial distraction from boredom and ennui. Kracauer sees half-erotic bodies to signal a lusty boom that has now gone bust: “They rolled uniformly over the boards, arousing just enough sensuousness to allow the audience to relax, exploiting with unflagging zeal the very boom they themselves were representing.” Remaining as a hollowly automatic, shadow of former “good” times, the ghostly Girls perform so rotely that they cannot know, in any meaningful human sense, what they are doing, thinking, and feeling. But by 1931 their smiling automatism has been beset by not merely by interim worries: it is nearing recognition of its catastrophic collapse and final end as “dead whole.” The Girls are dancing ghosts of the waking and walking dead.

For Kracauer, the great crash has demystified the revue Girls, transmuting their collective “happy dream” into “foolish illusion”: “…they come, a phantom, from a yesterday dead and gone.” They are residual spectral dreams, “a leftover from better days.” Kracauer foretells the final end of the Girls and their elaborate infra-system: “The crisis to which so many factors have fallen victim has also silently liquidated this machinery of Girls.” 9 A year before Kracauer wrote his double-edged FZ obituary in 1931, the conservative philosopher-engineer Eugen Diesel had similarly announced in the Deutsche Rundschau of 1930 that Americanism as a dominant cultural or psychodynamic impetus was collapsing all over the world—even in its native citadel of the USA. Diesel proclaimed its agonizing demise, indeed its petrified residue as rigor mortis, in a solemn but gloating death-notice, as self-confidently as others before him had earlier proclaimed its galloping hegemony over the whole earth: “Americanism is dying. Where it still moves about mechanically, it is nothing but the fading reverberations of a uniformly stereotypical epoch.”10 With brusque Besserverstehen from abroad (and above), Diesel insisted that not even Americans themselves believed any longer in Americanism. This impasse was indeed a final non-elegaic obituary emanating from the very source of its previous contagion—an obituary not only upon the passing of two massive Weimar surface waves but upon the enveloping “dead whole” of modern mass civilization and its “uniform stereotypical” and “templating” influences.

Kracauer’s friend and colleague Walter Benjamin shared much the same opinion about the templated, hallucinogenic sameness of the Girls and their final passing into unerotic passé obsolescence, if not quite yet final extinction and rigor mortis. Already in 1925 in “Revue or Theater,” he had anticipated Kracauer’s later dark assessment of the core dialectic and multiple surface motifs of “mass, math, and measure” operative in Weimar “Girldom”:

The revue caters to the bourgeois need for diversion, more in terms of number than in the nature and designs of its programs. It will soon exhaust its store of inspiration. Ever since it undressed the female body to the point of total nudity, its only available mode of variety was quantity; soon there will be more Girls than spectators.11

At one level, Benjamin’s comments of 1925 evokes the profound gendering of the widespread German craving for relief (or release) from its pervasive post-WWI mood of desperate misery, betrayal, dishonor, and impotence—but these comments also presuppose the mirage-like nature of all such superficial symptomatic relief: the Girl has become an illusory, unsustainable vehicle of “bourgeois” distraction and “mass” oblivion. On a more general level, Benjamin’s view of the Girl-revue, like Kracauer’s, reflects—or overindulges—the German “unhappy consciousness” of extrapolating more circumspect cultural criticism into grandiose—and gloomily eschatological— philosophies of history of different shades of darkness. Modern German philosophy of history and cultural criticism tended to indulge itself too freely (and less than critically) in overreaching “dead wholes,” in trying improbably to illuminate the gathering darkness of Hegel’s proverbial “night in which all cows are black.” This totalizing stance would be memorably expressed in Benjamin’s own staggering encapsulation: “The concept of progress is founded on the notion of catastrophe. The fact that ‘things move on’ is the catastrophe.” 12 Kracauer’s seemingly more focused obituary on the fad of the Girls could be taken as disclosing the death-throes of their undergirding “mass” system—as indeed for Americanism and for the very notion of modern progress itself. Whether conceived more modestly as “normalcy” or more ambitiously as “progress,” the very notion of “things moving on” in the system of modern society was not at all what it seemed—at least not from the ulterior vantage point of holistic conspectus as Besserverstehen. The apparent visible surface and the deep invisible system were at catastrophic dialectical odds. Goethe’s vibrant “green tree of life” was passing over into an inverted, mock-Hegelian “dead whole.”

The Larger Historical Screen of Girl-Culture: Weimar as Inverted System in Extremis

For Kracauer and many of his cohort on the left and the right, the future overturning of “mass” humanity’s spell-bound subjugation to machines, money, and media distractions would bring redemption from the inverted world of modern mass industrial, capitalist society—and from Weimar Germany as the contorted epitome of what, following Detlev Peukert’s influential phrasing, has been deemed “the crisis of classical modernity.” The latter designation remains an oddly suggestive label for what was at the time often called the “Gänsefüsschen-Republik” (i.e., the republic framed in ironic inverted commas or high-alert scare quotes), owing to its suspended limbo status, wherein nothing—least of all “classical” stability or any sustained normalcy— whether classical, capitalist, democratic, or socialist—could be taken for granted. Despite its exemplary constitutional framework, Weimar, unhappily, remained far more in a state of protracted and acute “crisis” than it ever had a chance to become “classical modernity” (whatever that might be.) Above all, Weimar modernity—massively disparaged as “mass modernity” at the time (especially by its modernist intellectuals and artists on left and right) —was perennially up for grabs, assaulted from almost all sides throughout what has often been called Weimar’s “permanent state of emergency” or “latent civil war.” As an index of this condition of chronic crisis, it can be noted that Weimar’s emergency powers provision, Article 48 of the constitution, was used 233 times in the Republic’s short, crisis-wracked lifespan.13 In Weimar, radical cultural critique and radical sociopolitical crisis were locked in a very unhappy—and finally dysfunctional—dialectic. 14

During Weimar, the faddish new “KKK” developments of Kino, Konsum, and Kosmetik (with perhaps Körper and Kleider in the mix) were treated by Kracauer and fellow-Frankfurters as closely conjoined in advanced, rationalized Kapitalismus, which underlay the more visible, surface waves of the Kulturindustrie. The new K’s (as the old ones treated below) were all thought to be contextured into a systemic “whole.” This civilizational whole was conveniently designated by many observers with the semi-alien yet still relentlessly familiar notion of “Americanism”— a new mass-modern, civilizational life-form resting on Fordism, Taylorism, Barnumism, the Hollywood-style “star-system,” and other appurtenant systems of rationalized existence, production, consumption, and entertainment. Americanism was thus a kind of super-system of multiple component sub-systems spreading around the globe. And entering its final throes of radical crisis, it was the penultimate “system to end all systems.”

The brand-new triple K’s of the “Americanistic” Girls that fascinated Kracauer were counterposed by him to the traditional Germanic, patriarchal KKK triad of “Kinder, Küche, Kirche,” which were esteemed by conservatives and many moderates as vital core elements of German culture and life—vital indeed to bare demographic survival, especially in view of the catastrophic manpower, monetary, and territorial losses of World War I. Kracauer remained intently focused—transfixed if also ultimately appalled—by the newest forms of KKK rather than the fading traditional ones, which had been understood to be solid pillars not only of the staid bourgeois family order but of most enduring culture and viable worldviews, insofar as the latter were anchored in solid values and traditions. The newest forms of KKK obviously called the old ones into radical question—and through the critical lens or ulterior “second sight” of Besserverstehen, Kracauer just as obviously put the newest forms into his own encompassing framework of ulterior radical questioning—seeing them (or through and beyond them) as surface ciphers or cryptograms of an inverted, inhuman “brave new world” of reifying rationalization. This ulterior perspective he shared with other adherents of Frankfurt critical Marxism: the ulterior, unmasking vantage-point offered a transient glimpse, if not a lasting epiphany, of the ultimate direction of human history. The new KKKs were even more de-meaning and inverted than the old ones.

Far more than his non-Marxian sociological mentors Georg Simmel and Max Weber, Kracauer saw the processes of modernity converging in a ghastly hyper-rationalization, exceeding all bounds of sane human reason. And as for Weber and many others, rationalization as Americanism (or as the ongoing dynamic of Americanization) became the handiest slogan or catchword for this engulfing meta-condition of mass modernity and its visible offshoots. The intricate Weimar journalistic vocabulary distinguishing among “girly,” “girlish,” “girlesque” and the untranslatable “männikin” (“mini-mannequinmanlet”), alongside “Americanistic,” “Americanoid,” “Americanitis,” “Americaneering” —all these locutions should not be waved away as mere frivolous or arcane flourishes; rather this lexical repertory reflected the bitterly polemical thrust of current debates about the overall direction of German, European, and Western development. The deep gendering and alien “othering” of the German sense of cultural identity was put totally at stake here, as reflected in Hermann Hesse’s acid slur that Weimar Germany was fast becoming nothing but the mechanical, dwarfish counterfeit of “ little America.” The tight Weimar linkage of mass modernity, up-to-the-minute Americanism, and the sheer taken-for-grantedness of the mundane order of things in an entirely “worldly world” (Weltwelt) was conveyed by the moderate Catholic conservative Adolf Bruck in his portentously entitled 1930 essay, “The Overcoming of Americanism”: “Perhaps all this Americanism seems so self-evident to us moderns that we are not readily inclined to ask if it could be—or should be—different.”15 To put it mildly, Weimar had a surfeit of prophets of radical “difference” and “transcendence” beyond the present order of things, which was almost invariably presented as desolating mass civilization, suffused with rapacious Americanism and glossy but empty Girl-culture.

In Weimar conditions, both Amerikanismus and Girlkultur conveyed something specific and circumscribed but also something much spookier in their engulfing all-sided contagion among the newly enfranchised “masses,” including the now highly visible masses of females at the office, workplace, sports park, and voting booth. Writers as politically far afield as Oswald Spengler, Otto Flake, Kurt Tucholsky, Ernst Toller, Hermann Hesse, Egon Friedell, Rudolf Pannwitz, and Ricarda Huch used these twin phrases as spectral synonyms for an trajectory of alienation and implacable degradation. But if often deemed (and damned) as superficial and soporific, devoid of human depth or profundity, Americanism and Girl-culture could also be invested by other commentators with a special dynamic élan, driving popular vitality, and sportive “animal” energy. Moreover, among strident voices calling for German and European rebirth, such as that of philosopher of “psychotechnics” and cultural “rythymics” (and later Nazi Party member) Professor Fritz Giese, both half-foreign currents could be reframed and presented as embodying a popular, pulsing liveliness that the Germans in particular—in their bereft post-Versailles condition of acute humiliation, economic weakness, and demographic decline—could hardly afford to spurn if they wished to survive and eventually to flourish again.16

Indeed, for all their repellant or even macabre horrors, these two overlapping waves also harbored a potent allure of vibrant vitality, dynamic energy, and sheer primal enjoyment, much of which was played upon in a postwar flood of images, photographs, stage-shows, and films. The latter were splashy visual media to which Kracauer devoted searching attention, in accord with his conviction that casual, evanescent surfaces, not deliberate, self-consciously reflective depths or heights, disclosed—if only indirectly—the real operative relations of the sociocultural order. The splashiness and spookiness were uncannily entwined for many Weimar cultural critics, who detected a characteristically modern “life-form” of phony ersatz-life—both distracting and inert in its lazy vicarious living off a fantasized other. Kracauer tried to explicate this depth-defying, fine-grained “surface tension” or correlational cohesion among the cultural “ornaments” of modern life, while also maintaining a totalizing critical perspective that plumbed their systemic understructure. Culture was moving (or “progressing”) not only forward into the desolating routines of wholly mechanized civilization—in much the same manner that Spengler had “morphologized” the conventional German opposition of creative Kultur cycling into blighted Zivilisation. But for Kracauer modern civilization was rather moving both backwards and forwards into what he deemed to be a hybrid archeo-modern “cult” (Kult). The modern black-magical cult of distraction (Kult der Zerstreuung) needed to be coolly demystified from beyond its own frenetic frame of reference in order to disclose and to dispell its surreptitiously exploitative grip on the human mind.

In rough summary, one could say that the tandem force of Weimar Americanism and Girl-culture lay in their uncanny combination of looming cutural/spiritual “death” and vibrantly incarnate, semi-eroticized “life.” This pairing was a confounding, quite modern amalgam of eros and thanatos (to use Freud’s own “classic” catchwords of archaeo-mythic resonance), which carried especially intense reverberations in postwar Weimar conditions. The Girl and homo americanus (often incarnated as Sinclair Lewis’s Babbitt) figured in current discussions, alongside other mass stereotypes and competing collective counter-typologies, such as homo europaeus, homo sovieticus, homo fascisimus, the bourgeois, the aesthete, and the white-collar “salaried masses” (of Kraucauer’s own astute analysis of 1930). All of these supposed “mass types” could be seen to culminate in the ultimate type-to-end-all-types: Martin Heidegger’s anonymous collective “das Man” (the neutral, unnamed One or Other). “Das Man” would become the central modern protagonist (neutered, de-gendered, and stripped of all authentic identity or personhood) in his epochal ontological treatise Being and Time of 1927, at nearly the same time Kracauer was explicating what he called the phenomenon of “Girldom [Girltum},” with its magnetic fascination for the little shopgirls and other salaried masses.17

Kracauer’s Girl-type and Heidegger’s “das Man” can properly be seen as kindred typological/ontological cousins with a close family resemblance, if not as outright clones of each other: they were the conceptual offspring of the vast Weimar discourse on “mass-man” and “the moloch-masses.” They represented the “classic masses,” as it were, in a mass mono-modernity of “dead wholes” from which all other “classical” attributes had departed. They remained anonymous anthropological or sociological abstractions, which by their entirely templated, utterly uniform qualities functioned more as reified“it’s” and consumable objects or ornaments than as existential “I’s” or authentic personal subjects. Their actual social function could therefore be most tellingly explicated through their penchant for ultra-modern, flashy images and trendsetting “looks.” Kracauer’s demystifying impulse aimed to unveil the inverted systemic “wholes” and configural gestalten underlying the surface appearances of modern life.

Both The Girl and “das Man” invited—indeed, required—ulterior Besserverstehen, for they were the very opposite of genuinely authentic “subjects,” selves, or persons: they lacked self-conscious reflection, autonomous or authentic selfhood, a sense of wider context and higher purpose, and were, ipso facto as mass types, creatures of robotic conformity, direct descendents of Nietzsche’s herd-like “last man.” Alloying Marx and Freud after 1926 into the distinctive Frankfurt School interpretive framework, Kracauer’s critical-Marxist perspective construed the faddish phenomena of Weimar into a vast totalizing, yet dialectically self-subverting fatality, framed as the ongoing material dialectics of modern history.

Kracauer was forthright in his avowal of critical distance, but his perspective remained closely allied with rapt attention to visual surfaces. Such a consciously double or binocular perspective of distance and proximity was intended to counter the otherwise largely unconscious systemic forces of manipulation, stupefaction, and distraction. He characterized his stance variously as “below the surface” “above the ordinary” or “beyond the margins” of commonplace, everyday taken-for-grantedness. With such delicate critical distance, Kracauer wanted to de-reify a burgeoning hyper-capitalistic world that was being evermore crassly stamped into exorbitant—indeed, berserkly engulfing—commodification and objectification. The inverted world was tempting to the many and the masses precisely because it was templated for them and onto them. But in the end his doggedly holistic interpretive stance wound up, ironically, reproducing the very “surplus” reification that he set out to critique dialectically. As with the old perceptual problem of the forest and the trees, Kracauer could not see the personal “otherness” of the Girls for his own male-projective, mechanically templated stereotypes of them. Through his relentlessly totalizing types, tropes, and trends, he reinforced the very alienation and reification he was trying to transform by a radical shock of self-recognition.

Kracauer’s ultimate aim remained closely akin to the radical imperative of the modernist artist/designer Oskar Schlemmer, who stated in The Bauhaus Stage: “Everything mechanizable is to be mechanized. The result: recognition of the unmechanizable.”18 But the eventual result, sadly, was a very different kind of recognition and a very different kind of radical shock—the cleverly concerted dynamic of National Socialism, which brought about in stages the actual “inverted world” and inhuman “dead whole” of longstanding German expectancy. It could be said that Weimar suffered and succumbed from too many radical shocks and not enough temperate recognition, from too much systemic “crisis” and not enough systemic “classical modernity.” Ever transcending beyond current mass (or class) modernity, radical prophets and ruthless partisans of new “transmodernities” (and dialectical “counter-Americanisms”) outmaneuvered—and out-shouted—the dwindling ranks of defenders of any temperate “classical” modernity. Weimar’s ominously proliferating doctrines of hard “types” and rigidly templated “systems” hardly worked to moderate its climate of radical extremism toward any semblance of “classical” balance or compromising normalcy. And the rampant holistic over-interpretation of mass modernity hardly served to instill much patience or forbearance for its manifest shortcomings in Weimar Germany. The wages of such extravagant Besserverstehen proved to be quite different from the more humane, 11 better world of the future, as hoped-for by Kracauer and other Frankfurt School critics. The dialectical logic of deliberately heightening contradiction did not necessarily lead—either logically or historically—to redemption or remediation.

The Nazi Transformation of Spectral Weimar Catchwords into Mass Motors of Total Mobilization

Contrary to Kracauer’s scenario of redemptive recognition of catastrophic capitalist rationalization followed by the dialectical overturning of latent oppression, Nazism worked another version of dialectical transformation upon both these iconic popular motifs. Simply put, the Nazis appropriated a cultural discourse which Kracauer helped advance, but they then contorted these motifs to opposed purposes and deployed them toward programs of massive regimentation and calculated violence. With the slogans of Hitler, Goebbels, Rosenberg, and other ideologues, the Nazi movement co-opted these Weimar shibboleths into yet another new configuration, stressing the redeeming effect of utmost solidarity with the newest “types” and “templates.” The invocation of totalizing catchwords to advance a yet newer totalitarian system—especially the new image of a single-party, single-minded “people’s community” embracing militant cadres of vibrant youth and selfless “girls in uniform” —became a key dynamic in the race-based Third Reich.

Purportedly overcoming the outmoded “liberalistic” life-forms of atomistic, mechanistic, and mammonistic mass modernity, this newest yet oldest Reich would restore the sense of spiritual “transcendence” and organic “wholeness” that had been cannibalized in the outworn forms of class- and mass civilization. The self-doubting limbo of the “republic in little goose-feet” gave way to the Third Reich of sharp eagle’s talons, regimented marches in perfect goose-step, and unshakable “belief in belief.” With Nazism, Kracauer’s deferred redemption underwent a ghastly dialectical inversion, the very opposite of the total “Copernican” shock of reversal/recognition he had wished for under Ernst Bloch’s ever-transcendent “principle of hope.” After 1930 the Nazis proved highly successful in exploiting both eschatological trajectories of cultural hope and cultural despair—of vitalizing eros and death-dealing thanatos—for their own ulterior purposes. Their project too was premised on ulterior Besserverstehen of the unthinking “feminine” or “juvenile” masses.

Nazi manipulation of the two motifs of Girls and Americanism after 1933 can hardly be characterized as outright radical rejection: it was more of an oblique or diagonal refraction. Given the polymorphous, open-ended modernity (and familiar futurity) of both the Girl and Americanism, it is no wonder that they would be revamped and enlisted in the media-driven Nazi vision of total mobilization, with a refurbished vocabulary of catchwords to summon hopes for new totalities and wholes. (As Kracauer had previously warned about the rule of the “ornament,” Franz von Papen in his veiled critique of Nazism at Marburg in June 1934, raised alarm about “the rule of the catchphrase”: Nazi propaganda proved ingenious in its manipulation of familiar catchwords

12 and catchy images.) Many Nazis besides Hitler reworked the ample but ambivalent stock of Weimar clichés, catchphrases, and spectral images for their own ends. The corrupt, sinister, and defunct Weimar “System” was to be supplanted by the heroic People’s Community, with the “old” Girls and “old” American-style rationalization playing key roles but newly revamped and renamed. Joseph Goebbels proved an adroit master at dialectically trumping Nazi adversaries and critics with their own phrases and devices, proclaiming the new imperatives of “progress beyond progress” “criticism of criticism” “emancipation from emancipation,” “liberation from liberalism,” and even the mind-boggling reflexivity of “belief in belief.” These newest beliefs heralded the transformation of “outmoded” mass civilizational, liberal-egalitarian, “Judamerican” modernity into the new realm of Germanic-Aryan “people’s” transmodernity. Nazism attempted to turn the tables on mass modernity not so much by summoning up visions of “reactionary modernity” but what can more aptly be called totalitarian transmodernity— aspirationally post-class, post-mass, post-democratic, post-liberal, and “postmodern.”19 Nazism conveyed a totalizing program of unshakable belief and “Copernican” certainty in place of Weimar’s ennervating doubt and skepticism. Its institutions consisted not merely of elite, male-centered “Männerbünde,” like the SA, HJ, and elite SS, but also counterpart Mädchen- and Frauenbünde, which were more than mere reactionary throwbacks to the patriarchal or pro-natalist old KKK ethos. Swiftly divested of “old-liberalistic” selfish, frivolous individualism, lipstick, cigarettes, and consumerist preoccupations (which were anyway mostly skin-deep), the Weimar Girl could be transmuted into a vital part of the vibrant whole of the People’s Community. The BDM for 14-to-17-year-olds attained in 1939 nearly as many members (1.5 million) as the males-only HJ. The newer sister-organization “Glaube und Schönheit” for 17-to-21year-olds, was founded in 1938. The pledge ceremony for new 17-year-old initiates took place annually on Hitler’s birthday, marking the steely yet feminine new alloy of “belief and beauty” fully in line with the biological “organic” continuity of race, people, and leader. The organization honored the selfless service of young women during the recent World War, directed them to similar roles in social assistance and auxiliary work, and adumbrated yet greater struggles (but certainly not world wars on the same scale) to come. Kracauer and Benjamin’s premature prophecy of “Girldämmerung”—like the notorious Götterdämmerung—was turned into a new dawn for a new generation of Girls and god-figures. Just as the American-style Fordism of Hitler’s unstinting admiration and envy could be promoted as Prusso-Germanic-Aryan duty-bound “service” (Dienst) rather than crass profiteering, so the Girlkultur would be reconstellated into selfless service and heroic sacrifice. The Girl could become a true pioneer or even eternal Eastern frontier woman of the future—resurfaced, retrained, and reuniformed into a new total system. The attractive young female test-pilot Hanna Reitsch could thus be turned into a pioneering “counter-American” aviatrix and Nazi poster-girl version of Charles Lindbergh and Amelia Earhardt in one—fearlessly daring and soaring toward magical new mytho-technical frontiers of flight.20 A decade later, Alfred Polgar’s mid-1920s prophecy of the Weimar Girls as a new style of mass militarization seemed totally fulfilled in fact, in a manner more in line

with the endless cadres of Leni Riefenstahl’s “Triumph of the Will” than with the 13 splashy Girl revues of Weimar days. The coy element of implied eros carried another dark dimension of death-dealing thanatos:

There is more than just erotic magic in the appearance and behavior of the Girls: there is the magic of militarism. The trained precision, the straight lines, the regular rhythmic beat [. . .] the obedience to invisible but ineluctable orders, the marvelous drill, the submersion of the individual into the group, the concentration of bodies into a single collective “body.”21

Weimar specters and spectacles had been translated into Nazi actualities, policies, and institutionalized “destinies,” including the looming fatality of what post-WWI Expressionist writer Robert Müller had chillingly termed a totally new (yet also repetitious) “re-war” (Wiederkrieg), thereby necessitating a highly concerted German form of what he called dialectical “counter-Americanism” (Konträramerkanismus).

There was a strange modern dialectic at work here too, summoning up the deep ur-urges of collective eros and thanatos, now racialized, re-gendered, and mobilized against a vast host of stereotyped adversaries and catchword enemies at home and abroad. As Goebbels was merging “Juda” and “Amerika” into the catchword hydra of “Judamerika,” Hitler avowed his detestation of Americanism and insisted shortly after American entry in WWII: “I’ll never believe that an American soldier can fight like a hero.”22 Hitler also insisted that the American military was so lacking in self-confident prowess as to use stale old “girlie” images from its own “uncultured Girl culture” to stoke up its trembling morale.23 American fighting spirit was really Schein not Sein, show not substance. Americans cowered behind quantitative mass and material because they lacked qualitative character and genuine spiritual morale for combat and struggle. As in his similarly disastrous assessment of Soviet Russian morale, Hitler’s megalomanical hermeneutical hybrid of Besserverstehen took on catastrophic proportions: the interpretive habit of ulterior understanding (deeper, higher, and longer) was fulfilling itself in tired old/new German clichés of American lack of concerted military will-power and surfeit of female-worshipping, skirt-bound “matriarchy,” devoid of manly heroism and true leadership. The civilizational “equivalence” of Americanism and Bolshevism, as of Juda and America, became wartime propaganda formulas, bringing to mind Karl Kraus’s mordant observation that “if human beings had no clichés, they wouldn’t need any weapons.”24

Hitler’s Armaments Minister Albert Speer was far less deluded, parochial, and self-preening about the actual mental and material bases of German capacity for “total war” after 1942. In the face of overwhelmingly adverse odds against the Axis in the recently “more total” (and more totally global) World War, the cataclysmic contest of “systems” (ideological, organizational, and technological) would undergo another round of overwrought, catchword interpretation. In an obvious throwback to Weimar discourses like Kracauer’s and the catchword semantics of systems and subservient “slaves of systems,” Speer himself repeatedly referred to his own “Speer System” by a seemingly incongruous yet patent name: both privately during the war and publicly in

14 retrospect, he characterized his bold “miraculous” methods of vastly increasing German armaments and technology as an intensified version of “Americanism.” For Speer this ready-made term meant the utmost systemic integration of elements of Taylorism, Ford-ism, German precision engineering, and the most advanced technology for weapons. Speer’s high-performance (counter-)“Americanism” emphasized the American style of industrial self-management supplemented with huge supplies of foreign and slave labor. (Speer earned considerable suspicion from other Nazi leaders for complaining to Hitler that German women were not employed in wartime production at levels even remotely commensurate with WWI.) Even more telling in light of the Darwinian infighting among the Nazi leadership was the fact that Speer was accused of fostering Americanism by Heinrich Himmler and his SS minions.25 In the contorted modern dialectics of “fighting fire with fire,” of system against counter-system, of type against counter-type, and catchword against counter-catchword, the Nazified versions of Girlkultur and Amerikanismus were summoned up to fight against their “classical modern” Weimar, American, and Western forms.

In Place of Closure: Kracauer Between Systemic Cultural Clichés and Open Futures without Finality

Krakauer’s critical exploration of the surfaces and infrastructures of Weimar Girl-culture and Americanism was certainly conducted in the higher cause of dialectical transcendence. He wanted to observe and to correlate surface manifestations in order to attain an ulterior understanding—that is, overcoming their roles (i.e., their rote function and templated type) by understanding them better than they could understand themselves. The ultimate aim was to move beyond them and their own blinkered viewpoint to something better, more progressive, more wholesome, more humane: their ultimate redemption would follow upon their ulterior understanding—a higher state of human affairs coming after the fact, as Hegel’s owl of wisdom taking flight after the final “completion” of the confusing facts and processes of human history have been sorted out by Absolute Reason. In an FZ article of March 1932 on the Lindbergh baby kidnapping, Kracauer referred expectantly, if not entirely convincingly, to “the coming golden age in which machines will be subordinate to man.”26 A decade before he had in 1923 boldly envisioned the radical Copernican possibility of “a complete change in the entire essence of reality [emph. SK]—and perhaps not only in it alone.”27 Amidst the darkening twilight period of “Girls and Crisis,” he defined the work of the intellect (and of intellectuals) as “the instrument of destruction of all mythical residues both around us and in us.”28 But the “golden age” kept being deferred by false gods and goddesses conjured up in new myths: the misplaced hopes and diversionary ornaments—especially the false hopes of the masses for progress and prosperity under the relentlessly rationalizing regimen of advanced or “late” capitalism—kept subverting their own pathway to higher humanization and genuine substantive meaning.

The false fool’s gold of Weimar as “Gänsefüsschenrepublik”—when modern machines, rationalization, abstraction, distraction, and mind-manipulating myths (such as “Americanism” “little America,” “Fordschritt,” “stab in the back,” or “Greater German 15 rebirth”) ruled hegemonically over human thinking—would somehow be dialectically transmuted into the true gold of the fulfilled promise of true humanity. The Girls and other mimetically mechanized group-types would go into eclipse when they were brought to recognize themselves fully for what they were—or had unthinkingly allowed themselves to become. If sufficiently total or “Copernican,” the critical reflection of the free-standing, far-seeing intelligentsia would also somehow inaugurate true revolution in thought and deed. Another refugee Frankfurt Marxian, Herbert Marcuse would later—like Kracauer, also in his American refuge—interpret the radical future-oriented meaning of Hegel’s philosophy of historical reflection: radical reflection passed over into radical action and revolution, almost as a logical matter of course. History evinced a higher logic of its own, when viewed from the ulterior dialectical vantagepoint of reflection passing into action, reason passing into revolution.29

But while Kracauer always insisted in a Kantian vein that instrumental ratio must not be confused or conflated with truly humane, self-reflective Vernunft (and that true reason must itself work to establish limits to its own rationalization and systematization), he also tended in a holistic (Hegelian/Marxian/Marcusean) vein to conflate reflection, recognition, and revolution into a potentially catalyzing totality to supplant the “dead whole” of the Girls and Weimar-style Americanism. It is significant that, like Marcuse and other fellow-Frankfurterians, Kracauer came to America from Paris in 1941 and began writing in more Anglo-American terms and tendencies that toned-down his earlier totalizing types and eschatological tropes. The facile Weimar catchword of Americanism was now considerably complicated by the actual lived experience of America itself; and the upper-case “Platonic” Girl-type and her dehumanized mass of mechanical imitators were, possibly, somewhat humanized by being experienced at the commonplace human level of lower-case persons rather than Platonic types. Ironically, those Frankfurt School Marxians who remained in America, such as Kracauer, Marcuse, and Erich Fromm, would later come to be characterized as having been “Americanized” into undialectical moderation by some followers of Adorno and Horkheimer, who returned to West Germany after the war.30

It is significant that the Weimar fixation upon mass-based types, collective roles, and systemic functions (which were conceptually constituted more by ulterior Besserverstehen than by empathic Verstehen) was met and countered by a parallel revival of the notion of personalist and empathic I-Thou understanding (instead of I-It objectivating knowledge) in the intellectual lineage of Schleiermacher, Feuerbach, Dilthey, Scheler, Buber, Jaspers and some more recent hermeneutics. Who in truth has ever thought of themselves as Heidegger’s “das Man” or Kracauer’s “little machinelette”? When do conceptual typifications pass over into outright clichés and blinkered “systemic” stereotypes? It is vital in the best sense to remain heedful of the words of psychiatrist Hans Gruhle in commemoration of Max Weber, whose own critical doctrine of types has come to be renowned for its heuristic value: “Life is not in the habit of producing ideal-types on its own. At most the searcher after truth needs these types as orientation only; he remains intent upon the singularity of character.”31 As Goethe had warned about

16 extravagant abstractions: “the particular is usually more than a match for the universal”—especially when the latter becomes a kind of generic Platonic cliché. The Other as a Thou is not—and ought not be allowed to become—simply a type or stereotype.32 It is of course necessary to remain fair-minded toward synoptic (and systemic) cultural critics, including radical ones in the turbulent and trying times of Weimar. Yet through his relentless Besserverstehen of the Girls, their supposed “System,” and their mass of slavish imitators, Kracauer compounded a media-stoked icon into a thoroughly reified entity like Heidegger’s “das Man”—an anonymously inhuman ( but still gendered), robotic “mass man,” lacking any vestige of core humanity whatsoever. What could threaten to veer off and to become a more flagrant, more wildly amok abstraction than the capitalized Girl or manipulated Mass Man—despite being wielded in the avowedly higher cause of true humanization? This essay has been offered as a moderating metacritique of Kracauer’s tendency to overgeneralized typification, systemic totalization, and his homeopathic style of dialectical abstraction, which he envisioned as bringing a final salutary “end” to dehumanizing rationalistic abstraction. But this essay certainly does not thereby claim to understand him in full—or better than he understood himself. His views, which were both radical and subtle, must be viewed on the broad but fragmented screen of the kaleidoscopic Weimar epoch, which was something more—and other—than an antechamber to Hitler. If Kracauer could not see the Girl for the System—or differentiate the actual person from the ornamental type and her mass of rote imitators—he still deserves to be esteemed as a perspicacious and provocative thinker; he therefore remains worthy of our best efforts of understanding. He gave deep and searching insight—if sometimes too deep and too searching—into the opaque antechambers of “the last things before the last.”33
