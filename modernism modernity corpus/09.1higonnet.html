Authenticity and Art in Trauma Narratives of World War I

Margaret R. Higonnet

There were others who suffered, to whose voices we must also attend.

--Jay Winter, "Shell-shock and the Cultural
History of the Great War"

I write because they left their indelible mark on me and writing is its trace.

--Georges Perec, W, or the Memory of Childhood

Fresh wounds break open older wounds. One of the most estranging images recorded by soldiers on the western front was that of cemeteries in which heavy bombardment had unburied the dead. If historians and literary critics in the late twentieth century turned to World War I to focus on representations of what the British called shell shock, they did so in order to find ways to deal with the legacy of World War II. This midcentury trauma silenced the generation that survived its combats and camps, and it left what Nicholas Abraham calls a concealed, "phantom" injury to be mourned by the children of camp survivors. 1 The return to 1914 was an indirect path taken toward understanding the present. Attempts to understand how the Holocaust and the Vietnam War had rendered narrative problematic, if not impossible, sent scholars back to consider how a similar breakdown of language and storytelling arose from World War I. As Walter Benjamin had written in "The Storyteller," "With the World War a process began to become apparent which has not halted since then. Was it not noticeable at the end of the war that men returned from battlefield grown silent--not richer but poorer in communicable experience?" 2 In recent years, historians' conferences and journal issues dedicated to the epidemic of nervous breakdown or "combat fatigue" suffered in 1914-18 have focused on those "silent" men, reexamining medical and autobiographical accounts that help us understand the kinds of meanings allotted to trauma and its symptoms by doctors, military leaders, and politicians as well as by the soldiers themselves.

Modern interpreters have brought to this task the psychiatric definition of post-traumatic-stress disorder (PTSD) that emerged from our experiences in Vietnam. This diagnosis has enabled us to frame the forms that shell shock took and to inquire why they became so central to the earlier war narratives that have often been identified with modernism. PTSD has offered literary critics a vocabulary to describe the symptoms of soldiers' mental disturbances that may re in memoirs and other autobiographical accounts: nonsequential memory, flashbacks, nightmares, and mutism or fragmented language. Those symptoms bear a suggestive resemblance to certain features of modernist experiment: decentering of the subject, montage, ellipses or gaps in narrative, and startlingly vivid images. This similarity--or, some would argue, connection--between a set of medical symptoms among veterans and a set of stylistic features in narrative has fostered a masculine canon of modernism. 3

Behind these moving accounts of combatants' psychological injuries lies concealed another history of wartime trauma, one that has only begun to be written. "One train may conceal another," runs a French aphorism, and the shock experienced by combatants during the war for a long time displaced our attention from noncombatant traumas. Margaret Darrow has argued that French women disappeared from the cultural memory of World War I not because of "memory-loss" but because the story was never created: "a failure of memory creation." 4 As descriptions of a disturbing gap in the historical narrative, both phrases suggest trauma, perhaps because women's experiences so dramatically broke with accepted scripts, shocking many contemporaries. Restoring this repressed narrative requires us to bring social history together with feminist history. To do so, Darrow and other historians have turned to the experiences of civilians, such as those in occupied zones or in cities under bombardment, and to the experiences of the noncombatant medical corps, which of course included women. Over the last two decades, a focus on original sources has led to the publication or reedition of civilian and nursing diaries and memoirs, and historians have begun to study the social and economic impact of the war on civilians. We are starting to uncover an alternate history of World War I traumas. 5

I begin here by reconsidering how war-induced trauma has been gendered through its identification with combat, not only by those who lived through 1914-18, but by scholars today. For alternative evidence, I examine the traumatic stress suffered by members of a specific noncombatant group--nurses and orderlies--who recorded repeated confrontation with men's mutilated bodies. I focus on this group for several reasons: their work in clearing stations, hospital ships, and other emergency units took place under conditions similar to those faced by combatants. The group includes women, which is important to me as I consider what it means to construct a gendered canon or countercanon. In addition, this group has produced writing that ranges from immediate diaries and letters to retrospective memoirs and fictionalized autobiographies. This range permits us to reflect on the consequences of genre distinctions in relation to testimony and the implied values that govern canon formation.

In the process, I want to underscore the problematic nature of trauma narratives. These were understood by some doctors and psychiatrists during the war as lies or fictions generated by malingerers. Other specialists have understood trauma narratives as authentically symptomatic testimony to the unspeakable conditions that precipitated psychological breakdown. Dori Laub goes so far as to declare that "massive trauma precludes its registration." 6 For Laub, the gaps in narrative, like black holes, expand to consume all narrative, resulting in mutism. More recently, historians such as Eric Leed, George Mosse, and Jay Winter have viewed shell shock as a useful metaphor for the costs of modernity that were made manifest in industrialized warfare. 7

The lability of the terminology for PTSD and accordingly of its definitions in 1914-18--from shell shock to male hysteria, from hypochondria to combat fatigue--points to the complexity of the concept. This essay will explore some of the difficulties faced by modern critics caught between the claims of authenticity and of art in examining trauma writing. To what extent should the canon of war writings be revised in light of the evidence that experimental techniques offered a repertoire upon which soldiers and other witnesses of the war could draw to compose their memories? 8 These are questions that have been raised by major critics such as Paul Fussell and Sam Hynes, but that continue to perplex our versions of literary history. 9

Despite a professional literature that already in 1914-18 referred to civilian experiences of wartime trauma, both historians and critics have focused almost exclusively on combatants' experiences. Benjamin remarks on the silence of "men returned from battlefield." 10 In a recent Franco-British collection of essays on shell shock, all but one article deal with combatants. 11 War is man's affair, in popular wisdom, and soldiers' sufferings lie at the heart of the matter. It is no surprise, then, to find that accounts of "soldiers' heart" (as one post-traumatic syndrome was called) dominate the scholarly reassessment of this period. When alternate voices have been sought out, they have been the voices of the foot soldiers, i.e. the working classes, in counterpoint to the eloquent poetic legacy of the elites.

Questions of masculinity have been fused with war trauma in curiously ambiguous ways. PTSD was understood by the military as a failure of masculinity--a failure of hardness, courage, or willpower--and a manifestation of latent effeminacy or immaturity. It was thought to be cured by violent electric shock, and was sometimes punished by execution. Mosse and others have gathered evidence that (especially among German doctors) victims of "male hysteria" were perceived as inferior specimens and malingerers, thus aligning "shockers" with women, Jews, and the working classes. 12 For many scholars, including feminist critics, the focus has been on a "crisis of masculinity," as Elaine Showalter puts it. 13 In her influential view, the medical discussion and the afflicted soldiers themselves expressed "more general but intense anxieties about masculinity, fears of acting effeminate, even a refusal to continue the bluff of male behavior" (FM, 172). She gives the eruption of this anxiety a particular twist. Since it was "manly" not to complain, "shell shock was the body language of masculine complaint, a disguised male protest, not only against the war, but against the concept of 'manliness' itself" (ibid.).

Equally ambiguous is the powerful force of male bonding in wartime. Just as trauma and disillusionment have become symbolic equivalents in the way historians have thought about the ruptures wrought by the war, so too trauma and the experience of brotherhood have become intertwined in medical analyses and literary texts. On the positive side, comradeship has been tied to a reduced incidence of shell shock. Denis Winter cites letters in support of his thesis that "the chief prop was mates." 14 Doctors and commanders speculated whether neurasthenia or "male hysteria" as well as breaks in discipline could be prevented by developing an "esprit de corps." 15 Comradeship sustained soldiers, protecting the unit against trauma, as we read in so many war narratives. Yet its intensity, when individual members of the unit were lost, could also trigger melancholia. Doctor after doctor remarked that the devastating "sight of dead comrades" or "the sight and sound of his lacerated comrades" was sufficient to arouse morbid agitation, nervous breakdown, with paralysis, cramps, or even delirium. 16 The bonds developed in trench warfare, it was thought, helped to explain the breakdown of those who had witnessed the fragmentation of bodies under protracted bombardment. 17 It was a male form of mourning. By the end of the war, therefore, trauma had joined brotherhood and disillusionment as hallmarks of the war experience and of the war narrative. In spite of underlying ambiguities, the gendering of war entailed a gendering of trauma, and trauma became a privileged, masculine form of testimony about the complex meaning of war, a status it retains among historians and critics today.

I propose to interrogate the view of war trauma as a male disease by inspecting wartime trauma experienced by noncombatants. The sufferings of the noncombatant have already attracted interest from some major historians and literary critics. Annette Becker's historiographically innovative contribution, Oubliés , for example, compares the occupied territories of northern France to a large internment camp, a militarized home front. She draws a parallel between the traumatized victims of the occupation (a high percentage of whom were women, children, and old men) and those long-term prisoners who suffered from "barbed wire neuroses." 18 Trudi Tate also juxtaposes men's and women's experiences in her study of modernism and World War I, devoting a chapter to civilian war neuroses on the home front. She cites examples such as a woman hospitalized when five of her seven sons were wounded, and devotes attention to fictional representations of secondary trauma ("shock even to people who are not present"), such as Rebecca West's The Return of the Soldier , Rose Macaulay's Non-combatants and Others , and H. D.'s narratives "Kora and Ka" and Bid Me to Live . 19 Tate asks key questions that shape any study of war trauma: "who has seen what," how is one "positioned in relation to war's trauma," and "who has the right to speak" (MH, 19).

These studies of direct trauma on the home front and of the melancholia that ensues from personal war losses are extremely valuable. What I propose, however, is to apply Tate's questions to those occupying a liminal position in relation to war's trauma: noncombatant medical staff (both male and female) in first-aid stations, clearing stations, and mobile units near the front. My evidence shows that these workers not only dealt with soldiers' physical and psychological traumas, but were themselves exposed to situations that triggered breakdowns similar to those experienced by combatants.

To be sure, the etiology of shell shock is still debated. Some hold that eventually, under corrosive conditions such as defeat, exhaustion, constant bombardment, and immobilization, increasing rates of breakdown or "combat fatigue" will be inevitable in any war. In the words of Lord Moran, "men wear out in battle like their clothes." 20 Norman Fenton thought that "in all probability, liability to neurotic symptoms is practically universal." 21 The conditions to which many doctors in 1914-18 as well as modern psychiatrists have pointed are extreme fatigue, the continuous confrontation with death and mutilated bodies, the shock of surprise (a sudden horrifying sight), and an unresolvable conflict between fear and duty or between two equally compelling responsibilities. 22 Thus W. H. R. Rivers, the doctor who treated Wilfred Owen and Siegfried Sassoon, recognized the power of forced immobility, a sense of powerlessness, and repression to foster war neuroses. Rivers notes that while camaraderie may help release disabling anxieties, the catalyst--"some disgusting sight or the death and mutilation of a friend"--may be overwhelming. 23

Like combatants, medical staff worked under unrelenting strain for days on end in confining quarters such as bunkers or dugouts and small operating sheds. They handled steady streams of mutilated bodies. And they were torn between their duty to the individual patients and their obligations to send as many able-bodied soldiers as possible back to the front lines. The situation of medical staff engaged in triage replicated in another register the untenable moral conflicts that seemed to doctors to be responsible for nervous breakdowns among officers. Yet surprisingly little work has been done on the records concerning medical staff. Certainly the term shell shock, its alliterative onomatopoeia so effective in evoking a semblance of physical injury, seems inappropriate for a discussion of the mental trauma experienced by medical staff. 24 Perhaps the terminology itself impeded this insight. Significantly, however, Fenton, an American doctor who reviewed the official statistics about war neurosis after service with the American Expeditionary Force in France, identified a heightened incidence of breakdown among the medical corps. "[T]he greater proportion," he wrote, "might be explained by the fact that these individuals witnessed terrible sights of mutilation and disease both at the front and in hospitals behind the lines" (SSA, 46). In what follows I review attempts by nurses and orderlies to record these aspects of their work, and explore the apparent impact of these traumatizing factors on some of their memoirs and sketches.

Many nurses themselves were fully aware of their oppressive situation. Thus Léonie Godfroy explained her dark vision of the war in her memoir:

War appears to be something abominable or something sublime, depending upon the side one encounters. I must declare that I have seen only the somber side. This is women's share. Those of us whom chance or vocation draws into the vast drama must resign ourselves to seeing only the saddest of realities. To men in the first rank of danger go the superb élan, the epic spectacles of the battlefield. To us, the other side of the decor. . . . They come to us afterwards, bloody, weary, mute. It is not surprising that our recollections often have an element of nightmare. 25

Ellen La Motte, a nurse on the western front who knew the former medical student Gertrude Stein from their days in Baltimore, chose likewise to describe "the backwash of war." 26

The nightmares to which Godfroy refers are not just the violent dreams of the damaged men who came to be treated, nor are they simply metaphors for the horrors of industrialized warfare that have so often been invoked by modern critics. Medical staff directly experienced the unrelieved stress of industrialized war through the mechanized treatment of injuries--for example, the constant irrigation of wounds and the mass production techniques in surgery wards that divided patients according to their injuries. The nightmares that erupted into Sassoon's consciousness also erupt in nursing accounts. Nurse M. Eydoux-Démians in a notebook published in 1915 asks, "Was this horrible nightmare a reality?" 27

Emotional and physical collapse resulted from the unrelenting press of work endured by nurses. In Italy, Sita Meyer Camperio, who founded the nursing corps, questioned her own stamina. In a diary entry of 18 May 1917, she describes how her physical exhaustion was exacerbated by mental fatigue: "I do not know if I will have the moral and physical force to remain long in this infernal mob, not being able to close my eyes at night and having days filled with more tragic visions." 28 An entry for the following day recounts how a fellow nurse came to her one night and threw her arms around her lamenting, "Sita, I cannot close my eyes any more!" 29 As Beth Tarini writes in an unpublished study of the Italian Red Cross, "Trauma eroded the strength to deal with trauma, and nurses found themselves caring for other nurses." 30

Lady Kennard, working in Romania in 1916, recorded in her diary an afternoon bombardment, followed by the press of work in her hospital:

as we neared the hospital shrapnel began to fall. The bombs, of course, fell all round. I picked up one man wounded and unconscious and took him on with me in the car. A woman was killed at the gate of the hospital and one man died on the doorstep. . . . [T]hree operations between four and seven. 31

As further wounded flooded in, operations continued while she stood for six and a half hours, administering chloroform. "One couldn't be excited in the hospital, there was no time. . . . One just works without the faintest understanding of what one is doing. After it was all over we collapsed . . . and drank hot tea . . . and tried to recover" (RD, 56-9). Kennard's ellipses punctuate the page like controlled pauses for breath that help to reestablish the rhythms of human life and prevent collapse. For some nurses, such breaks ultimately marked the point at which they themselves fell silent. Thus the diary of Mme. de la Boulaye stops short with the entry of 24 May 1917. A blank marked by ellipses records her breakdown. Only in January 1919, when she wrote her last framing pages to sum up the intervening months of collapse followed by a resumption of nursing, does she describe the "three hard months of illness [that] nailed me in my bed." 32 Across the gap of no-man's-land, a comparable staccato observation is made by a German nurse, Schwester Elfriede Schulz, who in 1917 was working nonstop in surgery. She records that "The floor was piled high with bandages, amputated limbs, blood, bits of flesh, the air was filled with narcotics, pus, the reek of blood," and describes "shattered limbs that hung from a few scraps of flesh, bad head wounds, hopeless stomach wounds--it was beyond consolation." 33 The days of 21-2 September she worked thirty hours, going from bandaging to operating to night watch. "You get frightfully tired," she wrote. "While you are harassed in the operating room, it's all right, but in your free minutes you collapse." 34 Some nurses turned to morphine, not only a painkiller but the agent of a euphoria that enabled them to continue to work. 35

The crush of patients overwhelmed facilities and induced a sense of helplessness among many nurses and orderlies. Nurse A. L. Walker recalled handling three thousand men in a day at her base hospital: "Operations went on unceasingly. As fast as a patient could be taken off the operating table another went on, and so on all night, the surgeons had been at it all day." 36 The pace inhibited thought and suppressed a sense of self: "The work would have seemed hopeless, if one had stopped to think. . . . Some had both legs off, some their side blown away, all were wounded in dozens of places. Doctors and nurses were hopelessly outnumbered, distractedly trying to meet the demands made on them" (ibid.). Ironically, the relentless routines of rapid-fire work sustained the flow of adrenalin, even while exhausting emotional reserves. Another British nurse writes of "the nervous strain and the hopelessness of struggling against heavy odds." 37 Her inability to relieve suffering adequately, she wrote home, "tries me so dreadfully" (ibid.).

Similarly, Mary Britnieva, a young Russian nurse of British extraction, was overwhelmed when she came upon victims of gas attack laid out for emergency treatment on a field:

The ghastliness of what we saw is beyond description, the memory of it making me shudder to this day. They lay on their backs mostly, their upturned faces terribly swollen and livid--some almost blue--choking and coughing, their bloodshot eyes protruding, unable to utter a word, yet fully conscious, only their eyes and their occasional spasmodic feeble movements proclaiming the supreme agony that they were enduring. Some were evencoughing up pieces of their lungs that the cruel gas had disintegrated in their living bodies. 38

The memory of these men whose very coughs tore them apart returns physically to punctuate--with vivid details such as the color blue--Britnieva's memoir. She insists on the despair she and her colleagues felt when faced with such cases, men whom they had known: "We felt utterly helpless, there was no remedy, we were powerless. . . . The realization of our helplessness was almost unbearable; a wound can be dressed and the flow of blood from a haemorrhage can be stanched, but this fiendish weapon had got science and surgery beaten" (ibid., ellipses in original).

Triage imposed upon doctors and nurses the sacrilegious responsibility of determining who should live and who should be allowed to die. While it in fact raised survival rates, triage came at a cost to those who performed it. Anna Cameron commented, "The lives of living men must not be risked for lives going out, but the memory of some things which have had to be often left undone in the stress of war nursing stabs and stabs in the quiet days." 39 Lucia Garelli, an Italian nurse who fell ill and died, wrote in a letter of August 1917, "How I have fought and how my poor, ugly face bears deep signs of the internal violence, of my strained will, of the lashes to the tired body. Whoever has not been with me and does not know me well cannot comprehend the t in which I am engaged day after day between feelings and nerves, between heart and head." 40

The despair accompanying impossible responsibility, especially the conflict between competing ethical and patriotic responsibilities, forms a leitmotif in some of the boldest accounts of nursing trauma. Madeleine Clemenceau Jacquemaire, daughter of the French politician, closes her fictionalized autobiographical account of service in the Red Cross with an anguished intrusive vision of recurrent horror. Her counterpart, the head nurse Madame Berton, meditates on the long-term responsibility of women to tell the truth of the war to future generations:

The wheel will continue to turn yet longer. We are at the point in the circle where the wounded man arrives from the battlefield, whence he will pass on (if he does not die and is not mutilated) to the interior, to convalescence, the base station, the trench, and then led by fate back to us, if he is not killed. We often see men who have been wounded five and six times. Ah! away, away! the awful vision that oppresses me . . . Those Spanish horses, sunk to the ground in their blood . . . The ring assistant comes close, thrusts the entrails spilling from the open flank back inside with a handful of straw! Ah! the poor animals! He forces them up with a pitchfork, to bring them back to the bull whose rage must be exhausted. . . . Are we doing the same thing? Yes, since we must buy victory. 41

The vision intrudes and punctuates her thoughts. The effectiveness of her ellipses lies in their visual evocation of what she wishes to repress. The macabre, insistent hallucination expresses the nurse's fear that war instrumentally reduces men to damaged bodies and to commodities exchanged for victory. Those who bandage them contribute to the perpetuation of the system.

One of the most important factors in trauma for medical staff was the disarticulation of bodies and the uncanny blurring of boundaries between the human and the inanimate, the living and the dead. Lady Kennard reports her violent illness and the "mute misery" of her pain after witnessing an accident in which a train from Bucarest, overladen with destitute refugees, women and children, derailed:

Last night we visited at sunset such a scene of horror as can never, and should never, be described. . . . No one knows how many hundreds died there by the roadside, some in the flames of the engine's exploded petrol tank, the greater number crushed into one huge formless mass of flesh and horse-hair, splintered bones and wood. [RD, 125-6]

That which cannot be described must nonetheless be indicated. Even the silence of her misery can speak only through the space opened up by words. The insistence in trauma literature that experience lies beyond language depends, ironically, on language.

The disassembly of bodies resulted not only from modern combat but from surgery itself. "I have ceased to be affected by corpses," Lady Kennard wrote, "but I hate amputations" (RD, 60). Amputations held a particular power to dismay for Pierre La Mazière, a male orderly on a hospital boat at the Dardanelles. He notes not only the frantic pace of operations, but his "horror, terror, and revolt" at the amputation of an arm. 42 His eyes are transfixed by severed fingers, and he is shocked by the surprising shortness of an arm without its hand, a leg without its foot. La Mazière's reflections evoke the care with which he tried to distinguish and understand his own reactions:

While I can observe impassively from the moment when the wounded man is put to sleep and throughout the operation, whatever its horror; while I can watch without emotion the lancet penetrate the flesh, the curette digging into infected wounds, the gouge grinding bones, my sensibility is provoked beyond measure as soon as I am confronted with some debris: arm, leg, foot, or hand whose warmth immediately evaporates and which so quickly assumes the waxy appearance of death. 43

The operation that detaches the dying from the living, the part from the formerly harmonious whole, troubles him because it suggests the complicity of medicine in the destruction of war. La Mazière translates this experience of terror and revulsion into a hellish scene in which he must carry a gangrenous limb for disposal in the furnace, darting here and there in the underwater maze of corridors as he hurries to dispose of this uncanny symbol of death.

The diaries and memoirs of nurses and orderlies such as La Mazière and Kennard display a precise detail, a power of self-interrogation, and a driving rhythm that make them as forceful as most fictional narratives. In a similarly detailed account Lydiia Zakharova, a Russian middle-class mother who volunteered as a nurse in 1916, describes no-man's-land as a vast labyrinth. Its trenches overflow with masses of dead bodies interspersed with strange objects--stolen china, a toppled child's high chair--that bear mute witness to combat. Frozen bodies sit in poses that make them seem alive, "eyes open and glazed with the tranquillity of non-existence, as if they were listening to the ominous cries of the crows flying overhead." 44 Like the surgical nurses Schulz and Walker, who are grateful that they have no time to think, Zakharova notes that at a certain limit "the human mind can perceive no more horrors, as a saturated sponge can soak up no more water" ("DRC," 185). When she suddenly finds herself separated from the orderlies, her disorientation exposes her situation in no-man's-land. Lost between life and death, she must walk over "some kind of mush," being unable to distinguish between mud and mute men ("DRC," 186). Eventually she traces a curve on the horizon that must be the Russian trenches, and her narrative resumes.

Of these assorted records, only the depiction of hospital life by Jacquemaire--a first person account in which the author is transposed as the third-person Mme. Berton--is overtly fictionalized. Many of them interpolate sharply observed soldiers' stories about moments of crisis or complex inner conflicts, subordinating their own insights to dramatic accounts by men who seemed closer to the front, perhaps to anchor their own accounts. In the past such interpolations have been read as transparent documents, unconstructed testimony, or sutured case histories (a genre that many of them echo). Such interpolations, however, signal the constructed nature of these texts, and remind us that the text of war is a hybrid whose site of origin is always displaced.

The simple artistry of these medical diaries and memoirs, with their startling details and fitful narrative rhythms, finds a more ambitious equivalent in the novel Balaurul , based on the Romanian novelist Hortensia Papadat-Bengescu's experience of nursing at Foscani. By focusing an entire chapter on the exposed heart of a dying soldier that she never permits us to see, Papadat-Bengescu invites the reader to imagine indescribable events and to explore the psychic and social wounds inflicted by war. The nurse Laura, through whom the experience is focalized, interrupts her thoughts with ellipses, punctuating physical description with questions about the soldier's unknowable emotion and mind: "Then she knew. He . . . he was afraid . . . afraid to move, to blink even, over the frozen pupil . . . afraid? And he kept his head like that, pushed back, not to see . . . To see what? . . . Maybe he had already seen . . ." 45 Her own thoughts interrupt each other, just as her intent focus on the symbolic wound interrupts her routine nursing tasks and breaks down the frame of understanding into which she attempts to place that naked organ or "altar" of human life. We cannot be surprised to learn that this novel was initially rejected by readers. Papadat-Bengescu finds shocking and sacrilegious images for the diseases she observes, describing gangrenes and strange new epidemics of "infectious pustules linking disparate cases" as "a flora specific to modern wars" ("MWH," 362). Her irony about the tawdry emotions unleashed by suffering must have been unacceptable in the postwar period. She describes the ingenuity that united climates, organisms, and nations in developing new formulae for destruction as "procedures which industrious and honest scientists were still offering with innocence and praise to the vampire of war" (ibid.). The diagnostic gaze is trained not only on the body but on the society as well.

Aside from Vera Brittain's memoir, the World War I nursing sketches that are best known today are undoubtedly those of Ellen La Motte, a trained nurse, and of Mary Borden, a wealthy younger writer who volunteered at the outset of the war. Both worked in the same French military hospital on the western front, which Borden herself had funded. Both moved in avant-garde circles, apparently having met through their friendship with Gertrude Stein; Borden had had an affair with Wyndham Lewis and became a novelist after the war, while La Motte published sketches about her subsequent experiences in China combatting the opium trade. Their accounts differ from most of the previous examples because they not only record immediate observations, but also display a self-conscious concern for artistic craft, deliberately fragmenting their texts. Their work is so powerful that one reviewer found it might suggest the hospital was "a place of horror." 46 Indeed.

Are their works memoirs or fictions? Authentic witness or artistic exploitation? Ernest Hemingway (himself in the ambulance corps) assumed that women's lack of "war experience" forced them to borrow their evidence from men who had the experience. 47 In its review of Borden, the Saturday Review of Literature worried about her poetic gifts: "Miss Borden has taken the rawest materials of life and turned them into a work of art. But what price art?" 48 Such a question loses its sharp contours today, when autobiographies have come to be understood as literary productions, and histories as emplotments. Yet for many critics and analysts, trauma writing has retained a privileged status as a form of virtually spontaneous truth telling. I have argued elsewhere that fragmentation, abrupt juxtaposition, deadpan description, and intrusively vivid images in La Motte's Backwash of War and Borden's Forbidden Zone constitute what I have termed a kind of deliberate "symptomatic writing" of the trauma of nursing. 49 It is important, however, not to confuse such a symptomatic writing with "unwitting" or "unconscious testimony" (TCW, 15).

If we think of the writing of trauma as a process of birth, in which, as Laub suggests, the emergence of narrative produces "knowing," then the imperative relationship between the teller and the imagined audience becomes salient. Psychoanalytic study has focused on the need to testify "to an absence, to an event that has not yet come into existence" because the violence and enormity of the traumatic event cannot be assimilated (TCW, 57). Just as the event seems incomprehensible, suspended between a before and an after, the story of trauma is suspended between life and death. As Cathy Caruth suggests, it exists "between the story of the unbearable nature of an event and the story of the unbearable nature of its survival." 50 Trauma theory is torn between the hope offered by the "talking cure," in which a listener enables the speaker, and the more pessimistic premise that, as Benjamin puts it, "In all mourning there is the deepest inclination to speechlessness." 51 Much medical work, as these quotations suggest, has focused on knowledge at the site of generation rather than that of reception.

I propose that we look closely at the kind of "knowing" that is at stake in these texts. We must consider the relationship between an obstructed, specific consciousness of violence in its ungraspable details and a more philosophic knowledge about the causes and consequences of violence. This second knowing is impelled by a desire to restore the self and to change one's audience; it can motivate and enable the telling by which the dark and obstructed first kind of knowing becomes possible. The two kinds of consciousness cannot be separated, but there is a movement from one to the other in the most creative and powerful of these World War I narratives. Borden, La Motte, Papadat-Bengescu, and even less self-conscious writers such as La Mazière draw from a bitter well, then pass their knowledge on to their readers.

The first kind of knowledge about war trauma, often symbolized by a confrontation with death that civilians can never understand, has generally been understood as incompatible with the second, more philosophic knowledge. When the critical measure of authenticity excludes noncombatants, and looks askance at artistry, it excludes consideration of works such as those I describe here. Sam Hynes chooses, like Tolstoy, to privilege the way soldiers "bear witness" to modern war: "I too have taken the actual killing to be the center of war's compound story." 52 He therefore concentrates on the simple, accurate accounts of soldiers that have "the authority of direct experience." 53 Such an exclusion may be contested by adducing the evidence of war trauma through the presence of its symptoms. Yet if we interpret war memoirs and autobiographical fictions symptomatically, as I myself have done, we run the risk of a circular logic. The symptoms do not guarantee the authenticity of the unique lived experience, since they participate in a larger, preexisting system of signs, and deploy those that are available both to invoke and to evade the trauma. As Robert Graves wrote, "the memoirs of a man who went through some of the worst experiences of trench warfare are not truthful if they do not contain a high proportion of falsities." 54

The interconnection between the symptoms of trauma and a widespread explanation of the rise of modernism gives this question special weight. Susan Stanford Friedman writes in her study of H. D. that "Art produced after the First World War recorded the emotional aspect of this crisis; despair, hopelessness, paralysis, angst, and a sense of meaninglessness, . . . chaos, and fragmentation of material reality." 55 Modernist writing, she explains, focused on the "agency of language" as a vehicle of meaning (ibid.). For some, trauma writing was defined by a fragmentary juxtaposition of intense moments and images, as emotionally powerful wording erupted in response to the war experience. It stood in opposition to the exhausted, cliché discourse of patriotism and traditional values that Henry James and Hemingway declined as overstrained and obscene. 56 As Edith Wharton wrote, "the meaning had evaporated out of lots of our old words, as if the general smash-up had broken their stoppers." 57

Already during the war the verbal symptoms of trauma experienced by soldiers had been linked to the experimental forms of modernist or avant-garde writing. The power of wartime trauma to filter into literary forms is testified to by André Breton, who served as an orderly in the psychiatric service at Saint-Dizier and in Paris at the Val de Grace hospital. Annette Becker comments, "If the Great War was not the creator of the surrealist movement, it was certainly its catalyst." 58 Charged with distinguishing simulators from those genuinely ill, Breton transcribed the ravings of his mental patients when he was on night duty. The dreams and free associations that burst out in these instances of acute delirium "were from the beginning at the heart of surrealism." 59 Importantly, Breton was consciously exploiting such forms of expression, in order to explore the possibility of "freedom from logical, moral, and other constraints" (ibid.).

Probably the most convincing argument for modernist features in nursing war narratives can be found in Jane Marcus's comments on Evadne Price's fictional diary of an ambulance driver, Not So Quiet . . . Stepdaughters of War. She compares the narrative voice in Not So Quiet to that of Stein, connecting the "explosive" sentence structures, ellipses, and hallucinatory outbursts to modernist montage. 60 Marcus borrows the metaphors of war to evoke this "brutally masculinized prose" in Price's work as a "bombardment" of the reader "in a text pock-marked with ellipses of silence and rushes of noisy belligerent words." 61 Calling attention to the "disorder and disorientation of the body at war," Marcus suggests that self-interruptive ellipses evoke the physical experience of war trauma and "gender trauma" ("CCC," 268-9). Indicative of repressed rage, these gaps comment on the lack of truthful communication in war, so that sentences become "fragmented like the bodies that litter this forbidden territory" ("CCC," 273).

Price, a popular novelist writing under a pseudonym, returns us to the question of authenticity. Price was commissioned to compose a response to Erich Maria Remarque's All Quiet on the Western Front . Marcus has tracked the echoes exhaustively. Perhaps more interesting are less overtly commercial enterprises. Lesley N. Smith's Four Years Out of Life reads like a parody of Mary Borden's Forbidden Zone, published two years earlier. Imitation is the highest form of flattery, one might say. The larger point is that such writers were drawing, ten and more years after the end of the war, on an established repertoire of forms for describing the war. Claire Tylee has described Rebecca West's anonymous War Nurse--The True Story of a Woman Who Lived, Loved and Suffered on the Western Front as a flagrant fiction that combined stock war motifs with gothic romance. 62 To be sure, it is not necessary in such a case to postulate a preexisting expressive repertoire for traumatic expression, since some writers, like politicians, seem to be able to invent a language of clichés. In general, however, even the major early war texts, such as Henri Barbusse's Le feu, journal d'un escadron , were shaped by an available discourse, both traditional and experimental, as Paul Fussell has demonstrated in his study of British war literature, The Great War and Modern Memory .

Questions about authenticity still arise in part because we have not yet integrated the concept of intertextuality into our understanding of trauma literature. Oddly enough, doctors observed that the forms of expression taken by trauma might occur in small epidemics. Fenton accompanied a train of "war neurotics" on which one man had a stutter: "when they detrained the next day almost all of them stuttered." 63 He did not mean that his patients were simulators, although the fact that hypochondriacs could be mistaken for hysterics implied that the signifying systems of trauma were available to others. Rather, he suggested, social codes helped shape the course of mental disabilities and channeled their expression.

Understanding the symptoms of neurosis as socially encoded took its most striking form in the class distinction British doctors drew between the forms that war neuroses took among officers and among "men." One consequence was a distrust of some symptoms as fictions. Similarly, German doctors suspected that war neuroses were really a covert display of the lazy working class desire for a pension (Rentenneurosen). 64

Conversely, it has seemed to some modern interpreters that secondary or imagined trauma, the aftershock felt by those who were not direct witnesses, does not substantially differ from lived trauma: the two then would be equal in weight. Tate goes so far as to claim in her commentary on H. D. that "fantasy might be constituted differently from the memory of real events, but it can be equally disturbing" (MH, 16). These issues of interpretation and attitudes toward fictionality are not idle: the difference could sometimes result in execution.

In Testimony, Felman and Laub ask, what is "the relation between narrative and history, between art and memory, between speech and survival?" 65 The difficult relationships among these pairings speak to the complex, unstable, and value-laden concept of a massive trauma such as that experienced during World War I. The instability or, as some would have it, inexpressible nature of trauma is in large part responsible for the problematic status of war narratives. Examining the testimony of medical staff is useful because these texts both observe and participate in the experiences of trauma. When their styles display the symptoms of trauma, the effect is both descriptive and expressive. As a result, they participate in the two kinds of knowing considered here. I would reject any polarity between authenticity and artifice--an opposition that (in the context of war literature) has been aligned with the opposition between combatant and noncombatant, between male and female. The evidence from nursing accounts indicates, I hope, that the distance between an "authentic" soldier's account and a noncombatant nurse's account is less great than many have supposed. In addition, I want to bear tribute to the artistry that even simple testimony can possess. That artistry is essential to the healing and constructive role of narration at a time when trauma and memory shape the modern subject.
