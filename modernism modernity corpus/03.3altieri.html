Whose America is Our America: On Walter Benn Michaels's Characterizations of Modernity in America

Charles Altieri

Our America and Nativist Modernism:
A Panel

I find it hard not to see this event as an inversion of the story of the three little pigs: here the three of us huff and puff to blow down the house of the big bad wolf. This pig thought he had a clever way to accomplish that. If I could agree entirely with Walter Benn Michaels there would be no remainder that his brilliant dialectics might subsume: his house would stand but have no visitors. But despite my deep admiration for his intricate arguments and my reluctant concessions to his accounts of cultural pluralism and nativist modernism, I cannot agree with the historical dimensions of Michaels's argument. Even if one grants that there is a nativist modernism, I doubt that it had the clear general dominance that Michaels seems to be arguing for most of the time. And, more important, I think his attributing such a single synthetic identity across various cultural domains ignores the various efforts to negotiate complex tensions among value systems that are for me the central, dynamic features of modernity. So I will have to make a few historical claims and then try to explain what I find problematic in the theoretical assumptions that seem to underlie Michaels's argument.

Let me elaborate my complaint about his claims for the dominance of nativist modernism by briefly addressing two issues: first, problems in the scope of his claims and, second, problems in how he deals with the agency of authors in his efforts to establish that scope. On scope, consider the following passage:

The Johnson Act guaranteed that aliens would not become citizens by putting a halt to mass immigration; the Citizenship Act guaranteed that Indians would not become citizens by declaring that they already were citizens. Both acts, that is, participated in a recasting of American citizenship, changing it from a status that could be achieved through one's own actions (immigrating, becoming "civilized," getting "naturalized") to a status that could better be understood as inherited. [OA, 32]

A series of brilliant leaps here establishes a forceful account of how ideas of citizenship changed for some agents. But what about those who were not immigrants, and hence who already experienced citizenship as inherited even (or especially) during ages of heavy immigration? Conversely, what about those immigrants who had received citizenship, or whose children had heard stories about that process? Would they now shift their understanding? And how much in common really is there between the groups of immigrants and American Indians, since they held quite different understandings of what citizenship did and did not afford in economic and cultural terms? Moreover, the very effort to establish commonness on the level of what these pieces of legislation meant for people requires overlooking the economic factors that are central in establishing different experiences of citizenship. 1 So as we pull at the threads of such assertions we find neat equations unravelling into problematic equivocations, and we begin to appreciate how many factors an analysis of general attitudes toward citizenship might have to account for.

Such omissions become more troubling when we turn to Michaels's dealings with specific authors. He is superb in showing how William Carlos Williams and Hart Crane enter into a discourse of nativist modernism quite different from the transnational modernism maintained by exiles like Ezra Pound and T. S. Eliot. But I wonder how much these writers ultimately share with the overall political concerns Michaels foregrounds. If, for example, Williams insists that to be "'Indianlike' the writer must make no likeness to Indians, he must devote himself to 'WRITING'" (OA, 85), then it seems that Williams's nativism is of a distinctly metaphoric kind, more involved with the republic of letters than with actual citizenship. To insist on the politics of his metaphors is not far from calling for a feline modernism on the basis of the analogies Williams draws between cats and the artist's constructive intelligence. Michaels could only be convincing here if he could show that Williams based specific political decisions on identifications with the natives he uses so blithely in his analogies. More important, Michaels argues that texts can "parody but also reproduce" (OA, 26) nativist attitudes, but he does not go on to explore the aspects within the culture that generate the distance necessary for parody. So he can use Jason Compson's speeches as illustrations of William Faulkner's dependency on racialist rhetoric (OA, 9). But in so doing he ignores Faulkner's need to distance himself from anything Compson-like (precisely because the author knows how much he is tempted to such identifications). Faulkner's own stance on race is probably closer to a tragic or Calvinist inversion of progressivist racism that becomes evident in the necessary deaths of Joe Christmas and Charles Bon, as well as of Quentin Compson. Moreover, even if I am wrong about Faulkner's specific beliefs, the gulf between Faulkner and his characters requires us to postulate within modernist culture more than one attitude toward these racial matters so that one can foreground the tensions from which these doublings emerge.

Where conflict and uncertainty should be, Michaels doggedly and brilliantly imposes a single dominant set of beliefs. From my perspective the greatest cost is the resulting picture of modernism. It simply does not suffice to equate modernist styles with the purity of the signifier without also tracing the ways that the artists and painters sought to make this emphasis on syntax carry various versions of semantic force. Think how often Williams speaks of realization and of bearing cultural testimony, and recall the power of his lineation to transform perception into Cézannian realization by embodying the role of mental energies in the emergence of sharp phenomenological detail. Similarly, when Michaels links his claims about purity of the signifier to the ideal of untranslatability, he ignores the degree to which untranslatability functioned within modernism simply as a way of opposing not impurity but discursivity. Indeed, an impure art like collage might provide the best version of untranslatability because of the diversity it forced into conjunction. Finally, we have to recognize the profound impurities that make it misleading to treat the literature of the period in terms of simple dichotomies between native and universal. Much of the energy driving the Harlem Renaissance emerged from the pressure of having to face fundamental conflicts between the ideals of a writing distinctive to race and of a writing that explored cultural resources not available to strict identifications with nativist values. 2 And while Williams insisted on the primacy of the local (often as access to a universal), even his most radical claims about American identity are accompanied by statements about the need to use the kind of singularity that artworks achieve to break down differences and weaken partiality. So when Williams describes, as Michaels puts it, "the success of a poem" as consisting in "its achievement of identity" (OA, 83), he is in entire sympathy with Pound's transnational modernist dreams that the achieved identity of artworks can establish energies then available for diverse cultural groups. 3

Given Michaels's intricate mind, I have to risk being patronizing in order to speculate on what theoretical assumptions underlie his case; then I can try alternative ways of addressing the same concerns, which here I will confine to concepts of identity and of cultural heritage. As long as he is concerned with transnational modernism, Michaels seems to have no problem dealing with fluid identities and allowing claims about culture to do work even though they are not attached to race. But when he turns to the domestic American scene, the standards seem to shift. Here his project of "work[ing] out the meaning of the commitment to identity--linguistic, national, cultural, racial" (OA, 3)--common to both the social and the aesthetic models of nativist modernism depends on Michaels showing that such commitments are inherently flawed by a version of what Kenneth Burke called "the paradox of substance." 4 There is a basic contradiction "between being something and trying to be the thing one is" (OA, 90), so that concepts of racial and cultural identity compose a slippery circuit in which each supplements and empties the other. Problematic notions of identity then seem inseparable from problematic notions of cultural heritage: "For racial identity to become a project, it must turn to culture; for cultural identity to become a project it must turn to race" (OA, 122; for fuller statement see 14-15).

In short, such identity-commitments lead to the dead end of contemporary cultural pluralism. But are these the only ways to think about identity--either as a basic practical concern or as a specific theme in modernist thinking? If we are to have a less thematizable history and a more intricate sense of how agents encounter and deal with cultural pressures, we have to explore both historical and theoretical ways of negotiating identity claims that do not depend on the ontological substrates responsible for the slippage so dear to Michaels's heart. Suppose for example that claims about personal identity can function as projections that take place on many levels. Identity is not a condition that need apply tout court in ways that require a language of grounding substance. Rather, identity claims wield that marvelous adjusting operator "as," and hence are relative to contextual frames. Therefore assertions of identity have varying degrees of comprehensiveness depending on the scope of self-reference called for by the situation--my identity as a tennis player may be more intense than my identity as a family man, but it still addresses a much smaller set of features of my life. 5

Once our sense of identity claims is reconred, we can then see how one might provide an alternative to Michaels's treatment of claims about cultural heritage. He sees these as at best misleading, at worst as charlatanry because for him such claims have to depend on attributions of shared substance for those who belong to the culture, and hence they sustain racism:

The real question, however, is not which past should count as ours but why any past should count as ours. Virtually all the events and actions that we study did not happen to us and were not done by us. In this sense, the history we study is never our own; it is always the history of people who were in some respects like us and in other respects different. When, however, we claim it as ours, we commit ourselves to the ontology of "the Negro," to the identity of "we" and "they" and the primacy of race.

This is not to say . . . that all accounts of cultural identity require a racial component, it is only to say that the accounts of cultural identity that do any work require a racial component. For insofar as our culture remains nothing more than what we do and believe, it is impotently descriptive. The fact, in other words, that something belongs to our culture cannot count as a motive for our doing it since, if it does belong to our culture, we already do it and if we don't do it (if we've stopped or haven't yet started doing it), it doesn't belong to our culture. (It makes no sense, for example, to claim that we shouldn't teach Shakespeare because he isn't part of our culture since to teach him will immediately make him part of our culture, but it also makes no sense to claim that we should teach him because he is part of our culture since, if we stop teaching him, he won't be any longer.) [OA, 128]

If, however, one has a more supple view of both identity and the kinds of pressures that ideals of a cultural heritage can help negotiate, one need not be as fearful as Michaels is of the illusions of substance creeping in. There are other ways to speculate on which history ought to be ours, ways that do not generate a strict binary between something belonging to our culture only because we do it and some netherworld of our forgettings. To dramatize this alternative we need only ask whether Michaels's version of culture will suffice for the many first-person plurals invoked in the passage just quoted. Can the "we" here do any work despite not alluding to race? And must "our culture" here depend on actual shared practices, or only on understanding what such practices might look like, even if we do not now actualize them? I suggest that the "we" invoked here does not depend on any specific set of shared beliefs but only on shareable competencies. That is so, I further contend, because a cultural heritage makes better sense when viewed not as actual concrete practices that are either present or absent, but as complex grammatical competence: the ability to use certain practices and make certain identifications and assertions of difference when occasions call for them. And then the test of belonging to the culture is not sharing any origin but being able to share the kind of projections Michaels invites us to take on. Sharing a cultural heritage does not necessarily mean reading Shakespeare but it does mean understanding the reasoning involved in making a case for reading Shakespeare. One might even say that so long as we teach literature or drama there are claims that anyone whose interests are directed toward such materials will profit from trying Shakespeare--not because there is some direct link to him but because there are possible uses for him that emerge from our grasp of related materials and the interests they may form. So there are considerable grounds for inviting or even requiring people to try out identifications with a heritage even if there is no racial basis for the identification. Culture as educational heritage belongs to us, in other words, not because we have direct rights to it but because we can visualize sufficient proximity to its projections that we can believe we have some interests in trying on what it offers.

For American modernism, shifting our theoretical terms also involves shifting the level of issues we make central and the range of sympathies we entertain--for example in relation to how agents like Coolidge might have been using notions of culture very differently from how Michaels reads them (OA, 35, 123) and in relation to appreciating why there could have been such tension between universalist and particularist stances on matters of race and culture. Moreover, understanding that ideals of culture as paideia offered an alternative to racial and class identifications seems to me necessary to appreciate both how the children of immigrants were educated into American and how at least many of the intellectuals found grounds for breaking with the religious and family ties that originally bound immigrant communities. The parents' concern with getting educated in order to get ahead made possible the children's concern for education in order to get away.

An adequate sense of history also requires a sense of how we might alter what we inherit. Acutely aware of this task, Michaels tries to make visible both the lineage and the contradictory structure of cultural pluralism. But for me the ultimate limitation of the theoretical assumptions enabling his work is their incapacity to be much help in suggesting any alternative practical strategies for reconring our imaginations in relation to racial issues. For Michaels's pragmatism this theoretical poverty is no weakness at all: in theoretical poverty there may be existential possibility. However, I do not see how productive possibility will emerge from the binary in his work between problematic strict notions of identity and some general sense that we can do without the notion of identity entirely (even though Michaels's own history seems driven by quite narrow categories). In my view, the best way to counter the heritage of nativist modernism is not to absolutize it and then demolish it but to use the conflicts and tensions its formation created in order to clarify just how complex identity issues are, and just what moral and social work can be done if one keeps a relativized sense of how identity claims are both imposed and negotiated. Then we need not dismiss the possible need for racial identifications, so long as we can also promise corollary identifications with imaginative sites not necessarily bound to any specific racial heritage. So long as race remains a term validated by historical patterns, there will have to be projections of imaginative ways for identifying with others who feel they share or are required to share similar histories. Developing these possible imaginations is the work of culture. But once we understand the relation between culture and identity we also have strong grounds for arguing that it is as impoverished to rely only on racial identifications as it is to deny by abstract argument that they ought to matter for historical practice.

Charles Altieri teaches twentieth-century American literature and literary theory at the University of California, Berkeley. His most recent books are Subjective Agency: A Theory of First-Person Expressivity and its Social Implications and Painterly Abstraction in Modernist American Poetry . After finishing a book on the end of postmodernism he will begin working on the topic of theorizing the emotions.

But why does white men's learning to sing and dance like blacks count as stealing black culture? However in the case of claims about popular music at least, the charge is not made on the basis of some abstract sense of cultural right.
