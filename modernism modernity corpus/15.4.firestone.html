Jackson Pollock's The Magic Mirror:
Jung, Shamanism, and John Graham
Evan R. Firestone

Lee Krasner was mightily impressed on the occasion of her first visit to Jackson Pollock's apartment in November, 1941, particularly with one painting ( 1). She recalled, "One work—the painting later titled Magic Mirror—just about stunned me."1 Ellen Landau has called it an "exemplary" work of the early 1940s, and Walter Hopps sees it as "a crucial early example of [Pollock's] radical fusion of abstraction and imagism."2 Yet, aside from Landau and Hopps, and more recently, Elizabeth Langhorne, there has been a paucity of attention to the picture, and their discussions can be enlarged.3The Magic Mirror may have been titled later, but its imagery appears to intentionally justify the title. It is well known that Pollock's painting, Moby Dick, was retitled Pasiphaë at the suggestion of James Johnson Sweeny, and that his first group of poured paintings in 1947 were assigned titles in picture-naming sessions with Ralph and Mary Manheim and Krasner, but there is no evidence, anecdotal or otherwise, that anyone other than Pollock was responsible for the Magic Mirror's title. The title conjures up the long history of magic mirrors in the annals of the occult, and while Landau has noted the painting's connection to shamanism, my line of inquiry examines in greater depth the Native American sources with which Pollock is usually associated, and goes beyond them to shamanism in the Russian Empire. Admittedly, this essay is, in part, speculative, but it rests on a number of established facts: Pollock's friendship with John Graham grew from a shared enthusiasm for the ideas of Carl Jung; Graham was an ardent devotee of the occult and was knowledgeable about shamanism in Russia's territories; Pollock had a keen interest in North American shamanism and according to a reliable source, they discussed their mutual interest in shamanism; and Pollock's attraction to shamanism is most evident in several works executed at the time he was involved with Graham. Together they shared the entrancements of the "primitive," the occult, and the unconscious that largely defined the modernist sensibility of their day.

The re in The Magic Mirror, apparently female as identified by a round breast with prominent red nipple, sits before a mirror located in the upper right-hand corner that reflects an ambiguous flurry of color. Above the almost equally ambiguous re is what Landau describes as "a distorted C-shape face with hair hanging down" that she believes was derived from Picasso's Woman in an Armchair, 1939 (Musée Picasso, Paris), while Hopps sees the same form as "a radically abstracted head with a feathered headdress."4 On the other hand, in lieu of a head, Langhorne proposes a "flying phallus," a reading to which I subscribe.5 This hovering or flying object, detached from the body, infers supernatural power. In fact, my argument for the shamanic content of the painting essentially turns on the supernatural aspect of this object. The head, then, would be faintly located above the re's right shoulder looking upward to the mirror. As for the concept of a female shaman, in a roughly contemporaneous drawing of a dancing shaman Pollock's re is ostensibly female ( 2), identified as such by a yellow crescent—a female symbol—in the pubic area.6 The dancer's third leg conveys successive movement, not unlike the jumble of anatomical forms containing one or more breasts in the painting. This suggestion of movement in a seated re can be ascribed to the phenomenon of shamanic soul flight discussed later in the essay. Then there is the painting's surface, which is textured with a granular material, sand or fine gravel. Sand paintings, created by medicine men in several southwestern tribal cultures, are part of ceremonies intended to cure patients by restoring them to a harmonious relationship with nature.7

In subject and composition The Magic Mirror is related to Picasso's Girl Before a Mirror, 1932 (3), an image reproduced in Graham's article "Primitive Art and Picasso," published in the April 1937 issue of Magazine of Art. This article made such an impression on Pollock that he preserved it, and wrote to Graham seeking an introduction, which occurred between 1937 and 1940.8 After their meeting, the older Graham assumed the role of Pollock's mentor (some have said guru), and facilitated his public recognition by including him in the "French and American Painting" exhibition Graham organized for McMillen Gallery in January, 1942. Girl Before a Mirror was highly visible in New York as it had been acquired by the Museum of Modern Art in 1938, and included in two of its exhibitions the following year, "Picasso: Forty Years of his Art" and "Art of Our Time." Graham and Pollock both were very taken with Girl Before A Mirror, the former considering it his favorite work by Picasso.9 His Untitled (Artist Sweating Blood), ca. 1942–43 (4), based on Picasso's picture, and apparently inspired by Pollock's, presents a woman adjacent to a mirror that is intersected at its upper right corner by what appears to be male genitals. 10 Graham and Pollock may have discerned a male presence in Girl Before A Mirror in the form of an upward thrusting scythe-like arm above two breasts that perform double duty as testes. Unlike Picasso and Graham, however, Pollock untethers his phallic object, signaling its extraordinary powers.

For Pollock, who was in Jungian analysis for alcoholism and depression from 1939 to 1942, Girl Before A Mirror would have had a special resonance. As William Rubin has written, Picasso's painting has an "echo of a troubled, internalized image in the mirror reflection" that embodies "the theme of psychic revelation." 11 Carla Gottlieb also noticed that the mirror reflection "renders the submerged world of the unconscious." 12 If Pollock needed help recognizing the psychological dimension of Girl Before A Mirror, Graham's article pointed the way with a caption notation asserting that Picasso's work evokes "primordial memories of the unconscious." 13 Carl Jung, too, could have pointed Pollock to the connection between one's mirror image and the psyche. In The Integration of the Personality, published in 1939, he wrote,

The Integration of the Personality helped make Jung's ideas highly accessible, and as Michael Leja has suggested, Joseph Henderson, Pollock's analyst at the time, may have either encouraged Pollock to read it, or discussed it in their sessions. 15

In view of Jung's passage above, and Graham's representation of himself as a woman in Untitled (Artist Sweating Blood), The Magic Mirror may be interpreted as a symbolic self-portrait. As "Pollock" looks deeply into the mirror he discovers and identifies with the female component of his psyche—the anima—which resides in the unconscious. In therapy he sought to achieve the Jungian goal of individuation, that is, the unification of opposites in the psyche that is necessary to establish psychic wholeness. What Langhorne and I take to be a flying phallus hovering above a female re, "propose[s] a union of male and female opposites as a way to articulate a fuller self."16 If, as I maintain, the re is a shaman we have an early instance of what Rushing, discussing Pollock's poured paintings in relation to his interest in Navajo sand paintings, describes as "Pollock standing for the shaman who is his own patient." 17 Similarly, Langhorne, in another study, asserts that Naked Man, ca. 1938–41, "is a presentation of Pollock's own identification with the shaman-artist, in effect a self-portrait." 18

Another aspect of Girl Before A Mirror, the sun and moon imagery of the girl's face on the left, also would have registered with Pollock in light of his Jungian therapy. The sun and moon, according to Jung, represent respectively the conscious and unconscious mind, as well the masculine and feminine dualities. The sun and crescent moon are among the most prevalent of Pollock's symbols in this period, and as Leja notes, "His determination to integrate them, to merge them . . . reveal him as striving to make the disk and crescent integral parts of a greater form." 19 This effort of visual synthesis parallels Pollock's personal struggle to achieve psychic harmony. The merger of sun and moon is evident in a large number of drawings, and in the conration of masks or heads in the paintings Birth and Naked Man, ca. 1938–1941, and Male and Female of 1942. Meyer Schapiro observed about the face and reflection in Girl Before A Mirror,

The exposed profile is cool and pale and what is hidden and imagined, the unseen face, is an intense yellow, like a sun . . . but in the face is also a crescent moon which occurs elsewhere on the body and there is a contrast like that of the sun and moon in the relation of the real body and the mirrored body and indeed the moon has a reflected light. 20

Picasso's amalgamation of frontal and profile views of the face, in this work and others, likely was the source for Pollock's solar-lunar heads. Whether he would have equated the girl's reflection with the moon is impossible to say, although in the popular imagination of many cultures the moon and mirror are interchangeable. 21 Pollock may have been familiar with the association of moon and mirror because of his close friendship with William Baziotes in the early 1940s. Baziotes was a great enthusiast of Baudelaire's poetry, whose prose poem, "Favors of the Moon," was the inspiration for his 1942 painting Mirror at Midnight I. 22

Jung's passage concerning the confrontation with the self in a mirror is a psycho-analytic gloss on an ancient and widespread belief concerning reflections and the soul. Pollock owned the abridged, one-volume edition of James Frazer's The Golden Bough that contains a number of examples of indigenous peoples who regarded their reflections in water or mirrors as their souls, including inhabitants of the Andaman Islands, New Guinea, and New Calendonia. Frazer then recounts superstitions about mirror reflections around the world throughout history.23 From ancient times mirrors were believed to have divinatory powers. Pausanius, the second-century Greek, reported that at Patrae, in front of the sanctuary of Demeter, "an infallible mode of divination" entailed lowering a mirror into the spring, which, after prayers were offered to the goddess, could be read to determine the fate of sick persons. 24The Encyclopedia of Occult Sciences, published in 1939, under a section subtitled "Magic Mirrors," refers to the specularii, those who read mirrors in ancient Rome, a practice known as catoptromancy.25 This is just the kind of book that would have attracted John Graham. In addition to being knowledgeable about Greek and Roman antiquity and well read in the classics, he had a strong interest in the occult, as noted in the 1920s by Duncan Phillips, an early patron. 26 When The Encyclopedia of Occult Sciences was published, he had just met or was about to meet Pollock, and was only a few years from total obsession with various forms of the occult, including alchemy, astral bodies and planes, astrology, and Theosophy, topics discussed at length in the book. Moreover, according to Lee Krasner, Graham collected mirrors. 27

Among the groups that the encyclopedia associates with magic mirrors are the Chinese, Hindus, Persians, and Renaissance Italians. Perhaps the portion of the text that would have been of greatest interest to Graham describes the so-called "cabalistic mirrors," which unite his fascination with mirrors and astrology. The cabalistic mirrors, in fact, "seven globes each representing one of the seven planets of Astrology, made of the corresponding metal and consulted on the appropriate astrological day," give counsel about superior beings, money matters, probabilities of success, quarrels and lawsuits, questions of love, the whereabouts of lost objects, and future plans. 28 During the years of his close association with Pollock, Graham painted several pictographs unique to his work that organize astrological symbols within grid-like structures. 29 His more rative Sun and Bird, ca. 1941–42, employs alchemical and astrological symbols to represent the transformative stages of the alchemical process. 30 Whether or not Graham and Pollock were familiar with The Encyclopedia of Occult Sciences, Graham's enthusiasms for what it contained captured his young friend's attention. Pollock's receptivity to the occult and Graham's interest in it no doubt was conditioned by his flirtation with Theosophy as a high school student. 31

Pollock and Graham, both Jungians, and admirers of "primitive" art, subscribed to Jung's belief that in certain circumstances modern man could access the collective unconscious:

. . . from the hidden depths of the psyche something arises to confront him . . . contents of an archetypal nature . . . that exert a strong influence of their own whether or not they are understood by the conscious mind . . . and so come to bridge the morbid split in his nature upon a higher level. 32

Primitive art affords this opportunity because, as Graham wrote in "Primitive Art and Picasso," "Primitive races and primitive genius have readier access to their unconscious mind than so-called civilized people. . . ."

Therefore the art of primitive races has a highly evocative quality which allows it to bring to our consciousness the clarities of the unconscious mind, stored with all the individual and collective wisdom of past generations and forms. In other words, an evocative art is the means and the result of getting in touch with the powers of our unconscious. 33

Graham refers to Maori jade, Eskimo and North American Indian masks, and Tlingit, Kwakiutl, and Haida carvings, as well as African art, of which he was a self-professed expert and dealer. Pollock's interest in Native American art is well documented. Growing up in Arizona and California he had often come in contact with Indians from various tribes. Pollock purchased twelve volumes of the Bureau of American Ethnology Report with his brother Charles in the early 1930s, collected clippings about Indian art, made frequent trips to the two New York museums that had extensive Native American collections—the Museum of the American Indian and the American Museum of Natural History—and in 1941 he visited the Museum of Modern Art's "Indian Art of the United States" exhibition several times, especially to observe the demonstrations of Navajo sand painting. 34 According to Fritz Bultman, an artist who knew both Pollock and Graham well, the former was aware of the "whole shamanistic dream culture of Indians," and Graham was knowledgeable about the literature on Russian shamanism. Consequently, they shared a "coinciding and reinforcing interest in primitivism and Indian art."35

Both Graham and Pollock must have been aware of the connection Jung made between shamanism and his spiritualized psychology. As early as 1918, in "Uber das Unbewusste" (The Role of the Unconscious), which went untranslated until 1964, Jung asserted, "Primitive man simply has a different theory—the theory of witchcraft and spirits. I find this theory very interesting and very sensible—actually more sensible than the academic views of modern science." And he observed that the recent appearance of spiritualism "is a modern belief in spirits and a rebirth of a form of religion practiced by our remote forefathers." 36 He expressed this idea again in "Seele and Erde ("Mind and Earth"), translated in English in 1928:

I have found in my American patients that their hero-re possesses traits derived from the religion of the Indians. The most important re in this religion is the shaman, the medicine man or conjurer of spirits. The first American discovery in this field—since taken up in Europe—was spiritualism, and the second was Christian Science and other forms of mental healing." 37

If Graham, who read German, did not make the connection between Jungianism and shamanism for Pollock, or if he did not come upon the association on his own, his Jungian therapists, Henderson and Violet de Laszlo, would have drawn his attention to it, as shamanism was a serious topic of interest to him. Furthermore, Pollock's and Bultman's mutual friend in the early 1940s, Tony Smith, according to Bultman, "was a walking encyclopedia of Jung[ian] shamanism." 38

Graham (né Ivan Drombrowski) apparently brought his enthusiams for the occult and shamanism to the United States in 1920 from Russia. Born and educated in the Ukraine, then part of the Russian Empire, he graduated from the University of Kiev in 1907, and subsequently received a law degree from the University of St. Vladimir in 1913. 39 Russia at the time was enthralled with the occult. Maria Carlson has observed that "Russian occult interests ran the entire gamut: Spiritualism and Theosophy attracted the most attention and generated the largest number of publications, but there was also Rosicrucianism, alchemy, psychographology, phrenology, Kabbalism, Yoga, sectarianism, Hermeticism, hypnotism, Egyptian religion, astrology, chiromancy, animal magnetism, fakirism, telepathy, the Tarot, and magic (both black and white)." 40 It was also a period of burgeoning Russian scholarship on the country's "primitive" peoples—the Finno-Ugric tribes on both sides of the Ural Mountains, and the tribes of the vast Siberian land mass—and although the efflorescence of occultism and the study of shamanism have separate histories, they became entwined. The reason is not difficult to discern. Occultism has both theoretical and practical dimensions. The former holds to a worldview that believes the divine mysteries can be penetrated by uncovering a secret body of knowledge, whereas practical occultism engages in a variety of practices to intervene in the supernatural. 41 Such intervention is precisely the shaman's role. The painter Vasilii Kandinsky provides a prominent example of the intermingling of ethnography and the occult. As a student at the University of Moscow Kandinsky did fieldwork in 1889 among a Finno-Ugric people known to the Russians as the Zyrians. Well versed in shamanism, Kandinsky later embraced Theosophy while viewing himself as something of a modern shaman. 42

Occasional travelers to the Siberian wilderness had brought back artifacts and tales of indigenous peoples, but it was in the eighteenth century during Peter the Great's reign (1689–1725) that exploration of the hinterlands began in earnest. In the absence of academically-trained Russians, German explorers were commissioned to map the Siberian territories, record its populations, search for mineral resources, collect animal and plant specimens, and bring back shamanic objects for Peter the Great's Chamber of Curiosities (Kunstkamera), founded in 1714. Accounts of The Great North Expedition of 1733 to 1743, followed by the Expedition of Orenburg and Astrakhan from 1768 to 1774, were published in St. Petersburg in German, thereby introducing an educated Russian public to shamanism. 43 Research in German continued to be published throughout the nineteenth century, and studies in Russian, which accelerated during this time, frequently were translated or summarized in German. 44 By the early decades of the twentieth century an extensive corpus of Russian language studies on the indigenous tribes of the Russian Empire had been published. 45 It was only a matter of time before books appeared in other languages, including two general treatises in English on Finno-Ugric and Siberian shamanism by M.A. Czaplicka and Uno Holmberg. 46 Graham, who claimed to know nine, at other times twelve languages, had an extensive literature on shamanism available to him.

In an essay titled "The Poetry of Magic and Spells" published in 1908, the Russian Symbolist poet Alexander Blok declared that "Ancient spells, and with them the whole realm of folk magic and ritual, are for us an ore in which the gold of authentic poetry glints. . . ."47 Blok was referring to the practices of his ancestors, not those of the Finno-Ugric and Siberian tribes, but his sentiments reflect the desire for primitivistic expression in Russia after the turn of the century. Velimir Khlebnikov was another writer who incorporated folkloric materials in his works. A self-declared Futurist, he was able to embrace folklore and ethnography because the "authentic" expressions of the past, in contrast to the moribund art of his time, could point the way the future.48 In his essay, "On Expanding the Frontiers of Russian literature," published in 1913, he cited various cultures within and without the Russian Empire that he argued should be enlisted by Russian writers, including those of the Finns (Finno-Ugric tribes) and the Mongols.49 References to the mythological tales of the Oroche, a tribe in eastern Siberia, and the religion of the Ostiak, a Finno-Ugric people in western Siberia, are found in his poems, short stories, and plays between 1911 and 1919.50 Khlebnikov was part of an avant-garde circle that included the painter Mikhail Larionov and the poet-painter David Burliuk, both of whom Graham knew in Russia, and later referenced in his System and Dialectics of Art, published in 1937.51 Graham likely encountered Khlebnikov as well.

Larionov, along with Natalia Goncharova, was a foremost exponent of Russian Neo Primitivism in the visual arts prior to 1914. Like Khlebnikov, he could mix primitivism and Futurism. At the same time he and Goncharova were producing Rayonist paintings utilizing Futurist "lines of force," Larionov displayed an awareness of the legends and religious practices of the Buryat people of Central Asia in a number of primitivistic drawings. His library contained M.N. Khangalov's monograph, New Materials Respecting Shamanism Among the Buryat, published in 1890, and he owned a sacred Buryat drawing.52 Larionov's drawings allude to shamanic rituals, trances, the bird as spirit animal, and supernatural journeys. His 1912–13 painting Gypsy Venus, with its shamanic references, suggests that Larionov and Khlebnikov, who published the poem Shaman and Venus in 1912, worked in close collaboration.53 Graham's years in Russia, and his contact with Larionov and other avant-garde res, appear to confirm Fritz Bultman's claim that he was knowledgeable about shamanism in Russia's territories.

The Swedish scholar Åke Hultkrantz has concisely summarized the religious, social, and individual elements of Siberian shamanism:

The central idea of shamanism is to establish means of contact with the supernatural world by the ecstatic experience of a professional and inspired intermediary, the shaman. There are thus four important constituents of shamanism: the ideological premise, or the supernatural world and the contacts with it; the shaman as the actor on behalf of a human group; the inspiration granted him by helping spirits; and the extraordinary, ecstatic experiences of the shaman.54

This general definition also applies to shamanism in the New World. Consequently, much of the content of The Magic Mirror can be explained by American Indian and Eskimo shamanism, but not all of it. Among the indigenous peoples of the Western Hemisphere, those in western North America and the Arctic were the most recent to migrate from Siberia some 9,000 to 8,000 years ago, which accounts for the many cultural similarities between tribes of these regions.

Pollock's subject, a female shaman, was commonplace in Finno-Ugrian, Siberian, and North American tribal cultures. Johann Gmelin, an eighteenth-century German explorer, encountered a female shaman among the Tungus in Siberia who was considered superior to any man, and as Czaplicka noted, female shamans in the early twentieth century continued to be more important than their male counterparts in some Siberian tribes.55 The prominence of female shamans can be traced to the legends of several tribes in which "the shaman's gift was first bestowed on woman."56 The Buryat believe the first shaman was an eagle, but when his mission was not understood, the great bird conferred his powers on a woman, the first human shaman.57 In 1938 the American ethnologist Willard Park listed over fifty tribes west of the Rocky Mountains in which women answered the shaman's calling.58 The percentage of female shamans varied in these tribes, but in a number of cases they predominated. Pollock did not need to go far to find a source for his female shamans.

The eagle, "the bird of the shamans" as Mihály Hoppál calls it, is the most important spirit animal for a number of Siberian tribes.59 As the shaman's principal helper, the spirit animal conducts the shaman's soul to distant worlds to summon spirits, t hostile ones, retrieve the souls of the sick, and settle the souls of the recently deceased. Ostiak shamans and those of the Yakut in eastern Siberia, for example, assume the attributes of the eagle by wearing feathered headdresses and cloaks, which give them the power of flight while they are in a state of ecstasy.60 Eagles, likewise, are the sacred bird of many North American Indian tribes. As a study of the Cherokee states in a volume of the BAE Report Pollock owned, "The eagle (awâ hǐlǐ) is the great sacred bird of the Cherokee, as of nearly all our native tribes. . . ."61 Park reported that the use of eagle feathers for "curing and other shamanistic performances. . . . [is] almost universal among the tribes of western North America."62 Given the importance of eagle paraphernalia and that of other high-flying birds of prey in shamanic rituals, a fact that could not have escaped Pollock, it can be deduced that the airborne form in The Magic Mirror—that is, the flying phallus—has a spiritual significance. The significance, I argue, pertains to the phenomenon of soul flight.

The shamanic trance that induces soul flight is ubiquitous in Eurasian tribal cultures. This experience also is prevalent in western North America, especially the Northwest, and among the Eskimo of Greenland and the Bering Strait.63 Graham reproduced an Eskimo mask (Inuit) in "Primitive Art and Picasso," and Landau and Rushing have discussed the utilization of Eskimo masks in Pollock's work of the early 1940s.64 Pollock owned two volumes of the BAE Report in which he would have read about the soul flights of Eskimo shamans. In his study of the Eskimo of the Hudson Bay region, Labrador, and Greenland, Franz Boas observed that most of the angakut [shamans] . . . fall into an ecstasy and really imagine they accomplish the flights and see the spirits."65 Similarly, the author of the study on the Eskimo around the Bering Strait reported that "the shamans claim to make journeys to the land of the dead, and upon their return relate to the people what they have seen in that region . . . the shades of dead people or animals which are invisible to persons not specially endowed."66 The concept of soul flight partially explains the feathered form in The Magic Mirror, but there is also "the whole shamanistic dream culture of Indians" of which Fritz Bultman spoke.

The study of the Bering Strait Eskimo recounts the experience of a man who upon having strange dreams believed invisible powers had called him to the vocation of shaman.67 This phenomenon was usual among American Indian shamans. According to Park, "The conferring of shamanistic power in unsought dreams seems to be general in the Great Basin [west of the Rockies]."68 Pollock's awareness of the acquisition of shamanic power in dreams, including the ability to fly, can be connected with the discussion of flying dreams in Freud's The Interpretation of Dreams, the classic work that spawned unending amateur dream interpretation and psychologizing. It has been observed that "A cultural historical survey of the reception of Freud's language of dreams after 1900 would include . . . the weird, wild and sometimes wonderful expropriations of ideas that were made—myriad loose borrowings from the school of Freud as well as that of Jung, through cartoons, poems, films, essays, witticisms and anecdotes, and through the general babble of conversation about psychology. . . ."69 The embodiment of shamanic soul flight in a flying phallus is elucidated by Freud's acknowledgment of a colleague in Vienna who

has propounded the fascinating theory that a great many flying dreams are erection dreams, since the remarkable phenomenon of erection, which constantly occupies the human phantasy, cannot fail to be impressive as an apparent suspension of the laws of gravity (cf. the winged phalli of the ancients).

Freud then follows with the viewpoint of another researcher, who, despite his aversion to interpretation,

nevertheless defends the erotic element as "the most important motive factor of the hovering dream," and refers to the strong sense of bodily vibration which accompanies this type of dream, and the frequent connection of such dreams with erections and emissions.70

Elsewhere Freud insisted that women's flying dreams also are associated with sexual arousal.71 If the shaman is a symbolic self-portrait then the phallus denotes the re's actual gender identity, as well as Pollock's psychological and spiritual journey. If the re is female, the phallus represents the shaman's animus—the male component of the female psyche—her powerful "other side."72 Regardless, the winged phallus as a symbol of power can represent the gravity-defying shaman of either gender. In the final analysis, the specific gender identity of the shaman counts for less than the Jungian notion that the merger of opposites in the psyche produces psychological harmony.

All of which leads us to real-life gender merging with which Pollock was familiar. In a conversation with Tony Smith, Pollock described the vision-dream of an Indian man called to the vocation of shaman in which a devil woman appeared, "a woman of two natures."73 The transvestite shaman, part male and part female, who seeks to restore the afflicted to a harmonious and natural balance in the world, would have had enormous appeal to Pollock's Jungian self. Such persons existed in Siberia and North America, and in some tribes were an established institution. A primary example of this practice was described by the Russian scholar Vladimir Bogoraz, known in the West as Waldemar Bogoras, in his monumental study of the Reindeer and Maritime Chukchi in northeastern Siberia. Bogoras met a number of "transformed" male shamans, and had reliable reports about a few of their female counterparts. Transformation begins in youth when the spirits command an individual to accept the role of shaman. The degree of transformation ranges from wearing the hair like the opposite gender, to dressing the part, and the final stage in which the shaman takes a spouse of the same gender. Depending on the new sexual identity, the transformed shaman either assumed a passive or assertive position in the marriage.74 On account of their considerable powers, transformed shamans inspired even more fear, if crossed, than ordinary shamans.75 Shamanic transvestism can help explain the flying phallus above the female re in The Magic Mirror—that is, the juxtaposition signals the shaman's dual nature—but there is another intriguing possibility. The "soft man," as the Chukchi called the transformed male shaman, was said to have a special protector, a supernatural husband who communicated his orders through his "wife."76 The flying phallus would seem an apposite image to represent the transformed shaman's supernatural husband. Czaplicka, who extensively summarized Bogoras, remarked, "It is scarcely possible to see in these cases a religious conception of a divine two-sexed shaman embodying in one being a perfect man- and woman-nature,"77 but Graham and Pollock would have seen it that way.

Park claimed that the berdache (a Persian word brought to the New World by the French to describe the Indian homosexual), who he equated with transvestism, rarely performed as a shaman in the tribes of western North America. He identified transvestite shamans only among the Klamath of Oregon and the Yurok of northern California,78 even though previous researchers reported the phenomenon was more widespread. In his research on the Mohave Indians in Arizona and southern California, George Devereaux learned from an elderly shaman that "From the very beginning of the world it was meant that there should be homosexuals, just as it was instituted that there should be shamans. They were intended for that purpose."79 All Mohave shamans were not homosexuals, but those who were lived as transvestites and assumed as much as possible the roles of their biological opposites. Devereaux observed that "Transvestites were exceptionally powerful shamans, especially the hwame," the female practitioners.80 Among their healing specialties, both alyha (male transvestites) and hwame were particularly adept at curing venereal diseases.81 Elsewhere in the Southwest, the Navajos accorded berdaches great respect. They assumed the domestic responsibilities of women, frequently dressed like them, and took spouses of the same sex.82 According to Willard Hill, these nadle often excelled as singers, the shamans who preside over healing ceremonies in which they perform specialized songs or chants.83 In his study of Navajo games, Berard Haile tells of a nadle whose name in translation was "stick bounder"—a reference to a game only played by women—who was highly esteemed because he was "a well-versed Blessing way singer and much in demand."84 Pollock may not have read the academic studies of Devereaux, Hill, and Haile, but knowledge of dual-natured shamans was not confined to academic journals.85 Frazer's The Golden Bough is another source of information on this phenomenon. He identifies a number of indigenous peoples, including "many Indian tribes of North America," whose male sorcerers, priests, and conjurors dress and live as women, but, in fact, he only discusses the "effeminate" healers of the Sauk and Omaha tribes.86

One aspect of shamanic ritual central to The Magic Mirror, the use of magic mirrors, is undocumented in North America above the Rio Grande. There are two plausible explanations for Pollock's pairing of shaman and mirror, and both implicate Graham. The first involves the Aztec deity Tezcatlipoca, the god of the North, night, the wind, the underworld, and numerous other domains. Unmentioned in the Pollock literature, he is variously described in Aztec myth as the rival or twin of the god Quetzalcoatl, whose symbol is the plumed serpent. The plumed serpent motif appears in Pollock's work around 1940, and is discussed at length by Langhorne.87 In representations of Tezcatlipoca he most often is shown with a mirror replacing one foot, but also with a mirror worn on his chest or head.88 Graham evinced great interest in Pre-Columbian art, and made numerous references to it in his 1937 book Systems and Dialectics of Art.89 In the early 1940s he could claim that "I am an expert on Prehistoric, African, Oceanic, Mexican-Precolumbian [sic], and North American Indian, as well as Modern and Antique art in general."90 Tezcatlipoca, which means "Smoking Mirror," could see everything that took place on earth, in heaven, and hell in a highly polished obsidian mirror.91 He was the patron deity of sorcerers (variously called magicians, witches and wizards in the early twentieth-century literature) who practiced divination by means of obsidian mirrors, a method that was widespread in ancient Mexico.92 Graham's conjunction of interests in magic mirrors and Pre-Columbian art surely was familiar to his protégé, with whom he spent many hours discussing such things.

The round, brass mirrors of the Siberian shamans, called toli, offered Pollock another example of magic mirrors. They have been traced to myths about a luminous people who existed before there was a sun and moon, but when they fell from grace the world became dark. According to Holmberg, the Altaic peoples believed that God's spirit emissary created the sun and moon by placing two metal mirrors in the sky.93 He observes that "It is commonly supposed that everything that takes place on the earth is reflected in the sun and moon and from these again in the magic mirrors of the shamans."94 Holmberg reproduces a photograph of a shaman's dwelling taken in an Altai district in 1909 ( 5), in which, in addition to the drum, Hoppál has identified "the shaman's cloak or gown on the wall of the yurt, with metal ornaments, mirrors (toli), and embossed work on it clearly distinguishable."95 (It is worth noting here that the garment the re wears in The Magic Mirror is defined by a series of wavy lines indicating the folds of a cloak or gown, thereby suggesting some sort of ritualistic apparel.) S.M. Shirokogoroff, writing in the 1930s, argued for the "intimate connexion" between the Buddhist use of brass mirrors in China and Tibet and the toli of the shamans.96 The diffusion of these mirrors in Siberia, not until long after the latest migrations from Asia, accounts for their absence in North America.

Shirokogoroff provides considerably more information on magic mirrors than Czaplicka, who only notes in passing that "a metal looking-glass with representations of twelve animals on it" was hung around the neck or sewn on the Buryat shaman's coat.97 Shirokogoroff informs us that these animals, representing units of time, appear on the unpolished side of the mirror. When the shaman looks at the polished side, it helps him enter a trance, a state in which "he can see everything."98 Seeing everything includes seeing into the future, an essential function of shamanism.99 In groups in which mirrors were customary, they were indispensable. The shaman, if necessary, could operate without costume, but there could be no shamanizing without the mirror.100 The Siberian shaman wore the magic mirror, whereas Pollock's shaman, seated left of center in his painting, looks up to a mirror on the right, a compositional arrangement that acknowledges its origins in Picasso's Girl Before a Mirror. Nevertheless, this mirror, as Pollock's title indicates, is indispensable for the divinatory and shamanic meanings of the painting. As we have seen, the association of mirrors with divination and the capture of souls has existed throughout history in contexts that were not properly shamanistic, but the flying phallus secures the shamanic identity of the re looking in the mirror. Insofar as magic mirrors were unknown to the north of ancient Mexico, John Graham's knowledge of Pre-Columbian art and Siberian shamanism must be accounted for in Pollock's conception.

Pollock's visual references to shamanism, which begin in the late 1930s, can be seen as signs of his intense need to find a way out of alcoholism and depression. The shaman's particular kind of healing offered Pollock a real-life example of psychic integration, as it did for Jung in references scattered throughout his writings. Landau describes Pollock's painting, Naked Man, ca. 1938–41, a full-length, nude re with mask-like features surrounded by a fiery, swirling disk, as his "most visibly intelligible presentation of the tribal artist/healer. . . ."101 She also reads two drawings of seated res dating to the early 1940s as a shaman in successive stages of spiritual transformation,102 the second of which depicts a winged, levitating re apparently in a state of ecstatic soul flight. A year or two later Pollock painted Guardians of the Secret, 1943, in which two totemic res flank an elongated, horizontal rectangle ( 6). Rushing has convincingly demonstrated that this work derives in part from a BAE Report illustration of a pair of Zia Pueblo secret-society guardians protecting a painting made for curative purposes.103 Scholarly consensus has it that the rectangle filled with indecipherable markings is a metaphor of the unconscious, but what physical object might this entity represent? With its uneven flashes of white pigment and suggestions of deeper blue spaces it resembles nothing so much as the reflective surface of a mirror. Confrontation with the self in a mirror is, as Jung remarked, "a test sufficient to frighten off most people." We protect ourselves by means of the mind's repressive mechanisms—Pollock's guardian res—because as the Siberian shamans undoubtedly knew, to "see everything" is painful.
