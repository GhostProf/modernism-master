Taking Dictation: Collage Poetics, Pathology, and Politics

Lawrence Rainey

res

The "Technical Manifesto of Futurist Literature," a brief work written and published by Filippo Tommaso Marinetti in May of 1912, has acquired extraordinary status among critics of recent years, increasingly considered one of the pivotal documents in the history of twentieth-century aesthetics. Issued three years after Marinetti first launched the movement of Futurism in 1909, it announces, by any standards, a radical departure from previously accepted norms of literary discourse. While proclaiming eleven rules that are to govern the new mode of Futurist writing, the manifesto enjoins the elimination of adjectives, the abolition of adverbs, and the suppression of all punctuation. It stipulates that verbs be used only in the infinitive form, a practice that would effectively sever logical connections between subject and predicate (e.g., "he to fly"). It insists that the new writing consist primarily of "images or analogies"; that the "analogies or comparisons be composed of terms as divergent as possible"; that all connecting conjunctions such as like, similar to, and so on be abolished; and even that the first term of any comparison be suppressed in favor of a continuous image-flow, a language composed of pure metaphors. Finally, it urges that onomatopoeia acquire an unprecedented prominence, and it advocates a dynamic typography modeled on the most recent graphic practices. The writer, Marinetti summarizes, should "destroy syntax and scatter one's nouns at random as they are born." 1 The result will be "words-in-freedom" (le parole in libertà), a new expressive language that will finally be commensurate with what Marinetti terms the "wireless" or "radio imagination" of advancing modernity, an idiom synchronized to the cutting-edge technologies of the day--the telephones, radios, phonographs, airplanes, cinemas, and mass-circulation newspapers which are inundating the new century.

Daring and deliberately provocative, the program of the "Technical Manifesto," for one critic, "stands behind or anticipates virtually ever ism" of the first half of the twentieth century. 2 It outlines, one art historian has stated, nothing less than "a new concept of the . . . work of art as an ideal synthesis . . . in which the practice of collage play[s] a dominant role." 3 More simply, another scholar writes, it announces, "although Marinetti never uses the word, collage" (FM, 58). Here, in short, is the birth-scene of aesthetic modernism, the initial formulation of principles widely held to stand at the foundation of twentieth-century experimentalism. 4 Moreover, the "Technical Manifesto" happens to be published at exactly the moment when Picasso is creating the famous Still Life with Chair-Caning (May 1912), and several months prior to the making of Braque's first papier collé that September, Fruitdish and Glass, the two works traditionally thought to point ahead to the great achievements of collage in the later cubism to follow. 5 As prescient as it is provocative, the "Technical Manifesto" is a path-breaking work that suggests voyages of artistic discovery whose endpoints have not yet been reached.

Although critics have rarely agreed upon a precise definition of collage, a rough consensus has nevertheless emerged about some of its essential traits in both literature and the visual arts. In textual collage, David Antin tells us, the "logical relations between word groups" give way to relations of equivalence, similarity, and identity, just as in visual collage a single coherent picture gives way to relations of juxtaposition and difference. 6 In both, writes Marjorie Perloff, "hierarchy gives way to parataxis" and "there is no longer a central ordering system" (FM, 75). Collage, Rosalind Krauss urges, enjoins "a systematic exploration of the conditions of representability entailed by the sign," a procedure in which presence is replaced by discourse, a "discourse founded on a buried origin." 7 Such remarks implicitly commend collage as an oppositional practice, a process of "systematic exploration" and "radical questioning" (FM, 75) which undermines received assumptions about the foundations of representation and thereby works "to break down existing economic and political structures and to transcend nationalist barriers" (FM, xviii). Yet these increasingly common observations may obscure as much as they reveal, slighting other dimensions of collage--or at least Marinetti's version of it in the "words-in-freedom" endeavor--that cannot be easily assimilated to a straightforwardly emancipatory project, but that harbor something more conflicted and compulsive, something more disturbed and disturbing. That haunted something, I shall argue, has much to do with the way that Marinetti's project concerns the formation of new subjectivities at the intersection of technology, language, and the body--at once textual, corporal, and social--in which the writing of modernity might be transcribed. Mapping that intersection more clearly, I shall also suggest, can help us take a different look at the much discussed question of Futurism and Fascism, a politicized aesthetics and an aestheticized politics.

A cursory glance at Marinetti's prescriptions for the new "words-in-freedom" suffices to suggest how much his project is indebted to the practice of automatic writing, writing which, to adopt for the moment the synoptic description of Richard Ellmann, "has many of the characteristics of dreams, being full of images, fragmentary, run together, by turns coherent and incoherent." 8 The affiliation of Marinetti's project with automatic writing is thematized in the manifesto's opening vignette, the short narrative that precedes the more famous rules and prescriptions. The vignette, we recall, depicts the narrator/speaker as he sits astride the fuel tank of a biplane which is soaring over the city of Milan. As he glances at the urban panorama below, he declares his ambition to destroy "the Latin period," a type of sentence he then characterizes with metaphors of the body: the classical period has "a head, a stomach, two legs and two flat feet, but," he adds proleptically, "it will never have two wings" (LMM, 92; TIF2, 46). 9 Quite suddenly, however, the narrative changes direction. The speaker abruptly disclaims his own authority and ascribes his statements to another source. "This is what the swirling propeller told me as I was speeding along at two hundred meters above the smokestacks of Milan. And the propeller added . . ." (LMM, 92; TIF2, 46). Then follows the manifesto proper, with its familiar list of instructions and interdictions. Yet the narrative vignette merits further attention: for while it plainly alludes to the familiar trope of the poet taking dictation from the muses, a trope which has been updated to portray the poet/aviator receiving his text from the airplane, it just as plainly rehearses what might be termed the primal scene of automatic writing, the scenario of taking dictation at a spiritualist seance, a scenario which has also been updated to give the role of "dead spirit" to the "swirling propeller" of modern technology and the role of "medium" to the speaker who vanishes in a machinic trance. Albeit briefly, the narrative vignette sketches a complex of critical metaphors that inform "The Technical Manifesto" and recur throughout Marinetti's early writings, a complex that involves mediums and automatic writing, analogies between the body and language, and, as we shall see, a metaphorics of shock, trauma, and pathology--a complex that Marinetti mobilizes in the service of an anxious engagement with questions about identity and authority, the self and technology, and the possibilities of representation under the regime of modernity.

Marinetti, of course, was not the only author to be intrigued with automatic writing at the beginning of the twentieth century. Automatic writing was studied or practiced by authors as different as Yeats, Gertrude Stein, and the Surrealists, and its appeal to writers seemingly so diverse from one another may owe much to its hybrid origins and early evolution. In early 1848 Margaret and Kate Fox of Rochester, New York, announced that they were conversing with spirits from the afterlife by a system of signs that consisted of rapping noises. To answer "no" or "yes" to their questions, a spirit would respectively rap once or twice; to respond to more complex enquiries, the spirit would allegedly rap at the appropriate moment as the sisters called out the letters of the alphabet. A few months later the Fox sisters acquired two accessories to their supernatural talents: one was a press agent and business manager named Eliab W. Capron; the other, a capacity to turn specters into public spectacles through their discovery that spirits could also relocate furniture, levitating tables and similar objects with ease. Their first series of public exhibitions (admission, 25 cents) was staged in Rochester in November, 1849. Capron, whose introductory lecture preceded the demonstrations, situated the "manifestations" at the conjuncture of two discursive traditions. One was scientific: the communications were like "'telegraphic dispatches' from the spirit land," while the new mode of communication was comparable to "the substantial discoveries and inventions" of "Galileo, Newton and Fulton." The other was the heritage of the marvelous: the new communications were "wonders," a term that evoked Biblical accounts of miraculous events and a broader tradition of interest in the marvelous. 10 His account of the exhibitions was printed two weeks later in the New York Daily Tribune, then reprinted a week later in its national edition, The New York Weekly Tribune, both edited by the famous Horace Greeley. Six weeks after that, in January 1850, Capron published a small book on the Fox sisters, and it too was favorably and prominently reviewed in both editions of the Tribune. Spiritualism's rise to national prominence and rapid diffusion were inseparable from modern communications media, so much so that the term itself, "medium," which was ultimately assigned to the role played by the Fox sisters, was typically used at the time to characterize the modern newspaper. 11 Four months later the Fox sisters set out on their first tour, stopping in Albany, Troy, and then New York City, where they became an object of ceaseless coverage by the contemporary press. 12 To our own age of copy-cat murders, the result may seem to have been predictable: "The phenomenon," one contemporary noted, "began, as it were, to propagate itself, and to be witnessed in other families." 13 Two years later witnessed the first American spiritualists, Mrs. Hayden and Mrs. Roberts, who went to England, and soon there were manifestations of the new movement reported in Germany and France, with seances held in Strasbourg, Bourges, and Paris. 14

Historians have typically viewed the rise of spiritualism as a response to the increasing secularization of Euro-American culture, while American historians, in particular, have accounted for the Fox sisters and their success by citing the earlier religious history of upstate New York, grouping it with the Mormon revelations of Joseph Smith, the region's strong strain of Shakerism, or its recent experience of Millerism. But closer focus on the city of Rochester may be more revealing. For during the period 1846 to 1848, the city's leading citizens, galvanized by the visionary dreams of their former postmaster and newspaper editor, Henry O'Reilly, were pouring their capital into a risky venture that planned to capture the westward corridors deemed vital to the fate of a novel communications technology to which they had also purchased the rights, the telegraph. In 1855, after crushing or buying out all their rivals, they would reorganize under the name of Western Union. 15 (Their counterparts on the Atlantic seabord were centered in New York and created the firm that became AT&T. "In New York. . . all the takers were newspaper proprietors. The most substantial sum apparently came from Horacy Greeley, a warm supporter of the invention from its infancy.") 16

Automatic writing, from its inception, was a mimetic counter-language, a way in which people restaged new forms of subjctivity increasingly shaped by contemporary changes in communications and transcription technologies. Appropriately, the first spiritualist newspaper would be titled The Spiritual Telegraph. The link between telegraphy and spiritualism survived even the long journey to Europe; as Allan Kardec, the major theorist of French spiritisme would write in 1857:

Mediums are simply electrical machines that transmit telegraphic dispatches from one point which is far away to another which is located on earth. Thus, when we wish to dictate a communication, we act upon [nous agissons sur] the medium just as the telegraph employee does upon his apparatus. 17

People, in short, were now being transformed into an "apparatus," a machine; while machines, for the first time, could now speak.

The technology of automatic writing underwent a rapid evolution already in the early decades of spiritualism. At first the primitive rapping procedures were supplanted by others associated with table-moving or levitation, as seance participants would attach a pencil or crayon to a table's leg, then place a sheet of paper beneath it and wait for the desired message to appear. Still a rather clumsy procedure, it too was rapidly supplanted by other practices. On the one hand, there appeared a series of machines, as if to embody in material terms the linkage between automatic writing and contemporary technology, machines which ranged from relatively simple devices ( 1) that translated table movements into the letters of the alphabet to more elaborate contrivances ( 2) that performed the same task but whose design more fully bespoke the contemporary fascination with the marvels of engineering. Some bore a suspicious resemblance to contemporary telegraphs, such as this one ( 3) used by Western Union from 1848 through 1855. On the other hand, practical issues of transportability led to the creation of devices much simpler and more amenable to mass production, such as the planchette ( 4), which first appeared in France around 1853. It consisted of a small, triangular piece of wood (in short, a miniaturized table) that was supported by two rolling casters and a pencil, a structure that let it move under the slightest impulse from seance participants. 18 (Much later, in 1898, an ambitious businessman named William Fuld in the U.S. would design a variant of it called "OUIJA, the MYSTIFYING ORACLE," a product which was soon launched to great success and still turns up at adolescent slumber parties, where it is consulted to predict romantic and marital destinies and similarly important affairs.) 19 Most mediums, however, dispensed with the use of machines and wrote directly on paper; after all, as Kardec had said, they themselves were machines, and it was also more practical and swifter. Historians have no doubt been right in noting that spiritualism offered its followers the promise of consolation and revelation from another world, a promise that had much to do with its diffusion among a substantial public; but it may be equally true that its success had much to do not with the content of spiritualist messages, but with the tacit allure of their form, their mode of transmission, the way in which they were rendered in idioms that were derived not from an other world, but from this one--idioms that were uttered in the silent syntax of advancing capital, murmured in the rhythms of its relentless machines.

Automatic writing might have remained only a footnote in the history of spiritualism had it not been integrated into contemporary discussions about the mind and human identity, discussions that were laying the foundations for the so-called modern "dynamic" psychologies taking shape between 1875 and 1900. The medical history behind these debates presents a rich and complicated interaction between an array of competing practices and overlapping theories. While much is inevitably lost in the cursory summary that follows, four related developments were crucial. A useful starting point might be the famous case, first publicized in 1876, of Félida X, a woman who would spontaneously lapse into a cataleptic state in which she exhibited a curious array of symptoms, chiefly anesthesia or hyperesthesia and enigmatic pains that had no discernible cause. Félida was found to have two distinct and alternating personalities, a condition that was soon characterized with the term dédoublement, doubling of the personality. 20 (In more modern parlance, she is the first example of multiple personality.) As it happened, Félida was "discovered" just when there was a revival of scientific interest in a second issue, hypnosis and the nature of hypnotic states, and it was not long before it was urged that "magnetic" or "induced somnambulism" (the state of hypnosis) presented important analogies with the "natural" or "spontaneous somnambulism" into which Félida lapsed, posing similar questions about the nature of identity, memory, amnesia, and will. 21 Contemporaneous with these was a third development, the enormous wave of scientific and public fascination elicited by Charcot's famous studies of hysteria; Félida, in fact, would have been classed by her contemporaries as having hysteria, a term that was expanding to encompass ever more symptoms throughout the period 1850 to 1910. 22 And fourth, there was the notion of trauma, a term acquiring its characteristically modern sense during exactly this period. Prior to roughly 1860, trauma had always designated a strictly physical lesion, an identifiable wound; it was in the later 1860s that trauma began to take on its modern sense in connection with injury claims that stemmed from railroad accidents. 23 It soon became the term of choice to designate a cluster of symptoms that evidently indicated genuine illness yet failed to correspond with discernible injuries or physical lesions, symptoms that included amnesia and mysterious pains like those experienced by Félida. Trauma, in other words, seemed to offer suggestive analogies with multiple personality, hypnosis, and hysteria, whose puzzling etiology remained an open question. Thus, the same period also witnessed a new taxonomy of hysteria that now included the category of "traumatic hysteria," a hybrid term suggesting the multiple analogies that late-nineteenth century psychologists discerned in an array of disturbing phenomena. 24 The outcome of this vast discussion was the gradual formation of what Ian Hacking has called "a free-floating sequence of associations"--an overlapping set of descriptive habits that coalesced in a metaphorics of hysteria, shock, and trauma. 25 The thread that would link these diverse pathologies to one another would be automatism, a concept elaborated in conjunction with automatic writing.

Of all the early psychologists who worked on these questions, none was more influential than Pierre Janet. Just when researchers were seeking a paradigm in the sense made famous by Thomas Kuhn, a matrix that could gather together the multiple debates surrounding hysteria, trauma, amnesia, and doubling of the personality, Janet provided one with the notion of automatism, a descriptive concept that he developed through his clinical experience with automatic writing. In a celebrated essay that he published in 1886 ("Unconscious Actions and the Doubling of Personality During Hypnosis") Janet reported his experiments with Lucie. 26 His starting point had been an observation already noted in previous studies of hypnotism: when in a posthypnotic state, Lucie would execute commands that he had given her while hypnotized. But Janet pushed his enquiries further. He began to give Lucie tasks that required her to execute complex mathematical calculations, actions that demanded not just rote or passive obedience, but forms of intelligent reflection. When these proved successful, he instructed Lucie to write letters, a task that she also performed with ease, still without knowing that she had done so. Finally, he began to ask Lucie to answer in writing, while in her waking state, questions that had been posed to her under hypnosis, questions that concerned the traumatic events whose repressed memory was the source of her hysterical fits. When he showed a sample of her script to a colleague, he was surprised to learn that it resembled that of spiritualist mediums. Janet, in short, had discovered an experimental counterpart to the automatic writing of the spiritualists, writing governed by a mysterious and hidden self that existed independently of normal consciousness, a "second state" as Jane would term it, that was produced "by a certain doubling [dédoublement] of consciousness." The effect of this discovery was to turn automatic writing into the thread connecting multiple personality, hysteria, dissociation, somnambulism and hypnosis, shock and trauma. Three years later Janet published L'Automatisme psychologique , a work that elaborated the concept of automatism and endowed the practice of automatic writing with new importance for discussion about the nature of consciousness. 27 Devoting an entire chapter to spiritualism, Janet concluded that mediums were "simply hysterics," and "almost always" what he termed "névropathes" (suffering from a neuropathological condition) (Ap, 404). The moment when the medium was possessed represented an hysterical convulsion, and at the core of the seance was "a violent nervous crisis" ("une violent crise de nerfs," Ap, 405). The medium's ability derived from "a morbid state analogous to one that generated hysteria or insanity" (Ap, 406); and at great length he detailed "more precise analogies between the states of mediumism and hypnotism" (Ap, 406). Janet, in short, had established a resonant chain of analogies that centered on the notion of automatism, strands of repressed memory and identity formed in response to traumatic experience and functioning like machines, independent of consciousness, the self, or the ego.

This is not the time or place to detail Janet's enormous influence on his contemporaries, among others William James and Morton Prince in Boston, W. F. H. Meyer and the Society for Psychical Research in Cambridge and London, or the early Freud in Vienna, who respectfully cites Janet seven times in his Studies on Hysteria . Janet's prestige remained undiminished until well into the mid-1920s in France and Italy, where Freudian psychoanalysis was initially greeted with significant skepticism, some of it sparked by Janet himself. 28 Likewise, one can only allude to Marinetti's extensive engagement with French culture--his education at a French collège in Alexandria, his baccalauréat in Paris, his residence in the city (alternating with spells in Milan) from 1895 to 1909--and the likelihood that he had read Janet's most important and widely publicized work. 29 Yet the particulars of Marinetti's engagement with Janet's work may matter less than a broader phenomenon, his sharing the implicit notions formed by a cultural metaphorics that articulated urgent themes of scientific and educated public culture. Marinetti's ambivalent achievement was to take that cultural metaphorics and turn it into the foundation of an aesthetics of shock and trauma, an achievement that he transmitted to the entire historical avant-garde. 30 Doing so, Marinetti constructs an imaginary and discursive space in which Futurism restages the critical themes of automatic writing and the problematics of modern subjectivity. It is not an accident, in other words, that the famous "Foundation and Manifesto of Futurism" begins with a gripping narrative vignette that recounts the domestic version of technological mishap, an accident, the overturning of a car which proves to be the traumatic birth-scene of the Futurist aesthetic. 31 Nor is it an accident that the sequel to that incident, in the long run, turns out to be the repetitive publication of manifesto after manifesto which announces yet another form of Futurist novelty, a mode of production that might almost have been designed to exemplify Freud's famous thesis in Beyond the Pleasure Principle, his attempt to reconsider the notion of trauma in the wake of World War I, that the chief characteristic of traumatic neurosis is a type of repetition compulsion, one which indexes a failure of symbolization--an incapacity to render unbearable experience into the symbolic language of dreams or mentation. 32 Over and over, with hypnotic uniformity, the Futurists issue manifesto after manifesto, each charged with recreating for reader and writer the traumatic experience of shock. By a strategy of mimetic assimilation, Futurism becomes a trauma machine. Which brings us back to the "Technical Manifesto," a work in which the reproduction of shock is now codified in the minute workings of syntax.

Marinetti's intention, as he candidly avers, is to "destroy the 'I' or 'ego'" of bourgeois subjectivity (LMM, 95; Tif2, 50), and consequently much of his attention goes to the noun, the syntactical unit that occupies the position of grammatical (and logical) subject. So much so that the manifesto offers three different precepts for the noun, each displacing the previous one and further dissolving the subject. Initially the manifesto enjoins a syntactic dédoublement: "every noun must have its double, that is to say, must be followed immediately by another noun to which it is related by analogy" (LMM, 92; Tif2, 47), a codification of the steps whereby traumatic experience was thought to initiate a process of psychological dédoublement, the creation of a "second self" that exists independently of the subject or his will. Yet this initial formulation proves inadequate, insofar as it hints at a survival of the original self (the first term or noun) and still adumbrates a principle of relatedness (analogy) that gainsays the notion of dissociation. Whence it is supplanted by a second injunction to "deliberately confound the object with the image that it evokes" (LMM, 92; Tif2, 47), a precept that more effectively dissolves the boundary between the primary noun/self and the secondary one. Still, this injunction also hints at a residual survival of the original term, however attenuated, and therefore it too is supplanted by a more astringent imperative, "to suppress all the first terms of our analogies and render no more than an uninterrupted sequence of second terms" (LMM, 97; Tif2, 53). Now the original noun/self is wholly effaced by the creation of a second self, a haunting specter that uniformly signifies the absence of its original referent, its self.

Though much briefer, the propeller's treatment of the verb turns around the same thematics of self and syntax. "The verb should be used in the infinitive form, because the infinitive adapts itself elastically to the noun and doesn't subject it to the ego of the writer who observes or imagines" (LMM, 92; Tif2, 47). Here again the object of suspicion is reflective consciousness and the distance it marks between the subject and reality, whether external (observed) or internal (imagined). The infinitive, in this account, is transformed into a variant of speed and velocity, that quintessential Futurist re for whatever most swiftly traverses the gap between reality and the self--or more accurately, that new and dissociated self which has been engendered by traumatic experience.

Perhaps the most notorious injunctions of the "Manifesto" concern the use of onomatopoeia, a practice more fully defended in a contemporary work by Marinetti, "Geometric and Mechanical Splendor and Numerical Sensibility," where he thematizes the link between onomatopoeia and violence:

Our growing love for matter, our will to penetrate it and know its vibrations, the physical sympathy that links us to motors, push us to the use of onomatopoeia. Noise, after all, is the result of solids, liquids, or gases that are in rapid motion and are either in friction with one another or crashing against each other. Onomatopoeia, which reproduces noise, is necessarily one of the most dynamic elements of poetry. As such it can substitute for the verb in the infinitive form, especially when one onomatopoeia is opposed to two or more others. [LMM, 109; Tif2, 105]

Onomatopoeia, in this view, recapitulates the primal syntax of shock, epitomizing and restaging the violent conflicts held to underlie all of matter, a universe of atoms whose continuous collisions are enlisted to lend "natural" sanction to the mechanical jolts of modern technology. Onomatopoeia, it turns out, can also substitute for the verb in the infinitive, for both are the syntactic counterparts of technological violence that is now projected as an attribute of nature, because both epitomize a common refusal of the human and a regression to the state of inorganic matter itself.

Nowhere is the premise that underlies the program of Marinetti more clearly delineated than in a passage from the manifesto "Destruction of Syntax--Wireless Imagination--Words in Freedom," in which Marinetti illustrates how the "words-in-freedom" are meant to function:

Now imagine that a friend of yours, gifted with lyrical ability of this kind, should find himself in a zone of intense life (revolution, war, shipwreck, earthquake, etc.) and should come, immediately afterwards, to recount his impressions. Do you know what your lyrical friend will instinctively do while he is still shocked (commosso)?

He will begin by brutally destroying the syntax of his speech. He wastes no time in constructing periodic sentences. He could care less about punctuation or finding the right adjective. He disdains subtleties and shadings, and in haste he will assault your nerves with visual, auditory, olfactory sensations, just as their insistent pressure in him demands. The rush of steam-emotion will burst the steampipe of the sentence, the valves of punctuation, and the regular clamp of the adjective. Fistfuls of basic words without any conventional order. 33

Here is a perfect depiction of the trauma victim who is compelled by overwhelming events--epitomized here by technological accidents and warfare--to repeat the auratic experience of violence; who, because he cannot integrate his experience into the temporal orders of narrative and memory, is subject to a form of repetition compulsion that obliges him to assault his auditor with the violent sensations of the traumatic occasion, sensations endowed with talismanic significance through their sheer resistance to ordered structures of meaning. Images will now be scattered with a "maximum of disorder" ("TM," Tif2, 50), 34 gathered into what Marinetti calls "a net of images" (LMM, 94; Tif2, 49) in which each acquires an equal space that is free of a central ordering system--a counterpart of the dissociated psychic nuclei that have split off from the ego--and all of them presented without punctuation, reinforcing the levelling of syntactic hierarchies.

All of which brings us back to the narrative vignette at the beginning of the "Technical Manifesto," with its metaphorical circuits connecting the "members" of the sentence with the limbs of the body. In another essay from this period, "Multiplied Man and the Reign of the Machine" , Marinetti sketches his vision of the new body to be engendered by modernity. Man, he urges, will soon "externalize his will and make it into a huge invisible arm . . . he will be endowed with surprising organs" such as "a prow" that will emerge directly from the breastbone and be "the more marked the better an aviator the man of the future becomes." Indeed, Marinetti declares, "We believe in the possibility of an incalculable number of human transformations, and without a smile we declare that wings are asleep in the flesh of man." To explicate these images, Marinetti turns to the world of automatic writing and spiritualism: "You can easily understand these seemingly paradoxical hypotheses by studying the phenomena of externalized will that continually reveal themselves at spiritualist seances" (LMM, 99; Tif2, 299-300). Marinetti here is referring to a phenomenon that is largely forgotten today, but was then receiving significant media attention. From the bodies of some mediums, a strange foamy or filmy substance dubbed ectoplasm ( 5) would seem to condense and form mysterious limbs: oddly shaped arms, twisted hands, or distorted heads. The most commonly reported and widely discussed phenomena were two: hands that would emerge from the medium's chest or bosom (. 6, 7), and a "second head" that would appear just above or behind the medium's own ( 8). Produced by the same hysterical mediums [Begin Page 136] once studied by Janet, they are the somatic counterpart of automatic writing, a histrionic language of the body and a prototype for the new limbs of "multiplied man," the Futurist who will at last possess "organs adapted to a world of ceaseless shocks" (LMM, 99; Tif2, 299); and in turn they are the model for the members of the new syntactic corpus that Marinetti proposes at the outset of the "Technical Manifesto," new limbs that will finally be expressive of "all the brutal sounds, all the expressive screams of the violent life that surrounds us" (LMM, 97; Tif2, 53). It is this hysterical and putatively female body-language that serves as paradigm and metaphor for the program of "words-in-freedom"--an automatic writing of shock in which the normalized, expressive subject of liberal humanism is replaced with a collective writing of dissociated selves.

This image of textual production, to be sure, is not without elements of paradox. When Marinetti praises the medium who generates automatic writing and ectoplasmic limbs, he does so by urging that the new organs exhibit the force of her "externalized will"--a paradox, of course, because the one trait that supposedly distinguished automatic writing was the lack of volition involved in its production, the absence of will. Elsewhere, too, Marinetti vacillates on the question of will and its relation to the new writing of Futurism. 35 In a subsequent defense of the "Technical Manifesto" he describes his own experience when writing "words-in-freedom," a passage in which the motifs of the dissevered hand and floating head are mingled with the question of volition:

It is impossible to identify exactly the moment in which unconscious inspiration comes to an end and lucid will begins . . . The hand that writes is seemingly dissevered from the body and its activity is prolonged in freedom from the brain, a brain that itself has become detached from the body in some way and become airborne, observing from on high, with frightening lucidity, the unforeseen sentences that issue from the pen.

Does this domineering brain passively contemplate, or does it actually control, the leaps of imagination that excite the hand? It is impossible to know. In such moments, I have observed, from a physiological standpoint, nothing more than a great void in the stomach. 36

Such a passage holds at bay a set of insoluble tensions which recur throughout Marinetti's writings, tensions between a state that simultaneously entails passive enthrallment and a sense of narcisstic omnipotence. Narcissism, of course, is an especially troubled concept in the heritage of psychoanalysis, beginning already with Freud's classic essay "On Narcissism" , which postulates a first and primary stage in human existence, within the womb and immediately following birth, a stage in which the psychological distinction between subject and object has not yet crystallized, in which all needs are gratified (and hence there are no desires, which are generated by a lack of gratification)--in short, a condition of pure pleasure, free of all conflict. 37 Postulating such a state, however, poses immense difficulties for the basic foundations of classical drive or instinct theory as previously constructed by Freud, grounded in the irreducibly conflictual dynamics of sexual and ego instincts, pleasure principle and reality principle. Freud's response to that difficulty was to postulate the existence of narcissistic libido, which would somehow take the narcissistic ego itself as its object. But this solution may have been no solution at all: it failed to specify how desire could arise in a state without want, or how it could become attached to an object (narcissistic ego) during a stage ostensibly characterized by a lack of subject/object differentiation, or how the individual would ever evolve from this monistic state of unity to the instinctual dualism otherwise presupposed by Freud. "Narcisstic libido," in other words, displaced but did not resolve the questions posed by the postulate of primary narcissism. Those questions reappear several years later in Freud's Beyond the Pleasure Principle. There, as Daniel Greenberg has argued, they are once again displaced by transforming narcissistic libido into an instance of a new and mysterious drive which Freud postulates for the first time, the death instinct, an innate impulse seeking to restore an earlier state of the unity of all things, specifically, "the unity of the inorganic" prior to the creation of life, or the organic. Freud, in effect, "projects the 'unity' of primary narcissism backwards in time, to the beginning of 'the organic,'" and in doing so he transforms it into a biological problem, the manifestation of a cosmic force inherent in every living thing, rather than a psychological one, and hence he effectively makes "the question of the specifically human implications of this 'unity' disappear from view." 38 More important, from my viewpoint, Freud does so in what is ostensibly an attempt to account for repetition, and in particular the kind of repetition compulsion that arose in response to shell shock, or traumatic neurosis, the encounter with technological violence. We need not, in other words, endorse Freud's untenable hypothesis of a death instinct, nor attempt to resolve the many dilemmas encapsulated in his notions of primary narcissism and narcissistic libido, so much as to recognize how those overlap with the kinds of mental states articulated in Marinetti's meditations on writing, technology, and the self, states in which a regressive urge to reestablish an earlier state of unity manifests itself by a dual impulse to merge and unite with the world of objects (the life instinct, or sexuality, in Freudian terms) and a concommitant desire to eliminate the self altogether, to vanish amid the dissevered ego nuclei that are themselves the outcome of technological violence. On the one hand, that impulse may owe much to our abiding ambivalence about modern communications and transcription technologies, which simultaneously promise narcissistic enhancements of the self and yet achieve them by processes that seem to efface individual agency. On the other hand, it also suggests a dynamics of identification and misidentification with the object world of technology, dynamics which have been probingly explored, admittedly in less social and more strictly psychological terms, in Lacan's account of the mirror stage, itself an outgrowth of his meditations on Freud's essay "On Narcissism." 39 But how might those dynamics be restaged, or reenacted, on a wider social scale?

The Great War produced a revival of public interest in spiritualism in every major country of Europe. The appalling quantity of dead combatants left behind a vast population of mourners seeking consolation and comfort, the assurance of continuity in the face of irremediable loss. The situation was no different in Italy, where the number of casualties had been especially high and certain features of Catholic tradition, with its cult of saints as intercessors and divine patrons, easily converged with the basic practices and motifs of spiritualism. Public disappointment with the meager gains achieved after such sacrifice crystallized in the myth of the "vittoria mutilata" which won such wide credence (i.e., the belief that Britain, France, and the U.S. had betrayed the territorial promises made in 1915 to Italy in order to secure its entrance into the war) and exacerbated an understandable need to find meaning and significance in the otherwise senseless slaughter.

Mussolini was already a celebrity by the time of the Great War's end, a re whose journalism was remarkably popular, and whose public identity had been solidified by his highly publicized break with the Socialist Party over the issue of interventionism and the serial publication of his Diary during the war years. With the meteoric rise of the Fascist Party in the course of 1920 and 1921, he acquired a status that can only be defined as stardom--he was often described at this time as the stellone, the great star--, status which is distinguished by the visual representation of personality. 40 Some of the earliest representations of Mussolini appear in postcards, a genre that is especially revealing, insofar as they were neither authorized nor produced by the Party (or later, the government), but were typically produced by smaller and more local entrepreneurs who appealed to genuinely popular markets. To that extent they offer a rare and important glimpse into popular representations and understandings of Mussolini. 41

Already in 1921, Mussolini's head, dissevered from his body and floating like a nocturnal apparition, appears in two postcards (. 9, 10) that enjoyed enormous popularity, harbingers of much to come. In 1922, shortly after Mussolini's seizure of power, another postcard designed to commemorate the "March on Rome" further elaborates these motifs ( 11). Mussolini's head is now a celestial apparition and serves as the composition's focal point. The long lines of marching citizens, which all converge toward the ghostly head, are ostensibly composed of party members, but their military helmets also transform them into reincarnations of the soldiers killed in the war. Mussolini is a tutelary deity who has redeemed their sacrifice, the spirit of those who have died, and, simultaneously, an ectoplasmic projection of whose who are alive. Balancing the head is a solitary hand raised in Fascist salute, which, while plainly connected to a young re in the foreground, might almost be a projection from the body politic of the anonymous files marching below. Images depicting Mussolini as a spirit who redeems the sacrifice of the trenches will recur throughout the life of the regime.

The regime itself also made use of such imagery, especially in the 1930s, images which forcefully connect the private body of trauma with the traumatized subject of the body politic. Consider, for example, Xanti Schawinsky's vintage poster entitled, 1934--XII, a collage designed for the 1934 referendum ( 12). A mass of individuals has been rendered into an abstract grid, on top of which il Duce's head is superimposed. "The undifferentiated masses function as the symbol of the breakdown in traditional markers of identity, such as class," Jeffrey Schnapp has astutely noted, while the superimposed head of state symbolizes the establishment of "a distinctively modern form of identity" and individuality established in the politics of illusory immediacy. "The communion of revolutionary masses and the head of state is wholly mediated by new image and print technologies," mediation that is emphasized by the prominence granted to the white screening dots that make up Mussolini's head, which in turn are mirrored--and in a Lacanian sense--by the white faces of the heads that make up his body. 42 The head of state becomes a spectral and specular projection of collective desire, one which is, literally and ratively, mass produced. Schawinsky's constructivist montage, however stylized, furnishes a depiction of a scene that was symbolically and literally reenacted at party rallies throughout the nation, as seen, for example, in this photo ( 13) of a mass rally in a town piazza, where a photo banner of Mussolini's head drapes the wall of a building and floats above the masses. The vast rally has been transformed into a collective seance in which the body politic is haunted by its "hidden self," its ghostly double.

Another contemporary photograph ( 14) records a party induction ritual in the famous "Casa del Fascio" in Como, transforming it into a species of living collage as the image triangulates three distinct elements: first, there is the line that is formed by the heads of the Party's new recruits, which leads upwards and right to culminate in the second element, a stylized head of Mussolini depicted on transparent glass above them, a head that now serves as their spectral emanation and looks down toward the third element, the marble monolith which symbolizes the regime. Its transparent text, written by piercing the stone, echoes the transparent glass depicting Mussolini's head, and it consists of three nouns, "Order Justice Authority," each a double of the other, with the whole creating what Marinetti might have called "an uninterrupted sequence of second terms." As urged in the "Technical Manifesto," the nouns are not connected by syntax or punctuation. Any identifiable grammatical subject has now vanished: now the only subject that speaks these disconnected terms is the state, which has become, in the words of one Fascist theorist, "a mystical subject that transcends all."

In another contemporary work, a bas-relief ( 15), we see a dissevered hand that is poised in Fascist salute, one attached to no body, an ectoplasmic projection of the totalitarian state, which in turn is counterbalanced by the geometricized fasces that serve as its spectral double. To the left is a list of three verbs in the infinitive which are otherwise unconnected by syntax or punctuation, a perfect embodiment of the precepts outlined in the "Technical Manifesto": TO BELIEVE TO OBEY TO T. Yet as speakers of Italian will know, the verb in the infinitive can also be used, as it is here, with the value of an imperative that is directed to everyone in general (i.e., BELIEVE OBEY T). It serves as a "mass imperative," so to speak, a grammatical construction which is conspicuous because it doesn't specify the subject whom it addresses. In effect, the individual subject has disappeared within the amorphous mass body of what was called "the corporative state." This construction, it should be added, is a specifically modern usage, one without precedent before the late nineteenth century, and one that comes into prominence under the Fascist regime. 43

The new syntax of the regime could also be transformed into new forms of writing, a somatic script written directly onto the body politic, as seen here ( 16) in a postcard from 1931 designed by Giulio Parisio to commemorate Mussolini's visit to Naples that year. Just as the ectoplasmic head that floats behind the above the medium can be converted into automatic writing, so here the head of state--and Mussolini's official title was "capo," or "head" in Italian--is transformed through the medium of technology into a rewriting of the corporative masses.

The ubiquitous head of the head of state floats everywhere above the masses in thousands of contemporary postcards and photographs, and it is not an anachronistic imposition or a fanciful collocation to connect these images with spiritualist mysticism. In early 1930, as part of its attempt to create a new elite that would restore and refresh the party, the regime established the "School of Fascist Mysticism," an entity located in Milan which operated under the direct patronage of Mussolini's brother, Arnoldo. Intended as a training ground for future publicists and propagandists, the School sponsored classes and lectures, a monthly magazine, biweekly radio programs, and occasional conferences (among the latter, one by Marinetti, 13 April 1939, on "The Revolutionary Function of Art"). Its most distinguished student, Fernando Mezzasomma, would become Minister of Popular Culture in the late Republic of Salò. Its task was to articulate the metaphysics of "reality" defined as "infinitely multiplex, flowing, imponderable . . . in which we feel . . . vague suggestions of mysterious forces that transcend us, obscure powers that remain inexplicable to our self or ego." 44 For the individual, as another Institute member explained, "has never existed as an historical reality: the individual is merely a conventional fiction never found in the real life of man . . . the subject is exalted to the degree that stringent hierarchical and disciplinary connections annihilate in him that freedom of action, that independence so dear to individualists." 45 The state, as another theorist put it, had become "a mystical subject, a worldly archangel." 46 Or in the words of yet another member of the School, "The nation is always a mystical body . . . and because it is a mystical body, it possesses us." 47 Nor were these merely idle statements. Notoriously, the regime's most sustained and successful attempt to commemorate itself was the "Exhibition of the Fascist Revolution" held in 1932, a massive and collective work dominated by Futurist designers which proved so popular that it became a permanent museum. Arranged as a historical narrative of Fascism's triumphs, it culminated in the so-called "sacrarium of martyrs," a circular crypt that culminated in a great cupola. Above a blood-red pedestal hung a metallic cross which was "bathed in a mysterious light in the close atmosphere." As the catalogue explained:

[Here], in response to the tacit evocation of the living--in the bluish gloom of the Sacrarium, behold the Martyrs who respond with, "I am here." There are thousands and thousands of voices that echo in the glimmers that infinitely repeat the speech of those who have sacrificed their lives and who now guide implacable legions on their path. 48 [Begin Page 148]

Perhaps the most forceful example of Fascism's capacity to reinscribe the members of the body politic within new languages are found in a series of photographic postcards (. 17, 18), which depict hundreds of school children whose bodies have been arranged to form a somatic script that bespeaks the new syntax of fascism. That somatic language, of course, cannot be deciphered by the citizens who have been, quite literally, conscripted into its making; it is legible only to the eyes of the great cameraman ( 19) who has directed and photographed the scene; discernible only by "the Duce, pilot of Italy," as he is termed in this postcard of 1942 ( 20), who now flies--well, over head. Here the corps morcelé of the body politic achieves its specular identity with the airplane that flies above, identity now written in a script that can be deciphered only by the master aviator, the one who has dictated it.

Inevitably we ask ourselves: what is the "hidden self" that speaks in the mysterious dictations received by spiritualist mediums, that murmurs through the propeller of Marinetti's airplane, or that inscribes the new language of automatic writing? Or again: what are we to make of the mysterious head that floats behind the "hysterical" medium, or that hovers above the "hysterical" masses of Fascist modernism? Some critics have urged us to see the "doubled" heads and ectoplasmic limbs of spiritualist mediums as part of a protofeminist language of the body, a somatic expression of all that has been repressed by patriarchal culture, a mirror image of libidinal desire. Yet most of us viewing the ubiquitous multiplication of Mussolini's head will doubtless think of the head's traditional association with reason and control, and especially here, with notions of seeing and surveillance, notions most typically linked with patriarchal ego or superego. Perhaps we err in supposing that we must choose one view over the other. Fascism was a form of politics that called into question the basic polarities of liberal thought--it was a politics of the marvelous, modernizing and yet archaizing, revolutionary and yet conservative, merging together the public and the private, blurring the traditional opposition between desire and authority. 49 Fascism promised to unleash the individual and the masses, to restore the primacy and authority of desire; just as it also promised to instill a culture of mass discipline, a desire for authority. In doing so, it may have only articulated, in a characteristically extreme form, a paradox central to modernity itself: the way in which it promises to enhance (in part through technology) individual autonomy and to enrich human agency, versus the way in which that promise has inexorably given way to a disturbing practice of collective conformity and increasing enthrallment. That same contradiction, after all, is the central paradox informing the rhetorical structure of Marinetti's "Manifesto." It is a work that offers the reader the promise of "words-in-freedom." But that promise can be realized only if the reader submits to the discipline of its rules--only if he agrees to keep taking dictation.
