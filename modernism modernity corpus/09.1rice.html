"A Peculiar Power about Rottenness":
Annihilating Desire in James Hanley's "The German Prisoner"

Anne Rice

We are two men, two minute sparks of life; outside is the night and the circle of death. We sit on the edge of it crouching in danger, the grease drops from our hands, in our hearts we are close to one another and the hour is like the room: flecked over with the lights and shadows of our feelings cast by a quiet fire. What does he know of me or I of him? Formerly we should not have had a single thought in common--now we sit with a goose between us and feel in unison, are so intimate that we do not even speak.

--Erich Maria Remarque, All Quiet on
the Western Front 1

There is a peculiar power about rottenness, in that it feeds on itself, borrows from itself, and its tendency is always downward. That very action had seized the polluted imagination of the Irishman. He was helpless. Rottenness called to him; called to him from the pesty frame of Elston.

--James Hanley, "The German Prisoner" 2

In the classic scene of male bonding from the great novel of World War I, All Quiet on the Western Front, Paul Baumer and his comrade, Kat, prepare a meal and find themselves sharing "a more complete communion . . . than even lovers have." 3 As Jane Marcus asserts, Remarque's enormously influential novel (translated into English in 1929) changed the perception "of war fictions roughly from epic invocations of individual heroism to initiations into brotherhood." 4 Further, Samuel Hynes declares that in the late 1920s and early 1930s, "the classic war books were published" and "the Myth of the War was defined and fixed in the version that still retains authority." 5 At the center of this myth is the idea that the "experience of battle was incommunicable" to those outside the "exclusive, quasi-mystical fellowship" of men who had seen action. 6 Written primarily by gentleman officers and middle-class soldiers, the war "classics" describe British soldiers as innocent victims, sacrificed by callous old men and especially by the women they were supposed to protect. In this familiar narrative the camaraderie between soldiers overcomes regional and class divisions, providing solace amidst the madness and horror. And while such love often produced passionate, homoerotic descriptions, critics have generally followed Paul Fussell's lead in perceiving homosexual desire at the front as resembling "the 'idealistic,' passionate, but non-physical crushes which most officers had experienced at public school." 7

A little-known war story by a working-class Irish veteran challenges the canonical version of what happened between men in World War I. 8 Privately published in 1930 and out of print for decades before its reissue in 1997, James Hanley's "The German Prisoner" undermines the idea of British soldiers as innocent victims, showing rather how the war machine exploits class divisions, misogyny, homophobia, and repressed homosexual desire, inciting soldiers to the vicious slaughter of other men. "The German Prisoner" describes in graphic detail how O'Garra and Elston, two Tommies lost in "the fog of war," take mutual pleasure from beating, torturing, raping, and murdering an enemy who has surrendered to them. The narrative culminates in their destruction when a shell blows them into the muck as they grapple together in a murderous paroxysm of desire. Despite its disturbing resemblance to postwar narratives that glorify an aesthetic of violence, Hanley's story exposes and indicts rather than celebrates theculture of war through its emphasis on the release of repressed desire through atrocity.

"The German Prisoner" speaks the language of the trenches--the language of bodies at war--and provides a rare glimpse of killing's emotional charge. Challenging the myth of the war's indescribability, Fussell insists that the English language could "perfectly well render the actuality of trench warfare," offering as proof a list that includes terms and phrases such as "blood, terror, agony, madness, shit, cruelty, murder, . . . screaming all night, bleeding to death from the rectum, and the like" (GWM, 170). Censorship and propaganda, however, sought to hide the truth about the war, silencing bodies in pain for the sake of "public decency." Hanley's story comes closer than most to communicating the horror and torture that British soldiers suffered and inflicted upon others during the war--too close, in fact, for the comfort of the state. Written at a time when Europe was already uneasily contemplating its next war, "The German Prisoner" describes a war crime involving sexually aroused British soldiers who horribly mutilate and violate the helpless body of their German enemy. Citing obscenity laws, the government burned every copy of this dangerous story it could find in 1933. 9

During the war, military training and anti-German propaganda strove to make men better soldiers both by encouraging homoeroticism among the troops and, paradoxically, by projecting homophobia onto the enemy. As Eric Leed cautions, understanding the war experience requires not merely balancing "its undeniably positive and negative features," but recognizing "how both the positive and negative sides of the war are emanations of the same phenomenon." 10 Accordingly, Joanna Bourke points out that while the development of intimate male friendships made the war "less unbearable and more human . . . male bonding was not [exclusively] an inevitable, organic sentiment of the war: it was in the interests of military authorities to foster in servicemen a sense of group solidarity." 11 Indeed, the military discouraged male bonding when it interfered with the war effort. Following the legendary Christmas Day truce of 1914, during which men from both sides of the conflict created a temporary domestic space by singing carols, sharing food, and exchanging souvenirs, "stringent regulations were immediately issued to prevent it from happening again, and the whole system of military justice was brought into play." 12 The proper lesson to learn from Christmas was not universal brotherhood, the military urged, but "redemption through suffering," which would "make men hard" (ibid.).

Even as soldiers were encouraged to make themselves hard, they simultaneously learned to mark the enemy as feminine and sexually deviant. Recent feminist scholarship perceives war as a "gendering activity that ritually marks the gender" of all its participants, whether combatant or civilian. 13 During World War I, the British gender system extended the binary opposition of masculine/feminine to battlefront/homefront and soldier/enemy as part of an overall pattern of control. The reality of trench warfare--where men cowered helplessly below the earth at the mercy of shells exploding above them--provoked a crisis of masculinity evident not just in "a generalized sexual anxiety" but also, as Sandra Gilbert observes, in "a sexual anger directed specifically against" the feminine. 14 Demonization of the enemy operated as a vital mechanism for the war's continuance: killing without remorse and maintaining aggression in an essentially defensive war required combatants to un-recognize their German opponent, to identify him as not-man and bond together as men in pursuit of his destruction. Thus, as Claire Tylee concludes, by valorizing heroic sacrifice and tragic martyrdom, the dominant myths of World War I gloss over the army's manipulation of masculine ideals in order to drive men to their maximum killing potential (GW, 254-5). "The German Prisoner" reminds us why men are sent to war--to kill and maim each other--demonstrating how ethnic and class divisions, homophobia, and misogyny help make this carnage possible.

Empire, Class, and "The German Prisoner"

Foregrounding the hostility and alienation of British imperial society, James Hanley's proletarian Irish perspective challenges the myth of a collective loss of innocence that suffuses officers' memoirs and continues to dominate cultural memory. Many soldiers experienced and remembered World War I as being profoundly influenced by their ethnicity and class status. Britain in 1914 was a nation troubled by the appalling conditions of urban slums, suffragist challenges to patriarchy, threats of strikes at home, and imminent civil war in Ireland. As Tylee points out, the "prelapsarian fantasy" of a prewar, unified nation reflecting British nostalgia for an idealized childhood and "lost imperial splendor" is specific to the upper-class English male (GW, 245). In Sassoon's Memoirs of a Fox Hunting Man, Rebecca West finds a "heartrending portrait of a class that was allowed to play at being children until the war came, and then was delivered, bound hand and foot, to the forces of hell." 15 Hanley's life and fiction, however, provide a powerful corrective to the notion that prewar Britain nurtured innocence and idealism in all its youth. His 1931 novel, Boy--in which a cabin boy is repeatedly molested by older men, infected with syphilis in an Alexandrian brothel, and then tossed overboard like garbage--describes a hellish alternative to Sassoon's extended adolescence.

Born in Dublin , Hanley spent most of his childhood in an immigrant community in Liverpool before going to sea at the age of thirteen to serve as a ship's boy. Whatever his experiences were at sea, combat apparently offered a more attractive alternative. Deserting ship in New Brunswick, he lied about his age and signed up with the Canadian army, eventually serving with shock troops in France. 16 After being discharged with injuries sustained in a gas attack, Hanley returned to Liverpool, where he struggled for years against poverty, working, among other menial jobs, as a docker and racetrack cashier. 17 Hanley thus describes the war from the perspective of a working-class soldier who experiences the dehumanization of the trenches as an intensification of, rather than a radical departure from, his position in society. 18

"The German Prisoner" opens as a battalion of men from Dublin, Cork, Donegal, and England's industrial slums hunker down for a night in a bombed-out house that resembles a stable. Treated like animals, the men behave and smell accordingly. They attack their food with "savagery" and fill the rooms with their sweat and "their almost pesty breath" ("GP," 7). Within this bestiary, two huddle apart. Hanley makes clear the closeted nature of their relationship when he writes, "In the darkest corner of one of the rooms on the ground floor lay two men . . . facing each other" in a "recumbent position" (ibid.). The taller and heavier of the men, Peter O'Garra, is Irish, impoverished, and inverted--on the ladder of the British Empire you can't descend much lower than this. O'Garra's companion, Elston, is also working-class. Born in the slums of Manchester, and described by O'Garra as the "suck-holing type," he occupies a position only fractionally better. Parodying the officers' familiar sentimentality, Elston misses the "smoke and fog and grease" of his home and feels nostalgic for his "little corner of the world," filthy and rotten though it may be. O'Garra, by contrast, harbors no regrets for the "drab life of Tara Street," with "its lousiness, its human animals herded together, its stinkin' mattresses" ("GP," 9). During World War I, Dublin boasted the worst slums in Europe, with one-third of its population crammed into single rooms that housed up to twenty people who slept as many as eight in a bed. Such conditions bred a tuberculosis rate twice as high as London's. It is therefore not surprising that Dublin,which also manifested the highest death rate and greatest percentage of people living in poverty of any city in the United Kingdom, inspired little nostalgia in those who left. 19 Further, Dubliners who migrated to English cities such as Liverpool often found themselves isolated in pockets of Irish abjection, reliving the circumstances they had fled.

Britain's justification of imperialism as a civilizing mission necessitated by the colonial subject's primitivism was fine-tuned in Ireland; and for the Irish who came to work in England, class antagonisms fused with ethnic stereotyping. 20 The discourse of British racial superiority required the Irish male to be less than a man, whether in Matthew Arnold's well-intentioned celebration of the feminine qualities of the Celt or in the vicious satires of Punch. One such satire, entitled "The Missing Link," detailed how explorers venturing into "the lowest districts of London and Liverpool" would encounter "the lowest species of Irish Yahoo . . . a climbing animal . . . ascending a ladder laden with a hod of bricks," ready to attack "civilised beings that have provoked its fury." 21 During the war, both the arrival of a German ship on the Irish coast and the Easter Rising confirmed fears of Irish treachery and violence. And the identification of the Irish enemy with sexual deviance was simultaneously reinforced by the execution of Roger Casement for treason following the revelation of scandalous diaries containing evidence of homosexual encounters throughout the colonies. According to Enda Duffy, Casement's "rebellious patriotic martyrdom" was thereby "transformed by insinuation into a discourse of forbidden sexuality," and his "choice of political action" reduced to "the merely predictable expression of an inner secret." 22

Given the wartime association of the Irish with treason, sexual deviance, and subhumanity, it is easy to see why Hanley rejected the "known literary forms" that Fussell insists war writers could not avoid (GWM, 174). Rather, Hanley's war memoir bypasses the mythic chivalry of English national literature for the idiom of jingoistic, working-class humor he had internalized from British satires of the Irish people's attempts to overcome biology. There is much of the "stage Irishman" about O'Garra (whose very name has echoes of "begorra," an expression prominent in Irish theater), and indeed, at times, Hanley appears to be writing stage directions rather than description. 23 O'Garra spits on the floor, "following up the action by drawing the flat of his hand across his mouth" ("GP," 8). As the troops prepare to move out, O'Garra and Elston execute a pratfall straight from the music hall:

Suddenly O'Garra slipped into a hole.
"Jesus Christ! Already," growled Elston.
He dragged O'Garra out, following up the action by falling in himself. ["GP,"11]

Such exaggerated actions emphasize the absurdity of the humiliating and degrading role they must perform in order to survive. There is no touch of elegy, no sense of sacrifice in Hanley's description of battle, which he records with darkly physical humor. A sergeant named Grundy announces his intention to "post certain men," provoking "all and sundry" to break into "loud cursing and obscene oaths" ("GP," 17). Brains belonging to a soldier named Gorman end up splattered on O'Garra's forehead. And in another scene Elston screams "like a stuck pig" on finding himself "drenched in blood":

"Oh funkin' hell. I'm killed. I'm dead. O'Garra. O'Garra. O'Garra."
"Shut up," growled the Irishman. "Can't you see that bugger behind you. You got a belt in the back with his head. See. He hasn't any now. That whizz-bang took his head and his arse off him at the same time. . . ." ["GP," 19]

The voices Hanley records with such accuracy in "The German Prisoner" are those from "the end of the line," the lowest level of combat troops, lacking either agency or access to information about their fate.

Joanna Bourke notes that "the ideology of male comradeship in war was never intended to subvert military rank and its association with socioeconomic class"; at the height of the battle the men do their job, which is not to think, but to move forward, to follow orders (DM, 145). They form a corporate body, a "huge eyeless monster that forge[s] its way ahead towards some inevitable destiny. Nothing more" ("GP," 12). At times, however, the mass disintegrates into its constituent parts. One memorable scene from "The German Prisoner" deflates the classic war texts' romanticization of the officer's relationship to his men. Knowing that a guide waits for them a thousand yards ahead, an officer worries about his men for strategic reasons alone: some might die, yes, but to leave them out in the open would "disclose (more importantly) the movements of troops . . . to the Germans" and ruin the element of surprise ("GP," 14). "Far back, at the very tail end of this file of men," Elston and O'Garra have been worrying about the officer's competence, complaining that "that bugger in front" can't re out what to do. As the cover of darkness lifts, the men begin to panic--"what had merely been a germ, became a disease, an epidemic. The torment was no longer private, but general. . . . When something went wrong, the more sensitive spirits became agitated. One saw it in their eyes" ("GP," 15). After a bombardment kills seven men, the troops issue their own orders: "RUN FOR IT" ("GP," 16).

As John Keegan shows, the average soldier answers to a different imperative than the officer: "the concepts 'win' and 'lose' through which a commander . . . approach[es] a battle are by no means the same as those through which his men will view their own involvement in it." 24 Studies of human behavior in combat reveal that men identify themselves in terms of a "very tiny" group, "perhaps no more than six or seven men." Group members begin to t and continue to t for two reasons: "personal survival, which individuals will recognize to be bound up with group survival" and the "fear of incurring by cowardly conduct the group's contempt." 25 Further, noting the "immense burden" masculine comradeship placed on men "to express martial values," Bourke observes that in World War I, "the fear of being thought to be a coward was intense" (DM, 151-2). Tylee adds that to feel fear provoked deep shame in a man, causing him to "despise himself as he might a member of an 'inferior' race or a girl" (GW, 125). Masculinity taught men to repress fear of the other within, thereby directing shame and self-loathing into aggression against a designated object outside themselves.

Of the two men, O'Garra seems to be the better soldier, having learned the masculine ability to maintain control over himself and other men. He brags about surviving the first gas attack, and he repeatedly pulls Elston out of danger as the other man whimpers for his mother. 26 Years of abuse on a filthy street in the most despised corner of the British Empire have steeled O'Garra against attacks. The litany of insults with which his Dublin neighbors revile him constitutes the same equation between homosexuality and treason found in British propaganda:

a lousy bugger--a rake--a closet--a quiet fellow--a tub of guts--a pimp--a shit-house--a toad--a sucker--a blasted sod--a Holy Roller--a Tara lemon--a Judas--a jumped-up liar--a book-worm--a traitor to Ireland--a pervert--an Irish jew--an Irish Christ--a clod. ["GP," 8]

Despite their rejection of him as "a traitor to Ireland," O'Garra's countrymen uphold British associations of Ireland with deviance, using "Irish" and "Tara" as intensifiers of their own scorn. Demonized as a pervert, an animal, and a traitor to race and religion, O'Garra seeks in aggression a more acceptable alternative to this identity. We learn that he "went for a soldier" to keep from giving in to "blood" that was "heavy with surrender" ("GP," 9). War is just "blood bitterness," he tells Elston, "and bitter blood is good blood" (ibid.). This struggle has taken its toll, however. His eyes, which Hanley compares to "the dried up beds of African lakes," reveal the double repression of his identity as racialized colonial subject and as sexual other.

O'Garra has so internalized society's prejudices that he can't open his mouth without a disparaging remark about "buggers" and "sods." Self-hatred translates his attraction to Elston as hate at first sight: "When first he had set eyes on Elston, he had despised him, there was something in this man utterly repugnant to him" ("GP," 10). From his first encounter with this "human rat," this Englishman of the "suck-holing type," O'Garra obsesses about how despicable he finds the other man, penning verses that testify to Elston's uniqueness as an object of scorn.

Annihilating Desire

A cultural complex of homophobia, misogyny, racial and class discrimination, and repressed sexual desire erupt into violence and brutality in the relationship between O'Garra and Elston, provoking their rape and murder of a German prisoner. The anxiety shared by cultural historians over pronounced homoeroticism in literary representations of the war is exemplified by Fussell's coining of the phrase "chaste homosexuality" to bolster his claim that virtually none of these crushes were ever consummated. In fact, between 1914 and 1919, "twenty-two officers and 270 other ranks were court-martialed for 'indecency' with another man." 27 Reminding us that "the war provided opportunities for erotic relationships among men which were not necessarily available in peacetime," Trudi Tate suggests we examine why the idea of homosexuality has been so obdurately resisted. 28 Certainly the identification during and after the war of homosexuality as a German practice provided an added incentive for men to remain silent about their own behavior: newspapers condemned infection by "German homosexuality" as threatening "the patriotism, stamina, intellect and morale" of the troops. 29

In "The German Prisoner," however, the fog of war is also the fog of desire, even if O'Garra and Elston consistently misname and misrecognize their passion. The lid is clamped down so tightly that neither can envision the realization of his desire without an eruption of violence. As the troops "go over the top" into no-man's-land,

every sound and every movement seemed to strike a responsive chord in the Irishman's nature. He hung on desperately to the Manchester man. . . . He could not understand this sudden desire for Elston's company. But the desire overwhelmed him. ["GP," 19]

Something about battle turns O'Garra on. Seeing a once-foolish officer's "headless body" stirs "something deep down in the bosom of his soul" ("GP," 28). After they lose contact with their battalion, the fog deepens and Elston regards O'Garra with "a sudden gust of fear and passion," wondering "did this man really mean to murder him" ("GP," 22).

When O'Garra looks "across at the huddled form of the Manchester man," he heaves a sigh, acknowledging, but still unable to articulate, his yearning: "Now the whole of his past shot across the surface of that mind" (ibid.). Remembering "those lonely nights, those fruitless endeavors" spent searching for a woman, O'Garra concludes, "surely they took on a richer texture now. . . . [A]chieved a certain significance" ("GP," 22-3). He defers his sexual confusion with a deep meditation on what will happen after the war. Unable to re out why he "had come into all this muck and mud and madness," he gropes instead for a suitable target for the grudge he believes "everyone in the war must bear," wondering "would they all demand retribution? Would they all wreak a terrible vengeance?" ("GP," 23).

Waking from this reverie, O'Garra realizes that his watch has stopped, imparting to the scene the strange, surrealistic quality of a dream sequence. Suddenly, out of the mist emerges a handsome German soldier boy embodying the homoerotic ideal described by classic war memoirs. 30 Hanley invokes him lyrically, as "tall, with a form as graceful as a young sapling," with hair "as fair as ripe corn, . . . blue eyes, and finely molded features" ("GP," 24). In the ensuing brutal scene, "The German Soldier" enacts the men's projection of unsafe desire on an enemy who must consequently be destroyed. 31 Calling out repeatedly, "Camerade. Camerade," the German boy pleads for recognition of their common humanity. Initially Elston recognizes this appeal, responding that they too are lost and asking, "Is it foggy where you came from? . . . [W]e'll never get out of this hole, only by stirring ourselves together and making a bolt for it" ("GP," 25). O'Garra, however, chokes off the possibility of fellowship with a torrent of homophobic slurs, warning Elston, "perhaps this sod is leading us into a trap. Why not finish the bugger off, anyhow?" In the fog of war, innocence provokes violence, and murder reveals its erotic potential. O'Garra and Elston savagely kick the screaming prisoner's face into a bloody pulp. The remorse and horror they feel afterwards ends quickly. Reminiscing about an earlier incident, in which O'Garra pulled another soldier off an old woman during what appears to have been a serial rape, the two excitedly urinate over the prisoner. And their subsequent expressions of renewed vigor are clearly postcoital:

"Hell!" exclaimed O'Garra. "After that I feel relieved. Refreshed. Don't feel tired. Don't feel anything particularly. How do you feel?" he asked.
"The same," replied Elston. "But I wish to Christ this soddin' fog would lift." ["GP," 30]

The world of "The German Prisoner" is liquid with water, blood, tears, urine, and fog, evoking a dissolution of control and leaking across borders associated with a pronounced anxiety toward the feminine. Elston and O'Garra experience desire as "a ceaseless yearning, the restlessness of waters washing against the floodgates of the soul" (ibid.). O'Garra kicks the German savagely in the face, and seeing "the stream of blood gush forth from the German's mouth," he bursts "into tears" ("GP," 27). Elston spits in the face of the German, whose own saliva will trickle down his chin later as he cowers in the hole ("GP," 25-6). The German prisoner seems almost entirely fluid--and feminine. He weeps; he calls out repeatedly for water; his sigh has a "liquidity of tone"; he reminds Elston of "all the beauty and music and rhythm of the Rhine"; and he seems ready to dissolve at any time into the mud ("GP," 31-2). The battlefield is also fluid, resembling a cosmic whirlpool--a vast, primal, devouring mother. In the heat of battle, "the earth was alive--afire. The earth was a mouth, it was a sea, a yawning gulf, a huge maw" ("GP," 19). Terms like "mouth, gulf, and maw" resonate with the threat of female genitalia. Indeed, O'Garra's own mouth suggests the monstrous feminine: "If one wished to know O'Garra, one looked at his mouth," the narrator comments ("GP," 9). O'Garra's mouth makes him "more than a man" and the false teeth clicking in his "ugly mouth" elicit a castrating potential, a vagina dentata ("GP," 8).

Desire in "The German Prisoner" expresses itself as contamination, pollution, threat: "There is a peculiar power about rottenness," Hanley insists, and O'Garra feels himself helpless before Elston's peculiar power. As their anxiety increases and they escalate their torture of the prisoner, the Tommies vent their "unclean" feelings through repeated references to defecation. In an obvious request for anal sex, Elston growls at O'Garra: "My arse on you. . . . Let's worry each other to death. Isn't that better than sitting here like soft shits. That time I fell asleep I did it in my pants" ("GP," 33). Elston calls O'Garra a "big shit house" as they stare at each other, transfixed with desire and rage ("GP," 35). Seeing the mangled body of the prisoner sink beneath the mud, O'Garra screams out, "I hate this thing so much I want to shite on it" ("GP," 36). Noting that "sexual fantasies played a large role in wartime propaganda," Mosse describes the "vogue" in Germany for "scatological postcards, uncensored because the fantasies were projected onto the enemy, depicted as covered with excrement, their bodies and even sexual organs exposed." 32 In Hanley's story, the abject body of the prisoner functions similarly as a field upon which both murderous aggression and transgressive desire can be projected and commingled. 33

The body of the German prisoner offers an acceptable outlet for the British soldiers' rage against their own victimization by a classist society. Their problems are not with any particular "Jerry" so much as with a society that considers them filth because of their class, race, or sexuality and sends them off to war to die. As he torments the prisoner, Elston asks him, "Why should you not suffer as well as us?" then repeats the words of his persecutor, Sargent Grundy--"Shit on you" ("GP," 25). Moreover, according to Eric Leed, such behavior announces the success of Elston's military training, in which the officer treats the recruit "the same way that he wants the soldier to treat the enemy in battle. To escape the low and painful status of victim," the soldier must identify with the aggressor. Such identification enables "the soldier's activity in war--all the shooting, maiming, and killing--to be perceived as moral, legitimate, and meaningful." 34 The complex system of division by which society operates becomes intensified in wartime, firmly fixing categories of good and evil. Because O'Garra and Elston do not initially blame the German prisoner for their predicament, we can see how "evil" is constructed and what it takes to assume the mantle of aggressor.

The men's increasing desire for each other provokes a corresponding intensification of their denunciations against the prisoner. Initially O'Garra condemns Elston for losing them in the fog, insisting that "its you and not this bloody Jerry who is responsible" ("GP," 26). When the German's sigh interrupts O'Garra's invective against Elston, O'Garra begins to refocus his rage, accusing the prisoner of being "as much to blame as anybody" ("GP," 27). Finally, he and Elston, far gone in their mutual seduction, decide that "That bastard lying there is the cause of all this," thus allowing them to transfer anxiety over illicit emotions to a safe object ("GP," 30). As soldiers, they have been taught to view the German as an acceptable target of violence and to consider murder as less transgressive than sex with another man. 35

Klaus Theweleit's analysis of narratives by the Friekorps--the "white troops" who graduated from postwar persecution of communists to the elite ranks of Hitler's S. S.--attempts to uncover systems of desire driving cultures of war and violence. He argues that versions of masculinity compel mastery of the body (sometimes through torture) to produce hardness and a destructive edge. Giving in to the female signifies "being swallowed, engulfed, annihilated. Women's bodies are the holes, swamps, pits of muck that can engulf." 36 The troops thus conceive their violence against women as "self-defense," a preemptive strike against a "threatening element," with the affective mode of such defense being "fear and desire" (MF, 183; italics in original).

"The German Prisoner" describes a similarly violent acting out of a simultaneously sexual and homicidal conflict. The feminine--including the enemy soldier--is marked as deviant and threatening, and must be destroyed. Accordingly, O'Garra and Elston objectify the German man as a "body," an "it": "With peculiar movements of the hands" they begin to "mangle the body" and to worry "it like mad dogs" ("GP," 32). Contact with the "youth's soft skin" and "the velvety touch of the flesh" leave Elston "infuriated," "demented," and "maddened" (ibid.). O'Garra and Elston use this body to bridge the gap of their repressed homosexual desire, relieving the pressure they feel by penetrating and puncturing the boy. O'Garra sticks his bayonet "up the youth's anus" and both insert a horse hair into his penis. Describing violent erotic murder by the Freikorps as "two male compulsions . . . tearing at the woman," Theweleit explains, "the man pushes the woman far away (takes her life), and gets very close to her (penetrates her with a bullet, stab wound, club, etc.)" (MF, 196). Once she has been reduced to a bloody mass, losing her "identity as an object with concrete dimensions and a unique name," the man can breathe a sigh of relief. . . .What we are dealing with here is the dissolution of the body itself, and of the woman as bodily entity as well as love object" (ibid.).

O'Garra and Elston, however, will not "breathe a sigh of relief" at the death of the boy, for the feminine also includes parts of themselves in conflict with rigidly structured ideals of masculinity that can be neither externalized nor destroyed. The bloody body of the boy operates successively as an impediment to the murderous rage and desire they feel for each other. The two decide to "bury this thing. . . . If we don't destroy him, he'll destroy us, even though he's dead" ("GP," 35). They "bury" the body by jumping up and down on it, trampling it beneath the mud, just as O'Garra has seen a Dublin woman trample a dog. Once the prisoner is gone, "their very beings" seem "to burst into flame," and they realize "there is no way out" ("GP," 36). They hurl themselves together, not realizing that the fog has lifted enough to reveal their position. After the smoke from an incoming shell has cleared, O'Garra can be seen with "tortured features," his eyes "gouged out, whilst beneath his powerful frame" lies "the remains of Elston" (ibid.). The pair is visible "for a moment only" before they sink into a common grave with their victim, "beneath the sea of mud" oozing "over them like the restless tide of an everlasting night" (ibid.).

According to Tim O'Brien, if you feel at the end of a war story that "some small bit of rectitude has been salvaged from the larger waste, then you have been made the victim of a very old and terrible lie." 37 Indeed, we see such a salvage operation in the "Introductory Note" to "The German Prisoner," written by Richard Aldington, author of war memoirs and the celebrated novel, Death of a Hero. Praising Hanley for the "force and vitality" with which he depicts "the unspeakable wrong . . . worked upon human souls by those who are supposed to be its guardians and guides," Aldington reconstructs O'Garra and Elston as suffering innocents who are "driven" and "tortured" to madness. Rounding up the usual suspects--old men and women distributing white feathers--Aldington rebuffs their objections to the "dreadfully dirty words" these characters use: "If you were not ashamed to send men into the war, why should you blush to read what they said in it? . . . Though the world will little note nor long remember what they did there, perhaps it will not hurt you to know a little of what they said and suffered." 38 In his insistence on what the men "said" and "suffered," Aldington himself seems to have difficulty remembering "what they did there." By refusing to acknowledge that they are rapists and murderers, Aldington recuperates a fantasy of sacrificial victims purified through suffering that works to perpetuate the mythology of war. 39

English military cemeteries of World War I are designed around a Cross of Sacrifice and a Stone of Remembrance hewn in the shape of an altar, projecting a Christian symbolism over rows and rows of identical graves and headstones whose uniformity is "reminiscent of wartime camaraderie." 40 Refusing such standard issue commemoration, Hanley chooses to remember the fallen differently. While in Hanley's description of O'Garra's disgust for his victim--"Everything I look at becomes Him. Everything Him"--the capitalized pronoun links the suffering of the German prisoner with the suffering of Christ, the re of Christ has been "plastered in shite" after "a five-nine landed behind it," pitching it "into one of the latrines" ("GP," 35, 13). Roadside crosses remind O'Garra of "the Irish Christ. All blood and tears," and he refuses to look at them, perhaps remembering his own persecution as an "Irish Christ" as well as a "shit house" ("GP," 13, 8). By establishing the connection between Peter O'Garra, Otto from Muenchen, and Jesus Christ, and by heaping them with shit, Hanley exposes the inevitable degradation of every person and every ideal that participates in the obscenity of war and forecloses the possibility of meaningful sacrifice.

"The German Prisoner" has been suppressed and ignored because its story of men achieving pleasure in brutality, murder, and self-destruction does not represent the way people have wanted to remember the war. According to the dominant narrative, only two legitimate manifestations of masculinity existed during World War I--heterosexuality and violence. Manliness required repeated acts of courage (violence) to "prove" heterosexuality, which nevertheless remained always in doubt. The myth that the love between men was limited to a transcendent, "chaste" homoeroticism sentimentalizes the compulsory heterosexuality behind an extreme form of masculinity that channels desire, rage, and fear into the savage murder of other men in battle. Comradeship in "The German Prisoner" cannot be separated from the horror of war because it is an essential ingredient of that horror. Hanley's profoundly disturbing memory of World War I raises powerful questions about the cost of unity during violent conflicts.
