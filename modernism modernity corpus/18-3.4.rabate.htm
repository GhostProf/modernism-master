Beckett’s Three Critiques: Kant’s Bathos and the Irish Chandos

Jean-Michel Rabaté

My ambitious title cannot hope to fulfill its promise, and thus risks becoming bathetic—hence my cryptic subtitle. However, my goal will be reached if I manage to provide a schema, put forward some questions, and perhaps reopen the debate about the links between Beckett and philosophy. One way to begin is to notice the ambiguity of the title of this special issue: Out of the Archive, which calls up two different meanings: “What can we get out of the archives that we didn’t know before?” and “How quickly can we flee the archives?” Is a “scientific” approach to Beckett’s texts one that is solidly established in archival research, or should we take our bearings in pre-publication documents, however illegible or incomplete, and then move on, out of the archives, back to the texts themselves? As legitimate as these questions are, they also sound familiar. It could be that, some thirty years later, Beckett scholarship is repeating debates about archives and “genetic criticism” that raged through the Joyce industry during the 1980s, when Hans Walter Gabler and John Kidd fought over the “corrected” and revised text of Ulysses. It has subsided since, and as Dirk Van Hulle has noted, there is a huge difference between Joyce’s “Know-How” and Beckett’s “Nohow.”1

As the head of the Institut des textes et manuscrits modernes in Paris, Ferrer has been immersed in discussions of the genetic aspect of archives for more than thirty years. Ultimately, he engages in productive dialogues with Nelson Goodman, Michael Riffaterre, and Umberto Eco. Ferrer is typical of the generation to which I belong, a generation that has shifted over time from pure, capital-T Theory—a field defined by Barthes, Kristeva, Derrida, Cixous, Foucault, Deleuze, and Lacan—to more pragmatic concerns associated with textual studies. Genetic studies will always be bound to the reality of a material archive, which may offer a cornucopia, or the scant remainders of an activity that has been all but obliterated. To give an example of my own evolution, I’ll present a vignette that doubles as a self-critique: I will confess to a blunder I made about Beckett at the beginning of the 1980s. Having worked on Joyce and Pound from the genetic angle, but not knowing much about Beckett, I was invited to join a group investigating Murphy at the instigation of Jacques Aubert. My task was to focus on the references to psychology and psychologists that abound in the novel. Spending a week in the British Museum, I took out all the books on psychology that were available to Beckett in the early 1930s. I knew that just as he was beginning his psychoanalysis with Bion in the Winter of 1933–34, Beckett was reading these books to get better acquainted with various schools and concepts. Among these, I saw a book by R. S. Woodworth, Contemporary Schools of Psychology, published in 1931, and took notes on it. I also discovered a book by a certain Gardner Murphy, entitled An Historical Introduction to Modern Psychology, published in 1929. Both included detailed summaries of all the new schools in psychology, explaining the experiments on association, reflexes, and perception made by Külpe, Marbe, Ach, Watt, Bühler, Koffka, and Koehler; all names that are mentioned in Murphy. Gardner Murphy’s compendium had a supplementary section by a certain Heinrich Klüver, a specialist who analyzed new schools of psychology in Germany. This was much more extensive than the survey found in Woodworth. Moreover, the coincidence of the names was irresistible. I had no idea of the existence of Beckett’s “Psychology Notes,” which, now at the Library of Trinity College, Dublin, were not yet available. So I jumped to the conclusion that Murphy was used by Beckett to write Murphy.

I was wrong. Beckett used Woodworth’s survey, complemented by notes from books 701 by Ernest “Erogenous” Jones, Otto Rank, and Wilhelm Stekel, to familiarize himself with psychological doctrine. Today, knowing better, I look to the archives before making this kind of assumption. The only defense I can make in retrospect of my admittedly terrible method is that Murphy’s book afforded me a useful insight. Murphy begins his survey with Descartes, which allows him to situate the development of experimental psychology at the end of the nineteenth century as a progressive rejection of Cartesian rationalism. This provided a useful hint, teaching me that Murphy’s ideas about the self are close to those of the school of Würzburg, which posited a dualism between mind and body, while those of Murphy’s teacher Neary are in line with the Gestalt psychology promoted by Koffka and Koehler. This gave me a basis from which to discuss the discrepancy in Beckett’s work between philosophical models of the mind and the psychoanalytic model based on Jung’s third Tavistock lecture—the lecture to which Bion had taken Beckett in October 1935.3

I still grapple with the problem of how to reconcile Beckett’s views on philosophy with what he learned from psychoanalysis. However, while I believed in 1984 that Beckett was a strict Cartesian, I have subsequently learned that the influence of Geulincx was more important. Whereas I took the impact of Jung too seriously, getting lost when trying to link Beckett with the works of Bion, I now realize that Beckett knew much more about Freud than I thought. Thus, in what follows, I would like to reopen my investigation. What matters most to me today is less the empirical content of the archive than the theoretical discourse through which textual “discoveries” will be presented and framed.

One could adopt either of two basic attitudes towards Beckett’s long-standing interest in philosophy, psychology, and psychoanalysis in the thirties. The first, exemplified by Matthew Feldman, relies on textual scholarship and manuscript evidence and tends to use the archive in order to place a limit on interpretation. In “Beckett and Popper,” Feldman adduces criteria of verifiability and falsifiability that he wants to bring to bear on the use of manuscripts.4 My vignette above should show just how vital such scientific rigor is. In his more recent “Beckett and Philosophy,” Feldman adopts a relatively conservative attitude towards chronology when he assumes that Beckett’s engagement with philosophy, intense as it was, was short-lived, lasting no longer than a decade, between roughly 1928 and 1938.5 This position is technically unassailable, based as it is on manuscript evidence and precise dating of Beckett’s allusions, letters, and note-taking. While I follow him in his Popperian caution by insisting that allusions and references should be verified in drafts or manuscripts, I am not sure that Beckett’s engagement with philosophy can be limited to ten years. Moreover, the wish to ground all textual remarks on pre-publication material risks narrowing the critical discussion to such an extreme that the uses of the text would be restricted to self-appointed professionals.

The other attitude is illustrated by those philosophers who want to engage with Beckett. Aiming to arrive at a new rapport with the novels and plays, they tend to read the published texts alone, using archives sparingly, and eschewing the work of specialist Beckett commentators. Philosophers like Theodor Adorno, Alain Badiou, Gilles

702 Deleuze, Stanley Cavell, Simon Critchley, and Martha Nussbaum do this because they believe that Beckett’s oeuvre can be read as philosophy, whether critical, ethical, ontological, or political. To illustrate this approach, I’ll begin with a 1988 article by Martha Nussbaum, “Narrative Emotions: Beckett’s Genealogy of Love,” reprinted in Love’s Knowledge. Her thesis is that we should learn from our emotions, not because they are natural or instinctual expressions, but because they have been socially constructed, mostly by narratives that teach us how to behave and how to feel. She calls this Beckett’s “project”: revealing a certain understanding of the oeuvre shared by those writers who refuse to acknowledge the division between philosophy and literature.6 The dialogue between philosophy and literature would always attempt to answer to the old question: “How should one live?” (LK, 289). She thus begins with Aristotle’s concept of practical wisdom and goes on to fine-tune it by attending to particular instances and literary models. In these cases form and content are inseparable, and their intermeshing matters most when we examine truths about human life. While I fully agree with this “project,” I am not sure why Beckett figures in it. But it is precisely because Beckett seems such an intractable nihilist that Nussbaum grapples with him, at least to check the validity of her Aristotelian theory of emotions. Nussbaum decides to focus on Molloy, in which she takes one sentence as a point of departure: “It is in the tranquility of decomposition that I recall the long confused emotion which was my life, and that I judge it, as it is said that God will judge me, and with no less impertinence” (LK, 297).7 Most of her commentary will revolve around these words. What follows is a paraphrase of the novel in which the dominant feature is Moran’s heightened relation to religion. This leads to the statement, “We could summarize the emotion story that is Molloy’s life by saying that it is the story of original sin, of the fear of God’s judgment, and of the vain longing for salvation” (LK, 298). In fact, Nussbaum uses two approaches at once, the religious one and the psychoanalytic one. Molloy’s obsession is anal birth; he and Moran share a similar disgust for bodily functions, and the body itself reduced to shit. Indeed, the Mother might be called “Countess Caca” (M, 14); what more do we need to say? One might contend that Nussbaum deliberately misses the possibility of seeing that the combination of “excremental vision” with a post-religious sensibility is not necessarily Calvinist or Jansenist. Instead, it could be read in an Augustinian manner, with an “Inter urinas et faeces nascimur” leading to something like Derrida’s “Circumfessions.”8 Another option might have been to assert that, like Joyce, Beckett derived from Augustine the insight that corruption is proof of the innate goodness of the things (or people) that have been corrupted. Since Nussbaum realizes that the solution found by Beckett’s narrator is similar to Proust’s—that there is no salvation except via writing—she decides to reject the critique of religion that she takes as the dominant feature of the Trilogy. She compares Beckett with two philosophical deniers who, according to her, had the merit of being more consistent: Lucretius and Nietzsche. Both sought to destroy the illusions of religion in order to assert the possibility of a truly human life once the nihilism condensed by religion had been overcome. She concludes that Beckett’s view of emotions, in which

guilt and disgust dominate, is not very healthy, and certainly not recommended for 703 impressionable children:

This is not a picture either of an individual child’s development or of a society’s evolution. Children actively select and interpret; and the society around them contains a plurality of active voices, striving to persuade us in new directions . . . It is in part because Beckett sees society as single and monolithic that he is able to omit the presence of argument, criticism, and change. In all this we sense, I believe, a deeply religious sensibility at work. (LK, 309)

Whether one agrees with this thesis or not, there is no doubt that it is a reductive reading. First, Nussbaum makes unwarranted jumps that amount to interpretive errors: she thinks that it is Molloy who speaks of “finality without end” when it is Moran; she also thinks that the whole book is written by Moran, who is therefore also responsible for the other texts authored by Beckett. She thinks that Youdi’s name means simply, “You die.” Such misreadings are not totally devastating, as they testify to her having grappled with the text without relying on the usual guides. But what is more worrying is that despite her basic contention, she wants to read literature and philosophy together following a single program, and so fails to do justice to either.

In the following paragraph, she adds:

There is a peculiar movement in Beckett’s talk of emotions . . . from a perception of human limits to a loathing of the limited, from grief to disgust and hatred, from the tragedy and comedy of the frail body to rage at the body, seen as covered in excrement. It is as if Beckett believes that the finite and frail can only inspire our disgust and loathing—that life (in the words of Youdi) can be “a thing of beauty and a joy” only if it is “for ever.” And this is because, as we said, mortality in Beckett’s world is seen not as our neutral and natural condition but as our punishment for original sin. The complete absence in his writing of any joy in the limited and the finite indicates to us that the narrative as a whole is an expression of a religious view of life. (LK, 309)

This is not the first time that Nussbaum evokes the Keatsian reference placed in Gaber’s mouth—“He said to me, said Gaber, Gaber, he said, life is a thing of beauty, Gaber, and a joy for ever” (M, 172)—but she never seems to recognize that this is a quotation. Nor does she acknowledge the pervasive irony of Moran’s comment: “Do you think he meant human life? . . . Perhaps he didn’t mean human life” (M, 172). That said, she does use this later to connect Beckett’s supposed critique of religion with Epicurus, for whom animals offer models for men since they “show us what it could be to be alive without hope or fear or disgust or even love” (LK, 304).

Nussbaum’s symptom appears blatantly here: her failure to acknowledge the presence of either Kant or Keats in Molloy. In the passage already mentioned, she asserts: “Molloy feels always, in this world, that he dwells in an atmosphere of finality without end” (LK, 299). It is certainly true that Kant is Nussbaum’s bête-noire in Love’s Knowledge because of his alleged “hostility to emotions” (LK, 76, 78). Thus, in a more recent essay she promotes the idea of “compassion” as key to our ethical be

704 ing by debunking Kant’s empty formalism; for her, Kant wrongly severs the pleasure provided by aesthetic attitudes from any sense of interest or emotional engagement.9 Nussbaum has not given us an approach combining philosophy and literature. Instead, in the name of a philosophy of affect and cognitive emotion, she has fallen into bad literary criticism. We are given either reductive or unfounded religious readings or simplistic psychoanalytic readings rehashing platitudes about the mother, the father, and the debasement of libido. For Nussbaum, finally, Beckett has to be rejected as an influence because he is not Nietzsche. The two passages of Molloy that we have quoted both deal with beauty. Why, when Gaber arrives and orders him to find Molloy, is Moran suddenly seized by a Kantian sense of the beauty of the world when seen from a teleological point of view? Why does Gaber sum up Youdi’s teachings with a parody of Keats’s last line in “Ode to a Grecian Urn?” In order to answer this in a way that does combine philosophy and literature, I’ll have to make another detour. Nussbaum’s reductive reading of Beckett was immediately noticed and attacked. In 1997, Simon Critchley made fun of her in his influential Very Little . . . Almost Nothing. He noted that silence was not the “goal” of Beckett’s work, as Nussbaum assumes because of her fixation on the religious reading, but rather a “desecration and desacralization of silence,” as he had said to Adorno.10 Critchley’s endnotes are more ferocious: “The consequence of Nussbaum’s reading of Beckett is depressingly familiar (not to mention being pre-Nietzschean), namely, that Beckett’s ‘search for silence’ is a ‘nihilism’” (VLAN, 259). In another endnote, he contrasts J. M. Coetzee’s sense that Beckett’s prose gives “sensuous delight” with Nussbaum’s rejection of Beckett as joyless. For Critchley, on the contrary, Beckett’s style is cruel, but his humor exhibits a joyous relation to finitude, “a celebration of human limitedness that is replete with sardonic, side-splitting, anti-depressant comedy” (VLAN, 265). Finally, he debunks the mixture of neo-Aristotelianism and classical psychoanalysis which underpins Nussbaum’s approach:

Despite the undoubted felicities offered by a psychoanalytic interpretation of Molloy, I find Nussbaum’s use of psychoanalytic categories a little too easy and fluent (the object of guilt is “parental sexual act” [sic] (p. 298), the object of disgust is “above all the female body” (p. 299)). What does it mean to employ such interpretive categories in relation to a literary text of such theoretical self-consciousness as Beckett’s? There is, I feel, the danger of a hermeneutic literalism there. (VLAN, 262)

It is clear that any attempt to combine literature and philosophy should be attentive to these reflexive layers; and it should forbid any reduction of the literary to the philosophical—which is what Nussbaum has done in the end—or, indeed, the reverse.

Sensing that the debate had not yet reached a satisfactory conclusion, Bjørn K. Myskja attempted to mediate between the positions of Nussbaum and Crichley in his cogent book, The Sublime in Kant and Beckett.11 Myskja favors a philosophical approach to literature, and it is not for nothing that his book was published as volume 140 of the Kantstudien series. His point of departure is the same as Nussbaum’s: how can a novel like Molloy, which is so full of reprehensible or disgusting actions and characters, and 705 also so hard to comprehend in any rational manner, have moral significance?

Myskja’s answer is to suggest that Kant’s concept of the Sublime provides the key. He writes, “The novel is sublime in the sense that its systematic negation of the meaning of the text denies the reader a coherent meaning, which gives rise to the complex feeling described by Kant as the main element in a judgment of sublimity” (SKB, 1–2). Myskja sets out three levels on which the Sublime can be applied: the self-contradictions that prevent any interpretive closure for the reader; the characters who are always striving toward a purpose, albeit absurd; and the poetry of the style, giving rise to the “murmur” as an ontological category. The bulk of the book is a thorough examination of Kant’s Third Critique. Myskja begins by opposing Nussbaum to Critchley, before exploring an older critical disagreement between Georges Bataille’s heterological reading of Molloy and Martin Esslin’s neo-existentialist reading, in which Molloy is a hero confronting the absurdity of human fate. Having found in Bataille an allusion to the “formless,” Myskja concludes that his is the first reading to have seen the novel as something Sublime for the reader.

There are several excellent passages in Mysjka’s book, especially when he evokes the way the Sublime “explores the agony and the pain of the powerless” (SKB, 52). But even though I follow Myskja when he proposes that Kant’s analysis of the Sublime, set forth in the Critique of Judgement, is key to Beckett’s ethics and aesthetics in the Trilogy, I cannot agree with him when he uses Bataille’s review of Molloy as a way of breaking away from Nussbaum’s Aristotelian pieties. Myskja attempts to establish a connection between Bataille’s informe and Kant’s Sublime, but Bataille should not be mistaken for a Kantian. Indeed, he tended much more toward the Hegelian and Nietzschean. For him, fundamentally, what Beckett accomplishes in Molloy is to question the limits of humanity by exploring the very legitimacy of that concept. Moreover, even Myskja is aware that by relentlessly returning to the question of positive ethical significance in Molloy, he is disregarding Bataille’s declared intentions—he knows he is “taking the risk of turning it into something more or less contradictory to Bataille’s aesthetic theory” (SKB, 50).

Bataille was the first to hail Molloy as a masterpiece after its publication in 1951. His philosophy of excess and transgression anticipates Nussbaum’s central insight: philosophy and literature are not separate and they should culminate in a living experience of the subject. And the title of Bataille’s review, “The Silence of Molloy,” will bring us back to the issue of silence in the novel.

Bataille met Beckett in Paris in May, 1951, and the writers had a brief exchange of letters, now lost. Bataille appears to have found something of a kindred spirit in Beckett.12 Here was someone, who, like him, was relatively lonely despite having belonged to an avant-gardist group before the war: Transition for Beckett, and the dissident Surrealist group surrounding Documents for Bataille. Having emerged from the war into a France eager for the resumption of normality, they generated an experimental prose that questioned the values to which their contemporaries were all too eager to return. Against the grain of contemporary cultural history, both attempted to think the

706 limits of humanity. Literature was to criticize philosophy’s efforts to achieve a totalization of human experience, whereas philosophy was to question the old confidence in the representational value of narrative. Unlike most reviews in Critique, which remained matter of fact, Bataille’s essay took quite a personal, even confessional, turn. A long footnote describes an abandoned first novel, containing a scene almost identical to a passage in Molloy: a man meets an old tramp in the woods and kills him savagely, without any reason. The only rationale offered is that, as Bataille writes, it was “perhaps in hopes of gaining access to the animality of his victim.”13 Molloy thus inspired in Bataille a sort of sacred horror. Not only had Beckett given form to an aborted project of his, but he did this with a savage wit and a verbal energy that Bataille had lacked. Bataille perceived that Beckett’s theme was the inhumanity of man, which explains why this literature would constantly try to destroy or negate itself as such. The memory of this tramp—rest assured, he survived the encounter with the young Bataille—who talked about his destitute life for a whole night, was enough to call up for Bataille “the silence of animals” (SM, 64). Thus, the silence that haunts Molloy is double: it is a silence about the human, and about literature:

It is even possible that literature may have the same fundamental meaning as silence, but it recoils before the final step that silence would be. Likewise this Molloy, who is its incarnation, is not precisely a dead man. The profound apathy of death, its indifference to every possible thing, is apparent in him, but this apathy would encounter in death itself its own limit. (SM, 58)

If Molloy inspires Bataille to a sacred horror, the category of the Sublime is inadequate to his insights. Bataille saw early and clearly the philosophical project of Beckett, and recognized a shared critique of humanism in the name of the “impossible.” This concept underpins Bataille’s novels after 1938 and Beckett’s essays on painting, both authors having founded their practice on an experience of impotence, dispossession, and unknowing, debunking humanism in the name of a perception of a deeper, “abjection” or “animality” in humanity. Against Sartrean humanism, both ushered in a new anti-humanism, expressed in a literature that negated or exhausted itself as fully as possible. This is the notion of philosophy that I would like to take as my model in an attempt at combining philosophy and literature through Beckett’s writings.

Beckett would repeat to friends and journalists that he was not a philosopher, and that he had never read Wittgenstein or Heidegger. From the start, Beckett affected to read in his own way, at his own pace, and without any regard for the fashionable or canonical. Reading Schopenhauer as he was struggling to complete Proust, his normalien friends thought the philosopher totally passé:

I am reading Schopenhauer. Everyone laughs at that. Beaufret and Alfy [Alfred Péron] etc. But I am not reading philosophy, nor caring whether he is right or wrong or a good or worthless metaphysician. An intellectual justification of unhappiness—the greatest that has been attempted—is worth the examination of one who is interested in Leopardi and Proust rather than in Carducci and Barrès.14

It did not matter then that Schopenhauer stank of fin de siècle nihilism: he brought 707 something home to Beckett, and not just because he was a “pessimist.” No doubt, this has to do with Schopenhauer’s insistence that philosophy should be as well written as literature. Schopenhauer’s attacks on Hegel were based on issues of style, and Nietzsche followed him to the point that he said that Schopenhauer had “educated” him at least as a stylist.

Kant could not be taken as a stylist, and he too was unfashionable for young French philosophers who had rejected the staid neo-kantianism of their teachers in the 1920s. Nevertheless, Kant can provide an entrypoint for a philosophy-inflected reading of Beckett. That said, Critchley, with whom I am usually in agreement, rejects any connection with Kant. Criticizing P. J. Murphy’s analysis, he writes, “I find some of the alleged parallels between Kant and Beckett, particularly on the question of synthetic a priori judgments and the transcendental imagination . . . slightly impressionistic and less than convincing” (VLAN, 257). Let us then return, then, to Myskja’s contention that one can make sense of Beckett’s radical departures, self-contradictions, and parodies in terms of the Kantian Sublime.

Myskja asks with some trepidation: “How can this form of self-negation give rise to the feeling of the sublime?” (SKB, 280). We can all agree that the Sublime presupposes that the imagination will be unable to form a stable picture of the plot or characters, while enjoying something that comes close to sacred terror. There is indeed a lot to gain from the Sublime, as Myskja argues: “The claim I want to make is the following. The sublime is experienced by a feeling connected to a ‘reality’ beyond the empirical world. This feeling indicates human freedom from the regularity of causal laws and social norms . . . because we are self-legislating as Kant says” (SKB, 59).

I will attempt to examine whether Beckett’s references to Kant warrant these assumptions. Thus I will focus on Kant, but a Kant taken out of the archive. One obvious place to begin is with Beckett’s notes in the library at Trinity College, Dublin.15 Given that most of the time Beckett simply copied from textbooks by Archibald Alexander, Wilhelm Windelband, and John Burnet, one might think to examine these directly, avoiding the notoriously difficult handwriting between the typed sections. Yet, I’d like to concentrate on Kant, and on a particular moment of Beckett’s note-taking process, precisely because something different takes place there.

Beckett’s notes on Kant, taken in the British Museum in 1933, all come from Windelband’s A History of Philosophy, with Especial Reference to the Formation and Development of its Problems and Conceptions (1893, revised 1901). Strikingly, amidst Beckett’s often passive, even school-boyish, note-taking was his single departure from his Windelband’s reading of Kant. When the History of Philosophy summarizes Kant’s notion of the moral law, Beckett matches him, writing:

The problem arises whether there is a practical synthesis a priori, i.e. necessary and universally valid objects of willing; i.e. anything which the reason makes its end a priori, without regard to empirical maxims; i.e. a moral law. This activity of the reasons, if there be any such, must appear as a command, in the form of an imperative, distinct from the empirical maxims of will and action. The true rational will cannot be determined by the particular objects and relations of experience but by itself only. It is necessarily directed toward something other than the natural impulses, and this something, required by the moral law as over against the inclinations is duty.16

Just under this, Beckett adds: “Tarataratata!” The ironical exclamation betrays Beckett’s impatience with what he sees as moralistic repression of impulse.

A little later, he inserts an exclamation mark in parenthesis after “Man does his duty out of reverence for himself”(HP, 553). However, Beckett does not quote the end of the sentence, to which I will return: “and in his intercourse with his fellow-man he should make it his supreme maxim, never to treat him as a mere means for the attainment of his own ends, but always to honour in him the worth of personality” (HP, 553).

He continued taking notes, shortening Windelband’s text and each time substituting for “moral law” his own abbreviation of “C. I.” for Categorical Imperative:

“Essence of this system the contrast between duty and inclination. Only that will done, only man’s duty is moral. Empirical impulses ethically indifferent, but become bad as soon as they oppose C. I. Hence life of man consists in his realising C. I. in its warfare against his inclinations” (TCD 10967/230 verso; HP, 554). But he adds in brackets: “But what the G. L. are the maxims?” for a passage not copied; “only acts of will done in conformity to duty, and wholly out of regard for maxims.”

This demonstrates Beckett’s attentiveness as a reader. Indeed, in Windelband’s account, nowhere can one find a definition of these maxims. It may not have helped Beckett much to find in Kant’s second Critique the pithy, “A maxim is the subjective principle of volition.”17

Obviously frustrated with Windelband’s summary, Beckett continues. Then he reaches the italicized statement, “Virtue alone is worthy of happiness”(HP, 556). Beckett copies Windelband’s explanation: “If, therefore, the ethical consciousness requires the reality of highest good, faith must reach beyond the empirical life of man, and beyond the order of Nature, on into the supersensuous. It postulates a reality of personality which extends beyond the temporal existence—the Immortal life—and a moral order of the universe, which is grounded in a Supreme Reason—in God.” (HP, 556). Beckett simply changes the first sentence to “requires another skip into the supersensuous” (TCD 10967/231 verso). The word “skip” is already ironical, but on the same line, Beckett adds the sarcastic French remark, “Rien que ça.” The deflation of the understatement is wonderful; “skip,” drolly summing up Windelband’s ponderous “beyond the order of Nature, on into the supersensuous” leads us to a sense of distrust facing such exorbitant moral demands.

Beckett’s subsequent notes are more spasmodic, completing only two pages of sketchy notes on the third Critique. But in this too he follows the example of Windelband, who, as a typical late-nineteenth-century Neo-Kantian, is primarily engaged with the first two Critiques, and has little invested in the third, merely skimming it in his History of Philosophy. Nevertheless, the last section he devotes to Kant is entitled “Natural Purposiveness” (§ 40) and explains competently the distinction between the Beautiful and the Sublime. This analysis, although quite canonical, doesn’t deem to engage Beckett’s attention, at least if we judge by the notes he takes. One might 709 conclude that by 1933 he rejected the concept of the Sublime along with any “skip” towards a reality that is postulated beyond the empirical world. What remains to be seen is whether he was able to find in Kant a treatment of the feeling that indicates human freedom; that which makes us forget “the regularity of causal laws and social norms” and reach an awareness of our own beings as self-legislators (cf. SKB, 59).

In Kant we find a well-known philosopher whom Beckett could be seen to read against and with. Given his initial coolness towards Kantian thought, it may come as a surprise to see Beckett decide to buy the collected works of Kant—in German—in 1938. Beckett wrote to McGreevy on January 5: “The entire works of Kant arrived from Munich. I had to go away beyond Gare de l’Est to collect them. I haven’t had time to open them, two immense parcels that I could hardly carry from customs to taxi.”18 The next day Beckett was stabbed in the street by a pimp, which prevented him from opening his new books. By May Beckett was sufficiently recovered to write: “I read nothing and write nothing, unless it is Kant (de nobis ipsis silemus) and French anacreontics” (L, 1, 622). His immersion went on for a while, but at a slow pace: “No work. I read an average of an hour a day, after an hour the illusion of comprehension ceases, Kant, Descartes, Johnson, Renard and a kindergarten manual of science” (L, 1, 643).

But this slow burning study explains why there are so many allusions to Kant in the “Whoroscope notebook,” which is thus datable to 1938 rather than 1934. I will return to these soon, but want first to suggest a more general use of Kant for Beckett’s development as a writer. As C. J. Ackerely has noted, the reference to “das fruchtbare Bathos des Erfahrung” that we find at the end of Watt sends us back to Kant but comes directly from the “Whoroscope notebook,” in which Beckett has: “das fruchtbare Bathos der Erfahrung (Kant)” and then adds “Bathos: deep (Gr.)”19 This remark, quoted in Watt’s Addenda—“das fruchtbare Bathos der Erfahrung” (the fruitful bathos of experience)—sends us back to the Prolegomena to Any Future Metaphysics.20 There Kant uses “Bathos” in the literal sense of “deep” or “low” place. Beckett’s “deep,” then, plays on “Bathos” in its literal and literary senses. Bathos is a term that was made popular by Pope, who described it as the “ridiculous and failed sublime.”21 And it is more than likely that Beckett was aware of the eighteenth-century discussion of the Sublime and the Bathetic.

In his Prolegomena, Kant makes it quite clear that he wants to avoid any unnecessary flight into the heights of metaphysical fancy or speculation, but keep things grounded:

Note: High towers and the metaphysically-great man resembling them, around both of which there is usually much wind, are not for me. My place is the fertile bathos of experience; and the word: transcendental—whose signification, which I indicated so many times, was not once caught by the reviewer (so hastily had he looked at everything)—, does not signify something that surpasses all experience, but something that indeed precedes experience (a priori), but that, all the same, is destined to nothing more than solely to make cognition from experience possible. If these concepts cross beyond experience, their use is then called transcendent, which is distinguished from the immanent use (i.e. use limited to experience). All misinterpretations of this kind have been sufficiently forestalled in the work itself; but the reviewer found his advantage in misinterpretations.22

710 Beckett extracts from Ernst Cassirer’s introduction to Kant, which formed the final volume of the edition that Beckett bought, the idea that Kant’s third Critique reformulated old metaphysical questions about the world. Instead of asking: “What is it?”23 Kant asks simply but also radically: “How can it be, what are the conditions of our perception or understanding of this object?” This launches a new investigation of things, especially if we keep in mind the issue of their finality. The question of possibility now conditions the question about the end(s). In the Prolegomena, Kant rejects the imputation of idealism that his critic Christian Garve had given against him in a hostile review of January, 1782. Kant distinguishes his system from that of Descartes (skeptical idealism) and that of Berkeley (dogmatic idealism) by launching what he calls “formal idealism” or “critical idealism” (P, 126–7). He concludes his counter-attack thus: “that clearly proves that my would-be judge has not correctly understood the least bit of it, and what’s more, has not correctly understood himself” (P, 127). The role of Fritz Mauthner’s “Sprachkritik”—an old chestnut in Beckett studies— is also significant here. References to the linguistic skepticism of Beiträge zu einer Kritik der Sprache intersperse Beckett’s notes on Kant throughout the “Whoroscope notebook.” Mauthner, I hypothesize, clarified Kant’s language-critique for Beckett, associating it with the kind of learned ignorance that has become iconically Beckettian.

From Kant’s work, one could deduce an unassailable critical epistemology of the “notknowing” (eine unangreifbare kritische Erkenntnistheorie des Nichtwissens), that would be even freer than the once famous docta ignorantia of Nicolas of Cusa. In his negative thinking Kant is already the destroyer of everything (der Alleszerstruemmerer); we bow before the intellect which in its strongest moments started the gigantic work, which was necessary to perform as a self-deconstruction of language or speech (als Selbstzersetzung der Sprache oder des Denkens).24

Returning to our subject, Beckett’s reference to Bathos in the “Whoroscope notebook” is followed by a long quotation from a passage in the second volume of the Beiträge. Curiously, it concerns the issue of animal language and freedom:

We should not think that we are freer than animals with our language. Surely, animals are under the yoke of necessity in their orientation. The chain can be longer or shorter, according to whether the senses of perception carry far or not; it is by this chain that necessity drags the amoeba exactly as it does with the intelligent dog. And the chain of man who is richly endowed with sense is so long that he thinks he is free. (BKS, 532)

Beckett transcribes this into the notebook, following Mauthner’s discussion of writing, a discussion that expands, curiously, the notion that freedom of the will is always, for animals and men alike, an “optical illusion” (BKS, 533).

Thus, three elements can be brought together: the Mauthnerian critique of language, the critique of the illusion of freedom, and the central function ascribed to writing. These are combined in a short text written by a disciple of Kant, Christian Jacob Kraus, who, in Kant’s name, reviewed Johann Ulrich’s Eleutheriologie: a severe critique of Kant 711 in science’s name.25 Ulrich, a professor of philosophy at Jena and disciple of Leibniz, combined determinism and rationalism in a theory that covered science and philosophy. He denounced what he took to be a contradiction involving Kant’s “Bathos der Erfahrung.” Ulrich argued that Kant opposed radical determinism by postulating that intellectual reason needs an absolute freedom—freedom becomes an absolute principle in the second Critique—when applied to practical issues. By contrast, Ulrich denied that morality requires absolute freedom, refusing timeless and abstract categories like the “categorical imperative.” If freedom remains an ideal, the key question becomes the possibility of mediation between abstract, absolute categories and the daily demands of experience. Should one not take chance occurrence into account? Using Kant’s own notes, Kraus tried to show that his friend had not overlooked the link between the practical and the theoretical, asking how one can reconcile an insight into natural necessity, by nature determined, with the “unconditional spontaneity” of understanding that alone can lead us to the world of morality. The very question proves that for these thinkers—and Beckett subsequently—the first two Critiques cannot be dissociated, or perhaps, that one needs the third Critique to reconcile their theses.

In Eleutheriologie, Ulrich sees a limited determinism as common to moral actions and scientific endeavors. By contrast, Kant and Kraus assert that nothing can be known of freedom: it is not an object in the world but a pure principle. Both reiterate the need for principles that are eternally valid, and not limited by contingent experience. Freedom is absolute because it is constitutive, exactly as time and space are constitutive, of our perception of the empirical world. Scientific determinism will thus never find a secure foundation in a vague providence. As fascinating as this debate is in its own right, I would like to suggest here that this is where Beckett took the Greek word for freedom, eleutheria, from, and used it as the title of his first completed play. Indeed, he thus revived in French a dramatic urge that had fizzled out in English with the uncompleted “Human Wishes.”

As always for Beckett, a concept is also a signifier whose shape is as important as its meaning. Thus éleuthéromane, which Beckett initially wanted to use as the play’s title (DF, 331), calls up “elation,” but also something more sinister like a mania, or hysteria. If eleutheromania is a disease we need to see how it functions within our Kantian coordinates. What I find most interesting in Eleutheria is Beckett’s innovative decision to have the stage divided in two parts, with shifting sides. Thus when we read the first act, we tend to forget that Victor, the “absent” object of the attentions of all the other characters, is actually on stage. The play revolves around a riddle: why is Victor, whose parents are rich and apparently concerned for him, unable to talk to them, to see them or to live a normal life? The answer, we soon discover, lies in his “eleutheromania.”

Victor’s self-negating statements make him a living riddle: “If I was dead I wouldn’t know I was dead. I want to squeeze pleasure out of my death. That’s where freedom lies: seeing oneself dead.”26 These trigger the mounting aggression of the Spectator, who even brings along the Chinese torturer Tchoutchi. Victor only speaks when the Glazier reminds him that he is really going to be tortured. The fact that torture and bathos can

712 be combined shows to which point the Kantian and the Mauthnerian categories have been spliced and merged. For ultimately, “bathos,” it seems, defines best the tone of the ending of Eleutheria, much more than the Kantian sublime that would give access to pure or moral freedom. Victor’s final fate remains bathetic, and can never reach sublimity. Beckett’s first French play is a farce that takes as its point of departure the pathetic consequences of a young man’s decision to be absolutely free, no matter what the consequences are for him and for the lives of his parents and lover. “Bathos” is not just a “bad pathos,” as Pope would have it, but also a “low pathos,” if pathos is seen to refer to a whole emotional spectrum, from the lofty to the obscene. Such bathos includes the human body caught in its baser functions. Thus, “epistemophilia” and eleutheromania can converge. Their motto is not the tragic awareness of a Sophocles who takes “pathos mathos” (one learns after one has suffered, or one needs to suffer in order to learn something) as a principle. Beckett leads us to a new “bathos mathos”: one learns after one has gone both to the heights and to the depths of anything. This would suggest that Beckett’s evolution could be seen as parallel to that of Kant. His first “critique” consisted in rejecting the representational logic and classical fiction: it was his critique of representation. This corresponds roughly to a pre-war Beckett, still very much an avantgardist in the Transition mode. The second moment is a critique of practical reason. It entails a meditation not so much on epistemology or the question of truth, via a “What is this?” leading to Aletheia, Heidegger’s notion of truth as “unconcealment,” as an exploration of the dialectics of freedom. Here, obviously, Eleutheria has replaced Aletheia. In Murphy as in Eleutheria, the central issue is an aporia generated by an insistence on the free possession of one’s self. Hence, Murphy is caught between his mind and his body, his sexual desire and his regressive psychosis. Only a form of law will halt this psychosis. (In Watt, this is the function of Mr. Knott, who evokes Kant in many ways. The moral law is, after all, a way of formalizing what one may “not” do.) Beckett explained his interrogation of the “madness of Reason” in an interview with Michael Haerdter:

The crisis started with the end of the 17th century, after Galileo. The eighteenth century has been called the century of reason, le siècle de la raison. I’ve never understood that: they’re all mad, ils sont tous fous, ils déraisonnent! They give reason a responsibility which it simply can’t bear, it’s too weak. The Encyclopedists wanted to know everything . . . But that direct relation between the self and—as the Italians say—lo scibile, the knowable, was already broken.27

What Beckett discerns here is that Sade’s cruelty was the exact reverse of Kant’s formalism.28 In this spirit, Watt relates,

But our particular friends were the rats, that dwelt by the stream. They were long and black. We brought them tidbits from our ordinary as rinds of cheese, and morsels of gristle, and we brought them also birds’ eggs, and frogs, and fledglings. Sensible of these attentions, they would come flocking round us at our approach . . . then we would sit down in the midst of them, and give them to eat, out of our hands, of a nice fat frog, or a baby thrush.

Or seizing suddenly a plump young rat . . . we would feed it to its mother, or its father, or 713

its sister, or to some less fortunate relative.

It was on these occasions, we agreed, after an exchange of views, that we came nearest

to God.

(W, 132–3)

The main characteristics of Sadean mischief and perversity are here on display. Rousseau’s pastoral is turned upside down. A moment earlier, Watt and Sam had been frolicking in lush meadows, apparently enjoying the charms of Mother Nature. Like Sade, Sam and Watt forge a conflicted relationship to this particular mother, and demonstrate that its fundamental law is murder. And the object of the transgressive gesture itself is parenthood. Beckett thus subverts the foundational notion of the family as the site of morality. Not unlike the libertine’s endless exactions and tortures, Sam’s and Watt’s actions shift vertiginously from a sham kindness to animals, to cruelty; surprising the poor creatures that had believed in their benevolence. Like Sade’s hyper-discursive torturers, the two friends “reason” upon their actions.

Like Adorno and Horkheimer’s Dialectic of Enlightenment, a near-contemporary critique of reason’s madness, Beckett’s sadism inverts the transcendence of Law above humanity.29 For Adorno and Horkheimer, Kantian reason leads to the calculating rationalism of a totalitarian order. Its counterpart is the systematic mechanization of pleasures in Sade’s perverse utopias. The Critique of Practical Reason stresses the autonomy and self-determination of the moral subject, and thereby defines the pure form of ethical action. Indeed, in Kant, enlightenment philosophy meets global capitalism with a vengeance: any human concern has to be ruled out, in favor of reason’s conformity to its own laws, a reason that must then appear abstract and devoid of any object. Consequently, all “human” affects are pushed further away from an independent and all powerful reason. Sade’s Juliette is more logical than Kant himself when she concludes that the bourgeois order of society justifies crime, providing that crime is regulated by a rationality that controls all activities and pleasures. The Sadean goal of “apathy,” achieved via sexual excess and vice, functions akin to Kantian disinterestedness, and both are underpinned by the brutal efficiency of the bourgeois conquest of the world. The “right to enjoyment” logically includes an absolute extension of its field, including my right to use the bodies of others in any way that will give me pleasure. The counterpart of this globalized rationality is the systematic mechanization of perverse pleasures in the Sadean orgy. Hence, Barthes’s observation that the orgy aims to be a model of mechanical efficiency in which everyone has a part to play, and nobody can be left solitary or idle.30

In his Metaphysics of Morals, Kant analyzes the importance of ownership, with special regard to sexuality. Here are some notorious definitions:

Sexual union (commercium sexuale) is the reciprocal use that one human being makes of the sexual organs and capacities of another (usus membrorum et facultatum sexualium alterius) . . . For the natural use that one makes of the other’s sexual organs is enjoyment, for which one gives itself up to the other. In this act a human being makes himself into a thing, which conflicts with the right of humanity in his own person. There is only one condition under which this is possible: that while one person is acquired by the other as if it were a thing, the one who is acquired acquires the other in turn.31

The involuntarily comedy of marriage presented as mutual commodification returns us to the bathos implied by Kant’s formalism: the law is the law only if it is devoid of content. Any other human consideration must be excluded. What matters is the simple conformity of practical reason to maxims that become as many general axioms. Here is the equivalent formulation of Sade’s law according to Jacques Lacan: “Proclaiming the law of jouissance as the foundation of some ideally utopian social system, Sade expresses himself in italics . . . ‘Lend me the part of your body that will give me a moment of satisfaction and, if you care to, use for your own pleasure that part of my body which appeals to you.’”32 Sade wishes to turn the subject inside out, like a glove. Only then can the subject of the fantasy come close to what he or she imagines to be the jouissance of God; a God seen as the Dio boia of Ulysses, a “hangman god,” a supreme being unsurpassable in evil.

Am I so far from Nussbaum’s analysis when I present this negative theology, or anti-theology, used by Sade and Beckett, to excavate the human subject? Perhaps not. However, one should account for links between this negative theology and the practice of a different writing. Pierre Klossowski’s book on Sade had insisted on this aspect, stressing that Sade was not a “pervert” or a monster, but above all a writer. A boring and repetitive writer, for sure, but an author in the strong sense. To use Foucault’s term, he is a “founder of discursivity,” whose texts allow us to understand the crucial link between a perverse imagination and the Law understood as the jouissance of the Other. Klossowski writes:

The parallelism between the apathetic reiteration of acts and Sade’s descriptive reiteration again establishes that the image of the act to be done is re-presented each time not only as though it had never been performed but also as though it had never been described. This reversibility of the same process inscribes the presence of non-language in language; it inscribes a foreclosure of language by language.33

Sade’s symptom is not sadism but writing itself, a writing that hesitates between the repetitive fantasy of an outrage against Mother Nature and a reiterated questioning of the jouissance of God.

A transgression that questions the limits of humanity and derides the Law presiding over its limits requires a different kind of writing. Such a writing could be exemplified by the “serialist” writing deployed in Watt (by “serialist” I mean the series of contradictory epithets or attributes undoing representation as such). This language relentlessly questions the limits of language while channelling Sade’s infantile rage against the mother’s body. Like Sade’s texts, Watt often triggers the hilarious serenity of pseudo-rationality. The ultimate locus of Truth would be Mr. Knott, and the sequences of his confusing attributes turn him less into a God of parody than into a debased father embodying indifference or ataraxy facing pain:

Watt suffered neither from the presence of Mr. Knott, nor from his absence. When he 715

was with him, he was content to be with him, and when he was away from him, he was

content to be away from him. Never with relief, never with regret, did he leave him at

night, or in the morning come to him again.

This ataraxy covered the entire house-room, the pleasure-garden, the vegetable garden,

and of course Arthur. (W, 179–80)

Ataraxy would thus combine the Sadean ideal of impassability or indifference facing the most extreme enjoyments mentioned by Maurice Blanchot, and Kant’s ideal of self-regulating reason. The central question of Watt would thus be a Sado-Kantian one: “But what was this pursuit of meaning in this indifference to meaning?” (W, 62).

By a curious inversion, the Kantian novel of Watt thus produces a linguistic machine that stages thought as torture or the torture of thought. Rational knowledge barely hides relations of fear and domination: “Too fearful to assume himself the onus of a decision, said Mr. Hackett, he refers it to the frigid machinery of a time-space relation” (W, 15). This is how Sade and Beckett denounce the seamy side of universalistic ethics. Again, key-terms such as “purpose,” or indeed “purposelessness,” underpin Watt: “And what is this coming that was not our coming and this being that is not our being and this going that will not be our going but the coming and being and going in purposelessness” (W, 48–9). Here is why, as we have seen, Kant’s famous “purposiveness without a purpose,” the main principle by which Beauty is known to man in the third Critique, appears in the second part of Molloy.

We should not downplay the ironic ring given to the formulation of a principle that basically asserts that there is meaning in the world, even in the most innocent forms of beauty like flowers in the mountains. Let us remember that Moran wishes to work for serious reasons; his motivation is “more serious and imputable less to pleasure than to business” (M, 115). He adds: “For it was only by transferring it to this atmosphere, how shall I say, of finality without end, why not, that I could venture to consider the work I had on hand” (M, 115). In his book on Kant and Beckett, refuting Nussbaum’s incorrect reading, Myskja makes sense of this passage: Moran needs to withdraw from the “spray of phenomena” first (M, 115). He only sees his goal when he is able to move on to a purely disinterested level.

If the Kantian paradox works better in French with “finalité sans fin,” what it asserts is that the aim of the quest is not the object (Molloy) but the establishment of a link.34 This link is both a “report” and a “rapport”; a connection more explicit still in French where “rapport” covers both meanings. It is both a narrative and an intellectual connection. The establishment of such a double “rapport” also implies a consideration of natural beauty as defined by Kant’s judgment on the beautiful, a judgment that attributes it a Zweckmässigkeit ohne Zweck. What the phrase means, ultimately, is that a reflective judgment (a judgment based on the perception of any beautiful object in nature) leads one to deduce that the world has the form of a teleology, even if that object itself is not necessarily teleological. If I can perceive beauty, then I will sense that the world is not absurd even if it is full of absurd people, absurd tasks, absurd commands, absurd objects. This is clear in Molloy, which states:

For where Molloy could not be, nor Moran either for that matter, there Moran could bend over Molloy. And though this examination prove unprofitable and of no utility for the execution of my orders, I should nevertheless have established a kind of connection, and one not necessarily false. For the falsity of the terms does not necessarily imply that of the relation, so far as I know. (M, 115–6)

This link between beauty and duty will not be made more explicit anywhere in Molloy. The Kantian proof of meaning via a perception of natural beauty does surface in Three Dialogues with Georges Duthuit, albeit obliquely, via Freud: “The stars are undoubtedly superb, as Freud remarked on reading Kant’s cosmological proof of the existence of God.”35

In New Introductory Lectures on Psychoanalysis Freud made room for the aesthetic argument that the beauty of the world could justify the invention of a God, even though no divine handiwork is discernible in the distribution of moral conscience among human beings. Beckett follows this skeptical lead, gently poking fun at Kant’s philosophy. But he seriously takes over the same term of “relation,” in the sense defined by Moran in Molloy, when he concludes the dialogues with his passionate statement that “to be an artist is to fail, as no other dare fail” (PTD, 125). This principle of failure—much as Moran fails to find Molloy yet finds Molloy in himself—becomes the new term of a “relation” of which the other term is the artist. “Failure” has replaced what was first posited as the object, or “representee,” and faces the same “representer” or “artist.” In a few pages, Beckett has scored a Kantian trick, whereby a question about the form of teleology invoked by aesthetics leads to an ethical principle entailing the duty to represent what cannot be represented. One might say that this condenses the link between aesthetics and ethics in Beckett, and it has a strong Kantian ring, yet it owes nothing to the analysis of the sublime.

Developing Alain Badiou’s ideas about torture and language, Slavoj Žižek cited the Austrian Nobel Laureate Elfriede Jelinek, who had stated in her Stockholm address:

“Language should be tortured to tell the truth.” Hence, language should be twisted, denaturalized, extended, condensed, cut and reunited, made to work against itself. Language as the “big Other” is not an agent of wisdom to whose message we should attune ourselves, but a place of cruel indifference and stupidity. The most elementary form of torturing one’s language is called poetry—imagine what a complex form like sonnet does to language: it forces the free flow of speech into a Procrustean bed of a fixed shape of rhythm and rhymes.36

This might suggest an important link between Beckett and this Austrian tradition, which includes Jelinek, Thomas Bernhard, and the film director Michael Haneke. The assimilated Austro-Hungarian Jew Fritz Mauthner was also undoubtedly a predecessor. It is in such a context that I’d like to question the cliché launched by Beckett himself that he had taken the opposite road from that of Joyce: whereas Joyce believed in his power over language, Beckett wanted to work with ignorance and impotence. This is a well-known topos of Austrian thinking, which begins with Hugo von Hofmannsthal’s famous “Letter” by Lord Chandos.

This Austrian idea suggests that form—often allegorized as music—will provide 717 the only possible synthesis between systematic meaning and emotional groping in the dark. When Beckett stated that he was no philosopher, he implied that, for him, the experience of thinking was closer to music than ratiocination. Such a musical thinking would be determined as much by form as by content The “brilliant forms” generated without a pre-requisite concern for truth will nevertheless appear capable of conveying dark or dire truths. These truths act on us because they open holes in what we take to be our knowledge of the world. Thus Beckett’s texts “think” all the time, and they do so dynamically, via performative perforations that play with philosophical axioms culled from authors like Kant, transform them and finally make them radiate with a dark glow combining laughter and a heightened sense of form. This is how they create both beauty and meaning. We witness how formal beauty can lead to deeper excavations, digging into the mind’s darkness and the opacity of language. One might say that Beckett’s texts always “think” even when they don’t; that is, even when they play the games of empty formalism. One lesson that Beckett learned from Kant is that formalism may be empty but it is never pointless. This is why he would testify to the power of language; to the piercing, drilling force of words whose negativity will, once in a while, renew the imagination. This feat is an immense help whenever we are tempted to doubt that life is worth living.
