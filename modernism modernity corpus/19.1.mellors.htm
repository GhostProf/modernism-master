Autopsia: Olson,Themis, Pausanias

Anthony Mellors

How different might the film narratives of Clash of the Titans have been had they followed Jane Harrison’s reading in Themis. The Titans, she says, were originally men who daubed themselves with white clay (from titanos, white clay or gypsum) in order to perform initiation rites associated with the passage from child-to man-hood; only later, ‘when their meaning is forgotten’, do they come to be regarded as mythological giants.1 The original Titans had the role of symbolically murdering the Kouretes, young men figured as daimones or attendants of the gods (Kouros is another name for Zeus) who invoke Themis and Dike, the spirits of a time before society and religion became differentiated. According to Harrison, this symbolic death is the basis of the boys’ rebirth into the tribe: until each boy has put away his childhood, he cannot become ‘socialized, part of the body politic.’2

Later in Themis, Harrison attends to the mythological presentation of the Titans familiar from Hesiod’s Theogony. Here, the Titans are understood as ‘fertility-daimones’, distinguished from the mythological Giants by being potencies of the sky rather than the earth: ‘In Homer and Hesiod they, unlike the Giants, are always Gods, Titenes qeoi. They are constantly being driven down below the earth to nethermost Tartarus and always re-emerging. The very violence and persistence with which they are sent down below shows that they belong up above.’3 The Gigantes are children of Gaia (earth) and the Titans children of Gaia and Ouranos (sky), though they are more sky than earth; unlike the Giants, they represent the region of ta metewra, the aither, becoming gods of the sun and moon. Harrison’s point is that this separation into a Gigantomachia and a Titanomachia

Harrison regards the fight against the Giants as naturally and ethically defensible: ‘[i]nstinctively a healthy stock will purge its religion from elements exclusively phallic. This expurgation ranks as first and foremost among the services Olympianism rendered to Greece.’4 Censure of the Titans, however, she considers to be something of an error, only explainable by the hostility (as Herodotus tells us) of the hoi polloi to a natural philosophy associated with barbarians (i.e. Persians). The worship of the powers of the upper air, she says, led to ‘exact observation’, therefore to mathematics, astronomy, and the doctrines of Sokrates and Herakleitos, as well as to mysticism and pantheism; so while the Olympians were right to reject or reform Earth-worship, they were wrong not to have embraced the new natural philosophy. A philosophy, that is, that was the development of an archaic sensibility. For Harrison, the Olympian spirit is the tendency to personalize and intellectualize the gods, so that they cease to represent the mystery and complexity of life and become objets d’art. Olympianism becomes reactionary, she argues, in its resistance to the influence of Persian religion in the sixth century B.C., when Orphism, with its ‘imported elements of Oriental and mainly Iranian nature-worship and formal mysticism’ reformed the archaic Earth-religion by broadening it into a cosmogony: ‘it fought the Giants but joined forces with the Titans.’5 The distinction between Giants and Titans, therefore, means far more than a scholarly footnote. For Harrison, it attests to a crucial turning point in the history of human consciousness: individualism, the sense of subjective separateness, appears with the breakup of totemistic societies; but when a more sophisticated cosmogony develops, one that seeks to integrate the new subject with the old Animism, individualism, spurred by its ‘barren’ drive for immortality, attempts to suppress it. In rejecting the primitive, Olympian individualism cannot go beyond itself to return to group consciousness. The retreat is into ‘mind’ as against ‘life’, self-consciousness instead of action. What Harrison calls ‘primitive consciousness’

for the most part lacks this sense of segregation, because it is mainly involved in activities, in ‘doing things’. The things of which the savage is mainly conscious are not envisaged as external objects, they are parts of his doing, of his ‘warm stream of human consciousness’. We have already seen this in the case of weapons and tools which are felt as extensions of personality. Your stick is part of your act of brandishing, or of your sense of walking. You are not conscious of it as a stick until you let it fall. The sense of action, of relation, is vivid and submerges subject and object.6

In contrast, the ‘chill remoteness’ of the Olympians make them ‘objects to a subject, they are concepts thrown out of the human mind, looked at from a distance, things known, not like the mystery gods felt and lived… 7

Eccentric though Harrison’s ideas were regarded by her peers, they are a classic 97 statement of modernist yearning for an organic lifeworld that would reunite archaic mysticism with scientific progress, fusing subject and object without losing sight of the distinctiveness of either. When supplemented by the occult typology of Carl Jung, this rationally irrationalist theory indebted to Nietzsche, Bergson, Levy-Bruhl, and Durkheim had by the 1950s and 60s become axiomatic of late modernist thinking about ‘mythic consciousness’. We know from the scholarship of George Butterick, Tom Clark, and Ralph Maud, among others, that Charles Olson was an avid reader of Harrison, and the passages just quoted from Themis read like passages from Projective Verse and The Special View of History. Yet there is surprisingly little recognition in commentaries on Olson’s work of Harrison’s influence. The tendency is to defer the questions of history and cosmogony to Olson’s obsession with Whitehead’s Process and Reality. For example, in an essay called ‘Charles Olson: Mythologist of History’, William McPheron makes Whitehead the primary influence on Olson’s history of subjectivity, even when discussing an early work such as Call me Ishmael.8 Yet Olson wasn’t to read Process and Reality until 1956. He had studied Jane Harrison before that, but not until 1946, after he had written Call Me Ishmael. McPheron’s analysis suggests reference to Harrison as well as Whitehead, but there is none. Similarly, Robert von Hallberg writes that one of Whitehead’s ‘basic points is that the division into subject and object, substance and quality, particular and universal “does violence to that immediate experience which we express in our actions…” ’9 Here, there’s no necessity for von Hallberg to mention Harrison, and he doesn’t; yet in the context of Olson’s ‘archaeology’ the similarities between Process and Reality and Themis are significant enough to require comment. Where Olson diverges from Whitehead, he also parts company with Harrison. He places Socrates firmly in the Olympian camp as a figure partly responsible for the sins of the ratio. This hardly fits with Whitehead’s platonism, even when—as von Hallberg argues—something like Plato’s theory of forms gets transmuted into Jungian archetypes in The Maximus Poems IV, V, VI. As we have seen, Harrison includes the occult in her notion of Titanism, yet she regards Socrates as part of the new consciousness too. It’s likely that Olson could not square mystic vision with rational philosophy, which in other works he was reading he saw treated as disjunct, for example in E. R Dodds’s The Greeks and the Irrational and Eric Havelock’s Preface to Plato.10 This is itself at odds with the rational pronouncements Olson derives from Whitehead. But he is rational when it suits him and irrational when he wants to be: from this equivocation stem all the inconsistencies and contradictions of his writing, not least the standoff between poetry and thesis, which we shall see to some extent corresponds with the categories of myth and history.

When Olson’s commentators do mention Harrison, it is only ever in terms of isolated nuggets of information about archaic civilization or as the source of Olson’s interest in myth as ritual practice. The crucial influence of Harrison’s Orphism passage and the notion of Themis as the ethical imperative behind the polis are passed over in favour of minor details.11 Butterick’s Guide is particularly thin in this respect, but perhaps it is hardly surprising that what references there are, are limited, since Olson himself shows

Olson called this 1963 poem ‘a reduction of Hesiod’, which meant stealings from Evelyn-White’s translation interspersed with mythic correlatives, especially from the Völuspa, the first poem of the Poetic Edda, and its prose elaboration by Snorri Sturluson.12 Building on the first and second ‘Maximus, from Dogtown’ poems, which introduce the theogonic figure of the Titan Okeanos, the poem recounts the aftermath of the battle between Zeus and the Titans and its ‘somewhat jejeune repetition’ in the battle between Zeus and the storm-monster Typhon.13 Okeanos, in Maximus the integrating figure of the ocean ‘9 times around / earth and sea’, occupies a space here that just about works with J. In the Theogony, Ouranos (Sky; Heaven) is castrated by his son Kronos in an act of revenge by the Titans for their imprisonment by the father within the mother, Gaia (Earth). Gaia herself furnishes the means by which Kronos commits the deed, and it is from the drops of blood that rain down on her from the severed member that she conceives the Giants, along with the Furies and the Nymphs. The Titans, then, are the ‘older gods’, preceding the Giants, and Aphrodite is the only daughter of sky and sea. Zeus is one of the children of Kronos, fated to usurp his father. So why doesn’t Butterick mention this? Perhaps because he doesn’t find enough evidence elsewhere that Harrison is one of Olson’s sources. To be sure, Olson shows no recognition of the distinction between the Titans and the Giants, as is evident from ‘A Work’ (1962), where ‘there are the wars of the Zeus and his brothers with the Titans, or Giants, who didn’t rebel with the brothers, and therefore insert a curious mixed evil set who trouble thereafter all the established edicts of heaven and confuse the general cosmology…’18 And Butterick repeats the elision when glossing the lines

If the cognate here is really cognate, it becomes hard to identify, as Harrison does, which Titans/Giants are gods and which are merely earthborn monsters and warriors. The confusion is replicated in various translations and accounts of the Theogony. Where G.

Apostolos Athanassikos, however, states that the firstborn imprisoned by Ouranos are the six sons and six daughters constituting the generation of the Titans. The Cyclopes and the Hundred-handers are not Titans,

100 and for Homer (Odyssey, 7.59) the Giants are merely a savage race of men.20 Since ‘Maximus, from Dogtown—IV’ acknowledges that Briareus and his brothers are the ‘guards’ of Tartaros, and Butterick explains that they are ‘the hundred-armed giants who fought with the Olympians against the Titans’, the only coherent explanation for the conflation of the Titans with the Giants is a generalized mythic distinction between the representatives of Earth and the usurping Olympians.21 Olson, then, is not concerned with setting two kinds of nature-worship, one phallic and one heavenly, against Olympian ‘intellectualism’, but one authentic archaic cosmogony against ‘this person / Zeus’—in other words, what G. The very origin of Indo-European man is located in Colchis, called Aia (‘the Land’) in Homer. By extension, the fight against the Titans/Giants/iotunns must be the fall from origin. The poem, therefore, becomes a lament for Tartaros as the source of archaic value, ‘but suddenly / a “loss” has been suffered: Tartaros / was once “ahead” of / Heaven was prior to / (in coming into being) this “child” / of Earth’.

But what of Themis? The ‘deeming’, which is also a ‘dooming’ is echoed in the next lines by the word ‘statutory’:

Tartaros in which all who have been by the statutory thrown down or overthrown, are kept watch on Night and Day

The Latin cognate of qemis is statutum. Doom, says Harrison, begins in convention and ‘ends in statutory judgement.’23 Are we to take it that Zeus’s triumph over his forebears is simply the Law, the inauguration of social order? Olson wrote the words ‘boundaries’ and ‘iotunns’ in the margin of a passage in Process and Reality that includes the proposition, ‘all social order depends on the statistical dominance in the environment 101 of occasions belonging to the requisite societies. The laws of nature are statistical laws derived from this fact.’24 In the poem, Olson refers to ‘the statistical Nebel / and “End of the World”…’ In Old Norse religion, Nebel is the home of the dead, linked with the destruction of the ordered cosmos. This associates the ‘Doomsday’ brought about by Zeus with the Norse Ragnarök, the Doom of the Gods. Depending on how one reads the syntax of the poem—which is always an issue with Olson’s mythic assemblage—Tartaros is located ‘beyond’ the statistical. Yet the point is surely that Zeus, as the bringer of Justice—by which is meant moral right rather than natural law—founds a new order which figures the dawn of individual as opposed to collective consciousness. This is nothing if not ‘statistical’. Harrison argues that the meaning of Themis is bound up with the ‘fall’ into social order; for the civilized Greeks, she claims, living without agreed convention is to exist barbarically. The Cyclopes, for example, are said to reverence the gods, trust that the earth will give them all they need, and cherish hearth and home; yet they are aqemistes, they have no sense of polis. For the ‘polis set itself to modify and inform all those primitive impulses and instincts that are resumed in Earth-worship.’25 Themis, therefore, personifies the Nomos, or convention, underlying all collective qua religious practices; she is more than the Physis, or nature, represented by her daughter Dike. Yet she encompasses the powers of nature, as well as the establishment of the agora, because she is the ‘mysterious’ impulse behind everything that is ‘greater than man’.26 Her law is broken only at the point when ‘religious man, instead of becoming in ecstasy and sacramental communion one with Bacchos, descends to the chill levels of intellectualism and asserts that there is an objective reality external to himself called Bacchos’.27 The lived experience of religion is not the same as belief in a god and may be diminished by the objectification of the infinite. Harrison regards that externalization as occurring with the shift from matrilinear to patriarchal rituals represented by the Olympians. Where Kronos stood for kingship founded on matrilinear principles, his son Zeus, influenced by the patrilinear northern gods, becomes the type of the father, the king as father, bespeaking an order based on the force of the individual rather than the mutual concession represented by Themis.

Themis, then, turns out to be a version of the Great Mother. In ‘Maximus, From Dogtown – II’, Olson writes that ‘the City is Mother - // Polis’ linked with the word MONOGENE, which Butterick shows comes from Jung’s discussion of Gnostic tradition in Psychology and Alchemy. The ‘metropolis’ is the Mother-City of the ‘monogenes’, the only-begotten son of God.28 Like Jung, Olson cannot conceive of an anima without an animus, and his reverence for the feminine principle is always phallic. This is why the ‘“Earth” mass mother milk cow body’ of ‘Dogtown – IV’ gets transformed into ‘our dear fatherland the Earth’. Caught between nostalgia for phallic Earth-worship and desire for the Olympian father, Maximus cannot accept the possibility of that other, Titanic—and proto-feminist—consciousness suggested by Themis.

Dreaming tends to be with associated with idleness and phantasy, but here it is an active principle. Olson does not use the romantic term ‘vision’; instead, he seeks to reclaim the dreamwork for Whitehead’s conjunction of subject and object, particular and universal, in the ‘eternal event’. The aligning of Thucydides with the age of mechanical reproduction wages war on the rationalist insistence on reporting an event as fact. According to J. A. K. Thomson, whose Art of the Logos Olson read in the early 50s, Thucydides made a false distinction between muthos, ‘fictitious narrative’, and logos, ‘truth’, determined to purge the mythical element from historical writing. For Herodotus, however, all reportage is a story, a muthologos, based on evidence gathered by the historian. The muthologos called ‘istorin ‘appears to mean “finding out for oneself”, instead of depending on hearsay.’30 By itself, this definition, much used by Olson and overmuch quoted by his commentators, doesn’t say a lot. After all, finding out for oneself hardly precludes gathering hearsay and doesn’t prevent one from mistaking hearsay for evidence. And if fact is subordinate to story—which might not be more than hearsay—evidence is shorn of the proof-value that makes it admissible. Olson’s own gloss is a little more forthcoming. His point is not just that Herodotus goes out and about ‘all over the goddamn Middle East and down into Egypt’ to discover as 103 much information as he can about the Persian War, but that the historian delves into the narrative traditions of the people who make war in order to understand evidence as archaeology, ideology, and even (in Robert Duncan’s pun) histology.31 This is the method of The Maximus Poems.

Nevertheless, Olson was always in danger of treating his own ‘istorin as being underwritten by ‘objective’ theories such as Whitehead’s. The tension between the self-certificated dream and the cosmic reality to which Olson wants to lay claim are never resolved. This is because his concept of myth tends toward the romantic and religious archetypology of Jung, which treats symbols as if they had universal, if occulted, significance, and bespeaks primordial reality. The disclosure or account (logos) of that reality, however, is frequently at odds with the multiform character of myths themselves; if myths themselves are genuinely a pre-rational way of explaining reality, like poetry they must defy assimilation by typological discourse. Olson understandably wants to erase this separation of mythos and logos, yet his writings equivocate between an assimilating discourse which spatializes and abstracts myth and a radical parataxis which reclaims something of its hybridity. The increasingly fragmentary use of ‘istorin as the basis for his poetry seemed perhaps the only way of remaining true to the heterogeneity of myth itself. At the same time, the naturism and magic Harrison defines as Orphic get ‘objectified’ as components of Olson’s personal traum:

The ‘voice of a sense of religion’ which he had projected to student disciples at Black Mountain College was now to find its home in a fetishlike analogical poetry transmitted as runic spell, inaccessible to the point of incomprehensibility to the general public, but to a small audience of acolytes nothing less than necessary data, the hermetic doctrines and protocols of ‘secret rites practiced by the initiates alone, just like mysteries.’32

The formal counterpart to this occult heuristic is the modernist belief that poetic texts, like myths, bespeak an order beyond themselves: associations, presented in fragmentary form, have the power to cohere, to cohere not in spite of being presented as fragments, but because they are fragments. The problem is that, while some individual myths are symbolically coherent, most are not; and while cross-cultural connections between myths do exist, the desire to find an underlying structural form tends to ignore historical and hermeneutic differences. Moreover, the allegorization involved in interpreting a given myth or set of related myths inevitably abstracts, not so much by reducing the ‘original’ text to a typology, but by producing an increasingly refined and complex intertext which throws up as many questions as it answers, so that the more one attempts to fix the amalgam of folktales, rituals, legends, sagas, exempla, etc. to generalizations based on religious, euhemeristic, and/or psychoanalytic categories, the more they slip away through holes in the expanding mythic fabric. In spite of all the pronouncements made by Olson and others about ‘wholeness’, the Maximus poems are too overdetermined—like the Theogony or any sophisticated mythic narrative—to yield to the kind of typological readings intended by their author.

104 All of which brings us back to the question of evidence. We have seen that Olson regards ‘finding out for oneself’ as a self-sufficient realism committed to discovering the universal in the local by way of mythologic association. History is what one does by prospecting for evidence—not truth—in whatever ‘objects’ itself into the field of vision. The subject’s intuitive, associative sensibility is what activates the object in a presentation that is a reaffirmation of the subject without—at least in theory—the interference of the ego. In this ragbag phenomenology, terms become imprecise because there is no system: ‘As Maximus, Olson makes no attempt at order or objectivity; he allows fact and story to enter the poems in the sequence and strength with which they come to his attention.’33 This depends on what we mean by ‘order’ and ‘objectivity’, for what Maximus contests are the very terms by which we rationalize events. Tracing ‘muthos’ and ‘logos’ back to their Homeric use is a key example of Olson’s attempt to return concepts to their pre-rational origins. In a similar manner, Heidegger attempts to re-evaluate the significance of logos in early Greek philosophy. Since antiquity, he argues, the term has been interpreted to mean Ratio, Verbum, cosmic law, and necessity in thought. But its origin is in ‘saying’, linked with legein, ‘to lay’; therefore

[i]t names the inexhaustible mystery that the speaking of language comes to pass from the unconcealment of what is present, and is determined according to the lying-before of what is present as the letting-lie-together-before. Will thinking finally learn to catch a glimpse of what it means that Aristotle could characterize legein as apojainesqai?34

Apophainesthai is the ‘showing forth’ that ‘Maximus, at the Harbor’ interprets as a different kind of laying, elevated to ‘the act which actuates the soul itself’. Like Olson, Heidegger contrasts this mysterious presencing or ontological difference with technologized thinking, which, he says

experiences beings as objects…It is through and for perception that the object comes to be a “standing against.” …Repraesentatio, representation [Vorstellung] is defined as the perceptive self-arrogation (to the self as ego) of what appears.35

Heidegger’s return to a non-conceptual, pre-rational way of thinking is theorized as theoria: ‘the highest realization of authentic praxis’.36 Yet there is nothing systematic about it. Its ‘mindful’ turn is not determined by modern notions such as intuition. Rather, it is a ‘method’, as long as we understand that term as meqodos (‘with(in)-a-path’), which is not a road already cut by man, but a ‘way’ suggested by things themselves as they show themselves to thinking. Thinking, then, is a form of travel that does not simply arrive at its destination; its openness is such that it may regress as well as progress from ‘origin’ to ‘arrival’.37 Unlike ‘technical-representational thinking’, which ‘forever wills to go forward and tears ahead of everything, paths which point out a way occasionally open upon a view of a solitary mountain shelter.’38 Olson would find the solitary mountain shelter lacking polis; yet the point is a serious one, because for all he shares with Heidegger in deconstructing rationalism, Olson is looking for an arrival that is public, political, a theoria that does not lead back to the nostalgic passivity which claims the philosopher in his rural idyll. Heidegger, though, suggests that the path to public 105 action is always in danger of turning into the road to the ratio; this is why his thinking retreats into wordy silence. From this perspective, the action Olson demands risks turning theoria into contemplatio, the ‘way’ of scientific instrumentalism, which means ‘to cut, to compartmentalize’ and is set against authentic praxis in ‘the beholding that watches over truth.’39 Terms become muddled: ‘truth’, for Heidegger, is ‘unconcealment’, whereas for Olson it belongs to the rational version of logos and needs to be traded for mythic saying; ‘contemplation’, usually associated with productive passivity (dream!?), now bespeaks the practical-instrumental. The Maximus poems are full of cuts,—from the separation of Ouranos and Gaia to ‘the Cut’, a toll canal representing Gloucester’s ‘pejorocracy’—eroticized landscapes which threaten and maintain Maximus’s individuation. Laying apart (‘Okeanos…screwing earth’), there is a sense in which Olson just won’t let letting-lie lie. Maximus is torn between Themis and Zeus.

It is not always easy to discern why Olson privileges certain concepts over others, as if their value as cardinal terms in the realignment of knowledge were self-evident. ‘Evidence’, which in Olson’s theory of knowledge stands in for something like Heideggerian truth, is particularly odd because while it means seeing—supporting the notion of finding out for oneself—it is rigorously empirical. At the same time as he wants the story, Olson wants factual proof; and, again, here is the modernist conceit: mythic method underwritten by rational imperative. Just as commentators on The Maximus Poems invoke Harrison’s work only in the most general terms, they never question what it means to be evidence. O. J. Ford, for example, writes that

[a]ll the poet needs is the evidence (Herodotus again), and the evidence is supplied by such careful scholars as the archaeologist, Jane Harrison, who uses relics and structures as keys to rituals and beliefs.40

Is careful scholarship really the key to muthologos? And why ‘Herodotus again’ when Olson himself is quite clear about Harrison’s association with another classical precedent, Pausanias? A precedent which leads to ‘method’:

how to find out: PAUSANIAS’ Description of Greece (the bible, you can’t read it, but for gawd sake own it, and whenever, look up anything, especially ARCADIA

how to find out now about then: MISS JANE HARRISON, Prolegomena, Themis, and her first book, which is Mythology and Monuments of Ancient Athens -and is, by god, nothing but Pausanias on Pausanias! She is Lady Pausanias.41

Before Sir James Frazer published his six-volume Pausanias’ Description of Greece in 1898, Harrison had translated the Athenian portion of the work (1890). This is why she is ‘Lady Pausanias’, though Olson was using Shilleto’s 1886 Bohn edition before Robert Creeley gave him a copy of Frazer in 1956.42 Pausanias’s Periegesis Hellados is the work of a Greek antiquary writing for Roman travellers in the second century AD. The title, Periegesis, which means basically ‘to show around’, indicates that the

106 work was intended as a guide for tourists; but the word also has sacred connotations, allied with exegesis, and therefore carries a strong interpretative sense.43 The genre of periegetic literature is Herodotean, though Pausanias refers to his work only as logos and syngraphe (textual account); his exhaustive historical/mythological journeys around ancient sites combine theoremata (the physical descriptions of ‘sights’) with logoi (digressions) on their traditional significance. As with Herodotus, who presented his writings as ethnographic and geographic ‘enquiries’, the emphasis is on original research, conducted in the sophistic spirit of paideia, or ‘education’ in Greek culture. ‘After all,’ writes Maria Pretzler, ‘an author’s claim to autopsia -personal experience, or, literally, “seeing for oneself”—could enhance his credibility’ with the elite tourists who made up his audience. Not surprisingly, autopsia involves sifting though accounts of what the often mysterious sites experienced represent, and Pausanias, describing places ‘which some of his readers were likely to know, would have to be particularly careful in his claims of personal knowledge. In fact, it is quite clear that his research was thorough and very detailed, and he carefully separates anything he has personally verified from information he knows only from hearsay.’44 His method, as such, is to decipher monumental inscriptions, collect and collate local history, assess what he hears in each vicinity of aitia (stories that explain aspects of local culture), learn the exact details of cult practice associated with each site, and compare this information with literary sources. Importantly, though, the rigour with which Pausanias conducts his research does not mean that it is objective. Disagreements over conflicting accounts of mythic genealogy are resolved by personal fiat: ‘I know because I have seen it.’45 Looking for continuity between contemporary Greece and its remote antiquity, he aspires to the historical precision of Thucydides, but because much of what remained of prehistory was opaque to the narrow political focus of that historiography, Herodotus’s looser approach is preferred, combining vague and often contradictory mythical accounts with later events more available to analysis. The result is the first real example of what has come to be known as a ‘cultural guide’, which is less interested in the practicalities of the journey itself than in history and monuments. With the injection of archaeological evidence into classical studies in the late nineteenth century, Pausanias became a heroic figure for scholars and travellers, and Harrison and Verrall’s Mythology and Monuments of Ancient Athens, which was intended to be an archaeological commentary on the first books of the Periegesis, ‘soon attracted many buyers who took it to Greece to enhance their visit to the ancient monuments.’46 In question, however, is the ideology of the ‘method’ itself. In a recent study, William Hutton remarks that ‘the sights lead Pausanias to stories; the stories do not lead Pausanias to sights.’47 In simple terms, this would confirm Heidegger’s ‘way’. But Jas Elsner takes the opposite view, arguing that Pausanias assumes it is the path that guides the content of his logoi, when in fact there is much more of an ordering, mythic principle at work: [h]is Greece is…a phantasy. It consisted of an enchanted past, of living myths and rituals whose apparent antiquity guaranteed their modern meanings, of ruins and monuments executed by the hallowed hands of the great.48 Elsner’s thesis depends on the idea that the Periegesis is essentially a work of pilgrimage. That is to

say, Pausanias was not simply learning about historical objects, but trying to confirm 107 traditional preconceptions about the mythic value of the past. As Peter Levi remarks, ‘his deepest engagement was in the local cults and legends of provincial Greece. There was no doubt that he was a true believer in the most sacred of these traditions; he accepted the warning of a dream and understood the punishment of a god.’ Like Frazer —and like Olson— ‘[t]he collapse of ancient religion or some deeper collapse was the unspoken object of his studies.’49 Typically, Pausanias begins his chapter on Messenia by recounting the prehistory of the region in ‘an elaborate series of rationalizations of tribal mythology, poetry, the chronology of legendary generations, place-names, and religious beliefs.’50 And when visiting the oracle of Trophonios at Lebadeia in Book IX, he takes part in the rituals. Yet pilgrimage competes with scepticism. For example, in Book VIII (the Arkadia chapter Olson singles out for Ed Dorn’s attention), Pausanias says of the Kronos myth, ‘[w]hen I began to write my history I thought these Greek stories were rather silly, but now that I have reached Arkadia I have decided to treat them from the point of view that the famous Greek wise men told their stories in riddles and not out of stupidity’.51 And, like an anthropologist, Pausanias maintains a level of impersonality even when consulting the oracle at Lebadeia.52

More contentious is the status of autopsia itself in terms of the observance/observation of ritual. Jane Harrison’s discussion of the ritual origin of the Kouros, where I began this essay, finds much in the Periegesis to support her ideas. Pausanias’s main contribution to uncovering ritual origins is his contemporary evidence, the fact that he ‘states what he knows from personal experience.’53 When he comes across a village called Titane, he discovers from the locals that its name derives from the ‘brother’ to the sun. ‘I think,’ he says, ‘that Titan was great at marking the seasons of the year and the time when the sun gives increase to and ripens the fruit of trees.’ For Harrison, this shows the Titans to be fertility-daimones of the sky rather than the earth.54 Then Pausanias’s lengthy description of the rite of passage at Lebadeia is cited as a prime example of the initiation of kouretes, which ‘casts considerable light on the figure of Themis as she shifts from being a projection of the social structure to her final form as a divinity of prophecy’, testament to ‘the undifferentiated beginnings of things, when social spelt religious.’55 The applicant to the oracle submits to elaborate purification rites before descending into an oracular chasm. He drinks the water of Lethe, in order to forget everything he had in mind before the ceremony, and then drinks a water associated with Memory, so that he will recall what he has witnessed at the oracle. Lying on his back clutching barley- and honey-cakes in each hand he squeezes himself feet-first into a hole in the chasm; at this point, he is swirled away, given a vision by sight or hearing, and returned feet-first through the hole. Afterwards, priests take him to the throne of Mnemosyne, where, full of fear and unconscious of his surroundings, he recounts what he has seen and heard. Eventually, consciousness is regained, and out of the attenuated death that is Lethe the suppliant begins a new life into the spiritual transformation figured by Mneomosyne. ‘I do note write from hearsay’, Pausanias attests. ‘I have myself consulted Trophonios, and have seen others who have done it.’ To which Harrison adds, ‘[n]o one who has read Pausanias will incline to credit him with hysteria.’56

108 But what exactly is Harrison crediting to Pausanias? Is it the evidence of a ritual that exemplifies regeneration, the essential mode of religion, which transforms social custom into magic? Or is it the magical reality of the ritual experience itself? That is to say, in the first case, Pausanias would be showing us an example of ritual practice that can be interpreted symbolically through a set of values deeply ingrained in archaic culture. From this perspective, the water of Lethe represents, for those who drink it in preparation for their descent into the chasm, Death and forgetting. In the second case, Harrison would be asking us to accept that Pausanias experienced a real occult transformation, one in which the water of Lethe actually does induce an attenuated death. If the account is seen as a muthologos, such a distinction is neither here nor there; the fact that the participants in the ritual experience a transformation is the self-sufficient meaning of the rite. Like ‘primitive man’, Harrison argues, we must distinguish between the objective and the subjective, only we know that the subjective world, however it might influence our actions, comes from within us. The ‘savage’, however, imbues his inner world with an outside reality, making ‘another world with a sort of secondary reality, supersensuous but quite real.’ The rite of passage is an essential act if the suppliant is to ‘pass out of the actual, sensible, “objective” world, into that other world of dream, of ecstacy, of trance, with its secondary reality, the world in which emotions, hope and fear, and imaginations, are blended with what we should call subjective hallucinations.’57 Here, then, is the meaning of Olson’s ‘traum’: a reordering of the collective origin of lived experience which unites contemplation with praxis, history with myth, phantasy with reality. It is both process and reality, as long as we understand reality to be inseparable from phantasy. In this dreamworld, a ‘story’ has the kind of narrative indeterminacy of James’s The Turn of the Screw, which challenges its readers to decide whether the governess is an hysteric or actually sees the ghost of Peter Quint, but will not tell either way. The mythic logos offers both secular and sacred readings, but does not need to tell, since in the end it makes no distinction between either. Hermeneutically (and experientially), the problem with this collapsing of the symbolic into the referential is that it depends on a consensus of meaning that no longer exists in modern, demythologized societies; the cultus that is simply lived by so-called primitive man now has to be marked in its difference from the primitive as Themis, ‘herd instinct’ or ‘collective conscience’. For the modern mythologist, therefore, the Being of primitive identity, which has to be theorized after the fact, needs to be translated into categories borrowed from the ratio. In order to get close to what it perceives as an originary identity, the mythological or anthropological commentary has to fall back on definitions that threaten to increase the distance between primitive and modern sensibilities, definition itself, like ‘evidence’, lying outside the parameters of the lived. Significantly, Harrison departs from the kind of explanation Olson calls muthologos when she insists on the truth-value of Pausanias’s seeing-for-himself. No one who has read Pausanias will incline to credit him with hysteria. Hysteria is an interesting word choice, not simply because it is a term derived from Greek in the context of a Greek study, but because it is used in a totally modern way. Were it not, Harrison suggests,

a clear case of evidence outweighing suspicion, Pausanias might be said to be subject 109 to pathological phantasy. There is a possibility that his account of Trophonios may be read as delusion, much as the hauntings James’s governess believes she sees with her own eyes might be attributable to hysteria. But for Harrison to ‘read’ the Periegesis is enough to dispel such notions, for unlike The Turn of the Screw the text evinces its supernatural reality without ambiguity. Why, then, does Harrison need to invoke this other reading except in order to remind sceptical readers of the self-sufficiency of the self-evident? More puzzling is why her example of sceptical reading takes the form of a disease traditionally associated with women. ‘usteron means ‘uterus’, which in the logos of the ancients is a living creature trapped inside the female body, desperate to bear children and causing extreme distress if left unfertilized. By the late nineteenth century, this account had come to be regarded a long-disproved myth, yet medics and psychiatrists continued to treat what they called ‘hysteria’ as a physically-induced disorder of women. Freud’s radical contribution to analysis was to show that the neurosis had no such pathological origin and was predicated on a crisis of identity affecting both men and women: the hysteric is one who asks constantly, Am I man or woman? Am I subject or object?58 The point here is that, while Harrison asks us to accept Pausanias’s supersensuous logoi as literal evidence, there is no sense in which she asks us to accept hysteria as anything more than a synonym of phantasy. Were we tempted to ‘credit’ (i.e., discredit) Pausanias with hysteria, we should be imposing rationalist criteria on an archaic sensibility. Harrison’s denial of phantasy functions to protect Pausanias’s discourse from modish psychological explanations; but in order to do so, it maintains the division between sensuous and supersensuous experience cancelled by the muthologos. That is to say, by invoking hysteria in its modern sense (or at least a casual version of its psychoanalytic meaning), she sets one story against another, privileging the ancient account over the modern.

But why should we not read ‘hysteria’ also in an archaic way? Perhaps because, in the twenty-first century, no one believes that hysteria, whatever else it might be, is caused by a uterine animal agitating a woman’s internal organs. Then again, few people believe that the water of Lethe has the power to make them forget their past lives. If, as Harrison and Olson insist, a story has cultural significance only when myth and reality are united in Themis, the collective conscience, both of these logoi could be valid, depending on the existence of consensus. Because the muthologist writes by definition after the time of Themis—otherwise there would be no need to hypothesize about what is a lived reality—her recreation of archaic identity is inevitably subject to the rationalization of evidence. Consequently, she reminds us of the archaic origins of words when it suits her to do so, for, without the grace of Themis, it is left to her to decide what is culturally significant and what not. Taken to its limit, muthologos offers two realities: the first is one ordered by universal consensus, the abiding myth of cultural totality; the second, lacking that consensus, is an ocean of disinterred (and mixed) metaphors, a poetics of the dream cast loose from the cultus that would heal the division between imagination and public significance. From a psychoanalytic point of view, the dream is a form of hysteria, a condition bound up with the demand for an

110 oracle. In their early work with hysterics, Freud and Breuer found they could effect a cure by bringing to consciousness an original traumatic event, which the patient could not verbalize except under hypnosis. The problem—which might be said to have wiped out the pretensions of analysis to science and transformed it into a mode of shamanism—was that these recollections invariably turned out to be imaginary. Instead of disclosing the repressed identity of the subject, the ‘cure’ had more to do with the analyst’s ability to legitimize the patient’s self-mythology by bringing it into the symbolic. Since Freud’s judgements were often mistaken, this was not a matter of correct interpretation, but rather the event of interpretation itself ritualizing the ‘truth’ of the subject. In other words, in societies in which individual and collective myths are at odds, the analyst stands in for the ‘collective conscience’ missing from the subject’s self-representations. As Jacques Lacan writes in the ‘The Function and Field of Speech and Language in Psychoanalysis’,

[t]he ambiguity of the hysterical revelation of the past is due not so much to the vacillation of its content between the imaginary and the real, for it is situated in both. Nor is it because it is made up of lies. The reason is that it presents us with the birth of truth in speech, and therefore brings us up against the reality of what is neither true nor false.59

Analysis is itself, therefore, a myth. A rational myth, nevertheless, because it treats its oracular magic as a necessary fiction in lieu of consensus. Lacan’s Imaginary approximates to individual phantasy and his Symbolic to the collective conscience, but neither of these categories (along with the complex notion of the Real as that which returns to haunt the subject) represents any reality beyond the nothingness of desire. The compact between analyst and analysand takes place in a utopia, as if the primitive bond, Themis, or polis were in existence. As Mikkel Borch-Jacobsen puts it, the analyst mimes ‘[t]he shaman, the sorcerer, and the magician (themselves ex-“mentally ill persons”)’ who ‘procure for both the sick person and the group a myth in which both can integrate ineffable (that is, the unsymbolizable) psychic or physical pain into a communal language.’60

Psychoanalysis, as a response to the demythologized culture of the ratio known as modernity, has its conceptual origins in the same anthropological space explored by Harrison in Themis. But its denatured occultism is the antithesis of Harrison’s nostalgic animism. Given its critique of subjectivity as the fictional construct of a fictive social contract, it is hardly surprising that Olson should look elsewhere for the ground of a self-sufficient realism. Finding out for oneself doesn’t look so pretty when the self is revealed as nothingness. Yet muthologos turns out to be not dissimilar from the psychoanalytic ‘reduction’ in that its longed-for primitive consciousness/consensus can never be substantiated by evidence. Olson’s conceptual horizon is cultural totality; his poetry is the record of repeated attempts to transform personal traum into public and cosmic evidence. Tom Clark’s observation that ‘Maximus / Okeanos was prone to extremes of subjectivity in vision’ and that increasingly the Maximus poems ‘fill up with the raw stuff of the creative process: found notes, journal musings, the ongoing log of the raging immediacy of the experiential moment’, indicates that Olson was losing 111 this conceptual battle.61 Which is not to say that the project fails poetically; indeed, the progressively associative and eclectic nature of Olson’s facture, its (con)fusion of story and document, locality and universality, becomes the modus operandi of much of the innovative poetry of the last half-century. It should also be acknowledged as a key influence on that mode of contemporary periegesis known as psychogeography, which, especially in the work of Iain Sinclair, Andrew Kötting, and W. G. Sebald, fully embraces the concept of logoi as digression.62 As Sinclair says of another work influenced by Olson, The Act of Seeing with One’s Own Eyes, Stan Brakhage’s film of what in Britain is called a ‘post mortem’ and in the United States an ‘autopsy’, ‘we are seeing something old, but corrupted.’63
