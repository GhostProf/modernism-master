Sentimental Education: Randall Jarrell Among the Women

Gail McDonald

Randall Jarrell (1914-65), American poet, critic, and teacher, was a refractory heir of high modernism. His genealogy is clearly traceable to T. S. Eliot via John Crowe Ransom and Allen Tate, but if he was an apt pupil he was also a wayward disciple, both in his criticism and in his poetry. Though his reviews of fellow poets inspired fear and respect--so fierce and devastating were his witty dismissals, so incisive his praise--his poetry has often been dismissed as sentimental. Bristling with ambition and competitiveness, he nonetheless preferred to more prestigious appointments his position at a small southern woman's college. He suffered fools badly and impatiently but was legendary for his kindness as a teacher of the unsophisticated. His longtime colleague Peter Taylor described his "irrepressible" laughter as "cruel and/or gleeful," a combination that typified his personality and was sometimes bewildering to his intimates. 1

Reviewing his career, one is reminded of the climactic lines of his poem "A Girl in a Library": "and yet--and yet." 2 These apparent contradictions result in part from Jarrell's testy and self-conscious resistance to pigeonholing. As he wrote the war poems that established his reputation with Little Friend, Little Friend and Losses , he sighed to his first wife Mackie in a letter that in the next war he would seek "an executive position with the Campfire Girls." 3 He provocatively declared to the conscientiously masculine Allen Tate that he himself had a "semifeminine mind." 4 Elizabeth Bishop described him, forgivingly, as "difficult, touchy" (RJ, 20).

Behind these stubborn idiosyncrasies lie Jarrell's quarrels with the categories that constituted American modernism between 1932, when he enrolled as a student at Vanderbilt, and 1965, the year of his death. His aesthetic values were shaped by, but also consciously contrary to, the increasingly professionalized form of literary criticism (usually labelled "New Criticism") that came to dominate university teaching during those years. He developed and sharpened that aesthetic for nearly twenty years in a classroom populated exclusively by women, a point that has been noted but never sufficiently studied. 5 If John Guillory is right in asserting that "the social space of literary culture is necessarily institutional," then attention to the particulars of where, how, and to whom modernist poetry was taught ought to constitute a significant chapter in literary history. 6 By considering the sources of Jarrell's contrariness and by analyzing his investment in his students, I seek to complicate that history. My analysis of his career, viewed in the context of high-modernist influence on criticism, poetry, and education in the 1940s and 1950s, furnishes a necessary reminder that neither modernism nor New Criticism--nor indeed the academy itself--is a monolithic institution.

The classroom matters to modernism. 7 The American strain of modernist poetry and criticism was, from its earliest phases, aware of and to a crucial degree dependent upon the schools. 8 Pace Ezra Pound's gleeful announcement to Harriet Monroe that T. S. Eliot had "actually trained himself and modernised himself on his own," the evidence is not weighted toward autodidacticism. 9 Instead, one may see the imprint of the intellectual ivy league everywhere, not only in allusions to the curriculum (philology, Dante, cultural anthropology, and so on) but also in the expectation that poetry, to be serious, must have intellectual heft. The ideal reader of the modernist poem must bring the fruits of higher education to the poems, or be willing to do the homework necessary to take those fruits away from the poem. Either way, the reader is exchanging what Guillory and others have called cultural capital. Over time, this demanding dimension of modernist poetry has become linked to the name of T. S. Eliot, who has provided a serviceable icon/straw man either for celebrating what Richard Poirier has called the famous "difficulties" of modernism or for unmasking those difficulties as covert intellectual fascism. 10 And of course Eliot has been widely mythologized as the father-re responsible for New Criticism, which dominated American classrooms throughout the years under analysis here. As literary historians investigate the legacies of modernism, then, colleges and universities will be necessary objects of that analysis.

The story of Eliot's association with New Criticism and its ascendancy in the American academy is one of the sturdiest narratives of modernist scholarship. The plot includes Jarrell's own entry into the academy. In its most schematic form, a crucial chapter of that narrative goes as follows: In 1937 John Crowe Ransom proposes to Allen Tate that "the professors are in an awful dither trying to reform themselves and there's a big stroke possible for a small group that knows what it wants in giving them ideas and definitions and showing the way." 11 In 1938 Ransom moves from Vanderbilt to Kenyon, founds the Kenyon Review, becomes director of the Kenyon School of English. Two of Ransom's former students, Cleanth Brooks and Robert Penn Warren, publish Understanding Poetry in 1938, and Brooks publishes Modern Poetry and the Tradition in 1939. By this series of events, a new kind of "university-based criticism" is, as Gerald Graff expresses it, "disseminat[ed]." 12 Despite some initial resistance among more traditional scholars (philologists, literary historians and biographers, and the like), the methods of the so-called New Criticism achieve wide acceptance and the professors cease to be uncertain about their methods and mission.

Even in much less condensed versions of this history, certain elements remain consistent: the project was tied to schools from its inception; it aimed to garner respect for the reading and writing of poetry, rescuing both from their reputations as pursuits for dithering professors and society ladies; it sought to protect literary criticism from charges of subjectivity by replacing appreciation with analysis; the leading textbook of the era led to a codified teaching method that strongly favored text over context; and--finally (as Graff's verb implies)--not only the leaders of the project but most of the influential students were male. Jarrell studied at both Vanderbilt and Kenyon during the seminal years (1932-39), published his earliest work in magazines edited by either Ransom or Brooks and Warren, and received his first academic appointment at the University of Texas on the strength of Ransom's, Tate's, and Warren's recommendations; thus his early career intersects at key points with this tale of the modernist tribe.

Recent scholarship has taken this narrative seriously by questioning and attempting to revise it. Terry Eagleton and Harold Bloom, for instance, see modernism's influence in the universities, particularly influence of the Eliotic variety, as a pernicious strain of authoritarian cultural ideology. 13 The linking of modernist poetry with formalist criticism becomes in this view elitist and repressive, an instance of the predictable practices of a self-referring and self-promoting privileged class. In another kind of critique, feminist scholars have devoted their energies to exploring and complicating the phallocentrism of the received narrative: recovering the erased female contributors to the modernist project, analyzing the misogynistic rhetoric of its manifestos, examining the gender preferences of its aesthetic values. 14 Both kinds of critical approaches have enriched and usefully complicated the portrait of what modernism was.

Paradoxically, however, the vigorous rewriting of modernist literary history has had the effect of reifying the old narrative. And, as revisionists chip away at the monument of high modernism, some important features are defaced. The result is a kind of amnesia about dimensions of modernism not defined by male-dominated, high-culture axiology, particularly when the re under consideration is male. Michael Levenson argues that "modernism was individualist before it was anti-individualist, anti-traditional before it was traditional, inclined to anarchism before it was inclined to authoritarianism," and there is ample evidence that these insurgent energies never entirely disappear, despite the reactionary elements that evolve out of revolutionary beginnings. 15 The oppositional forces within modernism are particularly apparent when we study books like Shari Benstock's Women of the Left Bank. 16 But such subversion, innovation, and excitement tend in current criticism to be attributed to the women of modernism, not the men, and certainly not to the southern sons of Ransom and Tate.

Clearly, Jarrell doesn't belong on the Left Bank. He neither drank nor smoked, felt uncomfortable at parties, and was something of a homebody, attached to his wife and his cat. Like other poets of his generation, he struggled with mental illness, but compared to the more operatic Robert Lowell, for example, his troubles were for the most part undramatic. 17 However, if Jarrell seems misplaced as a bohemian, neither does he fit comfortably into the discourses of tradition, authoritarianism, and conservatism so often used to describe his teachers. His politics were marxist, not Agrarian. His poems are often talky and inconclusive rather than dense and tightly organized. Indeed his poetic practice veers so sharply away from the complex, paradoxical, densely allusive, and highly finished aesthetic favored by textbook New Criticism as to suggest an explanation for his status as a minor poet. John Berryman complimented his "neglect" of theory about either poetry or criticism at a time when a systematic critical vocabulary was becoming established in both journals and classrooms (RJ, 13). 18 And finally, as a teacher, he chose to spend his time with Greensboro women, not Princeton men. These less than predictable choices, I suggest, are conscious choices, made with full recognition of both their symbolic value and their likely effect on his reputation. His conception of theory and literary criticism, his attitude toward his students, and his conscientious unpredictability are intertwined because all of them are responses to what he perceived as the professionalization of poetry. Literary and academic culture were increasingly inseparable spheres in the period from 1935 to 1960; for Jarrell, they were an unholy alliance that brought corporate practices into the study and the classroom. He aligned himself instead with those unbusinesslike values--sentimentality, femininity, gentility--that modernism had sought to jettison. 19

A key word for understanding Jarrell's choices is "sentimentality." The New York Times review of Jarrell's last book of poetry, The Lost World , accused the poems of flatness, passivity, tear-jerking, dismissing the poems as "indulgent and sentimental Mama-ism." 20 The cruel tone of such responses was devastating to Jarrell, but the message cannot have been entirely new to him. Mentors and peers had complained in letters that somehow his poems lacked backbone, and critics had been concerned about the "voice" of his poems since the 1948 volume Losses. In reviewing it, W. S. Graham complained that the poems were "loose and casual" and that Jarrell was a sucker for the "pseudo-profound." 21 Himself a regular reviewer of poetry, Jarrell knew the literary climate, knew what was likely to be admired, and yet he repeatedly turned to the rather unpromising subject matters of childhood, fairy tales, and sad women--in short, to subjects associated with naive or sentimental storytelling.

The tendency of the sentimental to occasion queasiness may be measured in the entry for "sentimentality" in The New Princeton Encyclopedia of Poetry and Poetics. "Sentimentality in poetry: poetic indulgence in the exhibition of pathetic emotions for their own sake; poetic indulgence of more emotion (often self-regarding) than seems warranted by the stimulus; excessively direct poetic expression of pathos . . . without a sufficient artistic correlative." 22 Among the possible causes are "self-pity," "the absence of mature emotional self-control," and an "uncritical romantic sensibility," which looks upon "dogs, children, old women, and the deprived" not only as "objects of pity" but as "objects of reverence and even envy." Dogs don't often re in Jarrell's poetic world, but the other characters do. He cannot have been unaware of the riskiness of his subject matter.

The disgusted tone of the Princeton Encyclopedia entry registers the degree to which sentimentality is understood to violate the decorum of serious poetry. Reading on, one finds the adjectives "popular," "low," "subjective." By implication good poetry would be none of these things. Most importantly, the entry continues, emotion or pathos must be "adequately registered in the specific means of poetry and thus transformed from emotion into art." The emotion must have "located its poetic correlatives." Some of these dicta seem sensible and persuasive, of course; bad poetry is often marked by free-floating abstractions and vaguely realized emotion. One scarcely need read between the lines, however, to see how much this definition of sentimentality is shaped by assumptions articulated by Eliot in The Sacred Wood and then given institutional life by textbooks like Understanding Poetry: the "objective correlative," and the "impersonal" theory of poetry are strongly implied. All of these formulations--those of the encyclopedist, of Eliot, of Brooks and Warren--work to keep the personal, the emotional, at bay or under control.

The aesthetics of antisentimentality, according to Suzanne Clark in Sentimental Modernism, exhibit a "gendered logic." 23 "The story of desire for American critics has had an Oedipal shape--the subject gets over his imaginary attachments in order to take his place in culture as a man." (SM, 8) By this logic, he must grow out of the tropes, tones, and subjects of "Mama-ism": the comforts of genteel domesticity, reassuring routines of dailiness, physical and emotional softness, unearned sympathy, accessibility or transparency of meaning, overt attempts to persuade, acknowledgment of audience, abundant verbiage, and (often) conventional choices of either form or subject. It is the province of what Patricia Parker has dubbed "literary fat ladies." 24 By contrast, he must adopt other postures and attachments: urbanity, anomie, irony, self-consciousness, asepsis, evidence of rigorous intellect, toughness, the line that declares its hard edge. By such shorthand, the tough-minded modernist maintains a knowing distance from weepy females, and, by implication, from the popular, the easy, and the base. He favors a correspondingly obdurate style. Modernist poetry is to be more rebarbative than genteel verse, more demanding than linear narratives, more stimulating to the mind than those lyrics and tales that appeal to the foolish heart. And in staking out those generic and stylistic territories, the unsentimental modernist establishes himself as quite different not only from his immediate, genteel precursors, but also from the generation once removed, the romantic poets. Statements like Pound's 1913 Imagist manifesto, by strongly favoring the hard, the clean, and the spare, make the break from the emotional excesses of romanticism seem irrevocable. 25 Such declarations privilege certain genres and tones for the expression of a modern sensibility and make it appear that the innovations of modernism eclipse the achievements of the poetic past.

Early and late in his career, Jarrell was publicly skeptical about this cutting of the apron strings. By 1942, just three years after receiving his master's degree from Kenyon, Jarrell confidently challenged the commonplace that modernism was a genuine break with the past. The thesis of his essay "The End of the Line" is bold and direct: "Modernist poetry--the poetry of Pound, Eliot, Crane, Tate, Stevens, Cummings, MacLeish, et cetera--appears to be and is generally considered to be a violent break with romanticism; it is actually, I believe, an extension of romanticism, an end product in which most of the tendencies of romanticism have been carried to their limits." 26 The essay has the quality of the enfant terrible that Ransom observed even in his earliest dealings with Jarrell. The voice of the precocious child who is only pointing out for informational purposes what is entirely clear to him--that the emperor has no clothes--would remain an important keynote of Jarrell's prose. But the essay also manifests his desire to praise what he believes is great. "[E]ulogy," Lowell wrote, "was the glory of Randall's criticism" (RJ, 104). Jarrell concludes the essay with a list of names: Wordsworth, Blake, Heine, Baudelaire, Corbière, Hardy, Yeats, Rilke. A "'classical' prejudice . . . that these poets were somehow deceived and misguided," he remarks, "seems every year more grotesque." (K, 83) He will not be a recruit to the antiromantic program of modernism, especially not if by signing on he has to relinquish his approval of those poets. There is something more than mere contrariness in this studied difference from the mainstream; Jarrell is endorsing a more inclusive and more complex standard of judgment than that permitted by the "'classical' prejudices" of modernism.

Twenty years later, in the essay "Fifty Years of American Poetry," Jarrell would repeat this rhetorical strategy and produce a similar effect. Speaking at the National Poetry Festival in Washington, D. C., on 22 October 1962, before the most distinguished American poets and critics of the day, he assumes the voice of the future in evaluating Eliot, "the most famous and influential of American poets":

Won't the future say to us in helpless astonishment: "But did you actually believe that all those things about objective correlatives, classicism, the tradition, applied to his poetry? Surely you must have seen that he was one of the most subjective and daemonic poets who ever lived, the victim and helpless beneficiary of his own inexorable compulsions, obsessions? . . . But for you, of course, after the first few years, his poetry existed undersea, thousands of feet below that deluge of exegesis, explication, source listing, scholarship, and criticism that overwhelmed it." 27

It is of course not the Future who speaks here, but the prescient Jarrell, who refuses to admire "the right poet" for the "wrong reasons," preferring the suffering, compulsive, and subjective man to the symbol of the critical industry. 28 He rejects the New Critical Eliot, rejects the impersonal bard channeling the "mind of Europe"--those dimensions that then made Eliot seem unassailable--and pictures instead a poet who is fallible, troubled, and driven--those dimensions that now make Eliot seem to some indefensible. 29

What links these two essays is Jarrell's stance of being attentive to poets and poems rather than to dominant theoretical templates for poets and poems. Here, his New Critical allegiances are evident: the poem is its own excuse for being and contains within itself all necessary explanations. Even his view of Eliot derives not from external biographical material but from intensive readings of the poems. Informed by, but not limited to, Freudian vocabulary, his psychological portrait of Eliot is grounded in the dense annotations observable in his copy of The Complete Poems and Plays, 1909-1950 ( 1). Next to the "yellow smoke" passage of "The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock," for instance, he has written "the wild bad alley Tomcat that does all the things well-brought up Persian kitten would, could never do--but he too ends in nothing." 30 Words are underlined, repetitions noted, puns observed, sounds of words connected. Why, given such tenets of poetic autotelism and such practices of microscopic inspection, should he need to defend poems from critics?

The answer, like the question, is based in the hegemony of New Criticism. The most sustained explanation Jarrell offers is in the essay "The Age of Criticism," first published in Partisan Review in 1952. In it, he declared that criticism had become "astonishingly graceless, joyless, humorless . . . self-important, cliché-ridden, prestige-obsessed, [and] almost-autonomous." 31 Again, as in the 1942 essay, he notes the patronizing dismissal of Wordsworth--"poor Wordsworth I almost wrote"--and associates the dismissal of Wordsworth with condescension toward his friend, a novelist who was not a professional literary critic.

But the critics could not help being conscious of the differences between themselves, and Wordsworth, and my friend: they knew how poems . . . are put together, and Wordsworth and my friend didn't, but had just put them together. In the same way, if a pig wandered up to you during a bacon-judging contest, you would say impatiently, "Go away, pig! What do you know about bacon?" [PA, 74] 32

Jarrell perceived that what had begun as a critical movement to make poetry autonomous had evolved into a movement in which criticism had made itself autonomous; he detected the inevitable moment at which a profession becomes as propelled by its forms, conventions, and hierarchies as by its reason for being.

Jarrell delivered "The Age of Criticism" as a lecture while participating in the Indiana School of Letters (founded by Ransom) in the summer of 1952. Some of the audience reacted, Jarrell reported in June 1952 to Mary von Schrader, "as if, to a bunch of junior executives, I'd questioned Free Enterprise" (L, 358). The letter dramatizes his outsider status. "All in all I had rather a sense of how lonely you are when you come out on the side of life and risk-taking and thinking works of art are live and mysterious and unaccountable; on the side of everything that can't be institutionalized and handled and graded by Experts. Most academic highbrow people really do go along with the critical abstract categorizing mind." (L, 359) 33 Jarrell had in 1939 [Begin Page 9] unapologetically explained to Allen Tate that he couldn't "put any real faith in abstractions or systems" (L, 19). It was his "really unsystematic" mind that he labelled "semifeminine" (L, 19; Jarrell's emphasis).

Taken together, the essays of 1942, 1952, and 1962 show a remarkable consistency in Jarrell's opinion of the appropriate relationship between the poem and the critic. Repeatedly he is drawn to discourses of capitalism or "free enterprise," competition, careerism, territoriality, and bureaucracy to decry the ways in which the act of reading poems has been, as it were, taylorized, made efficient and productive. The criticism business, for Jarrell, exudes the arrogance of power. Leslie Fiedler, who spoke the day after Jarrell that summer in Indiana and who was in sympathy with him on that occasion, later wrote that for Jarrell "an essential function of the critic is to free the loving reader from all orthodoxies as well as all methodologies" (RJ, 66). This notion of value-free, disinterested reading may, in the wake of post-structuralist criticism, seem hopelessly benighted. To take this view, however, is to tell a very clever pig he knows nothing about bacon. In other words, Jarrell's lecture and Fiedler's evaluation of his critical stance must be considered in the context of the orthodoxies and methodologies they worked within and against. For Jarrell especially, the agenda was to find a way to put romance, mystery, and sentiment (the stereotypes of a feminized sensibility) back into the mix of critical values. Jarrell's stance, Fiedler perceived, is that of a courageous, rescuing prince in a fairy tale; his princess, the loving reader, possesses that feminized sensibility. "Rescue" and "love" are very nearly embarrassing terms to use in describing the work of literary criticism. 34 This embarrassment, as Suzanne Clark points out, is tied to conceptions of maturity and gender: "Becoming an intellectual in America is sort of like being inducted into the army . . . and learning not to be a sissy." (SM, 12)

Randall Jarrell knew the code of the intellectual army. His academic posts, his fellowships and grants, his appointment at the Library of Congress, his prizes--all are the benefits of increasingly well organized professions. His competitiveness impelled him to seek those rewards and want others. Nevertheless, Jarrell insisted that this culture be accessible to amateurs, indeed that their disinterested and marginal status in the culture of reading gave them a particular authenticity. And, in his teaching career especially, he questioned whether the characteristics denigrated as "sissy" ought to be so cavalierly dismissed. To be a sissy is to "sit out" the contest, and Jarrell preferred that reading not be a contest. Describing his generosity to his students and herself, Eleanor Ross Taylor writes, "We were likely to do nothing for him in return, as people not on committees, not writing recommendations, not mentioning things to powerful people, not promoting, arranging, appearing. He battled for the survival of such people" (RJ, 237). It is precisely the absence of power that drew Jarrell to these women. To the problem of "The Obscurity of the Poet" , he offered no solution, believing there was none. High modernism's program to make poetry matter by making it new was a failed dream, and Jarrell agreed with Auden that "poetry makes nothing happen." 35 "There is nothing to do different from what we already do: if poets write poems and readers read them, each as best they can--if they try to live not as soldiers or voters or intellectuals or economic men, but as human beings--they are doing all that can be done." (PA, 21) To actors in the powerful institutional spheres of politics, education, and economics, Jarrell opposes human beings, presumably defined as somehow outside those spheres. Traditionally, and certainly at the time that Jarrell wrote those words, the person outside those spheres was female.

Jarrell came to what was known as the Woman's College of the University of North Carolina in 1947, on the recommendation of Peter Taylor, who was to be his colleague, and with a letter of support from Ransom. 36 In the background of his appointment stands Tate, who taught at the college before he moved to Princeton in 1939. Jarrell had had previous teaching experience: instructor of English and Director of Tennis at Kenyon, then instructor of English at the University of Texas at Austin. After military service, he acted as literary editor of The Nation while teaching at Sarah Lawrence, an experience that helped to shape his satiric novel Pictures from an Institution. His teaching career is typical of the time in its cobbling together of jobs (a poet who must write criticism and teach to make a living); it also signals one way in which the literary and academic establishments had become partners.

The institution at which Jarrell taught from 1947 until his death in 1965 was, in his own wry description to Lowell in 1947, "pastoral" (L, 177). In the late 1940s and 1950s, it enrolled two thousand to twenty-five hundred women, three-fourths of whom were North Carolinians, a substantial percentage of them from small rural towns. Some were the daughters of wealthy planters, but others were children of small-town newspaper editors, orphans on scholarship, and children of sharecroppers, again on scholarship. 37 It was not a finishing school, nor was it experimental in the way of Sarah Lawrence. Like many another woman's college, it was founded as a "normal" school with the primary purpose of training teachers. By the 1940s, however, students came with other aims as well, some to spend a couple of years preparing to go to other schools, some to study the "Home Ec." and "Phys. Ed." that Jarrell ridicules in "A Girl in a Library" (CP, 17), and some--the group that would most engage Jarrell's attentions--to avail themselves of the college's growing reputation as a school for serious study of the arts, creative writing, and literature. For nearly twenty years of his teaching career, Jarrell was in daily contact with a kind of student who, however bright, stood outside the corridors of intellectual power. Thus his work as a teacher helps one to focus on an overlooked group of legatees in the transmission of cultural capital. The ideal audience imagined by the influential male modernists did not include small-town southern "bobbysoxers." And yet this was the audience to which Jarrell dedicated himself. His pedagogic commitment is of a piece with his edgy relationship to the critical mainstream.

Robert Lowell's affectionate and perceptive description of Jarrell as a teacher is worth quoting at some length:

He once said, "If I were a rich man, I would pay money for the privilege of being able to teach." Probably there was no better teacher of literature in the country, and yet he was curiously unworldly about it, and welcomed teaching . . . in the shade or heat of his little-known Southern college for girls. . . . There his own community gave him a compact, tangible, personal reverence that was incomparably more substantial and poignant than the empty, numerical long-distance blaze of national publicity. He grieved over the coarseness, unkindness, and corruption of our society. . . . He said bitterly and lightheartedly that "the gods who had taken away the poet's audience had given him students." Yet he gloried in being a teacher, never apologized for it, and related it to his most serious criticism [RJ, 105].

Jarrell made the remark about paying to teach on more than one occasion (L, 435, 453). Because this is the sort of thing teachers say on a good day, when teaching feels like more than a job, it is a comment easy to dismiss. But Lowell's vocabulary coheres with the rhetoric of Jarrell's critical essays, echoing their defense of the unaccountable. Jarrell's attachment to the students in his classroom signals "curiously unworldly" allegiances. The world to which Lowell and Jarrell respond is one that expects seriousness to be linked to money, publicity, coarseness, and unkindness, one that denigrates the personal and the reverential as poignant, even sentimental. That Jarrell expected this position to be attacked as sentimental is evident in a 1959 essay, "Poets, Critics, and Readers." He offers as a counterproposition a diagnosis by Hugo von Hoffmannsthal: "Each epoch has its own sentimentality . . . its specific way of overemphasizing strata of emotion. The sentimentality of the present is egotistic and unloving; it exaggerates not the feeling of love but that of the self" (K, 310). This self-regard, Jarrell argues, made the critic the competitor of the poet and his criticism a means to a self-promoting end. At the conclusion of the essay, he recalls an interview with a critic in which the critic admits to reading Kipling's Kim for pleasure every year. "[F]or a moment I had left the gray, dutiful world of the professional critic, and was back in the sunlight and shadow, the unconsidered joys, the unreasoned sorrows, of ordinary readers and writers, amateurishly reading and writing 'at whim'" (K, 317). The "contemplative and sympathetic hours of our reading" are an alternative to "the active, greedy, and powerful hours of our lives" (K, 318). Jarrell's professional critic has become the Man in the Gray Flannel Suit, that icon of the 1950s rat-race. 38 The space he carved out at Greensboro was "pastoral" in this way, as well; in his estimation, it was the green-world alternative to the self-aggrandizing, product-driven occupation that had become literary criticism.

The green world has its annoyances, too, of course, and Jarrell acknowledged these with his usual piquancy. Shortly after his arrival in 1947, he wrote to Hannah Arendt, "I have several good students in modern poetry and writing[;] . . . the first day one of them, to my great amazement, turned in a faithful imitation of Robert Lowell. But the only thing to do with the freshmen here is to write a ballet with a Chorus of Peasant Girls for them" (L, 180). The Carolina drawl grated on him; in 1949, he described himself to Margaret Marshall as "knee-deep in violets and whine-y Southern girls" (L, 219). Over time, however, the caustic remarks diminish, giving way to frequent assertions of his devotion to the women at Greensboro. He wrote to Robert Penn Warren in 1962 that, after his two years as Poetry Consultant to the Library of Congress, his return to his classes "made me want never to be without them again" (L, 461).

The components of this dedication are a complex mixture of the personal and professional, and not all of the elements are flattering to Jarrell. He was by many accounts narcissistic, mirror-obsessed, and proud of his mind; friends and colleagues grant those characteristics even when they wish to be sympathetic to Jarrell. The role of adored teacher, inspiring the reverence to which Lowell refers in his description, is powerfully attractive to such a personality, and its potency was probably complicated as well by student "crushes" and erotic energy, heady boosts for any ego. 39 Jarrell's competitiveness is also a factor: at Greensboro he was the teacher to study with, the poet to know, the famous man. This adulation undoubtedly compensated for other ways in which he might have felt slighted by the literary establishment. One may suspect him, accordingly, of preening himself on his role as master and of dominating his students with his superior mind. In fact, however, the oral histories I have assembled in interviewing colleagues and former students and the memoirs of students I have read emphatically contradict that possibility, a point to which I will return. I do not want to move too quickly past the question of domination, however, precisely because the record of opinion Jarrell left in his criticism is so frequently concerned with the exercise of intellectual power.

Jarrell's poetic treatment of this subject is invariably ambivalent and entangled in complicated ways with gender. Langdon Hammer, in a persuasive reading of "A Girl in a Library," suggests that the poem's tenderness may be a veiled assertion of male superiority: "her passive cooperation with his vision, her lack, even, of dreams; and his decision to address her, which allows him to occupy the position of the master (even--or especially?--a gentle one), begins to look like a way of preserving male authority by making it appear benevolent." "What would the girl say," Hammer asks, "if she woke up?" 40 In one sense, this is an unanswerable question. What would the Solitary Reaper say to Wordsworth? It is, however, a question that provokes the imagination in reminding us of the ways that the verbally gifted dominate the silent in colleges everywhere. The poem invites us to notice gender, of course. The male speaker is both revolted by and drawn to the sleeping girl's stolidity; she is condescendingly portrayed as close to nature (thus base in her aspirations), while he is bodiless. The male is an intellectual, the library his workplace; she is an interloper there. But because the poem's texture relies upon literary and other forms of high-art allusion, "A Girl in a Library" should also be read as a meditation on the roles of teacher and student and on the sleeping girl's curriculum, particularly the limiting practicality of it. Her subjects--Home Ec. and Phys. Ed.--are the "narrow stream" that confine her (CP, 16). His subjects--opera, Russian poetry, the stuff of dreams and fiction--are the romantic visions that remove him from the things of this world. What good does it do the man who is awake to know the things he does? And if he values them, must the sleeping student? The speaker's tone is uncertain, though his own commitments are clear. For all his satiric wit, the speaker is a decidedly unauthoritative authority re, who admits that the world ("the world contracts around you" [CP, 15]) has made a student "good enough for it" (CP, 17). The bookish speaker asserts that "the soul" that "neither cooks / Nor referees" (CP, 16) is superior to the "calm slow factuality" (CP, 17) of this contracted world; yet he also allows that the "inconceivable enchantment" (CP, 17) he values so highly is of little consequence to it. "each poem guaranteed," as he puts it in "A Conversation with the Devil," "a lie or permanently irrelevant" (CP, 29). Furthermore, his fictions depend upon the facts she embodies; the consolations and pleasures of her life, on the other hand, are "innocent" (CP, 17) of his fictions. "A Girl in a Library" is, in this sense, a poem about a teacher whose student does not want what he has to offer. What matters is not only that she is a girl, though gender-focused readings help us to understand the effects of that representation, but that she is a girl whose world has taught her to seek intellectual sustenance in Home Ec. and Phys. Ed.

In "The Taste of the Age," first published in 1958, seven years after "A Girl in a Library," Jarrell returned to the subject of home economics. He discovers in conversation with an eighth-grade student that she does not know who Charlemagne was.

The little girl who didn't know who Charlemagne was had been taught, I found, to conduct a meeting, to nominate, and to second nominations; she had been taught . . . the right sort of story to tell an eighteen-months-old baby; and she had learned in her Domestic Science class to bake a date pudding, to make a dirndl skirt. . . . She was being given an education suitable for the world she was to use it in; my quarrel was not so much with her education as with her world [K, 300-301, 303].

The world that Jarrell deplores on her behalf is the same one that absorbs the sleeping girl in a library, the same that causes the woman in his poem "Next Day" to move in a trancelike state down the supermarket aisles, that makes the hallucinating speaker of "Seele im Raum" feel she must choose between "sense" and "truth" (CP, 39). In this world, to return to the terms Jarrell uses in "The Obscurity of the Poet," women are voters (able to second a nomination) and economic entities (able to shop and do domestic work), but something less than human beings. "Marriage with the Corn King was the only game in town," Mary Jarrell said in a recent interview about Jarrell's teaching. "Randall thought they were better than that." 41

Imagery of entrapment and confinement is countered in Jarrell's portraits of women by imagery of animals and wilderness. The observer thinks of a trapped animal as he watches the girl in the library: "Many a beast has gnawn a leg off and got free / . . . The trap has closed about you and you sleep" (CP, 16). The image of the eland, real to the woman in "Seele im Raum" and imaginary to her family, shifts by the poem's end to the German elend (wretched), registering her inescapable domestication. "The Woman at the Washington Zoo" says to the "bars of my own body, open, open! / The world goes by my cage and never sees me" (CP, 215). The troubled but liberating dream that concludes "The Night before the Night before Christmas" takes place in fields where "There is not one thing that knows / It is almost Christmas" (CP, 50). Evocations of wildness are often poignant in these poems because they are imaginings only, a strongly felt but ineffectual protest against the various cages in which the women find themselves: the bars of their own mortal and aging bodies and the compartments of their social personae. Stanley Kunitz's essay "Out of the Cage" summarizes the centrality of this topic: "All the voices in all of Jarrell's poems are crying, 'Change me!'. . . In the mythic imagination metamorphosis is the great theme underlying all others. To the individual psyche it is the way out of the cage. 'Self-transformation,' said Rilke, whom Jarrell revered and translated, 'is precisely what life is'" (RJ, 99-100). For Jarrell, uncaged wildness is an assertion of that vitality.

Jarrell also employs the metaphor of wildness in describing his function as critic and teacher. The enfant terrible is an enfant sauvage. After lunching at Princeton in 1952, Jarrell lamented R. P. Blackmur's having become part of the "whole literary-academic . . . world." "Believe me," he wrote to Mary, "I'm glad I'm marrying a wild tribe and am not institutionalized myself." (L, 338) His self-positioning outside this institution allied him with the amateur reader he imagined as ideal. These readers, described in "The Age of Criticism," are "almost as wild a species as writers" (PA, 80). Critics, on the other hand, "are so domesticated as to seem institutions"; they remind him of "the Wall standing between Pyramus and Thisbe" (PA, 80). Beneath the cleverness of this image, which evokes the cloddish stupidity of the domesticated critic and makes lovers of the reader and her book, Jarrell expresses serious disapproval of the divisions and obstacles he consistently associates with the academization of reading. Wildness, for Jarrell, resists the psychic, social, bureaucratic compartments that make lives small.

That there could be bigger lives--and that the study of literature might include the possibility of self-transformation--is a repeated theme in the comments of Jarrell's students. "It is difficult," wrote Bertha Harris Wyland (class of 1959),

to describe the effect, as it was then, on someone like myself who came from a small town of regular church-going, Saturday night picture shows, and Saturday Evening Post habits. It was like being suddenly snatched off the assembly line, taken apart, reassembled. Most of us were first or second-generation college students. . . . We walked into the place inviting it to reassemble us; we demanded to be lighted up. 42

"Teaching was his essence, his core," Heather Ross Miller (class of 1961) believes. "Randall cared more about our silly stories and poems than we did ourselves. It was his special talent, his gift--and those of us in the writing workshops felt baptized in some exuberant mixture of fire and spirit and mineral water." 43 Student testimonials to beloved teachers tend, by their nature, to be hagiographic, but as Jarrell's biographer William Pritchard also found, there is remarkable consistency in what they praise. The memoirs of Jarrell's students reiterate the metaphors of electricity, explosion, change. His colleague Robert Watson describes the students of that period as "skyrockets waiting to go off." 44

This sense of nascent vitality may be one of the reasons Jarrell compared the college at Greensboro to a fairy tale, a genre to which Jarrell was drawn throughout his career. "Gee, I'm glad to get back here," he said to Robert Watson in 1953 after two years away. "This college is like Sleeping Beauty." (RJ, 258) Perhaps Jarrell referred only to the general sleepiness and small-town atmosphere, perhaps to the restfulness of being out of the more competitive atmosphere of Princeton. But Fiedler's characterization of Jarrell as wanting to "free the loving reader" comes irresistibly to mind. And since sleep in this tale acts as a form of captivity, the allusion recalls the captives and enclosed res of Jarrell's poetry. That he should see himself as come to liberate these res from their sleep may betray the arrogance of which he accused American literary criticism. "I think that he felt he was in a prince capacity," Mary Jarrell laughed when I questioned her on this point. But she was quick to add that the students "were not puppets on a string. They were being guided and encouraged. . . . Leadership is one thing, leading by the nose another." 45

Jarrell acted on two principles that make accusations of intellectual domination seem oversimplified. First, he knew that education was not a panacea. As much as the girl in "The Night before the Night before Christmas" may want to believe that the world could be saved by study of Marx and Engels, the poem's conclusion makes plain the inescapability of loss and injustice. "There is something deep / Under her will, against her will, / That keeps murmuring to her, 'It's so'" (CP, 47). Second, he knew that the fairy-tale princess could become, as in "Cinderella," "A sullen wife and a reluctant mother" (CP, 217). Unhappy endings were just as likely as happy ones, and magic kisses might in any case be refused. In "Hope," he revises the story of Sleeping Beauty.

. . . It was the old story
But ended differently: when the Prince kissed her on the lips
She wiped her lips
And with a little moue . . .
Turned over and went back to sleep.

[CP, 308]

Despite these potential failures and rejections, Jarrell remained both imaginatively and practically dedicated to the possibility of transformation. What Fred Chappell, his colleague at Greensboro, has said about his poems is also true of his classroom: "In Jarrell's poems the discourse of books and pictures and statues and buildings and music impinges upon the characters and makes a real difference in their lives." 46

To assess this real difference, emphasized repeatedly by his students, is to understand not only Jarrell's investment in his work as a teacher but also to understand what poetry meant to young women at a small-town college at midcentury. I suggest that the now-suspect notion of the transformative power of poetry, which motivated Jarrell's commitment and which he knew was rooted in a highly romantic ethos, seemed viable at Greensboro precisely because of the feminine nature of the institution and its students. Robert Watson told me that Jarrell "hated teaching at Princeton. Their attitude was, 'Show me. I'm bright. I'm very bright. I may be brighter than you are. I know I am going to be much richer than you are and more powerful.'" 47 He spent his time there answering challenges that distracted attention from the poetry. The Greensboro women were not without questions, nor did Jarrell simply hold forth in the classroom. On the contrary, he customarily encouraged discussion and response. But the Princeton experience was, for Jarrell, infected with the competitive atmosphere of professional criticism in ways that Greensboro was not. In preferring his quiet principality at Greensboro, then, he was opting out of that particular contest. His was not merely a negative choice or evasion, however; it was taking a stand.

If the history of New Criticism's influence on the academy may be seen as a series of strategems to separate the reading of poetry from the emotional, the personal, the feminine, and all taint of the self-indulgent, then Jarrell's choice seeks to restore gravity to what had been deemed trifling. In the same letter to Allen Tate in which he explains his "semifeminine mind," he reports an anecdote about a conversation with Philip Rice. "Rice was talking the other day about women--and Southerners, as I remember--taking people just as people, and putting too much importance on that, instead of considering mostly their ideas . . . and I laughed to myself and thought, 'I should hope.'" (L, 21) Jarrell's defense of the particular and ordinary human being, linked paradigmatically to southerners and women even in this early letter, is the sign of his opposition to a programmatic criticism that makes expertise in critical vocabulary and "ideas" necessary concomitants of reading, and thus the province of sophisticated male experts. Did he, in his defense of the feminine, romanticize women in ways that make his defense of them suspect? Considered from some perspectives, the answer must be yes. Heather Ross Miller recalls that "it was always a shock for him to discover how much we knew about mules and sex and hard work and other crudities." 48 Suzanne Rodgers Bush (class of 1955) remembers having to educate him about majorettes and cheerleaders: it was possible to lead cheers and be a serious student of poetry. 49 Although he took these lessons with grace and good humor, he remained faithful to an idealized femininity that was unspoiled and humble. To assume that he would therefore see the women as inferior, however, is seriously to misapprehend his principles. Jarrell consistently affirmed the superiority of unspoiled humility, particularly that of readers unspoiled by the sophistications of the critical profession and humble before artistic achievement. He wished himself to be such a reader. His culture, in any case, presented him with narrow choices: to be attached to the things he was attached to was, within his time, to be "semifeminine" in one's preferences. To be a poet at all, as Frank Lentricchia has demonstrated, was to wrestle with anxieties about gender. 50 Sentimentality about women's education looks daring in that context.

For the women at Greensboro, the Jarrell era was not sentimental, but thrilling. The Woman's College sponsored a student-edited literary magazine, Coraddi, that published the writing not only of Greensboro students but of students all over the country. The 1947 issue included work by Anthony Hecht, Donald Justice, and Flannery O'Connor; the 1948 issue included Guy Davenport and "Jim" Dickey; the 1951, Donald Hall. At a yearly Arts Forum, student writing was discussed by visiting artists and critics. Between the years 1944 and 1964 visitors included Lowell, Lionel Trilling, Robert Frost, Robie Macauley, Robert Penn Warren, Richard Wilbur, Jean Stafford, R. P. Blackmur, Katherine Anne Porter, Elizabeth Bowen, Karl Shapiro, John Dos Passos, Caroline Gordon, and Adrienne Rich. 51 Every year, the largest room on campus was filled to capacity, and not only by the students who considered themselves artsy but by the Home Ec. and Phys. Ed. students as well. These were the wonder years; every teacher or student I spoke to expressed a sense of astonishment that this could have been happening at Greensboro, never a wealthy school. The students were the beneficiaries of the network of Jarrell's and other faculty members' professional contacts. Some undoubtedly came to the Arts Forum the way another student might go to a football game, as something to do. Some may have been lured by the famous names, the phenomenon of poet-as-celebrity. These are motives with which Jarrell would have had little patience. He wanted his students to see and hear as people the poets whose poems they were reading. Poetry was to be an ordinary occupation, as Robert Watson put it: "People like Randall and Peter [Taylor] expected their students to turn out to be writers. . . . It was the natural thing to do. It wasn't something special and different that you were doing." 52 The visiting poets read and discussed the students' poems as if they mattered--and to Jarrell they did. He was, Betty Watson said, "wild with joy when he liked something." 53

What Jarrell kept in the foreground of his teaching, in the classroom and out, was the idea of reading and writing poetry as a normal human activity. "Mr. Jarrell was good," as Suzanne Rodgers Bush put it, "for us ordinary students." 54 Pritchard notes that "his method was unacademic, insofar as he did nothing more complicated--or more difficult--than to read poems and stories aloud and then discuss them with his students . . . [who] were held by the look of his face and the sound of his voice." 55 His methods were intense and perceptive appreciation, excited praise, an enactment of the series of discoveries that careful reading revealed. The "ordinary" student, Mrs. Bush, recalled with pleasure that as she wrote her examinations she was quoting the poems from memory, so strongly had her teacher's readings impressed her. Forty years later, she was still able to quote lines from poetry she studied under Jarrell. 56 In the context of modernism's argument for poetry as a specialized language requiring specialized techniques of analysis--a context that worked to separate form from content and erudite from common readers--Jarrell's method looks retrogressive, a throwback to the "appreciative" writing of the previous century. His method was in fact a conscious protest against the elevation of poetry to a "members only" status. And paradoxically his insistence on the ordinariness of poetry worked simultaneously to restore an aura of enchantment around poetry, a point not lost on his students. Heather Ross Miller detects a subversive underside to the story of Sleeping Beauty: "the possibility of danger: the dangerous bride. You remember what happened to all those first princes who tried to get through the thorns to her? . . . [Jarrell] certainly brought to me the strong sense of something going on, an enchantment, that was both beautiful and dangerous--and you'd better be careful how you woke that thing up." 57 It is the interdependence of the enchanted and the quotidian ("What a girl called 'the dailiness of life,'" as he expresses it in "Well Water" [CP, 300]) that shaped Jarrell's writing and teaching.

At the conclusion of "Poets, Critics, and Readers," Jarrell writes, "The work of art, Rilke said, says to us always: You must change your life" (K, 318; Jarrell's emphasis). Jarrell knew that such metamorphosis, though a powerful possibility, would remain for most merely that--a possibility. But on the strength of that possibility he imagined his students waking up not as brides, voters, and shoppers, but as readers and poets. Contra James Joyce, he restored the idea of poetry for ladies, but with the difference that Jarrell did not consider "ladies" a derogatory term. A point that has been made repeatedly in recent years--and with considerable force of persuasion--is that Jarrell sentimentalized women, appropriated them, and impersonated them. 58 Another interpretation, one that does not necessarily foreclose those readings, is that Jarrell chose to align himself with the feminine as a means of advocating values that had been dismissed by the modernist movement. The Lost World, Jarrell's last volume of poetry, is a compendium of the forbidden sentimental subjects--animals, women, children, nostalgia--tough-minded modernism thought it had left behind. And, as James Longenbach has remarked, Jarrell is now speaking in his own voice, not through a female mask. 59 These are, I believe, his best poems; they evince a settled air of accepting his own sentimentality and a finely nuanced understanding of its consolations and liabilities. These would include the danger of writing poetry that would be dismissed as self-indulgent and tear-jerking. In a 1942 appreciation of Marianne Moore's What Are Years, Jarrell concludes by noting the usual objection to her poems: "'They are so small.'" "Yes," he responds, "they are as small as those animals which save the foolish heroes of fairy tales--which can save only the heroes, because they are too small not to have been disregarded by everyone else." (PA, 184) Jarrell might be said to have made a speciality of regarding what others disregarded. His oppositional stance is similarly "small" in dimension. Writing his poems, crafting his essays, teaching his students, he undramatically but persistently labored to deflate the self-importance, pretension, and mystification that separated the reader from her book.
