“The Art of Radia” Pino Masnata’s Unpublished Gloss to the Futurist Radio Manifesto Introduction

Margaret Fisher

Pino Masnata, co-author of the 1933 Futurist Radio Manifesto with Filippo Tommaso Marinetti, wrote a second work, “Il Nome Radia,” to explain the first.1 Masnata’s gloss securely places the Radio Manifesto within the context of “new” physics—what Kenneth Ford calls “the physics of the very small” and “the physics of the very fast.”2 The intent, however, is nothing less than to conquer the infinite cosmos with the acoustic art of radio and the exploitation of the electromagnetic spectrum by Futurism’s “new man.”

Introduction

From 1925 to 1930, the directors of the new Italian Radio service, a triumvirate of top engineers from Fiat, Western Electric and Radiofono (the Marconi Co. Italian subsidiary), devoted a small number of program hours to experimental content focused chiefly on international developments in radio technology and information sharing, education, and music (including electronic music). Futurist radio broadcast was of a different stripe, and rarer still. The Fascist Party (PNF) in 1930 sought to marginalize these interests as so much “dilettantism.”3 Then, in May 1933, the Italian visit of Joseph Goebbels to align Italian cultural policy with the centralization doctrine of his Reich Ministry for Public Enlightenment and Propaganda had a chilling effect on all apolitical radio experimentation. 156 founder of Futurism and a member of the Royal Academy, was on a list of approved speakers vetted by the PNF, though his radio appearances were also limited. Against this background, Marinetti and Masnata published Manifesto futurista della radio in the Torino Gazzetta del Popolo on September 22, 1933, proposing a completely new radio sensibility resonant with a Futurist poetic on the one hand, and the latest quantum theories of matter and energy on the other. The Radio Manifesto appeared late in the chronology of Futurist manifestos; it was neither the first theoretical discussion of radio in Europe, nor in Italy. Before most countries had broadcast stations, Russian Futurist Velimir Khlebnikov predicted that radio waves would transport color, image, taste and odors in addition to sound. A decade later, Enzo Ferrieri, Artistic Director of Italian Radio, published Italy’s first radio manifesto, Radio as a Creative Force, which introduced the seminal idea that the source of radio’s true, paradoxical power derives from silence.4 Marinetti and Masnata built upon both of these concepts in the quest to conquer time and space, their given mandate from the Second Futurist Congress (Milan, 1933). Futurist focus on the machine turned to the science behind the machine: wave motion and the behavior of sub-atomic particles. The Futurist agenda for the “new man,” whose renewed spirit would manifest as an extension of Futurist willpower and the evolution of the senses, moved to a new stage, the electromagnetic spectrum. The Manifesto denigrates a radio culture spawned by both producer and public; it denounces radio’s adherence to realism and dismisses the reliance on studio production and light musical entertainment. Its twenty bullet points prescribe an entirely new radio art, “la radia”—neither cinema nor theater, a radio art independent of government control while fiercely loyal to the Fascist cause. On the one hand, we must shed our expectations of recovering a Futurist radio tied to the broadcast station; on the other, we can only speculate as to what is intended when the Manifesto hints at the dissolution of boundaries between the spiritual and material realms, the transference of thought and matter through vibration, and communication with the past. Cognizant of the difficulties presented by the Manifesto’s cryptic twists and turns, Masnata as the Manifesto’s principal author penned a 44-page gloss to the Manifesto, alternating between naming the inspirational sources behind the art of radia, and explaining what was meant. The work remained in Marinetti’s possession, unpublished. Masnata’s comments adhere to two overarching themes: 1) atomic theory and the electromagnetic spectrum and 2) the free-word style (paroliberismo)—the Futurist literary style that dispenses with syntax to experiment with disjunctive words (parole in libertà), onomatopoeia, signs, symbols, numbers, colors and shapes. Both themes support Masnata’s premise: radio sciences hold the promise of a gadget-less telepathic future in which Futurist parole in libertà, joined to radio waves, would create new shapes out of ethereal silence. Radio artists of the future would move their thought and extend their will at the speed of light without the radio apparatus; endowed with paranormal perceptive powers, they would receive and transmit vibrations directly.5 Such a person is mentioned four times in Masnata’s manuscript. New developments in physics fuel Masnata’s unbridled enthusiasm and speculative conclusions. His idiosyncratic arguments progress from the vibrations of living cells to atomic theory, from the science of thermionics to the periodic table of elements.

Thermionic valves, which detect radio waves, are vacuum tubes that control the unidi-157 rectional flow of electrons emitted from an incandescent surface. It becomes in Masnata’s disquisition, a metaphor for unlocking the secrets of the universe, and the ideal technology for realizing the Radio Manifesto’s call for a “metallization of the human body,” the Futurist biological project to revolutionize cell chemistry with engineered atomic bonding.6 The key to this bonding would be potassium, a highly reactive alkali metal, and, as noted by Masnata, found in every living cell. Masnata reminisces that he and Marinetti, when writing the Manifesto, felt as if they were thermionic valves unlocking nature’s secrets.

When asked during a lecture tour, “How do you hear silence?” Marinetti answered that humanity hears silence differently after exposure to new technology, “Can we deny, on the other hand, that the thermionic valves have immeasurably increased the field of our sensitivity ever since an Italian (thank goodness for this) scientist managed to receive the vibrations of a brain as differing graphic signs according to the different thought issuing from the brain itself?”7 The scientist is identified in Masnata’s gloss.

Masnata also presents the particulars of a new art. Scenic space will be rendered in cosmic proportions to exploit infinite changeability and infinite space. The audience, too, will be infinite and indeterminate. Gone were assumptions about the “absoluteness” of space inherited from “classical physics” and promulgated as cinematic and theatrical realism. Albert Einstein proposed that space is independent of objects in the world. The concept of matter—fundamental postulate of classical physics—gave way to the concept of a field made up of both particles and waves, the “new fundamental constituents of matter.”8

With the infinite and the indeterminate for his setting, Masnata turns to his main theme, the relationship of the human nervous system to the electromagnetic spectrum. Cell biology is central and bio-electric pioneer Georges Lakhovsky (1869–1942), among the most important scientists in Masnata’s canon. Lakhovsky proposed that living cells have “oscillating circuits [that . . .] vibrate electrically under the stimulus of electromagnetic waves: cosmic, atmospheric and telluric.”9 Lakhovsky’s concluding remarks place Masnata’s speculations about telepathy in high relief:

The study of electromagnetic phenomenon has overturned our old mechanical conceptions of the constitution of matter. For its turn, the study of the universal and the cosmic waves broadens the boundaries of science and will provide, such is my personal belief, the solution to fascinating problems of life, of telepathy and of the transmission of thought.10

Masnata envisions the Futurist endowed with paranormal perception, able to tap into and decipher ancient thoughts and words still radiating through the atmosphere, “People will be surprised that the [Radio] Manifesto speaks of tapping light and voices of the past.” The basis of his argument is that sound and light waves, once generated, weaken with distance but never die; they continue to radiate and are everywhere around us, albeit in an extremely tenuous state. Radio, he wrote, “would lead to the death of time in art, it would lead to an art of four dimensions, the simultaneity of yesterday with today and tomorrow.”

158 Giuseppe Masnata (1901–1968)

Surgeon, poet, dramatist and soldier, Pino Masnata at age eighteen joined with the Futurists in Milan and with the PNF. He helped to found political “fighting groups” (fasci di combattimento) and participated in the March on Rome, October 1922.11 When not in active military service, he was a practicing surgeon at Milan’s Ospedale Maggiore to 1942. He continued his Futurist activities until his death in 1968.

Since the 1920s, Masnata brought psychology and science to bear on poetry and fiction; he experimented in the field of theater to eliminate boundaries between the real and unreal, between thought and action, as described in his first manifesto, Visionic Theatre. In the 1930s, he wrote a number of radio sintesi, or condensed works, in collaboration with composer Carmine Guarino. They broadcast one of the first operas written specifically for radio, the fifteen-minute “Wanda’s heart” (Il cuore di Wanda), aired on Italian radio on December 20, 1931.

On June 15, 1933, the Second Futurist Congress named Masnata its National Poet Champion. Marinetti wrote of his poetry, “The Free-Word style of Pino Masnata is original, expressive, aggressive, condensed and suggestive like a slab of ice in the sun. He mixes up a violent cocktail combining obscure states of mind with lyrical landscapes in clear prose.”12

The archival document

A manuscript of 51 hand-numbered pages is found in the Marinetti, Filippo Tommaso Papers in the Beinecke Rare Book and Manuscript Library (General Collection 130, Box 34, Folder 1561) at Yale University. A cover page in Marinetti’s handwriting bears the title La Radio futurista and names of the authors, F. T. Marinetti and Pino Masnata. Typescript pages 1-6 contain the text of the Futurist Radio Manifesto. Page 7, in Masnata’s hand, is a record of the Manifesto’s publication in foreign journals. The 44-page exegesis (pages 8-51), titled Il Nome Radia, is divided by 20 section titles. There are 41 handwritten pages and three and a half typescript pages.

The undated gloss contains markers indicating 1935 as the year of composition: the world speed record set by the Italian seaplane, the Macchi M.C. 72 (October 23, 1934); Masnata’s book Canti fascisti della metropoli verde (published 1935); and the interpolation of a typescript ms. authored by Masnata, “F. 162 Excerpts from “The Art of Radia”

Pino Masnata

The Futurist Radio Manifesto needs some explanation because it contains a synthesis of numerous modern scientific and artistic tenets. Only someone who stays informed of the current trends in human ideas can understand the full significance of our Manifesto and dispense with the explanation.

The Futurist Radio Manifesto was published in the Gazzetta del popolo of Torino on 22 September 1933; it was written as a collaborative effort at Lake Garda where we were graciously hosted by the lawyer Piccoli at his villa.1

. . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . .

The Manifesto calls for the creation of a new art form particular to radio transmission.

After much consideration and discussion we decided not to refer to this new art by the name of Radio Theater [Teatro radiofonico], which is inaccurate as it presupposes the necessary condition of theater, i.e., performance. Cinema, however close to theater, was given its own name. The names, for example, of theater of celluloid, theater of shadows, theater of the screen, etc., etc., would have been odd.

And so we decided it inappropriate to simply assign the name radio to this new expression of human thought, in order not to attribute to this unfortunate word yet another meaning to the many it already possesses. We gave it a name with a feminine ending because that is the gender of almost all the arts, from painting [la pittura] to sculpture [la scultura], from architecture [l’architettura] to poetry [la poesia].

We called it radia [radio art].2

. . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . .

The English responded that they have the word broadcasting, which means the diffusion of information by radio. But implicit in the word is the concept of transmitting a performance, not of creating one. And so we would prefer that radia not be used for all broadcast transmissions but be earmarked for creations of radiophonic art (we call it radia), art that is inevitably and clearly Futurist.

Against Tradition

We Futurists by definition are opposed to traditions, especially when these traditions expend energy defending or glorifying the pointless copying of masterpieces of the past. In the majority of cases, these masterpieces were, for the times in which they were created, original, rebellious, and anti-traditional.

In every art, “traditional” is synonymous with slipshod, ordinary.

But if every art has a past that we can more or less return to, radia is new, pure, different; it cannot be linked in an obvious way to the other arts, which were conceived to be expressed in their respective media and with totally different means.

Poetry, for example, is tied to the person reciting and the fact that this person, save for exceptional futuristic attempts, is alone on a silent stage.

The poet must convey everything with his words—color, light, sound, music, back-ground—using rhythm, contrast, images, simultaneity, and with all the techniques of literature, but always and only with words, and by changing the way they are arranged.

Theater is tied to the stage, to the hall, to the lights, to time durations equal to the attention span of the spectator (the division into acts), to the time required to be able to send the audience on its way before midnight, to the time required for the scene change, etc.

Theater, with the exception of some of our attempts at Futurist theater, is tied to the famous Greek unities of place, time, space; furthermore, it is clearly tied to vision, that is, to the spectacle.

The novel is tied to the book, whether to length or style. Novels that are considered masterpieces would not withstand a live reading in a room, a theater, or in front of the microphone.

And such is the case for all other art forms which, like water in a container, must adapt to the shape of the container.

. . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . .

Radia should naturally indicate how to prepare a program suited to the listener’s attention span, how to exploit the possibilities presented by sounds, noises, music, voices; and accept the impossibility of presenting colors and shapes.

A brilliant lecturer can hold the public mesmerized for about an hour, but at the microphone he ought not to exceed ten minutes. He can fill out his ideas with more words when he speaks in a hall, but he ought to condense his words to the utmost when he is speaking from a broadcasting station.

The loudspeaker is a perfidious jack-in-the-box that shows the defects in high relief, exaggerates the little things, and makes rhetoric loathsome. Few are spared.

In the face of this frightfully irreverent box of mechanistic mockery, clarity and speed are essential to comprehension.

. . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . .

Universal or Cosmic Scenery

Many authors have expressed persistent concern regarding the scenery in their vain chatter about radio, or directly in their “radio dramas.” How can one convey the scenery to the listener?

164 Some fancy it can be described by an announcer;3 others more attuned would have the scene arise from the context of the speech, where sentences inadvertently make the scenery evident. In any case everyone reveals their terrible obsession with the theater.4 Often, the authors of dramatic works hope to launch their already more or less successful work on this invisible stage. But the stage has collapsed; it ceases to exist. These authors pray and trust that radio is equivalent to theater for the blind, for whom the authors deem it necessary to supply an obligatory friendly witness who explains everything: “The curtain rises on a garden; the protagonist is a beautiful woman dressed in red; she is seated on a chair; she gets up, she picks a rose, etc.” All this, unfortunately, is needed to broadcast a theatrical work on the radio; and it all demonstrates perfectly how tragedy, comedy, etc., are invariably compromised when spoken at the microphone. We writers for radio, not beholden to a visual scene, are instead free of the oppressive nightmare of always situating the actors in the same place. We are pleased that the scene can change with every cue; it can be external, internal, real, unreal, points in space, small, large, universal. Cinema already has achieved a larger scenic space than found in theater, but, given realism’s resistance to change, remains tied to anti-art. Scenic space will reach its maximum expansion with radia; the infinite (to use the word in its true sense) scene will open out to a listening audience of infinite dimensions.

Tapping Into Vibrations Emitted By The Living Or The Dead

The dream of settling the question, What is life? stems from time immemorial, and perhaps man will never be able to answer it. To be able to do that, it would be necessary to be outside of oneself or completely outside the universe.5 If it is true that stars still exist one thousand million light years away, it’s reason enough to lose hope for the living and the dead. Those who nowadays don’t believe in religion have convictions that do not differentiate them very much from the occultists of the Orient who believed vital energy was a radiant force in space (prana).

One is dealing more or less with what Confucius called the luminous body; Plato, alchemy; the Kabbalist school, the astral light; Mesmer, the universal fluid;6 Barety the radiant beaming force,7 Flammarion the psychic force;8 etc., etc.

Many writers subscribe to the belief that the origin of life ought to be found in the radioactive emanations of potassium, an element found in every cell. Our own radioactivity has already been implicitly acknowledged, verifying the phenomena of telepathy and thought transmission.

In 1903, Blondlot reported he had been able to experimentally prove the existence of a form of essential radiation.9 He gave it the name “N rays” (after Nancy, the city where he worked), and said that in the spectrum they occupy the place beyond ultraviolet rays. Then Charpentier reported he had succeeded in isolating radiations produced by the nervous and muscular systems with experiments and studies on fluorescence obtained with calcium sulphite.10 Other writers who would promote and perfect these 165 areas of research followed in their steps, among whom, we mention Starr White, Albert Nodon and the Italian Calligaris.11

A very important theorist is George Lakhovsky, author of the books The Origins of Life [L’origine della vie], The Universe [L’universion], The Secret of Life [Le secret de la vie], Cellular Oscillations [L’oscillation cellulare] and Matter [La matiere], volumes which have caused and continue to cause quite a stir and have many enthusiastic readers.12 He was the first to formulate these five fundamental principles:13

1) All living beings emit radiation.

2) The majority of living beings are capable of receiving and transmitting waves, because every system of radiation enjoys such characteristics.

3) Living creatures capable of leaving the ground because they are furnished with wings, have an enormous ability to emit and receive waves, while animals that are earth-bound have infinitely less capacity for emitting and receiving.

4) The influence of solar light on wave motion induces some birds and insects to travel or to feed themselves at night, their receptivity being truly marvelous, while other animals with weak receptivity devote themselves to their usual activities.

The radiations according to Lakhovsky would be either the long electromagnetic waves (Herzian) or short waves or cosmic waves. These waves would cause the cellular circuit to oscillate, influencing life (every cell would be an oscillatory circuit).

Situating his work at the furthest reaches of modern and atomic physics (to which we will allude below), the Paris-based philosopher-scientist sought to demonstrate further on in his book Matter that each element that is a component of a substance is no longer matter but a condensate of radiation, characterized by its own oscillating frequency. For each material thing there is a corresponding radiation that propagates it and that can cause it to materialize by means of resonance if it meets the same wave length disposed to provoking the condensation. So that, all the heavenly bodies of the firmament, the suns, the planets, the stars as well as the molecules, the atoms with all their attendant satellites, electrons, neutrons, positrons, protons, etc., would be but the products of the materialization of inter-astral radiations that plough the infinity of space and time.14

Lakhovsky gives this example: Inseminate a colony containing only the bacteria staphylococci, a ten millionth of a milligram of iron and phosphorous, over gelatin, closed in a Petri dish and bring it to 37 degrees centigrade. After 24 hours, retrieve the Petri dish and you will be able to confirm with the naked eye that there are billions and billions of staphylococci, and that you can harvest an infinite number of these particular microbes.15

The only rational explanation of this consists, according to the author, in the materialization of the specific radiations of each material; these radiations condense when they find a wave length which allows them to enter into resonance.

Lakhovsky’s example serves only to clarify rather than to demonstrate. We’re not being propagandists nor sponsoring these theories; we limit ourselves to collecting them to show what is being said and thought. In fact, if the above-mentioned theories

166 are debatable, no one today would dare challenge Gurwitsch’s mitogenetic radiation, namely, [weak electromagnetic] rays originating in cells which then cause cellular proliferation.16 On the other hand it is certain that short waves provoke hyperthermia and are already widely used as such for therapy. A new science was then founded—the Science of Electro-Radio-Biology—which had its first international congress in Venice in 1934 under the presidency of Guglielmo Marconi.17 In addition to this, Professor Cazzamali [sic], using radio equipment to capture extremely short waves, has been able to receive and record the vibrations of the brain, or, better, the vibrations of thought, with his thermionic valves, apparently without margin of error.18 Guglielmo Marconi’s opinion is as follows, “The human brain is an instrument that is more delicate even than any apparatus invented by man, and it is evidently possible that one can receive messages at distances even greater than any mechanical transmitter.”19 Will we, in the future, become radio receivers without the radio apparatus? And, on the other hand, will we, in the future, be able to directly express our thought or fantasy without a mechanism for argument—mouth, tongue, word? There are those who hold that the ultimate conclusion of modern physics is this: the universe is only a thought. Radio functionally presents many analogies to our own brain: its universal elements, simultaneity, interference, fading; radio that one doesn’t see; radio where one imagines everything; radio that is everywhere; radio that allows us to hear two or three stations simultaneously, so much so that one speaker is interrupted by another or by music; radio that diverts us, disappears and reappears. And if it is true that the universe is only thought, radio ought to be the instrument best suited to investigate this. In the future might we become radio receivers without the radio apparatus? Friends will ask us if we believe all of these fantasies. Alas, speaking as men of science, we will tell them we don’t believe in anything any more, like all scientists of the twentieth century. We don’t believe in the microscope, which made us smaller, we don’t trust our senses, we don’t trust in experiment, we don’t believe in theories. How could it be otherwise? Today there is a rise in the number of scientists, disciples of Maxwell, the English physicist and mathematician; Maxwell refuses [to speak separately about] the light and heat of the sun, the sun being [as he understands it] a singular large magnet emitting electromagnetic waves.20 As poets, however, we believe in everything; everything interests us and affects us: the experiment and the theory, the calculus and the poetry, everything. We hope one night to steal a small star (30,000 times the diameter of the earth) and to exorcise the evil spirits with it.21

Tapping Into Vibrations Emitted By Matter

Everyone knows one can reduce matter down to the molecule inside compound bodies, and to the atoms of simple bodies or elements, without fear of changing its characteristics. The atomic theory until a few years ago appeared unsurpassable.22

Then, it was proved that the atom is made up of electrons, negative electron streams around a heavy positive particle called the atomic nucleus (Rutherford’s nucleus).23 The atom appears to be in equilibrium within its electrical complex and therefore neutral.

The number of electrons (the atomic number) differentiates the elements. Hydrogen 1, Helium 2, Lithium 3, Beryllium 4, Boron 5, Carbon 6, Nitrogen 7, Oxygen 8, Iron 26, Gold 79, etc. There are 92 elements in all (uranium is 92). Bohr was the first to rationalize a calculation of the atomic system, which is very similar, architecturally, to the planetary system.24

But what are electrons?

Heisenberg, Born and Schroedinger studied the problem. These are names of Nobel prize winners. [There are those of] Dirac, Jordan and Fermi, as well.25

We tend to think of the electron as no more than an extremely small bit of physical matter, that is, a minute particle or corpuscle.26

Then appeared [a theory of] wave mechanics [to explain electron behavior].27

To explain the various phenomena we need to have recourse to a new theory: the components of matter (electrons) are something that are, at the same time, both like a corpuscle and like a wave. They are similar to radiation.

Radiant energy, in fact, is not emitted continuously but by [a particular kind of] quantity (quantum) that cannot be further subdivided (Planck).28 But to penetrate and explain the physics in depth requires an extensive detour that takes us too far from our topic.

Let’s accept this much and conclude.

Does matter exist?

What is the world? A universe of trapped waves and of waves that are constantly shifting.

Here again, the importance of radio is proved: tomorrow it could tap the secret of the universe, the trembling soul, the living essence of flowers or metals, the voice of God.

. . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . .

Art Without Time and the Destruction of Time

The first part is clear: just as scenic boundaries do not exist on the radio, time boundaries also do not exist. People will be surprised that the Manifesto speaks of tapping light and voices of the past.

Yet, this was Edison’s dream, and he devoted the last years of his life to it.29 He began with the concept that nothing is lost: the ray that illuminated Napoleon and was reflected still endures: one only needs to track it! The voice of Julius Caesar gave rise to a vibration that, in a very attenuated state, is still with us.

168 Thermionic valves have accustomed us to the miracle of tapping the most tenuous vibrations and amplifying them. The difference that exists between the wave received by our radio equipment and the voice coming out of the loudspeaker is equal to the difference that might exist between our ordinary voice and the voice one might be able to hear normally and clearly through the air from Milan to New York, without machines. The possibility of tapping into voices from the past might not interest us as much historically as it would interest the historians, but perhaps a bit, because it would put an end to the many false illusions about antiquity. But also, apart from the value of the method of inquiry, the discovery by itself would very much interest us artistically: it would lead to the death of time in art, it would lead to an art of four dimensions: the simultaneity of yesterday, today and tomorrow. We will leave to historians and philosophers the joy of going back to the source of life and of the world, of the power to discover the mystery of our appearance on the terrestrial planet.

. . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . .

Interference Between Stations and Fading

Every radio listener knows of two phenomena typical of radio reception: a) interference caused by another station which whistles throughout the music or competes with it;

b) fading, which in Italian we call “evanescense,” that is, the mysterious appearance and disappearance of sounds due, so it appears, to a layer of ionized air that envelops the earth and reflects or refracts the electromagnetic waves (the Kennelly-Heaviside layer).30

While these phenomena are normally bothersome, because they are accidental, we believe they could be used artistically.

They could have value as techniques characteristic of radia. This does not refer to them [i.e., the types of interference], because they are not subjects of our will, but to their effects. Just like the perspectival effects in Futurist photography and cinematography: [these] can be artistic when they are applied with artistic taste; they are not human but typical of machines.

Geometric Demarcation and Construction of Silence

Everyone, undoubtedly, when turning on the radio, even before the announcer tells us, will have received a clear impression of the ambient environment in which the microphone has been placed. 31

Outdoors, in a room, in a theater, in a café, etc. But we are speaking about silence that is not eternal. Silence between two sentences, two words, two musical bars.

This silence can be of any duration. This in-between time has a prime geometric 169 quality: length. In addition, we can have variable depths of silence and variable volume.

Silence is also very important to theatrical recitation.

The audience, in fact, knows perfectly well the tragic silences of Ruggeri, the dense, dark silences of Zacconi, those anxious silences of Cimara, etc. which are the best part of their art.32

Further, silence is extremely important to declamation, especially for parole in libertà. The words ought to be separated frequently and the pauses should be of different lengths.

Silences in radia, naturally, will have other lengths, depths, volumes, diverse values. People still need to understand how to create these [silences].

. . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . .

Conclusion

When we wrote this Manifesto, we were on Lago di Garda in the park of a hospitable villa. Innumerable blue waves were swept to the foot of [Mt.] Altissimo, smashing into the rocks on which we found ourselves.33 We felt like two active thermionic valves highly sensitized to the waves coming from the infinite. We trust we snatched from nature another of its many secrets of beauty and of art.

172 20. James Clerk Maxwell (1831–1879), Scottish mathematician who demonstrated that electric and magnetic fields travel as waves at the speed of light.   1. Atomic theory dates to the fifth century B.C., when the Greek philosopher Democritus proposed that matter is made up of discrete units called atoms. Our basic understanding of atomic theory is from the English scientist John Dalton (1766–1844): all matter is made of atoms; atoms cannot be divided or destroyed; atoms of a given element are identical in properties and mass; a combination of two or more different kinds of atoms results in a compound; a rearrangement of atoms results in a chemical reaction. Atoms were thought to be indivisible until 1897, when the British scientist Joseph John Thomson (1856–1940) discovered the electron. Thomson at first postulated that atoms were made entirely of electrons (Roads, 2001, 51–52).
    1. Ernest Rutherford (1871–1937), New Zealand chemist, physicist, and 1908 winner of the Nobel Prize in chemistry. A student of J. J. Thomson, Rutherford proved that the atom was divisible into electrons, which have a negative charge, and a nucleus with a positive charge. He based his “Planetary Model of the Atom” on the solar system of orbiting planets. The model did not account for radiating energy that results when the electrons are subjected to acceleration.
    2. Masnata uses the word “reotanti,” demonstrating he was aware of recent research that described the electron path in terms of mathematical probability rather in terms of an orbit. Today, the word “orbital” is used to make the distinction.
  2. Niels Henrik David Bohr (1885–1962), Danish physicist who worked in the laboratory of Ernest Rutherford and received the 1922 Nobel Prize for furthering the understanding of atomic structure and the radiation emitted by atoms. The Bohr model of the atom shows a positively charged nucleus surrounded by electrons moving in circular orbits. It improves on the Rutherford model by showing that electrons are attracted to the nucleus by electrostatic forces. The electrons in the outer orbits determine the reactive properties of the elements. Building on Einstein’s research, Bohr proposed that electrons could emit photons, units of light measured as a form of discrete energy. Photons are emitted when the electrons move from a higher-energy orbit to a lower one. The now-outdated Bohr model is still used as an introduction to atomic structure.
  3. Werner Karl Heisenberg (1901–1976), German theoretical physicist and Niels Bohr’s assistant, was winner of the 1932 Nobel Prize in physics for the creation of quantum mechanics. Heisenberg authored the Uncertainty Principle, which specifies uncertainty in mathematical relations regarding the position and momentum of an electron or any other particle. It states that position and momentum cannot be determined simultaneously; if one is known, the measure of the other is blurred or less precise.

Max Born (1882–1970), German physicist and mathematician who, with Heisenberg and Pascual Jordan (below), developed mathematical matrices to represent electron orbits based on probability parameters. The result was “matrix mechanics,” an adjunct to quantum mechanics. The significance of their work was to open up pure mathematics to physicists. Masnata mistakenly thought Born, nominated by Einstein to share the 1932 Nobel prize with Heinsenberg, was awarded the prize. Born did not win the prize until 1954.

Erwin Rudolf Josef Schrödinger (1887–1961), Austrian theoretical physicist, founder of wave mechanics, and winner, with Paul Dirac, of the 1933 Nobel Prize for contributions to atomic theory. Based on the premise that a particle has a wave associated with it, Schrödinger was able to develop a “wave equation” to describe how the quantum state of a physical system changes in time; this is called “considering the wave function.”

Paul Adrien Maurice Dirac (1902–1984), British theoretical physicist, contributor to the field of quantum mechanics, and winner, with Schrödinger, of the 1933 Nobel Prize. Dirac described electron behavior and, based on Einstein’s Special Theory of Relativity, theorized the existence of antimatter, or antiparticles, before they were discovered in the laboratory.

Ernst Pascual Jordan (1902–1980), German theoretical and mathematical physicist who, with Born and Heisenberg, founded quantum mechanics, a way to express the parameters used to account for uncertainty in an electromagnetic field as “an infinite set of harmonic oscillators.” Simple harmonic oscillation is a model of periodic motion. Jordan’s contributions to quantum field theory might have won him the Nobel Prize, but his membership in the Nazi Party in 1933 appears to have disqualified him, as well as his re-entry into politics after WWII.

Enrico Fermi (1901–1954), Director of the Institute of Physics at the University of Rome and winner of the Nobel Prize in 1938, interpreted Einstein’s equation E = mc2 in a way that proved the potential for small amounts of mass to be converted into enormous quantities of energy. His main work was with induced radioactivity. After Fermi moved his family to the United States to avoid the racial laws instituted by Mussolini, he participated in the Manhattan Project to develop the first nuclear reactor and atomic bomb. Fermi was still working in Italy when Masnata wrote this gloss.
