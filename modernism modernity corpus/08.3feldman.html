Modernism's Victorian Bric-à-brac

Jessica R. Feldman

Victorian Modernism

Artists and critics alike view high modernism within scenes of rupture and yearning. Even when artists vigorously stage "the new," anxiety and nostalgia shade desire. Wallace Stevens's "poem of the mind in the act of finding / What will suffice" once the past becomes "souvenir" conveys this need for a modernist rescue operation in a present defined by loss. 1 Modernist writers, as we often acknowledge in one way or another, want to separate themselves and their work from the recent past as well as from other modernist writers and to preclude the possibility of rescue from their thrilling and despairing isolation.

To this understanding of literary modernism, we must add the idea that modernists were interested not just in rupture, but also in continuity, and specifically in continuities with the Victorian period, 1837-1901. We must try to hear, within the rallying cry of "make it new," the harmonic of "preserve it through change." It is important to tell critical stories of both destruction and preservation, and one way to do so is to bring the mid-century, high Victorian into relation with high modernism. As we do, a Victorian modernist aesthetic of both rupture and continuity, of stark differences and relations across gaps, develops.

Studying Victorian modernism, we learn to consider works of art as webs of relations and ideas with multiple centers and gaps, a filigree-in-progress. Or, in weightier terms, we should learn to pay attention to collections of things, arranged but subject to rearrangement. In parallel, our critical goal must be to chart coherences that begin and end with critical venturings from divers centers. As we shall see, George Eliot writes of "multiplex" structures, and William James of "concatenated unions." Both urge us to consider delicate and complicated structures, not solid blocks.

Victorian modernist criticism might speak of seeming solidities, for example, "the modern epic" or "the New Woman novel." But like the structural designs of buildings that John Ruskin often found less interesting than their surface ornamentations, the solidities such studies describe exist for the intermingling patterns they make possible, not the patterns for the solidities. 2 The house of modernism may be aggressively streamlined or built anew; but it also embraces yesterday's house, absorbing the patterned bric-à-brac of the Victorian parlor and carefully rearranging it. It is no accident that, in examining the cozy and ornamented characteristics of Victorian art and literature, we will see that nineteenth-century domestic and sentimental novelists in both Great Britain and the United States were engaged in creating works taken up by twentieth-century modernist writers, high and low. Indeed, distinctions between high and low or serious novels and domestic novels no longer serve us well. Henry James was a domestic novelist just as surely as Elizabeth Stoddard wrote difficult, impressionist, experimental prose. This connection is not meant simply to redraw the lines of recent literary history, but rather to challenge a central truism of literary study: that modernist writers, beginning in the 1890s, made an art that clearly departed from what had come immediately before. 3

But why posit Victorian modernism in the first place? One answer--that some modernist artists themselves did so--may be exemplified by William Butler Yeats's "Introduction" to his anthology, The Oxford Book of Modern Verse, 1892-1935. In their darker tableaux, modernist artists--and Yeats is no exception--often choose rupture as an alternative to repeating now emptied forms of the recent past. Yet when modernist writers insist upon radical discontinuity, we should not take them at their word. We should examine those words and the resulting works of art carefully, because Victorian practices often vitally inform modernist works. What is dismissed is also summoned. Yeats, for example, in writing the introduction to his anthology, describes the advent of the "modern" as the "wringing [of] the neck of rhetoric." It appears that Victorian poetic practices had to die in order that modernism could be born. Nothing less than revolution would do:

The revolt against Victorianism meant to the young poet a revolt against irrelevant descriptions of nature, the scientific and moral discursiveness of In Memoriam--"When he should have been broken-hearted", said Verlaine, "he had many reminiscences"--the political eloquence of Swinburne, the psychological curiosity of Browning, and the poetical diction of everybody. 4

We should not blind ourselves, however, to the fact that such revolt is only part of Yeats's programme for modernist poetry. Yeats weaves a contrasting tale throughout this very introduction that has gone largely unnoticed by scholars of modernism.

Yeats's poetic manifestoqua introduction not only announces a break with Victorianisms but also embraces them. In the very sentence in which Yeats states that young poets believe that discursiveness must go, he discursively interrupts the rhythm of his sentence to quote Verlaine--as if to challenge in expansive prose the chiseled reticencies of modern poetry. He tells us that modern poets dismiss rhetoric, yet the force of this statement depends on the ornate parallelisms of its lists and the satisfactions of its rhythms. In substance, too, Yeats argues with himself. Although the passage describes rupture from Tennyson, Swinburne, and Browning, Yeats continues to reject and embrace so-called Victorian discursiveness, eloquence, curiosity, and poetical diction as he describes modern poets and poetry worthy of our admiration.

He tells us that Walter Pater's "pure gem-like flame" [sic] gave modern poets an alternative to moral earnestness and a desire to "purify poetry of all that is not poetry" but--and here it's important to keep reading--that these poets found the flame "was an insufficient motive." As the essay proceeds, Yeats establishes a shimmering ironic stance conveying dual desires: simplification and complication, concentered gem-like flames and unruly flights and divergences. Increasingly calling into question the efficacy of attacking things Victorian as a way of setting modernist poetry apart, Yeats insists, "When my generation denounced scientific humanitarian pre-occupation, psychological curiosity, rhetoric, we had not found what ailed Victorian literature. The Elizabethans had all these things, especially rhetoric" (OMV, xvii).

So what did call for amendment in poetry, if it wasn't Victorianisms per se? For Yeats the answer seems to lie in the need for passion--and he proceeds, in this argument with himself, to find such passion precisely in Victorian poets and poems. Modern poets, he argues, must revivify people and poetry that have become too passive, wrest control of events and speak for the mind's "responsibility." Intellectual passion and engagement must have their day as "man's naked mind" claims its place (OMV, xxviii, xxix). Along with the Victorian virtue of responsibility, other aspects of Victorian poetic culture appeal to Yeats--his own taste (which he attributes to his Irish nationality) tends not to man's naked mind but to "a man so many years old, fixed to some one place, known to friends and enemies, full of mortal frailty, expressing all things not made mysterious by nature with impatient clarity" (OMV, xxxvii). The modernist sage of 1935 might have been describing a Victorian triple-decker novel, with its social world represented in painstaking detail.

The differences between "them," the modern poets, and himself, the always respectful Victorian modernist (Irish) outsider to modernism, accumulate throughout the essay. The modernists, Yeats tells us, may look for hard, positive subject matter and an avoidance of political ranting in poetry or in Trafalgar Square, but he prefers a poetry of passionate politics, a poetics of the heart. When in 1900, "everybody got down off his stilts and Victorianism had been defeated," the modern movement, in Yeats's view, was fruitfully dominated by two late Victorian writers, Rudyard Kipling and William Watson, "who had never heard of that defeat or did not believe in it"(OMV, xi-xii).

Why have Yeats's own Victorian passions been all but invisible to the central narrative of modernist literary study? And what will we begin to see once we seek the Victorian in the modern? The answers to those questions will, of course, depend on one's own modernism, since there is so little agreement about modern literature's formal qualities, its decade of origin, its longevity, and its groupings of artists and texts-- whether generic, national, philosophical, or historical. Yet as Stevens says, "It is time to choose," and so we shall. One conception of modernism calling for revision often goes by the name "high modernism," and its dates are roughly 1880-1945. It features a literary work whose mask of darkness and suffering may be flipped to reveal the mask of a retrieved Romantic, visionary illumination.

Conceived in a time of spiritual crisis, such "anti-Victorian" modernist art communicates an exile from the homeland of certainty as it sits high on the shelf of autonomous art. An object to be admired, or in some cases excoriated, for its self-sufficiency and self-involvement, the modern work is purely sculpted, durably masculine, paradoxically spiritual or cerebral for all its hardness, and preferably not for sale. Its internal incoherencies are bounded by its autonomy, made whole by its separation from the familiar. It takes the long and impersonal view, turning away from the ordinary and the fleshly, the vulgarly emotional and the preachy. One must work hard to understand and appreciate such a modernist work of art, and most people will fail to do so.

What happened between Romanticism and modernism--namely, the mid-century Victorian--has been largely left out of the story. This is not to claim that there are no studies of mid-nineteenth-century writers with modernist desires, or of modernist writers with ties to that portion of the literary past lying between the Romantics and the turn of the century. 5 Rather, it is to say that the task of integrating the two is far from complete, and that we as modernists should become self-consciously intradisciplinary. Over specialization, in particular the periodization of literary study, has narrowed our view for better and worse.

This modernist portrait, like all composites, presents a being that never existed. It is nonetheless one that we shall choose to take down and handle in order to move toward a conception of Victorian modernism. Chronologies of modernism often begin at the turn of the century, as the airless Victorian parlor, filled with bric-à-brac, is replaced by the "Aesthetic suite" of decadence, mysteriously layered with exotic objects and moody atmospheres. This is replaced in turn by the modernist "living space," cleared of conflicting patterns and sleek in its functional elegance. The chronology of Victorian modernism, in contrast, imagines these three periods as gathered into an incomplete unity because they share important ideas about artists, works of art, and audiences. Aesthetic notions and artistic practices intermingle, entwine; or they flow and pool, even metamorphose, one into the other. Juxtapositions become intimate relations. Phenomena of rupture and loss give way to relation and addition, and the patterns thereby formed link to other patterns.

Victorians themselves had been pattern-makers, reimagining a Romantic organic unity as a process of bringing internally linked complexities into expanding sets of relations with external complexities. George Eliot, for example, describes artistic form as a process of knowledge that grows "by its alternating processes of distinction and combination," arriving at the "conception of wholes composed of parts more and more multiplied and highly differenced." Multiple wholes are themselves internally multiple, and the highest form in art involves "the relation of multiplex interdependent parts to a whole which is itself in the most varied and therefore the fullest relation to other wholes." The best artistic form creates not a monistic unity of through-and-through synthesis, but something more complicated: "The most varied group of relations bound together in a wholeness which again has the most varied relations with all other phenomena." 6

What Eliot imagines is echoed by the modernist William James, in part because he came of age in a Victorian household. Eliot's multiplex interdependencies become James's "concatenated union" as he describes

the world . . . as a collection, some parts of which are conjunctively and others disjunctively related. Two parts, themselves disjoined, may nevertheless hang together by intermediaries with which they are severally connected, and the whole world eventually may hang together similarly, inasmuch as some path of conjunctive transition by which to pass from one of its parts to another may always be discernible. Such determinately various hanging-together may be called concatenated union, to distinguish it from the "through-and-through" type of union, "each in all and all in each". . . which monistic systems hold to obtain when things are taken in their absolute reality. 7

George Eliot emphasizes the wholes, James the transitions between and within them, but they share a notion of incomplete completions, multiple and multiply connected centers, and discontinuous continuities. Yeats, too, describes "the ultimate reality" as "neither one nor many, concord nor discord," the tertium quid, as it were, of all antinomies. 8

Dante Gabriel Rossetti, this essay's exemplary re of Victorian modernism, embodies these notions in the patterns of his daily life which are complexly linked to the patterns and arrangements of his artistic creation. The "multiplied and highly differenced" arrangements and rearrangements within his painting and poetry are akin--in the full sense of the word "kin"--to the interior decoration of his London house at 16 Cheyne Walk. This is the representative story of Victorian modernism that I will tell. But first, a brief look backward, to an eclipse of Romantic Imagination that made modernism possible.

Concatenated unions or multiplex relations have a Romantic pedigree of inferiority that goes by the name of Fancy. For the Romantics, Imagination trumps Fancy, because it allows the artist to work as a divinely creative force. The Victorian modernists' Romantic forebear, Coleridge, describes the supreme, God-the-Father-like powers of the Secondary Imagination. He even coins a term, esemplastic (to shape into one), both to describe such imagination and to enact it as he collapses two Greek words into one "Angloid" word. The Secondary Imagination "dissolves, diffuses, dissipates, in order to re-create . . . struggles to idealize and to unify. It is essentially vital, even as all objects (as objects) are essentially dead and fixed." In contrast to Imagination, Fancy is of the kingdom of death, for it "has no other counters to play with, but fixities and definites . . . [like ordinary memory] the Fancy must receive all its materials ready made from the law of association." 9 Fancy is, as it were, the interior decorator of the art world--it never creates from unformed matter paintings or poems of the first order, but merely rearranges existing artistic furniture. Surely, the moving of received, dead objects about is--in comparison to the vast seminal power of the unifying and idealizing Imagination--woman's work. 10

Yet through the rearranging energies of a latter-day Fancy, modernist writers such as Marcel Proust, Henry James, Virginia Woolf, William Butler Yeats, and James Joyce brought dead objects back to life: railroad timetables, cracked teacups, golden bowls, heather-mixture stockings, Connemara cloth, vaseline and orangeflowers. And they moved these fixities about not just as objects, but as objects that require us to rethink the nature of artistic unity as we come to grasp the Victorians' avant-garde notions of discontinuous continuity. Gertrude Stein's patterns of words on a page as objects on a page continue this tradition: "What does literature do and how does it do it. And what does English literature do and how does it do it. And what ways does it use to do what it does do." 11

Fancy has its own power of life in Victorian modernism, and once we reassess its value, hitherto marginalized texts of many kinds will suddenly appear more worthy of study. For example, mid-nineteenth-century domestic, sentimental, or highly wrought novels by American women will come into focus as a center, not the center, of Victorian modernism. Henry James read widely in such popular fiction, and wrote, for example, a review of Elizabeth Stoddard's fiction which conveys not just his dissatisfaction, but also his fascination with the links between his (high) art and her domestic and sensational fiction. In her children's tale, Lolly Dinks' Doings , Stoddard tells of a spider fairy named Fancie who moves in with the Dinks family and enchants them with her miniature domestic arrangements. Fancie is kin to the "little airs" who explore the Ramseys' abandoned house in Woolf's novel of aesthetic domesticity, and who "all sighed together, all together gave off an aimless gust of lamentation." 12 Summoned home to fairyland, Fancie tells Mrs. Dinks what she will miss:

"And dust, cinders; above all the human-crowd smell. I am not quite sure how I can get out of the tangle in your air of human speech. Missis, I see a thousand curves and threads crossing each other, making a great web, that rises and rises like a balloon in the sky. It is all words; and where is it trying to get to?"

"Heaven, Fancie."

She looked dull and dumb, and then I recollected that fairies have no spirits, and so cannot understand what the land of souls means. 13

Fancie takes a ride on the back of the pet bird, "up and down my new chintz lambrequins." She calls out, "Am I high enough for your heaven, Missis?" 14 Stoddard asks directly in Lolly Dinks' Doings whether fancy (Fancie) can get to heaven. It (she) cannot, but instead locates a new kind of earthly transcendence in the heights of lambrequins, in the chintz patterns of a domestic scene that Mrs. Dalloway herself would have recognized, and in a web of human speech akin to that which Joyce offered to Stephen Dedalus as he tried for heaven but never arrived.

Indeed, the very notion of decorative art, ornamentation and detail work--as opposed to one central infusion of breath that, like God creating the world, makes a work whole--has a gendered history. 15 To the extent that detailed ornamentation can obscure the central subject, or even take on an important life of its own, to decorate or ornament is intrinsically to challenge the notion of unified, mastering form. When Adolph Loos writes of the crime of ornament and captures the spirit of high modernism by articulating a coolly functional aesthetic of purity, he has repressed, but not erased, Victorian plenitude. 16

If the notions of fancy, concatenation, or multiplexity are to be useful, they must place us on paths that lead somewhere, to borrow an image from William James's Pragmatism. In one of the earliest treatises on aesthetics that we have, Alexander Baumgarten's "Reflections on Poetry" , we read that "The more that is gathered together in a confused representation, the more extensive clarity the representation has." 17 Baumgarten articulates a central finding of Victorian modernism: mixings, minglings, and confusions within the home as well as between homemaking and art-making, may yield clarity. Again, it is time to choose. Let us start down the path that links Dante Gabriel Rossetti's artistic practice with his actual domestic arrangements. Rossetti was a man who knew his lambrequins, and who--in poems, paintings, and rooms alike--assembled bric-à-brac.

Rossetti at 16 Cheyne Walk

As an artist, Dante Gabriel Rossetti moved among a confusion of categories that nevertheless, to borrow William James's phrase, hung together. First, he decorated the interiors of his homes, most notably that of 16 Cheyne Walk, Chelsea. Second, he depicted, intensely and repeatedly, domestic spaces and objects in both his poetry and his painting: bedrooms, halls, sitting rooms; nests, bowers, cages; windows, doors, thresholds; cups, washbasins, chairs, cabinets, screens, and mirrors. His "fine art" on its face represents a choice of spaces and things resolutely quotidian in nature, if frequently exoticized. Third, and in a sense encompassing these two domestic exercises, Rossetti passionately made "arrangements" with paint, words, and people. "Applied" art (interior decoration, crafts), "high" art (painting, poetry) and "household" art (practical affairs of home, hospitality)--these three arenas of aesthetic arrangement intermingled in Rossetti's creative life, fueled by tender sentiments as well as by pain and fear.

Rossetti lived at 16 Cheyne Walk from 1862 until his death in 1882. To understand his house, by many accounts strangely or fascinatingly decorated, and to appreciate its relation to his painting and poetry, we should begin with a few facts, consider a few legends, and then examine some of the best evidence we have of its appearance: the catalog of its contents printed for the auction held shortly after his death. 18

The London City Council tells us that the house, Georgian in style, was built in 1717, that it was called Queen's House because of its association with the flight of Queen Catherine Parr on the death of Henry VIII, and that it has especially well-preserved and beautifully wrought ironwork. 19 Rossetti's studio evidently occupied the dining room on the ground floor; a vast drawing room on the first floor runs along the entire front of the house, and the second floor is primarily given over to bedrooms. 20 Judging by exterior and interior Council photographs, the house is large and elegant, a gentleman's residence. In addition to these photographs, we have paintings by Treffry Dunn of the studio, dining room, drawing room, and--reflected by one of Rossetti's many convex mirrors--part of his bedroom. 21 Most of Rossetti's friends mention aspects of the house; but George Meredith, T. Hall Caine, Walter Theodore Watts-Dunton, William Michael Rossetti, and Dunn provide the fullest accounts. 22

Descriptions of 16 Cheyne Walk have tended to focus on the mysterious quality of its bedrooms, probably because the concept of the Gothic was most available as a descriptive device for the house's hodgepodge of strange furnishings. Watts-Dunton writes that the guest bedroom where he frequently slept

got the reputation of being haunted; for it was hung about with sombre hangings and antique picture panels. . . . Scattered about the room in most admired disorder were some outlandish and unheard-of books, and all kinds of antiquarian and Oriental oddities, which books and oddities Rossetti had picked up at various times during his ramblings about Chelsea and elsewhere--such as old carved heads and grinning gargoyles, and Burmese and Chinese Buddhas in soapstone of every degree of placid ugliness. . . . Rossetti's own bedroom was scarcely less weird in character. There again were to be seen heavy curtains drawn closely around an antiquated four-post bedstead. . . . 23

Caine writes a similar description; Dunn bewails the unhealthy darkness, airlessness, and gruesome pictures of the bedrooms. 24

But the rest of the house strikes Dunn as, above all, filled with interesting and beautiful objects, which he breathlessly catalogs:

The mantelpiece was a most original compound of Chinese black-laquered [sic] panels, bearing designs of birds, animals, flowers and fruit in gold relief, which had a very good effect, and on either side of the grate a series of old blue Dutch tiles, mostly displaying Biblical subjects treated in the serio-comic fashion that existed at the period, were inlaid. The fire-grate itself was a beautifully-wrought example of eighteenth century design and workmanship in brass . . . in one corner of the room stood an old English china cupboard, inside of which was displayed a quantity of Spode ware. I sat down on a cosy little sofa. . . . [RDR, 17-8]

Like Dunn as he awaits his first glimpse of the great man, others describe coziness rather than Gothic horror. William Rossetti perhaps sums up this position when he passes judgment on the house: "old-fashioned, many-roomed, homelike, and comfortable." 25 After looking at Dunn's paintings, a comparatively recent critic, Mario Praz (himself a house-obsessed man), complains of the eclecticism of Rossetti's house: "in the end there was no style at all. Non-style was created from an excess of styles." 26 When the texture of Victorian eclecticism becomes extreme, it shades into modernist minimalism.

The common observation among these varying assessments is of the striking number and variety of decorative things in Rossetti's house. These he collected (in excursions ranging from the elegance of Murray Marks's premises to the junk shops of the East End), received as gifts, ordered to specification, and sometimes made himself. To explore this pattern, we can consider primarily the auction catalog, with a bit of contextual help from Dunn's paintings. An impressionistic perspective is helpful here, not only because consideration of the material history of Rossetti's belongings is beyond the scope of this study, but also because there is no evidence to suggest that Rossetti considered himself an expert in anything but blue and white china. He bought what he liked, and the evidence suggests a happily roving rather than a professionally focused taste. Even allowing for the randomness of anyone's household belongings, the list of Rossetti's household effects indicates an aesthetic of incoherence, repetition, and complication: all qualities that have been remarked within autonomous modernist works--think of The Waste Land--even as we have insisted that they represented a break with the Victorian past.

Almost everything in the house is out of context, collected from other places, times, and cultures. One particularly intriguing page of the catalog lists Indian, Japanese, Albanian, Venetian, Chinese, and Irish objects. Perhaps the candidate for the most stunning example of the unassimilated "other" would be a foreign object that also announces itself as freakish: "A curiously carved mahogany frame chair with cane seat, formerly belonging to the Chinese Giant 'Chang'" (VCR, no. 104). At the same time, Dunn's paintings of Rossetti's house portray a mood of serenity and sumptuous ease. How to reconcile these two accounts?

Perhaps this sense of order issues from the very quality of confusion, or mingling, which these objects possess, in both their internal and external multiplexities, to borrow George Eliot's term. Rossetti's arrangements of these objects within the rooms of his house throw the sentimental context of "home" into high relief. We might say that feeling provides the energy that draws them into patterns. Rossetti liked, even adored, these things--his feelings about them provide the only aesthetic principle informing his acts of acquisition and arrangement. Many parts are here gathered, however loosely, into a whole. Certainly that is one tide of energy within 16 Cheyne Walk. But complication within individual objects can create confusing crosscurrents. The Elgin Marbles these are not. Consider "Pair of Chinese antique girandoles [the very descriptive terminology appears to fail], fitted with 4 shelves each, elaborately carved and heavily gilt" (VCR, no. 257). Or "A very handsome plated centre stand, inlaid with reflected glass, fitted at the ends with 3-light candelabra with serpentine arms, the centre supporting a cut glass flower dish" (VCR, no. 400). Rossetti liked this kind of complexity: such an item sits quietly, yet scintillates with multiple shapes and reflections.

And he liked repetition as well. Although classification can be a problem, a conservative count of items in the catalog reveals twenty-five cabinets, chests, and cupboards, and thirty-one glasses and mirrors. Such repetition--think of Stein's "otherwise there is a pleasure in adding / a doubling of their plan. / They will add adding to their tender care"--has, like incoherence within a house, two contesting qualities (W, 116). Mirrors everywhere indicate either a comforting similarity or an unnerving replication both of the mirrors themselves and the scenes they endlessly reflect. They can make us feel at home, eerily unhoused, or even entrapped. As for the cabinets, the issue is not just numbers--after all, this era antedates the built-in closet. Most of these cabinets, however, have interior shelves and drawers enclosed by highly decorated folding doors in various arrangements. Within cabinets are smaller enclosures, themselves holding discrete items, repetition within repetition.

Such proliferation makes of Rossetti's house a patchwork of borrowings that announce both their common ground (they rest with a sigh in the sentimental space of the Victorian middle-class home) and a nervous, modern sense of contingency. Furthermore, his house is in flux as items come and go, or are moved about: what Elizabeth Bishop, in a poem about fishhouses at the water's edge, would call knowledge itself, "flowing and drawn, and since our knowledge is historical, flowing, and flown." 27 Even the status of items within the house can never be clear. How heavily carved or exquisitely veneered must a chest be before it metamorphoses from storage bin to objet? Or perhaps at the very limits of artful impracticality the ordinary suddenly reappears: "A curious cabinet of Burmese glass workmanship, having grotesque res, with stand for same (suitable for a hall as a receptacle for carriage rugs)" (VCR, no. 217).

Such complexities will yield clarity once we learn their syntax. To do so, we must take seriously Rossetti's carefully arranged relations to other people, those relations interwining with the various uses of 16 Cheyne Walk: studio, gallery, literary salon, family home, stage set, and shelter from nature. Things, people, and works of art together form a rich confusion, "a collection, some parts of which are conjunctively and others disjunctively related."

Consider, for example, the hawthorn pot in Rossetti's painting Monna Rosa (1), a bit of the "blue and white" china that has become the emblem of aestheticism itself. 28 Our understanding of the painting might begin with a formalist appreciation of the pot's decorative texture in relation to res on the woman's robe and the background of peacock feathers and roses. We might then proceed to an appreciation of the pot as an incipiently Symbolist image of imagination and artisanry, and from there to a consideration of the woman herself as a beautiful, "vital object" and so on. But we should also attend to the aesthetic arrangements Rossetti made beyond the painting's frame, at the center of which the pot still beautifully rests. Rossetti's carefully cultivated and deeply affectionate relationship with the art dealer and collector Murray Marks, one of his principal suppliers of oriental ware; his own collector's obsession that led him to entertain other collectors at dinner and thus to people his solitude meaningfully; his feeling that an earlier and better culture of beauty and virtue inhabits the hawthorn pot, and his belief that such significant beauty can be placed both in his home and on his canvases (themselves to be placed in homes, not public galleries): all these and other arrangements must inform our appreciation of Monna Rosa precisely because Rossetti painted and lived them simultaneously, interchangeably.

Above all, the hawthorn pot reminds us that translation is one of Rossetti's defining practices, as it will be for Ezra Pound. The "bringing home" or domesticating of foreign speech is central to Rossetti's intense practice of domestic aestheticism. He translated not only Italian poetry into English poetry, but also real Chinese pots (already translated from the Chinese when Rossetti named them "hawthorn") into items of English domesticity--part of the ensemble of his sitting room--and then into painted pots, themselves carefully placed in painted bowers and rooms.

Just as a vase could be bought and sold in the marketplace, used to develop relations with other people, treasured for its living qualities, transformed into a painted vase, and placed, beautifully, on a real shelf in a real house as part of ongoing domestic arrangements, so people, too, could be patterned into art at the same time as they worked in his home, itself an artistic studio and work of art. For Rossetti, objects were not, as Coleridge programmatically states, dead. Rossetti understood the "vital" as a free-floating energy that can infuse (and ebb from) people, objects, rooms, paintings, and poems alike, as he arranged them in complex patterns.

Rossetti conducted daily household affairs because he had to, and since he worked at home, his domestic concerns and his artistic practice blended. Like many a middle-class and upper-class Victorian woman, he felt harassed by the "servant problem." But for him the issue had a twist: he continually felt the lack of models, and sought them either on the street (the "stunners" were both sexual and artistic prey) or among the serving class. "What I really want," he writes to Mrs. Alexander Gilchrist, who has been advising him on the servant issue, "is a very handy man or youth, capable of turning his hand to odd jobs of carpentry, etc. . . . another desirable quality would be that he should be fit for a model" (LDR, 483). Just as such a handyman must be fit to enter the studio, so the women hired as models must be fit for the workroom; he requests, for example, that Alexa Wilding mend the fabric and then remake the dress to be worn as she sits to him. Fanny Cornforth acts not only as model, but also as housekeeper, for several years. At Kelmscott Manor a local serving girl becomes the model for Marigolds. A curious circularity of domestic and artistic activity develops. As completed paintings are sold and money flows in, it must be disbursed to cover household and studio expenses. Such expenses make it possible for the next painting to be completed, but also bring on the next round of bills for canvas, paint, artists' dinners, and models'/servants' wages.

Over the course of the years, Rossetti engaged three men to ease his dual domestic and aesthetic burdens: W. J. Knewstub, Dunn and, toward the end, Caine. Rossetti required Dunn, in particular, to find his way among the complexities of a household that was part nest, part artistic factory (with himself the principal pieceworker) and part artistic salon. Dunn's memoirs indicate his initial enthusiasm for the job of artistic factotum, because he feels at last that he is entering the world of London art. He reminisces about a particularly vivid scene of domestic sublimity that takes place in Rossetti's studio as a thunderstorm crescendos, and with it, Swinburne's verse declamation:

Faint electric sparks played round the wavy masses of his luxuriant hair. . . . I lay in the sofa in a corner of the studio and listened in wonder . . . for it appeared to me as though the very res in the pictures that were on the easels standing about the room were conscious of and sympathised with the poet. . . . The 'Proserpine' gazed out more mournfully than I had been wont to see her gaze. [RDR, 64]

Dunn understands that the vibrations of weather (brought indoors), fellowship (Rossetti returns soon after, and he and Swinburne sit up until dawn together), and works of art (conscient) lead, one to the other, at 16 Cheyne Walk. He not only fabricates frames, scale models, and drawings to Rossetti's specifications, but also shops for artistic props, advises Rossetti on appropriate household budgets, and searches the house for misplaced items during his employer's absences. He absorbs visits from angry merchants, kills rats, mails information, books, and furniture, and even goes house-hunting for Rossetti, making sketches of promising properties. But all of his activities require of him artistic talent, aesthetic judgment, and sympathy with his artist-employer's special needs. Spaces must be filled with appropriate light, objects must be located or bid upon, projects must be finished so that "tin" for Dunn's, at best, intermittent salary might be forthcoming.

Although the term exploitation comes to mind, it is important to remember that Rossetti himself never drew a sharp line between work on paintings and domestic concerns. William finds in his brother's writing book the following contiguous scraps:

5. From John Marshall. Eat meat, poultry, game, fish, oysters, kidneys, green vegetables, stewed fruit, ripe fruit. . . . Avoid or reduce much bread, potatoes, sugar, beer, spirits, cocoa, chocolate, olive oil, eggs, bacon.

6. For plain scarlet: try laying ground with venetian or Indian red, and white, to the full depth of tone, and glazing with orange-vermilion. 29

The slimming man and the accomplished artist are one. Domestic upsets easily became artistic incidents--such as Fanny's alleged breakage (or hiding) of a blue and white jar he needs for a painting. A misplaced medieval costume means a slowdown in work.

Household matters, however, may also have unexpected value for artistic work. When Rossetti receives a sofa ordered through Howell, he discovers it "covered with the most lovely chintz of a Japanese pattern admirably designed and coloured like greyish blue china" (LDR, 1205). To pay for actual blue and white china--cherished as decoration for both paintings and living quarters--he paints a picture, and makes it worth £200 "to cover a previous account" (RP, 62, 70). It is perhaps only part of the story to say that Rossetti knew at heart that he had "sold out" as an artist by engaging in the impure world of the marketplace. If he sold out, he also, as homemaker, bought in. Pots, paintings, sofas, pounds sterling, models, dresses, create a complicated pattern. To keep house, however frantically, is to manage affairs in the name of beauty--the hallmark of modernist aestheticism. And to write poetry is to make aesthetic arrangements that include, as part of their very composition, personal and domestic arrangements.

Consider, for example, the "lustral rites" in the introductory sonnet to The House of Life:

A Sonnet is a moment's monument,--
     Memorial from the Soul's eternity
     To one dead deathless hour. Look that it be,
Whether for lustral rite or dire portent,
Of its own arduous fulness reverent:
       Carve it in ivory or in ebony,
       As Day or Night may rule; and let Time see
Its flowering crest impearled and orient.
A Sonnet is a coin: its face reveals
     The soul,--its converse, to what Power 'tis due:--
Whether for tribute to the august appeals
       Of Life, or dower in Love's high retinue,
It serve; or, 'mid the dark wharf's cavernous breath,
       In Charon's palm it pay the toll to Death. 30

"Look that it be, / Whether for lustral rite or dire portent, / Of its own arduous fulness reverent": Rossetti includes as the aims of poetry the creation of an autonomous object and the care of art's readers by purification, illumination, and warning--and all this by placing them in the presence of special, even magical objects. As Rossetti invited poets into his company, he wrote sonnets in order to bring his poetry into a community of readers. The view of art as "lustral rite" bespeaks a cyclical process (the lustrum was originally conducted every five years by the censors at Rome), meant to purify a people, a piece of land, or even the community's home, the city. A ritual cleansing, it conducts us to the realm of the spiritualized domestic. Sonnets themselves become the objects used in lustral rites or as dire portents; they must be carved, decorated things. In fact, the sonnet as Rossetti conceives it is another "medley of bric-à-brac" like that of the paintings. 31

"The Sonnet" is, to summarize, arduously full, carved in ivory or ebony with a flowered and impearled crest, and also rounded, flattish, and engraved differently on each side. Such a medley, whether it appear in a sonnet or a painting, might then be recognized for what it is: the modern jumble of life sanctified, arranged by the artist-priest-householder (or, later, by Stevens's "Man on the Dump") into an intimacy of related aspects rather than an organically conceived unity. Details and ornaments cannot be clearly distinguished from the things themselves (who can tell the engraving from the coin?), and we feel ourselves moving toward an abstraction of texture that signals the presence of the modern. As David G. Riede acutely notes, "the paradox is that [Rossetti] consistently saw surfaces as the most profound symbols of emotional and psychological depths.32

Furthermore, when it came to composing poetry--arranging words, images, and sounds artfully--Rossetti retained habits he had formed as a householder. His writing often involves the actual staging of complex and artful arrangements of people he knows as he links his verbal "compositions" to acts of generosity performed or received that make it possible to paint or write. Simply stated, for Rossetti, the personal favor becomes an element of creation that works in tandem with inspiration--his insistence on "fundamental brainwork"--and execution. His dealings with people can be considered as so many scripted dramas, first acted, then captured forever in beautiful objects: poems and paintings which themselves insist on their source in a personal, contingent, and often sentimental milieux.

Late in life, for example, Rossetti wrote a series of sonnets entitled "Five English Poets" (CW, 337-9). The sonnet on Blake exemplifies a confused mirroring of sentimental word and tender action.

William Blake
(To Frederick Shields, on his sketch of Blake's work-room and death-room, 3 Fountain Court, Strand.)
This is the place. Even here the dauntless soul,
    The unflinching hand, wrought on; till in that nook,
    As on that very bed, his life partook
New birth, and passed. Yon river's dusky shoal,
Whereto the close-built coiling lanes unroll,
    Faced his work-window, whence his eyes would stare,
    Thought-wandering, unto nought that met them there,
But to the unfettered irreversible goal.
This cupboard, Holy of Holies, held the cloud
    Of his soul writ and limned; this other one,
His true wife's charge, full oft to their abode
    Yielded for daily bread the martyr's stone,
    Ere yet their food might be that Bread alone,
The words now home-speech of the mouth of God. [CW, 338]

Here is a confusing field of reflection. This is a poem about an artist by an artist. It describes the domestic life of Blake--bed, nook, cupboard, bread--as intimately linked to his "thought-wandering" and to the speaker's vision of heaven. Artistic creation at home is artistic creation as home for both Rossetti and his imagined Blake, the sentimental home-speech that is both human and divine.

Less obvious than these echoes and links, however, are the artful arrangements of Rossetti's life that made this sonnet possible in the first place. As one artist writing about another, Rossetti does not address Blake's poetry or pictures directly, nor does he himself visit Blake's house at 3 Fountain Court. Instead he turns to a "picture by another artist of a room once inhabited by Blake." 33 Such a choice indicates Rossetti's love of arrangements as part of his fascination with domestic scenes. That Frederick Shields probably made the sketch after Rossetti suggested he do so; that Shields sent him the sketch and the floor plan of the rooms; that Rossetti could then reciprocate with the gift of a sonnet based on Shields's gift; that both Shields and Rossetti, along with William Michael Rossetti, helped the widowed Mrs. Alexander Gilchrist complete her husband's Life of William Blake: all of these circumstances speak of Rossetti's continual efforts to create bonds of mutual attention that would literally make art out of human arrangements as well as words. Taking into consideration all these aspects of the poem, "inlaid with reflected glass" as it were, we feel the very texture of Victorian modernism: complexity so fine-grained that it verges on abstraction. And we feel it, too, within Stevens's sentimental elegy for George Santayana, so similar to Rossetti's elegy for Blake: "The bed, the books, the chair, the moving nuns / The candle as it evades the sight" equally acknowledge the vision of "The extreme of the known in the presence of the extreme / Of the unknown"(CP, 508).

"How to Live. What to Do."

Domestic arrangements are not the centralizing force of Victorian modernism; they are but one of its energies. It happens that for Dante Gabriel Rossetti, such arrangements provide a relational energy that offers him the experience of--and the words and images for--a pragmatist and empiricist building out of reality from the flux. When William James tries to explain the finding and making of pragmatist truth by the following out of connections, he writes, "If I am lost in the woods and starved, and find what looks like a cow-path, it is of the utmost importance that I should think of a human habitation at the end of it, for if I do so and follow it, I save myself." 34 To understand that Victorian "coziness" provides both an actual laboratory and a philosophical metaphor for truth in the making that pervades art in the twentieth century is to see both "the modern" and "the Victorian" in a usefully mingled way. The aesthetic and the domestic pragmatically entwine to create an important Victorian modernist pattern.

Rossetti does not, of course, found Victorian modernism. Rather, he provides for the moment a contingent center from which we might set out in exploration, and he leads us to reexamine some truths which might have slipped into truisms. We should question, for example, the modern conception of the actual Victorian home--as opposed to its Gothic metamorphoses--as a place of Philistine prejudices, airless and overstuffed. 35 That the Victorian parlor, well before the turn of the century, might have been a testing ground for modern culture, that it might have provided both the space of reaction and the space of rebellion, is a possibility we must consider. Within this familiar context, Rossetti envisioned the very aesthetic values that would be taken up by decadent artists in England and the creators of art nouveau in France and enter the mainstream of what we regard as high modernism. 36

The highly particularized, crowded, and flattened surfaces of Rossetti's paintings, like the abstraction-strewn, sentimental surfaces of his poetry and the strange decorations of his drawing room, are protomodernist, or what we should more accurately characterize as Victorian modernist. Modern artists introduce "ornamental" patterns into paintings and literary works, such as the decorative borders and actual textile patterns of Matisse, the patterned furniture of Picasso's portraits, the incantatory repetitions or iconic images of modernist verse, or the odd collection of objects in surrealist texts. These works of art represent not a rupture from the immediate Victorian past, but its flowering. Victorian sensibilities live a renewed life in modernist art. Because Proust simultaneously describes in detail the rooms his characters inhabit, depicts remembered rooms, furnishes the rooms of his capacious sentences, and memorializes the ornate Gothic buildings beloved of John Ruskin, he invites us to set out in search of his Victorian modernism. As we do so, we may link it, perhaps, to Ruskin and through him to the domestic practices of best-selling American women writers of the nineteenth century. Harriet Beecher Stowe, Augusta Evans, Elizabeth Stoddard, and many others, acolytes of Ruskin all, take their place in an emerging filigree of Victorian modernism.

Many are the directions we might take, the writers and works we might bring into relation, as we explore a literary terrain that is richer for its detailed, complex, and confused minglings than it ever was for its clean breaks and firm categories. Overlooked or deliberately ignored aspects of high modernism will catch the light--for example, its sentimentality. Old concepts will take on new shapes: perhaps the sublime is a phenomenon of Willa Cather's communities on the plains, as much as it is the effacement of social relations and even human identity before fearsome mountain peaks or ocean furies. Aesthetics might even pragmatically link hands with ethics, as we come to see that Stevens's questions of "How to Live," the adverbial and adjectival styles we adopt, can make their way toward questions of "What to Do," the actions that make us human (CP, 125).
