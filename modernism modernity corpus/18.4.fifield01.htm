Introduction to Samuel Beckett,“The New Object”1

Peter Fifield

“The New Object” was originally published in the catalogue of the Samuel M. Kootz Gallery in New York, in 1948. The occasion for the catalogue’s issue was an exhibition, Introducing Two Modern French Painters: Geer Van Velde, Bram Van Velde, held from 8 to 27 March, and the essay has been unavailable to a wide audience since that time. It constitutes a translation of roughly three quarters of the French essay “Peintres de l’empêchement,” omitting the first long paragraph, and has numerous interesting differences. The English text was published three months before the French, which was included in the June 1948 issue of Derrière le miroir, the journal of the Galerie Maeght in Paris. It was Beckett’s second essay on the work of either of the Van Velde brothers, following “La peinture des van Veldes, ou le Monde et le Pantalon” (1946), and preceding the Three Dialogues with Georges Duthuit (1949). It anticipates the later work by identifying a shift in the artist’s task from expression to the statement of expression’s impossibility, asking, “What remains to be represented if the essence of object is to elude representation? There remain to be represented the conditions of that elusion.”

There has been some disagreement over the translator of the English text. In their 1970 bibliography, Federman and Fletcher state that the text is “translated into English by an anonymous translator (not the author),” while Ackerley and Gontarski suggest that Beckett is responsible.2 Ruby Cohn remains uncommitted with “Possibly by Beckett.”3 None of them present any textual evidence for their claims. The Kootz catalogue mentions no translator, and so poses as a work without a foreign language precursor, needing only the concluding “Samuel Beckett. Paris.

The surviving typescript of the French essay, held at Dartmouth College, shows the change from “Le Nouvel Object” to “Peintres de l’empêchement,” revealing that the title used by the English text is the earlier. The typescript is dated to March 1947, a full year before “The New Object” was published, and fifteen months before the French text emerged. Although there are roughly twenty small alterations and corrections, there are no substantial changes between typescript and final French essay, suggesting the completed text waited over a year for publication. Similarly, comparison of the “Peintres” typescript and “The New Object” shows that the English translation was probably based on the text that stood before the author’s corrections, which include the change of title. The typescript contains Beckett’s change from “recherche” to “deuil”; a change retained for the published French text, which reads: “Une assurance, une double assurance, car le même deuil les mène loin l’un de l’autre, de deuil de l’objet.”4 “The New Object,” however, carries the earlier sense and offers “An assurance, a double assurance. For they go very different ways, on the same search, the search for an object.” This change shifts the meaning from the search for a missing object (English) to its mourning (French), and so to the impossibility of its recovery, very much “turning from [the field of the possible] in disgust” (D, 139) rather than resisting or denying that impossibility. Other curiosities ought to be noted, however. The odd translation of “deux attitudes liées l’une à l’autre, comme le repos à l’effort” (D, 135) as “two attitudes are bound together, turn in turn fatigue and rest” introduces an uncomfortable foreign note, where “turn and turn about” would be the more usual phrase. Indeed, this is the expression used repeatedly in the sucking stones episode of Molloy, where it appears to be something of a favorite for the narrator.5 But the strangeness of the catalogue text is compounded by the fact that the phrase is a not-quite idiomatic non-translation of a non-idiom. Indeed, for the English language audience, this determined oddity may have hinted at a French original, despite its clumsiness being easily avoidable. The French is closer to “two attitudes bound to one another, as rest is to effort,” the English “turn in turn” giving a sense of repeated interchange rather than sustained interdependence. In place of reference to “l’Ecole de Paris (cf. l’heure de Greenwich)” (D, 135) is “what goes by the name of the Paris School,” reproducing the distrust of the official grouping, but omitting the specific reference to the similarly empty orthodoxy of Greenwich Mean Time. The inclusion of the distinctly English reference in the French text—or rather its omission from the English translation—is likely due to Beckett’s contemporaneous translation of Murphy into French for Bordas. That text reached its achevé d’imprimer on 15 April 1947, a month after the “Peintres” typescript, suggesting that by this time, the Greenwich reference had Gallic currency in Beckett’s mind. Murphy refers to a certain “bewitching Miss Greenwich,” who represents a temporal order that must receive restitution from “Time that old fornicator, bald though he be behind.”6 One alteration appears to have been made specifically for its setting in the catalogue. Kandinsky, Rouault”—referred to wearily as “all the usual inevitables”—is “—what’s 875 the man’s name?—Picasso.” In a period of heavy promotion for American art among New York galleries, the Kootz Gallery presented a more balanced artistic scene, promoting American and European painters equally. Its most important exhibition was held in 1947, when it hosted the first post-Second World War display of Picasso’s work in the United States. Such was the success of this show, and the personal rapport between Samuel Kootz and Picasso, that the gallery owner became the artist’s exclusive American representative and dealer for a time. Indeed, at the artist’s suggestion Kootz temporarily closed the gallery so as to concentrate on selling Picasso’s work, a closure beginning in 1948, closely following the Van Velde display.7 As such, Picasso was the essential reference point for Kootz and the gallery’s patrons, and Beckett’s wry “what’s the man’s name?” may be read as a gentle tease at the importance that the artist had assumed for the gallery and its owner.

Beckett’s appraisal of Van Veldes’ paintings is all melodrama: “An endless unveiling, veil behind veil, plane after plane of imperfect transparencies, light and space themselves veils, an unveiling towards the unveilable, the nothing, the thing again. And burial in the unique, in a place of impenetrable nearness, cell painted on the stone of cell, art of confinement.” As such, the text echoes and amplifies the 1937 German letter to Axel Kaun, with its “veil which one has to tear apart to get to those things (or the nothingness) lying behind it.”8 This section of the essay, moreover, was repeatedly included in gallery publications of the period, suggesting that such rhetoric was striking if not exactly subtle.9 Interestingly, Beckett prepared a later English translation of this fragment, which can be found in the “Été 56 Notebook,” held at the University of Reading. If he was re-translating the excerpt for a particular volume in 1958, and I suggest the most probable intention for the piece is a catalogue for New York’s Knoedler Gallery, it did not reach the final publication. It also suggests that the Kootz translation was not to hand at the time, as there are some minor differences in word choice between the texts, although the striking neologism “unveilable” remains common to both. This later translation is something of a curiosity, neither confirming nor disproving Beckett’s translation of the Kootz text, but complicating the story of his relationship to an essay that he returned to a decade after composition, despite reservations.

Much of the authorial disinclination of “Peintres” is lost with the omission of the long first paragraph. The French begins with a statement of inability-cum-reluctance and moves on from there:

J’ai dit tout ce que j’avais à dire sur la peinture des frères van Velde dans le dernier numéro des Cahiers d’Art . . . Je n’ai rien à ajouter à ce que j’ai dit à cet endroit. C’était peu, c’était trop, et je n’ai rien à y ajouter.10 (D, 133)

But these feelings remain visible, albeit less clearly so, in the Kootz text, where the rhetoric is already aware of its own excess. The rhapsodic talk of veils and unveiling is clearly self-parodic, as if admitting that, as Beckett said of the German letter in 1983, it is all “bilge” (D, 170). Following the statement in “Peintres” comes self-reproof and

A moins d’être un critique d’art” (D, 137).11 This is absent from the English text, replaced by the reluctant sentiment in a milder miniature. Thus Beckett interposes the single-line paragraph “Ici on chante” before starting upon “The history of painting.” The obligation to trot out his pensum has a bitter humor to it, its overtones sounding somewhere between “altogether now,” and “here we go again.” The very use of French here seems calculated to create distance between author and reader, and suggests a bitter in-joke, of which the author is both teller and butt. Equally, his authority and enthusiasm is put in doubt by another addition to the English text where, following the distinction between Bram and Geer, Beckett hesitates with “In my precarious opinion,” implying a shaky edifice all too likely to fall down. Taken together, these differences suggest Beckett’s hand behind a translation that, for all of the author’s importance in Anglophone literature, has dropped from view for over sixty years. Indeed, his importance to comparative literature and translation studies makes this essay’s rarity more surprising still. Dismissing as it does “all the usual inevitables” of the modern artistic canon, its own status as a rarely cited text might now appear as a surprising choice for celebration. More than a mere oddity, however, its formulation of the artist’s predicament, combined with a wit and reluctance that are proper to the strongest period of Beckett’s writing, should ensure renewed attention.
