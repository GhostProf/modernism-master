Zora Neale Hurston and the Federal Folk

David Kadlec

[res]

When Zora Neale Hurston published her now-celebrated novel, Their Eyes Were Watching God, in 1937, a handful of critics objected to its feathery characterization of rural southern African Americans. Richard Wright was among those who urged Hurston to attempt a more "serious" and politically responsible portrayal of black life in America; and Alain Locke advised her to tone up her "surface[y]" "folklore" narratives with hard-hitting "social document" techniques. 1 It would be a stretch to suggest that Hurston turned to the Federal Writers' Project in 1938 in an effort to answer Wright's and Locke's appeals to social realism and the gritty substance of the modern document; the production of a federally subsidized tour guide can hardly have been the sort of social project that her critics had in mind. Still, it was in her position as an editor of the American Guide Series book, Florida , that Hurston first came to try her hand at documentary writing. Her contributions to the Florida guide are scant, but they are characteristically complex. The "Negro spirituals and secular tunes" that she recorded and transcribed for Florida may not be as "authentic" as they seem; and their uncertain status as documented "folk" materials suggests that, despite the progressive efforts of many who participated in the Federal Writer's Project, Hurston's conceptions of "culture" clashed with the varieties of culture fostered by the governmental documentary projects of the late 1930s.

The state and regional guides produced by the Federal Writers' Project did more than feed and clothe writers during the Depression. They also promoted new varieties of national pride during a decade rife with radical political sentiment. As the art historian and theorist John Tagg has noted, the liberal Roosevelt administration compiled its massive factual and photographic archives in an effort "to retrieve the status of Truth in [liberal governmental] discourse." This "Truth" status had been "threatened by crisis," and its "renegotiation was essential if . . . national and social identities [were to be] resecured" in a manner that served both corporatist and state interests. 2 As part of a widely praised effort to provide a documentary "roadmap for the cultural rediscovery of America from within," the 378 books and pamphlets of the American Guide Series provided more than a compilation of the "raw cultural material" of myth and fact. 3 They also functioned as the popular literary vanguard of a project that shaped radical dissent toward the imperatives of liberal reform.

The publications of the American Guide Series served both informational and commercial purposes. Richard Wright's "sociological" contributions to the Illinois project, for example, laid a groundwork for the stories that he later published in Uncle Tom's Children. The gap between the informational and commercial directives of the Federal Writers' Project can be measured in the interval between the Guide Series' Florida and Alabama projects. Hurston had traveled extensively through these contiguous states when she gathered data for her studies of rural southern African American culture in the late 1920s and early 1930s, and the divergent Alabama and Florida guides therefore offer an apt index of the imperatives that bore down upon the native Floridian who later undertook government-sponsored documentary work. Although Hurston did not participate directly in the making of the 1941 Alabama guide, this book does list Hurston's Mules and Men among its bibliographical sources, and it contains a short but notable account of Cudjo Lewis, the recently deceased African slave from the Mobile area whom Hurston had interviewed for her as yet unpublished book-length study, "Barracoon." 4

Of the two southern state guides, the Alabama guide is renowned for its unsentimentalized portrayal of the Deep South's violent settlement. The Roosevelt scholar William Leuchtenburg, for example, singles out the Massachusetts and Alabama state guides for their exemplary historical analysis and writing. "Though the [Guide Series] writers labored under some constraints," Leuchtenburg claims, "their comments could be unsparing." Offering an example of such sharp commentary, he praises the Alabama guide's account of the disappearance of the state's "indigenous" peoples:

In remarking on a site where the Colonial Dames of America had placed a marker on the triumph of Civilization over Savagery, the authors of the Alabama guide observed: "Here, in 1802, was negotiated one of the series of treaties by which the United States absorbed the Choctaw lands at a price of one broken promise per square mile." 5

Not surprisingly, the guide's authors are more muted on the subject of the ongoing strife between whites and blacks in Alabama. To their credit, however, they do acknowledge that mutual racial "antagonism" had been a feature of Alabama's history; and they discuss the rise of racist groups including the Ku Klux Klan during the state's "Reconstruction" years. 6

The Florida guide, by contrast, is little remembered for its documentation of southern history. In this guide, Florida's history and culture are introduced and discussed as a kind of currency for the contemporary tourist trade. The southernmost state is a place not of anguished monuments but of simulacra: its "palm-thatched" "Indian villages" and "tribal flags" have been "hoisted" quickly in an effort to lure passing motorists. In Florida, history is written first on profligate "highway signboards." According to the guide, the doubtful "Seminole names" that adorn these signboards "are more numerous and widespread . . . than are the living members of the race. . . . The Indians themselves have made the most of one profitable name. Since they discovered that the story of Osceola is popular among tourists, that fiery war chief has acquired many descendants, and most of the present-day Osceolas display their names along the Tamiami Trail." 7 With its frank discussion of the state's self-fashioning as a tourist haven, and with a large proportion of its pages outlining tours for the modern sightseer, the Florida guide plainly functions as a subsidized lure to visitors.

The difference between the informational Alabama book and the more openly commercial Florida guide is apparent not only in the contrast between their respective treatments of regional history but also in the guides' photographic illustrations. The Alabama guide features stark photos of the state's power plants, coal miners, and white and black sharecropper families; and these pictures are attributed to the then-celebrated "social" photographers--Dorothea Lange, Marion Post, Arthur Rothstein--who took them for the Farm Security Administration in the mid- to late-1930s (. 1 and 2). The neighboring Florida guide, on the other hand, is sprinkled with mostly unattributed portraits of gardens and golf courses, well-posed Seminoles, and turtle fishermen who decorate idyllic beaches ( 3). Even the federally sponsored FSA photos that appear in the guide serve to distance Florida from nationwide political and economic concerns. Attributed to sponsoring agencies rather than to the witnesses who breathe behind governmental lenses, the Florida photos depict well-groomed laborers who seem unburdened by economic hardship and environmental corrosion (. 4 and 5). Florida may well have been a prettier place than Alabama, but in the Florida guide, the call for popular support for initiatives such as rural electrification and farm subsidies gives way to the siren call of tourism. 8

In Florida, not just history and industry but also the arts revolve around tourism. Apart from Hurston, the best-known contemporary writer to have contributed to the guide was Wallace Stevens, whose praise of lush, dewy Florida appears in a section on "Florida Literature." Stevens labored as an insurance executive in Hartford, Connecticut, but here he qualifies as a Florida writer because, like many other "poets" who have been "aroused" by the southernmost state, he was "a regular visitor" (FL, 147). In a tribute to winter getaways, the Florida guide reprinted the following lines from Stevens's "Nomad Exquisite":

As the immense dew of Florida
Brings forth
The big-finned palm
And green vine angering for life, [Begin Page 476]
As the immense dew of Florida
Brings forth hymn and hymn
From the beholder
Beholding all these green sides
And gold sides of green sides,
And blessed mornings,
Meet for the eye of the young alligator,
And lightning colors,
So, in me, come flinging
Forms, flames, and the flakes of flames.

(FL, 147)

The tourist Stevens, we are told in the book's "Preface," graces the pages of the Florida guide courtesy of Alfred A. Knopf, Inc., publisher of Harmonium . No such fanfare accompanies the appearance of Hurston, whose influence as a folklorist can be felt in the series of Florida work songs that appear three pages later. Hurston's unattributed contributions to the Florida guide appear in a chapter on the "Music and Theater" of Florida, and they consist of a series of "Negro spirituals and secular tunes" (FL, 149). The first six of the seven fragments of spirituals and secular songs included in this chapter are work songs. These range from a rowing song attributed to Fernandina slaves to chain gang songs that "illustrate the Negro's relief in rhythm":

Little corn, UGH!
Yellow gal, UGH!
Little t, UGH!
Lotta time, UGH!

(FL, 150)

There is a tightly compacted narrative to many of these song fragments, but the guide's "Music and Theater" commentator is more concerned to discuss their social function and form. In apparent conformity to the directives that Sterling Brown issued as the Editor of Negro Affairs for the Federal Writer's Project, these work songs are transcribed in a manner that highlights not their phonetic divergence from "standard," or Anglo-American, forms of "English" but rather their distinctive idiomatic and musical properties. 9 Freed from the crippling imperatives of minstrelsy, these slight fragments begin to challenge traditional distinctions between "low" forms of folk expression and "higher" literary forms. The repetition of single lines and verses, for example, can convey nuanced expressive differences. In many of the work songs of the Florida Negro, the guide informs us, "contrast and embellishment are obtained by stress and accent in the tune, rather than by a change of words" (FL, 151). A song that is made up of a single phrase, with different words italicized in each of its three repetitions, illustrates such expressive embellishment. The last of the seven song fragments reads as follows:

Love ain't nothing but the EASY-GOING heart disease,
OH LOVE ain't nothing but the easy-going heart DISEASE;
LOVE ain't NOTHING but the EASY-GOING HEART DISEASE.

(FL, 151)

This excerpt is really more of a blues than a work song, and it is noteworthy not only because it exemplifies Brown's progressive call for "unmeasured" transcriptions of African-American oral material, but also because it helps us to identify Hurston as the author of the "Music and Theater" section of the guide. Moreover, there is a story behind this fragment--one that tells us something about Hurston's apparent frustration with the Federal Writer's Project and with the larger aesthetic and commercial directives that fueled the production of state guides like Florida's. (Even in their most progressive phases under relatively enlightened administrators like Sterling Brown, and also Benjamin Botkin who became the head of the FWP's Folklore division in 1938, the American Guide Series books would ultimately remain the instruments of the liberal government that produced them.) 10 As it happens, the genesis of the heart-disease lyric that caps the Negro "Music" of Florida is as questionable as the ancestry of the state's "signboard" Osceolas. This blues song may not be from Florida, and it may not even be a Negro folk song in the way that the guide presents it. By tracing the heart-disease lyric to potential urban sources that raise doubt regarding its "authenticity," however, we can use this so-called Florida folk song to understand the conceptions of racial and cultural identity that vexed and enriched Hurston's work as both a cultural anthropologist and a state documentarian.

Hurston's discomfort with her roles as both an anthropologist and a documentarian should surprise no one who is familiar with either modern anthropology or with state-sponsored documentary initiatives of the late 1930s. In America, at least, both efforts were fundamentally liberal, which is to say that they worked toward the refinement of a fundamentally racist status quo. To call the anthropologist Franz Boas, who was Hurston's teacher, a "racist" is probably something of a cheap shot. Few public intellectuals contributed as much to the repeal of blatantly racist scientific and legislative initiatives as did Boas. In the 1920s and 1930s, however, his progressive conceptions of "culture" were institutionalized in ways that Hurston quietly resisted. Boas himself used the relative "difference" of the Negro's physiognomy as a tool to combat the scientific racialist belief that Europeans could be divided into stratified races or "sub-species"; and this may have contributed to Hurston's privately expressed belief that "white people could not be trusted to collect the lore of others." 11 Despite her lavish tributes to "Papa Franz" in Mules and Men and then later in her autobiography, Hurston remained uncomfortable working under Boas's sponsorship, and she often endeavored to conceal her fieldwork in the rural South from the hands of meddlesome New York anthropologists affiliated with Boas. When she was interviewing Cudjo Lewis, the elderly Alabama farmer who claimed to be America's last surviving African slave, for example, Hurston conspired with Alain Locke and Charlotte Mason to keep the Boasian anthropologist Paul Radin from making contact with Lewis. 12 Later, in 1940, she complained to the anthropologist Jane Belo of the burden that Boas's technical crew had placed on her Sanctified Church project in Beaufort, South Carolina. "Now I went to work right away," Hurston wrote, "to lay the foundation for the white men to come in to take the sound pictures. I think we had better make them [the Church members] a present because they are not taking to it any too kindly." 13

If Hurston was less than enthusiastic as a participant in the project of cultural anthropology, however, she was by all accounts a thoroughly noncooperative member of the Federal Writers' Project. As one of the editors of the Florida state guide, Hurston is said to have spent most of her time working on her novel, Moses, Man of the Mountain, and doing fieldwork for a second FWP project, The Florida Negro. 14 Modeled on The Negro in Virginia, a much-praised federal project headed by Roscoe Lewis and edited by Sterling Brown, The Florida Negro never quite coalesced as a historical or ethnographic document. Its rambling and sometimes bitterly polemical 200 manuscript pages remained unpublished until 1993, when they were recovered amid the late-century revival of interest in the literary Hurston. Importantly, however, several of the songs that made it into the Florida state guide also appear in The Florida Negro. Similarities in their formatting and in the commentary that surrounds them enable us to identify Hurston as the author of the "Music and Theater" section of the Florida guide. In The Florida Negro, for example, Hurston's distinctive voice rings clear when she discusses accent as an integral feature of "Negro blues songs."

The Negro blues songs, of which Florida has many fine examples, belong in the lyric class; that is, feelings set to strings. The oldest and most typical form of Negro blues is a line stating the mood of the singer repeated three times. The stress and variation is carried by the tune and the whole thing walks with rhythm.

Look at the "East Coast Blues" and see how:

Love ain't nothing but the easy-going heart disease;
Love ain't nothing but the easy-going heart disease;
Oh, love ain't nothing but the easy-going heart disease. 15

Here, not only the blues lyric but also the commentary that surrounds it links Hurston to the closing portions of the "Music and Theater" section of the state guide. Discussing an earlier and yet unpublished draft of the "Folklore and Music" section of The Florida Negro, one entitled, simply, "Folkore," Lynda Marion Hill quotes a portion that strongly resembles the discussion of work songs in the Florida guide:

The Negro blues songs belong in the class of moods set to strings which the ancients called lyric because they were sung to the lyre. The form is a line indicating the mood of the singer repeated three times. It betrays the mood of the singer and it walks with rhythm. Contrast and embellishment is [sic] obtained by stress and accent in the tune rather than a change of words. 16

The final sentence of this excerpt makes it clear that Hurston's contributions to the 1939 Florida guide were written in concert with, and perhaps even composed out of, early versions of her "second" FWP project, The Florida Negro.

For the "Folkore and Music" section of The Florida Negro and for the more hastily composed "Music and Theater" section of the Florida guide, Hurston collected, transcribed, and analyzed folk material as a federal documentarian. As a member of the FWP, Hurston had in fact procured a tape recorder from the government during a trip to Washington, and she began carrying it with her as she collected folk stories and work songs in Florida in the late 1930s. Hurston had traveled with recording equipment before, but when she worked as an anthropologist in the late 1920s and early 1930s, she had been inclined to gather and record her data from memory. She described her earlier song-collecting methods to the folklorist Alan Lomax in 1935:

I just get in the crowd with the people when they sing it, I listen as best I can, then I start to join in with a phrase or two and then finally I get so that I can sing a verse. Then I keep on till I learn all of the songs with verses then I sing 'em back to the people till they tell me I can sing them just like them and then I take part and try it out on different people who already know the song until they are quite satisfied that I know it, and then I carry it in my memory. 17

Now, however, with her 1939 government-issue recording device in tow, she would appear to have been participating in the more empirical documentary methods that were being championed by various government agencies under the Roosevelt administration. (In 1939, she did make a series of recordings for the Library of Congress and the Folk Arts Committee of the WPA; and these recordings can be heard today in the Library of Congress.)

It may be no accident that the federally sponsored and technologically enhanced fieldwork that Hurston performed in 1939 and 1940 more or less put an end to her work as a folklorist and anthropologist. She continued writing novels and essays after 1940, perhaps in part because novels and essays were always what she wanted to write most. None of these writings won her as much acclaim, however, as her earlier book-length anthropological studies and the so-called folklore fictions, Jonah's Gourd Vine and Their Eyes Were Watching God, that she published alongside them in the 1930s. If the mainstreaming of a documentary culture in the late 1930s served to dampen Hurston's interest in fieldwork, it may have been because the social realist and commercial directives that lay beneath this aesthetic prohibited Hurston from representing Negro "culture" in the way that she imagined it.

The pithy song fragment that rounds out the Negro "Music" of Florida is a case in point. For the visitor to the southernmost state, the words "Love ain't nothing but the easy-going heart disease" are indigenous words that should issue forth like dew, releasing hymns from beholders like Wallace Stevens, who, even when vacationing in Florida, remains, by the guide's lights, "one of America's best modern poets" (FL, 147). But are these Florida lyrics? Here, Hurston seems not to have used her Folk Arts Committee tape recorder in the way that FSA photographers had used cameras to document Florida's culture of migratory farm workers, citrus packers, and bean canners. Rather, she drew these lyrics from an earlier anthropological study of rural Southern African American culture. "Love ain't nothing but the easy going heart disease" is the last stanza of a so-called social song---"East Coast Blues"--that Hurston included in an appendix to her 1935 study, Mules and Men:

Po' gal long ways from home,
Po' gal long ways from home,
Oh, I'm po' gal a long ways from home.
Don't you hear that East Coast when she blows,
Oh, don't you hear that East Coast when she blows,
Ah, don't you hear that East Coast when she blows.
I'm going down that long lonesome road,
Oh, I'm going down that long lonesome road,
Ah, I'm going down that long lonesome road.
I'm going where the chilly winds don't blow,
Oh, I'm going where the chilly winds don't blow,
Ah, I'm going where the chilly winds don't blow.
You treat me mean you sho going see it again,
Oh, you treat me mean you sho going see it again,
Ah, you treat me mean you sho going see it again.
I love you honey but your woman got me barred,
Oh, I love you honey but your woman got me barred,
Ah, I love you honey but your woman got me barred.
Love ain't nothing but the easy going heart disease,
Oh, love ain't nothing but the easy going heart disease,
Ah, love ain't nothing but the easy going heart disease. 18

Since most of her material for Mules and Men was taken from migrant African American workers in sawmills, turpentine camps, and phosphate mines in Florida and Alabama, "East Coast Blues" may not have been the native Florida material that Hurston represented it to be in the Florida guide. (The "East Coast" in the title may well refer to Florida's Atlantic coast, but the song clearly describes an exile's wish to return to a distant home: "I'm going where the chilly winds don't blow.") But was this lyric even indigenous to the rural southern African American culture that Hurston treated in all of her Florida writings? Even this is not as clear as it would seem.

Hurston's letters to Langston Hughes indicate that the very trope that distinguishes the "East Coast Blues"--the lines that brand love a kind of "heart disease"--should have been known to Hurston before she began her first round of folklore gathering in the rural South in 1927. An urban variant of the lyrics to this so-called social song appears to have reached Langston Hughes in 1926 through the New York-based blues popularizer W. C. Handy. 19 Handy, who began collaborating with the New York- and Washington-based poet in 1926, had published words and music to a couple of blues songs that may be related not only to Hughes's poetry but also to Hurston's Florida song, "East Coast Blues." One of the two Handy songs that appear to have contributed to Hughes's poetic rendering of the bluesy heart-disease trope was "Careless Love," published in 1926.

Love, oh love, oh careless love,
Here is all that I can say,
Just like a Gypsy I'm roamin' 'round,
And just can't keep the blues away. 20

The other was "Shoeboot's Serenade" (a play on Schubert's "Serenade") from 1915. "Do say the word and give my poor heart ease, / The blues ain't nothing but the fatal heart disease." 21 In combination, these two blues songs seem to mingle in the last stanza of Hughes's poem, "Gypsy Man," published in 1927. 22 In "Gypsy Man," Hughes's speaker, an abandoned woman, complains that

Love, Oh, love is
Such a strange disease.
Love, Oh, love is
Such a strange disease.
When it hurts yo' heart you
Sho can't find no ease. 23

The striking similarities between Hughes's "Gypsy Man" of 1927 and Handy's "Careless Love" and "Shoeboot's Serenade" of 1926 and 1915 do not necessarily mean that Hughes's blues poems are derived from Handy's blues lyrics. Hurston, who knew Hughes well in 1927, in fact claimed that some of his blues poems were taken straight from the mouths of urban blues musicians themselves. 24 If this is true, it is possible that the last stanza of "Gypsy Man" represents but one of numerous variations on a trope that echoed through both the rural South and the Harlem cabarets of the 1920s. What is interesting about this particular lyric is that Hurston mentioned it in a letter written to Hughes at the outset of her first folklore expeditions in the rural South in 1927. As a Barnard-trained anthropologist, Hurston had had trouble "pass[ing]" among the workers whose culture she wished to study and record. 25 Beginning in 1927, Hurston began writing to Hughes that she was reading his poetry in order to break the ice and to gain credibility among her rural Southern subjects. She described the reception of Hughes's poetry in great detail in a letter written in the summer of 1928:

In every town I hold 1 or 2 story-telling contests, and at each I begin by telling them who you are and all, then I read poems from "Fine Clothes." [Fine Clothes to the Jew was the controversial title of Hughes's second collection of poems.] Boy! they eat it up. . . . You are being quoted in R.R. camps, phosphate mines, turpentine stills etc. I went into a house Saturday night (last) and the men were skinning--you remember my telling you about that game--and when the dealer saw his opponent was on the turn (and losing consequently) he chanted
"When hard luck overtakes you
Nothin for you to do
Grab up yo' fine clothes
An' sell em to-ooo de Jew Hah!!"

(Slaps the card down on the table).

The other fellow was visibly cast down when the dealer picked up his money. Dealer gloating continued:

"If you wuz a mule
I'd git you a waggin to haul --
But youse so low down-hown
You ain't even got a stall!"

So you see they are making it so much a part of themselves they go to improvising on it.

For some reason they call it "De Party Book." They come specially to be read to & I know you could sell [your book] if you only had a supply. . . . They adore "Saturday Night" and "Evil Woman," "Bad Man," "Gypsy Man." 26

What Hurston is describing in this passage is improvisation. The Saturday night "skinners," or cardplayers, are not only quoting stanzas of Hughes's "Hard Luck" from memory, but they are also adding their own verses to the poem that yielded the title of Fine Clothes to the Jew. These rural southern workers' enjoyment of poems like "Gypsy Man" may be due to the fact that Hughes's urban blues poems derived from some of the same sources as rural songs like "East Coast Blues." In fashioning love as a kind of heart disease, Handy and Hughes had very likely drawn from the reservoirs that Hurston was now trying to tap. 27 In feeding the material of "Gypsy Man" back to the work camps, however, Hurston was "contaminating" this folk reservoir with urban African American art forms. The rural southern workers who taught her the lyrics to "East Coast Blues," then, conceivably could have improvised the closing verse to that song--"Love ain't nothing but the easy going heart disease"--in an echo to the urban influence of Hughes and Handy.

The exact genesis of the love-as-heart-disease lyric that Hurston recorded in the Florida state guide can probably never be ascertained. This is not due to Hurston's carelessness as a folklorist but rather to her understanding of what sort of work she was doing when she ventured into the field. As she stated over and over, "Negro folklore" was vibrant not because it was authentic or indigenous to any people or place but because it was "still in the making." 28 It was not something to be documented but something to be done. As they were used by anthropologists and government archivists alike, cameras and tape recorders did not play much of a role in this sort of vital activity; and the documentary aesthetics that underlay even the best FWP projects of the late 1930s were fundamentally at odds with Hurston's efforts to place process at the root of identity. The documentation of people and places signified for Hurston only if the act of documentation itself became a kind of cultural activity. This notion was lost not only on the social realist writers who objected to her "surfacey" characterization of rural southern African Americans in Their Eyes Were Watching God but also on the federal folk who, in administering a New Deal for American tourists, portrayed America's emerging culture as a dewy thing to "behold."
