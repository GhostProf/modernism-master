Anyone entering the debate on hybridity that has transformed academic discourse over the past decade is forced to confront two conflicting but equally persuasive narratives of transcultural production. One is a rather optimistic story emphasizing the types of syncretism and productive collage that James Clifford has associated with a "poetics of process." 2 According to this story, the increasingly frequent contact between different cultures (the "syncretism" or juxtaposition of cultures that have evolved differently) is not a phenomenon to be feared: cultural cross-fertilization merely accelerates the historically consistent rhythm of contact, exchange, and influence by means of which supposedly authentic and homogeneous cultures are formed.

The second narrative exposes some of the weaknesses of this seductive view, providing a cautionary tale in which intercultural contact, while potentially a rich resource for the development of hybrid identities, tends nonetheless to fortify the domination of one culture over another. By celebrating the ongoing process of cultural cross-fertilization, the counter-argument goes, contemporary critics like Clifford risk erasing the often bloody history that leads to intercultural contact in the first place. Marianna Torgovnick, for instance, has accused advocates of postmodern syncretism of being more fashion-conscious than scholarly. To "revel in the postmodern mélange of us and them," she warns, is to risk "abjuring [our] responsibilities." 3 Modernists are guilty of irresponsible and ahistoricizing appropriations, but postmodernists, too, may be implicated on the same grounds.

As opposed as these two narratives obviously are--the first places faith in hybridity, the second is highly suspicious of hybridity's compromised pasts and contaminated futures--they nonetheless share a common plot structure. Each depends upon a chronological and evolutionary conceit that imposes a sharp break between modernism and postmodernism, that is, between a coercive modernist hybridity on the one hand, and, on the other, a liberating postmodernist syncretism. When contrasted with Bob Marley or the Aboriginal Warumpi Band, canonical modernists inevitably appear to be perpetrators of exploitative appropriations, patently lacking a political consciousness and disinterested, for the most part, in the original contexts of the "exotic" voices they introduce into circulation. For members of both critical camps, modernists unselfconsciously exploit third world artifacts, naïvely believe in cultural "authenticity," and fetishize the instinctual purity of the "primitive" (often in opposition to what they consider to be the decadence and emasculation of "advanced" European art).

There are certainly many instances of modernist appropriation that appear to confirm the arguments outlined above. But whether all modernists are equally insouciant, unselfconscious, and ahistorical is a highly debatable question. Modernist modes of appropriation are often far more complicated than they initially appear. In fact, the genealogies of cultural cross-fertilizations are lengthy and surprisingly twisted, and not all modernists are unaware of the implications--historical, political, and aesthetic--of the further twists they add to the chain. The case I consider here, that of Blaise Cendrars, a Francophone (Swiss) poet, novelist, journalist, and screen writer, is particularly enlightening in this regard. Cendrars's contacts with postcolonial Brazil produced a set of curious writings on hybridity that raise some disturbing questions about modernism's relation to cultural cross-fertilization. Anticipating the work of Claude Lévi-Strauss, whose most significant ethnographic experience also took place in Brazil, Cendrars discovers in the former Portuguese colony an "authentic" alternative to Europe's cultural trajectory, yet views this "authentic" alternative as, paradoxically, the result of hybridization, of the commingling of heterogeneous elements occurring over several centuries of colonial conquest and genetic and cultural métissage. 4

Cendrars demonstrates a high degree of sensitivity with respect to the historical circumstances that produced the métissage of Brazil's population. For instance, he refers repeatedly to the violent intercultural contacts responsible for the palimpsestic Brazilian cultural identities he so admires. Cendrars's aesthetic investment in such contacts and their perverse and unexpected consequences is manifested throughout his prose and poetic works, but nowhere as pointedly--or as ambiguously--as in "La Métaphysique du café" (The metaphysics of coffee). By studying "La Métaphysique du café" I intend to complicate the now dominant image of modernists as Eurocentric collectors of deracinated non-Western exotica. I will also provide a sense of the irreducible strangeness and singularity of Cendrars's own role in the modern cross-fertilization of cultures. His example suggests that in certain cases modernist hybrid production participates in networks of contact and exchange that are far too complex to be encompassed by the manichean categories critics sometimes employ. 5 My intention is not to condemn or celebrate any particular modernist nor to "save" modernism, but rather to direct attention to the ways in which oppressive and liberating forces co-exist and interact in both modern and postmodern instances of aesthetic appropriation.

Cendrars's first contacts with Brazil occurred before he even set foot on the South American continent. In 1923, through his friend Fernand Léger, Cendrars met the Brazilian painter Tarsila do Amaral, who was in Paris studying with the French cubist constructivists, Léger, André Lhote, and Albert Gleizes. 6 Do Amaral was accompanied by her lover, Oswaldo de Andrade, a Brazilian modernist poet, later famous for his "Cannibalist Manifesto" of 1928, a provocative effort to "épater" the Brazilian intelligentsia that placed Brazil on the avant-garde map. A friendship sprang up between Cendrars and the Brazilian couple, nourished by dinner parties in do Amaral's studio where guests included both the major French modernists of the day and the rising generation of Brazilian writers, composers, and artists (Sergio Milliet, Anita Malfatti, Vicente do Rego Monteiro, Heitor Villa-Lobos, and Victor Brecheret). 7

According to some sources it was through Oswaldo de Andrade that Cendrars made his first contact with Paolo Prado, the grand mécène of Brazilian modernism. Prado, who had studied literature in Paris prior to World War I, met regularly with Cendrars to discuss modernism and Brazilian culture at the Librairie Chadenat, a bookstore specializing in the history of the Americas located on the Quai des Grands Augustins. It was here, in the thick atmosphere of erudition, that a bridge between Cendrars's brand of avant-garde practice and Prado's Brazilian-inflected notions of modernism was built. By the end of the year Prado had extended an invitation to Cendrars to visit Brazil that Cendrars accepted a few months later after securing assignments as a journalist from two French newspapers. After his arrival in Rio on 5 February 1924, Cendrars spent the next six months living with the Prados in São Paulo, traveling through the interior with groups of Brazilian painters and writers, and basking in the sun at his new acquaintances' coffee plantations or fazendas. 8

Before any assessment of Cendrars's Brazilian experience can be made, however, it must be understood that the Brazil that Cendrars encountered was one already colored by the primitivist imagination of the early twentieth-century avant-garde. His trip to Brazil, as well as his earliest contacts with Brazilians, took place under the sign of France's already well-developed fascination with third-world exotica, a fascination that often failed to distinguish between African, African American, and Luso-African cultures. Although Josephine Baker would not arrive in Paris until October of 1925, the rage for African art and American jazz had already reached a feverish pitch by the time Cendrars met Prado, de Andrade, and do Amaral. 9 At first, the Brazilians attracted Cendrars for the same reason that, five years earlier, African languages and jazz rhythms had interested him: all seemed to serve equally well as novelties to be thrown into the avant-garde mix.

Clearly Cendrars shared with his generation what Jean-Claude Blachère has identified as a modernist nostalgia for the archaic, a desire for renewal in the midst of a (high) European culture seen as "old, sickly, inert, and bloodless." 10 Similar in this respect to his modern contemporaries, Cendrars was also looking for "new sources of energy" and yet the specific nature of his interest in the "savage" and the "exotic" was somewhat different. 11 In contrast to authors like Tzara or Cocteau, what Cendrars appreciated most was not the "new source of energy" provided by an idealized, childlike, and "authentic" Other, but rather the energy generated by internal division and hybridity, the productive tension that he saw emerging from a clash of conflicting velocities and rhythms operating simultaneously within a single (cultural, geographic) space. He did express admiration for what he considered the "savage" aspects of Brazil--e.g., the bush and the jungle; but far more striking to him, far more revelatory as a model for both aesthetic practice and cultural organization, was contrast: bush and city, "savage" and socialite. His devotion to Brazil was consistent with his earlier aesthetics of "simultaneous contrast," an aesthetics that sought a syncretic collage of distinct elements, each of which retained, despite the juxtaposition, its visual or linguistic independence. As Salvador de Reyes has put it, "Cendrars possessed a great love for Latin America because in its spectacular scenarios he saw a new world . . . full of joie de vivre and audacity, a mixture of hyper-civilization and primitivism." 12

De Reyes's words capture well the peculiar nature of Cendrars's fascination with Brazil (as opposed to his general interest in things "primitive"). It was the hybridity and not the purity of the exotic Other that seduced him, a fact borne out by many of Cendrars's writings on Brazil, including "Saint Paul," the poem cited as an epigraph to this essay. 13 Cendrars may have expressed a desire to visit the "forêt vierge" of the "sauvage," but he also hoped to reside at the precarious point where "[p]roducts from the five corners of the world turn up in the same dish, on the same dress." 14 Faithful to the aesthetic program of simultanéité, Cendrars emulated alterity not for its own sake, but rather for the sake of the startling effects that foreign elements could create when juxtaposed with elements drawn from more familiar European fare.

Given Cendrars's marked preference for the hybrid and the métissé, it appears all the more curious that he ever took pen to paper to compose "La Métaphysique du café." This essay, first published in Rio de Janiero's "O Jornal" on 15 October 1927, 15 has been treated by critics as an unqualified celebration of coffee cultivation, a hymn to colonization, modernist standardization, and capitalist enterprise. Alexandre Eulalio, the first chronicler of Cendrars's travels through Brazil, considers "La Métaphysique" to be, without doubt, "one of the most expressive documents of the Paulist economic euphoria and the feeling of omnipotence consequent to it," 16 while Adrien Roig, without a trace of irony, calls it a "very beautiful text . . . of captivating realism and forceful inspiration. 17 Indeed, on a first reading, "La Métaphysique du café" does appear to invert Cendrars's habitual hierarchy, jettisoning diversity in favor of single-crop agriculture, the most ecologically devastating and dehumanizing form of land management that Brazil ever developed. Why Cendrars would have chosen to write what appears to be a propagandistic piece of journalism privileging monoculture over the wealth of botanical diversity he praises elsewhere is a question that none of his critics has answered adequately. 18 "La Métaphysique du café" is rarely mentioned in comprehensive studies of Cendrars's work and, if so, then only in passing--usually in the context of his fascination with famous entrepreneurs. 19

The critical neglect of "La Métaphysique du café" probably stems from the circumstantial quality of the piece, the fact that it reads somewhat like an advertisement for the industrialization of Brazil. Yet, paradoxically, while "La Métaphysique" seems to address specifically the advantages of coffee cultivation in Brazil, its first six paragraphs were in fact written for an earlier piece on economic development in the United States entitled "The Principle of Utility," published separately in 1925, then integrated into his epic on terrorist anarchism, Moravagine, the following year. The narrator who intones the hymn to monoculture in Moravagine can only with some difficulty be fused with the person of Cendrars in 1927. "La Métaphysique" is therefore far more equivocal than it first appears and should not be classified so quickly as evidence of Cendrars's fondness for large demonstrations of the will. The equivocal nature of the essay is due in large part to the tension produced between two distinct discourses, one metaphysical and the other economic. In a sense, "La Métaphysique" is itself métissé, internally non-self-identical, and structured as a kind of dialogue between an advocate of monoculture and a poet invested in difference, as a source both of diction and creative energy. The ambiguity of "La Métaphysique"'s point of view towards both monoculture and hybridity makes it significant reading material, not only for specialized scholars of Cendrars, but also for those interested in the concept of hybridity and in the important place this concept has acquired in postcolonial theory.

The best entry to the essay is perhaps provided by the subject matter itself: large-scale coffee cultivation, or what Cendrars calls "the enigma," the "force of attraction and exchange" at the root of intercultural contact. Coffee cultivation, however, is not simply a theme, it is also a symbol of the globalization of Brazil's economy and of its principal protagonist, Paulo Prado. This highly influential and powerful re was Cendrars's closest friend and constant companion during his Brazilian trips; he was also, however, the head of a coffee oligarchy and therefore part of the power structure responsible for the growth of monocultural agro-economics. "La Métaphysique du café" was obviously written in Prado's honor; it is in some sense a metaphysics of Prado himself, an attempt to understand the law that could make coffee--personified by Prado--simultaneously a force of liberation and oppression, of intercultural and monocultural standardization.

Just who was Paulo Prado and what was the nature of his hold on Cendrars? In short, Prado encapsulated in his own genealogy the entire modern history of Brazil. The Prados arrived in the São Paulo region from Portugal in the eighteenth century, entered into livestock trading, slave trading, sugar growing, politics, and finally established ascendency in the domain of coffee cultivation. When Paolo Prado went to study literature in Paris before World War I, the coffee boom, which lasted from approximately 1840 to 1900, was already on the wane. But the Prado name was still firmly associated with wide-spread coffee cultivation, a nineteenth-century phenomenon responsible for an irreversible "shift," as historian Darrell Lévi has written, "in the nation's economic center of gravity and a major sea-change in Brazil's economic history." 20

The cultivation of coffee imposed by oligarchies such as the one controlled by the Prado family resulted not only in the heightened prosperity and greater visibility of the Brazilian economy, but also in mass destruction of the Amazon jungle, the repression of an economic underclass, the solidification of a police state, and the eradication of previously isolated Amerindians and their diverse cultures. However, coffee was also an agent of cultural cross-fertilization. It permitted the Prados to travel, to establish connections with important European families such as the Rothschilds, and to collect examples of European avant-garde artworks. Finally, coffee was the indirect cause of the famous racial hybridity of the Brazilian population: during the nineteenth century, Amerindians were "domesticated," slaves were imported from Africa, and immigrants were seduced from Italy and Germany in order to supply labor power to the burgeoning coffee and sugar cane plantations on Brazilian soil. For Cendrars, then, Prado emblematized the two faces of modernity. On the one hand, he stood for economic development, the raw force of will allowing humans to intervene in the constitution of ecosystems and the migratory patterns of entire populations. On the other, he represented internationalism, the end of cultural isolation, increased possibilities for intercultural contact, and thus the most avant-garde tendencies in the arts and letters. 21

What drew Cendrars to Prado, and what underlies the strange prose of "La Métaphysique du café," is Cendrars's sense that everything valuable in modernity--the openness to the new, the desire to redefine the locus and function of art, and, perhaps most importantly, the decision to jettison originality and authenticity in favor of citation, pastiche, hybridity, and appropriation--in short, all that was dearest to his generation, was in fact predicated on a deeper principle, one that Prado's entrepreneurial spirit illustrated perfectly. For Cendrars, the dynamic responsible for modernity's richness, the impulse governing its two major manifestations--monoculture and hybridity, The Grand Hotel and the carnival--was quite simply appetite, the "furious appetite" alluded to in his poem "Saint Paul."

In order to seize the relation Cendrars sketches between hybridity and conquest, heterogeneity and the implacable domination of the same, it is necessary to return to the pages of "La Métaphysique du café." Structurally, "La Métaphysique" is presented as a kind of mise en abyme: it contains two very distinct scenarios, a frame narrative in which monoculture dominates, and an internal narrative in which increased transcultural hybridization takes center stage. By means of this framing structure, Cendrars implies the interdependence between imperialism and hybridity, between cultural hegemony and greater "choice" (the multiple "interpellations" to which, according to Jim Collins, the modern subject is exposed). 22 He begins the essay by evoking the railway, the road, and the canal, familiar late nineteenth-century symbols of expansionism, in order to suggest at once the discovery of difference and its inverse, the imposition of geometric regularity on previously immeasurable expanses of the globe:

Roads, canals, railways, ports, warehouses, stations, factories, high tension electric lines, waterways, bridges, tunnels, all these lines and curves that dominate [qui dominent] the contemporary landscape, impose [imposent] their grandiose geometry. But the most powerful agent of transformation is without contest monoculture.

In less than fifty years monoculture has transformed the visage of the world, which exploits with astonishing mastery [avec une maîtrise étonnante].

Monoculture needs products, primary matter, plants, animals to grind down [à broyer], to masticate [à triturer], to transform. It dissociates [dissocie] and disintegrates [désagrege]. Without the slightest care for the nature of each region monoculture tames this climate, prescribes that plant, overwhelms whatever secular economy might be in place.

Monculture tends to transform, if not the entire planet, then at least each zone of the planet. 23

The opening paragraphs of "La Metaphysique du café" combine a tone of awe-- "maîtrise étonnante"--with a vocabulary suggesting moral repugnance--"dominer," "imposer," "exploitation," and so on. Monoculture "dissociates," "disintegrates," and "masticates." Like a huge digestive apparatus monoculture transforms "primal matter, plants, and animals" into one identical substance. It is merciless ("sans aucun souci"); it "prescribes" and "overwhelms," eventually "reducing" rather than increasing the diversity of forms of life on the planet: "In comparison to the vegetal overabundance of nature, monoculture only cultivates a very small number of judiciously selected species" .

All this activity of violent assimilation and mastication is motivated, Cendrars then explains, by man's perverse impulse to render difference uniform, his seemingly compulsive need to dominate nature's chaos through any means at his disposal: "On the part of man there is a need for simplification that tends to satisfy itself by any means whatsoever. And this artificial monotony, this monotony that increasingly takes over the world, this monotony that certain people deplore is actually the clearest sign of our grandeur" .

It could be argued, and indeed has been, that Cendrars was entirely in earnest when he wrote these words, and that, therefore, any tonal inconsistencies one might discern between expressions such as "artificial monotony" and "our grandeur"--as well as the irritating effect created by the thrice repeated "this monotony"--are simply inventions on the part of the reader. The conclusion to the essay tends to confirm this reading, for it insists upon a causal link between the violent transformation of delicate ecosystems and a "moral progression" leading to greater civic freedoms and democratic reforms:

everywhere progress, wealth, and vast material transformations are accompanied by a moral progression: the rapid evolution of society and its new conceptions of civilization, of democracy, of the citizen and of the citizen's rights.

It is in this sense that coffee can be said to be a metaphysical entity, just like other products of the earth and all the labors of man. [131]

The optimism Cendrars expresses in this conclusion now appears hallucinatory; however, at the time, two years before the Great Crash of 1929, capitalist expansion indeed seemed to promise a higher standard of living, if not for the indigenous Amerindian, then at least, as Cendrars remarks, for "the poorest colonizer." "This little coffee bean [Cette cerise de café]," Cendrars predicts, is the capital capable not only of financing the newest "skyscraper in the great city of São Paulo," but also of supplying greater comfort and luxury, "Yes, luxury, even to the poorest colonizer" . Only in his later, deeply pessimistic works such as Le Lotissement du ciel (The parceling out of the sky) does Cendrars fully admit the fallacy of the causal link he had assumed previously between monoculture and altruistic reform. 24

Yet even "La Métaphysique du café" shows signs of internal conflict; a pastiche of texts patched together, it is often self-contradictory on the levels of diction and tone. The conclusion may perhaps have been intended merely as a formal bow to the audience Cendrars was addressing. He should not be forgiven his formal bow, however; Cendrars was in fact often guilty of flirting with technocrats, industrialists, and repressive regimes of many colors, and thus of compromising his reputation as a prophet of literary and cultural freedoms. 25 At the same time, though, the destabilizing elements embedded in the essay militate in favor of a more thorough and careful reading. The essay as a whole is not reducible to the propagandistic bow with which it ends. Cendrars's discourse is never monocultural or monoglossic; it is as hybrid as the society it describes, constantly shifting its position of enunciation and undermining its own authority as autonomous, unified speech.

The beginning of what I am calling the "internal narrative" (the original part of the essay excerpted for publication in L'Intransigeant under the title "Devant les champs de café" [Before the coffee fields]) is presented as an autobiographical account of Cendrars's arrival in Brazil and his subsequent visit to a coffee-growing fazenda:

No where else have I been so struck by the manifest grandeur of today and by the immutable beauty of human labor as when disembarking for the first time, three or four years ago, on the shores of Brazil.

The wharfs of Rio and Santos spread out in rectilinear lines, littered with merchandise.

What! all this magnificent disorder, these automobile bodies, these locomotives in pieces, these dismantled wagons, these piles of scrap-iron, these machines, these statuettes of Saint Theresa of the Child Jesus that are being introduced by the thousands, these barrels of wine, these tons of gasoline, these bathtubs, roles of toilet paper, mountains of gramophones and loud-speakers, these electric gizmos, these trunks full of pretty linen and rare apparel, of fashionable perfumes and pearl necklaces, these postal sacks, books, letters, newspapers from all over the world, . . . all these things have sprung from a coffee bean [tout cela est sorti d'un grain de café]: ports, cities, men who disembark every day . . . all this is the direct product of coffee, representing its force of attraction and exchange, its palpable value, its strength, its richness. What an enigma! [128-9]

Cendrars's first snapshot of Brazil is taken on the wharfs, from the vantage point of a geographically and narratively liminal region where the bustling traffic between Brazil and the rest of the world is most in evidence. The poet's first impression of Brazil is thus not of its autonomy and isolation but rather of the permeability of its borders. Brazil's cultural identity is shown to be in flux: the country is undergoing an invasion of commodity items, all of which serve as fetishes of modernity, "statuettes of Saint Teresa" in the tabernacle of capitalism. The wharf scene is an inversion of the scene that preceded it--the spectacle of monoculture's "artificial monotony" and "grandeur"; and yet the two scenes are clearly linked by a causal connection. If monoculture is a large stomach, a machine for devouring and transforming everything into the same, monoculture is also, he discovers, a catalyst for exchange and increased intercultural contact. Objects of consumption from all over the world mingle in a chaotic mosaic: from words ("newspapers from all over the world") and apparati ("scrap-iron" and "machines") to belief systems ("statuettes of Saint Theresa"), cultural product ("gramophones"), and household items responsible for the dramatic transformation of hygenic rituals and daily life ("rolls of toilet paper," "bathtubs," "tons of gasoline"). Cendrars even implies that he is himself a kind of produit de consommation brought over from abroad to be assimilated into the "magnificent disorder" of Brazilian cultural life. The repetition of the verb "disembark" to refer both to his own arrival on Brazilian soil and to that of the hundreds of thousands of immigrants who come to work on sugar and coffee plantations establishes surreptitiously a connection between the types of cultural crossings (and labor patterns) enforced by agro-capitalism and the types of cultural crossings celebrated as internationalism or the globalization of culture. Cendrars's relation to Brazil transforms him into a thing, a fetishized representative of European modernism, expected, in turn, to transport back to Europe an assimilable--and marketable--snapshot of Brazil. It is as though Cendrars had a precocious intimation that, in postmodernity, cultural hybridity would largely be a matter of consumption. 26

Upon witnessing the spectacle of commodity diversity played out on the wharfs of Rio, the narrator then takes a vow that he will seek out its hidden motor; he will "go back as quickly as possible to the source of this marvelous richness and . . . go see, see with [his] own eyes, how this source overflows with such furious energy, such furious abundance" . The next section of the narrative then describes the author's visit to the plantation of São Martinho, a visit made with the ambition of locating the "source" of "such furious abundance." The original publication of "La Métaphysique du café" was accompanied by a photograph of the main building at São Martinho, a detail lending a note of journalistic rigor to the piece. But Cendrars's written description is far less informative than it is impressionistic, drawing on a metaphysical, poetic discourse and replacing the prose structure of the earlier paragraphs with a telegraphic, paratactic style.

The passage's sudden shifts in tone, style, punctuation, and diction correspond to the nature of the experience being described. The abrupt revelation of the "source" or the "force of attraction and exchange" is staged by Cendrars as no less than a confrontation with the mathematical sublime. Indeed, all the traditional elements of the sublime aesthetic experience are in place: he and his companion "contemplate" the "colossal" expanse; the identical rows of coffee-plants are metaphorically transformed into one of Kant's privileged res for the natural sublime, the ocean and its innumerable waves ("an emerald sea, a profound ocean"); and finally, the observers of this spectacle are reduced to an anguished silence ("too much grandeur strangles, stifles, and troubles"). Descriptive prose collapses into the exclamation "What a spectacle!" In the end the viewer is left speechless, resonating in awe to the miracle of redundancy ("I couldn't find a word to say").

Once again, the reader is arrested by the problem of tone. Cendrars may delight in the spectacle of agricultural standardization but he also indicates that such standardization "silences," "stifles," and "strangles." The experience as described is thus by no means uniformly positive; the author depicts himself as transfixed on the spot, immobilized, just like a coffee plant, "les pieds plantés dans la terre rouge." He has not been energized by the spectacle of monoculture; on the contrary, he has been struck dumb by it, like the victim of an Ovidian metamorphosis dehumanized by too intense an exposure to passion. At least momentarily, the sublimity of monoculture transforms the spectator into stone: he is now reified, transformed into an object, "somber, taciturn, and as if immobilized." The author experiences a loss of his faculties, a sense that the imagination has been overcome and defeated by something it cannot encompass; only after a few moments does this experience of silencing give way to an overflow of words, the repetitive, appositive sentences found in the paragraphs that follow.

Cendrars's caffeinated sublime is an implicit commentary not on monoculture alone but rather on its relation to the Kantian sublime and to the laws of Western aesthetics in general. Just as the experience of the mathematical sublime described by Kant involves simultaneously a movement of recoil and a sensation of awe, so too Cendrars's experience of monoculture's "artificial monotony" provokes in him sensations of morbidity and vertigo as well as respect and admiration. The original title, "La Métaphysique du café," already evoked the connection between Kant's theory of Universal Taste and Brazil's discovery of the world's taste for coffee; however, in 1931, when the essay was republished as part of the collection Aujourd'hui, Cendrars solidified the connection by adding an epigraph that places Kant--or at least his ghost--in a direct relation to monocultural production. The epigraph, which I have resisted citing until now, is drawn from a French newspaper article that appeared four days after the first version of "La Métaphysique du café" was published in O Jornal. It reads:

Panama, 5 September 1927. 'The last direct descendant of the great German philosopher, Immanuel Kant, recently passed away in this city. Kant's last descendant was the proprietor of a coffee plantation.' 27

In a few succinct sentences, Cendrars establishes no less than a genetic relation between the great German philosopher and the owner of the coffee plantation and, as a corollary, between metaphysical principles of universal taste and the geopolitics of global monoculture. The connection Cendrars establishes is somewhat loose, however, in that Kant's sublime is that of the natural wonder, whereas the experience of the sublime he describes is one provoked by the spectacle of man's own nature, his unyielding will to impress his stamp on the disorderly productivity of the earth. The aspect of Kant that Cendrars retains intact is this: a law of human nature exists commanding man to seek experiences of immeasurable magnitudes only to search for ways to encompass these magnitudes by recasting them as reflections of the immeasurability of the human mind. According to this law, an initial feeling of repugnance or fear is followed by an intimation of what the human mind is capable of achieving: a conception of the supersensible, of that which is beyond estimation or, put differently, that which can only find its measure in the inestimable thing man's mind is. "Therefore," concludes Kant,

the feeling of the sublime in nature is respect for our own vocation, which we attribute to an Object of nature by a certain subreption (substitution of a respect for the Object in place of one for the idea of humanity in our own self, the Subject); and this feeling renders, as it were, intuitable the supremacy of our cognitive faculties on the rational side over the greatest faculty of sensibility. 28

In a move that will be crucial not only to the future of Western aesthetics in general but also to Cendrars's specific understanding of the monocultural sublime, Kant shifts the locus of immeasurability from the Object to the Subject, thereby, in a sense, absorbing the other, making it a re, an equivalent of the Self. The law of the mind as described by Kant turns out to be the necessity experienced by the subject to seek out equivalences, to insist upon the fungibility of the non-fungible:

in the aesthetic estimate of such an immeasurable whole, the sublime does not lie so much in the greatness of the number, as in the fact that in our onward advance we always arrive at proportionally greater units. [Critique, 105; emphasis added]

It is, in other words, "for us a law (of reason), which goes to make us what we are," Kant explains, "that we should esteem as small in comparison with ideas of reason everything which for us is great in nature as an object of sense; and that which makes us alive to the feeling of this supersensible side of our being harmonizes with that law" (Critique, 106).

In essence, then, Kant proposes that our natural instinct as humans is to resolve every sublime experience of the external Object into an experience of the sublime Subject, to elevate an experience of terror and stifling awe to the order of an epiphany, an affirmation of Reason. As victims (or beneficiaries) of this law, we transform our silence into words, our non-knowledge into knowledge, and by doing so we neutralize the threat of the radically other by incorporating the other into ourselves. It is in this respect that the sublime can be seen--by Cendrars, is seen--as productive, capitalist, even cannibalistic. Our nature, Kant seems to say, is quite simply to be hungry, to have an appetite for--to take a "negative pleasure" in--the "absolutely great" which we then succeed in assimilating to our transcendental being (Critique, 106). It is this law of human reason, and not the little "cérise de café," that proves to be the dynamic source, the generative motor that Cendrars discovers in "La Métaphysique du café."

"Then, overcome with dizziness, I began to reflect," Cendrars continues:

Men came. They set fire to the virgin forest. They cleared the ground. They tore up the time-honored roots [les souches séculaires]. They weeded. They worked the soil so that it could nurture hundreds of thousands of young coffee plants, comfortably nestled in their little twig baskets . . . They worked. [ . . . ] They planted. And the plantations spread out, spread out into the interieur, over millions and millions of square miles.

It's enough to make you dizzy. Whose will made all this happen? [Quelle est la volonté qui a déclenché ça?] [Aujourd'hui, 130]

Here, as in "The Utility Principle," a momentary vertigo in the face of apparent infinity produces the desire to discover an underlying principle, a motor or law ("la volonté"). "What keeps men here and makes them submit themselves to this new discipline?" asks Cendrars; "Is it the brutishness brought on by work, by too much work, or is it simply the promise of profit, of reward?" . In evoking man's will to profit Cendrars seems to fall back on purely pragmatic explanations for monoculture's transformation of "each zone of the planet"; and yet his search does not end here. The answer to Cendrars's question--"Whose will made all this happen?"--seems to be located instead in a slightly earlier passage, one devoted to coffee in particular rather than to monocultural production as a whole. Finally, in this passage the full implications of the title, "La Métaphysique du café," are exposed:

And why so much coffee? Is humanity unable to resist yet a new toxin? And what exactly is this need for intoxication found among all peoples at all times? Is it just a mere vice like any other, or, on the contrary, is it an idiosyncracy peculiar to the human race, a necessity, an ineluctable need, to recuperate losses [récupérer les déperditions], to stimulate always more the mind's force, the mind's sharpness [stimuler les forces de plus en plus vives, de plus en plus aiguës du cerveau]? [130] 29

In what has become a familiar interrogative mode, Cendrars suggests that the will (or the law) governing Brazil's economic prosperity, its increasing political importance, and even its new visibility in the aesthetic domain, is related not simply to monocultural agro-economics in general, but to the obsessive cultivation of coffee in particular. The need that causes people of all ages and locales to intoxicate themselves with a stimulant is, he implies, identical to the law that requires man to experience as fully and intensely as possible the seemingly limitless force and acuity of the mind. To put this in Kantian terms, we might say that to "pass beyond the narrow confines of sensibility," to feel the full scope of the mind without, however, being able to identify its borders, is the ultimate goal towards which all this feverish activity strains (Critique, 103). Man suffers from an ineluctable need to "recuperate losses," says Cendrars, 30 just as Kant, employing a vocabulary that is, after all, arguably more economic than it is metaphysical, posits the sublime as a variety of investment, the experience of a loss that must be tolerated in order to create a surplus, an expansion of the self. For the experience of the sublime, writes Kant, provokes "a pleasure that only arises indirectly, being brought about by the feeling of a momentary check to the vital forces followed at once by a discharge all the more powerful [ . . . ]; the [sublime] object lends itself to the presentation of a sublimity discoverable in the mind" (Critique, 91-2; emphasis added).

In sum, Cendrars's "metaphysics" of monoculture produces a theory of human nature as essentially appetitive, forever extending its reach to encompass what at first appears terrifyingly Other. Paradoxically, humans seek not to satisfy their appetite, but to stimulate it, for any immediate satisfaction, any cessation of longing, would place limits on what appetite, and thus on what the mind, could be. Monoculture, like Kant's Subject with a capital "S," eats up everything around it, reducing difference on its own soil but simultaneously permitting commerce with that which, up to that point, it has not been. Greedy as the mind, monoculture is destructive of the other, but infinitely re-productive of itself. Ultimately Kant belongs in Brazil because his theory of the mind harmonizes perfectly with the metaphysics of monoculture: both propose that the non-fungible can be--must be, by law--reduced to the fungible, and that, through the very process of the Other's reduction, man should be enlarged "de plus en plus."

How, then, is the reader to situate Cendrars's voice in "La Métaphysique du café"? Exactly what attitude towards monoculture is one to attribute to him? There is no doubt that Cendrars admires the force of the law of assimilation he discovers in San Martinho, but it is equally clear that he appreciates its contrary, the non-law of uncontrolled diversity--hybrid forms such as the carnival, the métissé, as well as the heteroglossic mix of languages and lifestyles he finds piled up along the quai. Most worthy of notice is his recognition that monoculture and hybridity are inextricably intertwined: intercultural contacts of all varieties--ethnographic, artistic, cultural, religious, and of course commercial--owe their existence to, and cannot take place without, economic developments of the kind motivated by the law of the caffeinated sublime. Although the two major forms of hybridity Cendrars confronts are markedly different--the racial and cultural hybridity of Brazil's past must be distinguished from the commercial hybridity of its present--both can nonetheless trace their roots back to the same Kantian ambitions of the agricultural entrepreneur. Cendrars does not want to relinquish either the entrepreneurial rigor ("the need for simplification") or the carnivalesque excess of syncretic play. The unsubstantiated premise of "La Métaphysique" is that the former will continue to engender and support the latter. Such a premise, however, will not be maintained as Cendrars, like many modernists of his generation, becomes increasingly aware of the dangers of capitalist exploitation. His attitude toward monocultural standardization and industrial development grows increasingly skeptical and "irreverent." 31 And even in 1927 he can provide only weak assurance that monoculture's suppression of botanical, zoological, and racial difference will be offset by an exponential increase in the number of hybrid ways to live, worship, and make art.

A year after "La Métaphysique du café" appeared, Oswaldo de Andrade published what would become one of the major documents of postmodern "hybridity-talk": the "Manifesto Antropófago" or "Cannibalist Manifesto." 32 Brazil's first indigenous avant-garde intervention was based on a recuperation of the previously pejorative term "cannibal" to refer to the active assimilation and transformation of foreign aesthetics by Brazilian-born Portuguese-speaking writers. Many contemporary postmodern theorists place de Andrade's aesthetics of "anthropophagy" in the same category as Rogério Sganzerla's more recent "aesthetics of garbage," Paul Leduc's "salamander" poetics, and the phenomenon of American hip-hop. 33 All these examples of postmodern and, in some cases, postcolonial hybridity are thought to manifest the single most progressive tendency operating in culture today. This tendency is one that, as Kobena Mercer describes it, "critically appropriates elements from the mastercodes of the dominant culture and 'creolises' them." 34 Cannibalism is an appropriate metaphor for this positively valenced mode of "appropriation"; it suggests the gesture of incorporation (otherwise known as pastiche, collage, grafting, or sampling) and exemplifies such an incorporation, a parodic recycling of a pejorative term that displaces the term's previous value.

No one has yet thought to resituate de Andrade's "Cannibalist Manifesto" either in the context of Cendrars's "La Métaphysique du café" or in that of Kant's Third Critique. This is hardly surprising, given that Cendrars is not a particularly well-known re in postcolonialist studies and that, further, Kant's is not the first name to pop into one's mind when speaking about cannibalism. But such a recontextualization is worth attempting, for it is reasonable to posit that de Andrade would have been familiar, if not intimate, with Cendrars's "Utility Principle," and that, moreover, he would have continued to follow Cendrars's career after 1924 and therefore would have read "La Métaphysique." Intertextuality or literary cross-fertilization is the major theme of the "Cannibalist Manifesto," and de Andrade makes no attempt to disguise the fact that his own peculiar brand of avant-garde poetics is largely a cannibalization of previous European models, including--but not limited to--that of Francis Picabia's "Manifeste Cannibale Dada" of 1920. 35 But there is further evidence to suggest that the influence of Cendrars was pertinent to the thesis of "Cannibalist Manifesto": in de Andrade's "Manifesto of Pau-Brasil Poetry" of 1924, where the author first tests out ideas he will later develop in the more radical "Cannibalist Manifesto," he specifically mentions Cendrars as an important model. Moreover, the manifesto's typography, diction, and argument all manifest the influence of Cendrars's work, especially the 1919 volume, Dix-neuf poèmes élastiques. 36 Finally, both the dominant metaphor and the specific language in which this metaphor is articulated in the "Cannibalist Manifesto" recall "La Métaphysique." "Cannibalist Manifesto" is clearly another troping on Kant, but a troping to the second degree.

"Cannibalist Manifesto" begins with a gesture similar to the one that opens Cendrars's "Métaphysique": a matter of taste, or what we might call culinary ritual, is declared to have metaphysical implications: "Cannibalism alone unites us. Socially. Economically. Philosophically." 37 Critics have understood the "us" in this passage to refer to Brazilians in particular (and indeed, the subversive nature of de Andrade's transformative, appropriative gesture depends to a large extent on the particularity of the "us"). However, it soon becomes evident that cannibalism is a practice that unites not simply Brazilian writers and artists in search of a historically resonant, indigenous aesthetic; rather, cannibalism is a practice that unites the entire human race: "I am only concerned with what is not mine. Law of Man. Law of the cannibal" . "Law of the cannibal" is in fact the law of the capitalist, who concerns himself only with what he does not already possess. In quest of the not-yet-possessed, or the Other, "We push onward," states de Andrade . If we wish to be good cannibals, he continues, we must transform "the equation 'Self, part of the Cosmos' into the axiom 'Cosmos, part of the Self'" . This latter axiom is, for "us," our "Law": "Subsistence. Experience. Cannibalism" .

By now, an echo of Cendrars's "Metaphysics of coffee" should be audible. Just as Cendrars discovered the "principle" behind monocultural assimilation, so de Andrade articulates a "Law of Man" or of the cannibal who incorporates every Other into the self. But behind this echo it is also possible to retrieve the even fainter reverberation of Kant's own axiomatic metaphysics. While de Andrade's cannibal inverts the pantheistic "Self, part of the Cosmos" to form the proposition "Cosmos, part of the Self," Kant's "outraged imagination" strives, in its own cannibalistic "onward advance," to incorporate the sublime Other, to assimilate it to the sublime dimensions of the mind.

In what way, then, does the cannibalist impulse of the manifesto differ from the standardizing impulse of "La Métaphysique du café"? How are de Andrade and Cendrars positioned differently vis-à-vis the devastating, exploitative program of modernist standardization? The aesthetics championed by "Cannibalist Manifesto" have generally been associated with the subversive project of détournement: the appropriation of, or troping on, an element of the hegemonic order in order to create a new hybrid, an entity situated in the "neither . . . nor" region of anti-essentialist politics. 38 In the words of de Andrade's English translator, Leslie Bary, the manifesto "challenges the dualities civilization/barbarism, modern/primitive, and original/derivative, which had informed the construction of Brazilian culture since the days of the colony"; "Oswald's anthropophagist," she concludes, "neither apes nor rejects European culture, but 'devours' it, adapting its strengths and incorporating them into the native self." 39

The difference between a cannibalist aesthetics and a monocultural economics is thus not to be located in their respective underlying metaphysics, for the Kantian "Law of Man"-- the law of devouring and appropriation--underlies them both. Rather, this difference is to be found in the positionality of the subject who exercises the Kantian "Law": Does the subject exercise it from a position of subservience or domination, in order to resist or, alternatively, to oppress, in the role of "parodic" mime or "authentic" originator? At several clearly identifiable moments, Cendrars positions himself on the side of the colonizer; "La Métaphysique du café" was, after all, intended to please his benefactor, the Europeanized intellectual and coffee plantation owner, Paulo Prado (most likely the "companion" to whom Cendrars refers in the text). Entire passages of "La Métaphysique," not to mention the full text of "The Utility Principle," read like apologies for empire, hymns to the pure will to dominate the Other. De Andrade's manifesto, in contrast, assumes the position of the colonized. Although de Andrade, like Prado, was a Europeanized Brazilian and a member of the ruling elite, he attempts to speak in "Cannibalist Manifesto" as a descendent of the Tupi, not of the Vieira, as a son of the Great Snake, not of the Bad Mother, Cornelia. 40 Ironically, it was Cendrars himself who had encouraged de Andrade to identify more fully with the indigenous tribal past of Brazil. 41 However, as a foreigner dependent for his knowledge of Brazil's interior upon the very system that was destroying it, Cendrars could hardly have assumed the position of the colonized that de Andrade, perhaps with a bit of bad faith, could. As a member of the oligarchical elite, however, he was appropriating a cannibalist identity from a race to which he did not belong. The politics of de Andrade's gesture are thus a little less clear than they have been assumed to be. At the same time, though, de Andrade manages to execute a more radical gesture; he cannibalizes Kant not with the intention of mobilizing him for monoculture but, rather, in order to hybridize Kant, to unveil the "primitive," cannibalistic discourse embedded in and informing Kant's own, and thus to suggest that the "law of human reason"--that which "makes us what we are"--is the law of the sublime cannibal "only concerned with what is not mine" ("Cannibalist Manifesto," 39).

Whether de Andrade would have hybridized Kant without the intervention of Cendrars is a question worth asking. By making the link between a "devouring" monoculture and a Subject in search of the sublime, Cendrars provided de Andrade with a certain form of avant-garde nourishment. Because of this debt (not to mention his debt to European modernism in general), de Andrade can only with difficulty claim to speak from the position of the indigenous Brazilian cannibal. The equivocal position in which de Andrade finds himself is not exceptional but rather typical of the predicament encountered by many cultural producers speaking from the position of the disenfranchised. As Gayatri Spivak has persuasively argued, the subaltern--the truly subaltern--rarely (if ever) gets a chance to locate himself or herself in discourse, and thus cannot lay claim to the honor of introducing new (pure) voices into circulation. 42 In this light, all cultural producers are cannibals, inevitably subject to the "Law of Man," the law that bids us find a language, a measure, for that which initially appears beyond "the narrow confines" of the hegemonic (European) self.

A close reading of "La Métaphysique du café" and its use of pronouns, diction, and citation, reveals that Cendrars's authorial voice is hybrid as well; it does not emanate from a self-identical position but is instead composed of various, and sometimes opposed discourses in what is perhaps ultimately not enough of a "critical syncretism," a poetics of simultaneity explicitly concerned with exposing power relations between heterogeneous cultural traditions or practices. But one thing Cendrars does at least acknowledge is that all forms of making, speaking, and cultivating are to some extent acts of homogenization (they suppress radical alterity) as much as they are acts of hybridization that--in the vegetal as well as the commercial world--produce consequences never entirely predictable or subject to control. By placing Kant at the heart both of homogenization and hybridity, Cendrars introduces an irony into all attempts to maintain their separation (and thus to politicize them in any simple or clear manner). Cendrars demonstrates that even an aesthetics of difference has to pass through a moment of digestive recuperation, of communication in which discourse--monoculture, the Transcendental Subject--absorbs the Other and thus risks reducing this Other to the same.

The discernably ambivalent tone of Cendrars's "La Métaphysique du café" is a bracing corrective to the kind of celebratory excesses of critics like Jim Collins, Iain Chambers, or Ulf Hannerz. According to Hannerz, for instance, multiplied contacts between first-world and third-world countries (contacts based, he does not mention, on the exploitative practices of agricultural, industrial, and chemical conglomerates) are not decreasing but rather increasing the number of cultural alternatives available to the world as a whole. "The people in my favorite Nigerian town drink Coca Cola," Hannerz writes, "but they drink buru-kutu too; and they can watch Charlie's Angels as well as Hausa drummers on the television sets which spread rapidly as soon as electricity has arrived." 43 In the same vein, Collins writes with unalloyed enthusiasm that the problem of "accessibility [has] virtually disappeared"; there is no Grand Hotel "that somehow orchestrates all cultural production and reception according to one master system." Anyone can be interpellated by anything, and thus we are subjects without boundaries, constantly able to incorporate the Other as just another version of the self. 44

There is reason to rejoice in the increased circulation of third world musics, art traditions, and hybrid practices. Still, as Robert Stam reminds us, hybridity "has never been a peaceful encounter, a tension-free theme park; it has always been deeply entangled with colonial violence." 45 And Torgovnick counsels that reveling "in the postmodern mélange of us and them" should not obscure other, less developed historical knowledge of how, precisely, these new and exotic treats came to appear on the dinner plate. 46 All forms of cultural syncretism must struggle to remain critical; the absorptive gesture is not arrested by creative re-citation; without the traumatic work of memory and productive alienation--without, that is, the very ruptures in tone that characterize Cendrars's "La Métaphysique"--such gestures can submit too easily to the law of the cannibalistic and homogenizing sublime. Resisting complete assimilation, letting the seams show (as Cendrars invariably did), is perhaps a peculiarly modernist way of registering the history that brings different cultures together and that too often tears them apart.

41. Until Cendrars came to Brazil, Oswaldo de Andrade and Tarsila do Amaral's avant-garde aesthetic had consisted of the stream-lined cubism of Léger and the machinism of Le Corbusier. Cendrars encouraged the couple to explore Brazil's indigenous forms of cultural expression. He organized group trips into the Brazilian interior--an interior that even de Andrade and do Amaral had never seen before. It was only after the group made a trip together to Minas Gerais in April 1924 that de Andrade came up with the idea for Pau Brasil, the movement that would establish the necessity for a properly Brazilian avant-gardism and that would spawn the "Cannibalist Manifesto" three years later. (Perhaps this is why de Andrade wrote in the dedication to Pau Brasil: "To Blaise Cendrars, on the occasion of the discovery of Brazil.") Serge Milliet writes that "the influence of Cendrars on the Brazilian modernists was decisive. These rich young people could travel every year throughout Europe, but they knew nothing about their own country" (Milliet, quoted in Pierre Rivas, "Cendrars, le Nouveau Monde et l'Homme nouveau," in Europe 54, no. 566 [June 1976]: 54; my translation). Cendrars even founded a Sociéte des Amis des Monuments Historiques du Brésil whose mission it was to preserve colonial ruins and tribal artefacts from neglect, destruction, and deportation to foreign museums.
