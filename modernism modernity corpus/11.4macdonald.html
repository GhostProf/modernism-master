The Shanghai Foxtrot (a Fragment)

by Mu Shiying

Introduction and Translation by Sean Macdonald

Introduction: About the Author

Mu Shiying was born in 1912 in the town of Cixi, Zhejiang province, but his family moved to the city of Shanghai when he was young. According to his friend Shi Zhecun, Mu wasn't a very serious university student, and his knowledge of classical Chinese wasn't much better than that of a middle-school student. However, Mu Shiying's first published story in 1930, "Zanmen de shijie" (Our world), was astonishing in its use of working-class slang.1 His first stories were praised by left-leaning critics in China at the time for their use of vernacular. But this assessment would soon be reversed and has given rise to rumors of an ideological shift that occurred in his work around the time of the death of his father in 1933.2

Mu's fiction is sometimes divided between "proletarian" works, where he used mostly first-person narratives in "dialect," and wildly experimental work that used repetition, montage, and interior monologue. However, it would be a mistake to read the divergent views of Mu Shiying's fiction as indications of ideological shifts in the writer. Mu Shiying's fiction is often described as belonging to the "New Sensationist" (Xin ganjuepai) style of writing in China. The name "New Sensationist" was derived from the Shinkankahu ha, a group of Japanese avant-garde writers from the 1920s and1930s that included Yokomitsu Riichi, Kawabata Yasunari, Nakagawa Yoichi, and Kataoka Teppei. However, the evidence is against the actual existence of a group of writers who called themselves "New Sensationist" in China. Indeed, the term "New Sensationist" would seem to have been (and to a certain extent remains) an umbrella term attached to certain forms of experimental writing and writers in China in the 1930s.

Reputed to sit in dance halls with a notepad and pen, Mu attained quasi-celebrity status in Shanghai in the 1930s, marrying a hostess he met at a dance hall in 1936. By the mid-1930s, Mu turned to film criticism, publishing no fiction after 1937. In 1936, as a result of marital problems, Mu left Shanghai for Hong Kong in pursuit of his wife. In 1940, he returned to Shanghai and started working as the manager of the official paper of the collaborationist government of Wang Jingwei.3 That same year, one evening as Mu rode in a ricksha, he was shot from behind and killed by a Guomindang agent. Suspicion has surrounded the incident ever since, with claims that Mu Shiying was a Japanese collaborator or a double agent actually sent by the Nationalists to spy on the collaborationist government. At his death, Mu Shiying left a mark in China and Japan. Shortly after the shooting, the Japanese literary magazine Bungakudai (Literary circles) published a special issue to commemorate Mu's death and included a short article written by Yokomitsu Riichi that recalled a meeting the two had had a year before.4

Mu Shiying's "Shanghai Foxtrot" was first published in Xiandai (Les Contemporains) in 1932.5 Edited chiefly by the writers Shi Zhecun and Du Heng, Les Contemporains ran from 1932 to 1935. A magazine of considerable importance in Shanghai in the 1930s, Les Contemporains published works by most of the major writers of modern Chinese literature from almost every political and aesthetic bent of the period. Running only eight pages in its original form, "Shanghai Foxtrot" has garnered a considerable amount of attention in modern Chinese literature studies. Nevertheless, it would be an exaggeration to say that "Shanghai Foxtrot" is representative of Mu Shiying's oeuvre. Subtitled "a fragment," the story was republished in the 1933 collection Gongmu (Public cemetery), which is made up of mostly first-person narratives.6 In his introduction to the volume, Mu claimed that "Shanghai Foxtrot" was a "technical exercise and experiment" that was to have been part of a longer novel entitled Zhongguo yijiusanyi (China 1931).7 Unfortunately, Mu never got around to writing his novel.

Mu Shiying's fiction is often held up as a significant example of the appearance of urban novelistic prose (dushi xiaoshuo) in modern China. One aspect of "Shanghai Foxtrot" that has fascinated critics is the depiction of the historical, semicolonial metropolis of Shanghai. The Johnny Walker billboard advertisement, the cigarette-smoking American beside a "real-estate utopia," the omnipresent Indian policemen, the churches and racing clubs, the department stores and hotels: such everyday sights of "Old Shanghai" function as specific reminders of the city's status as a treaty port, as a space deterritorialized by foreign extraterritoriality. Mu Shiying's "Shanghai Foxtrot" is truly a modernist gem of description and form, a cinematic arc light illuminating the exoticized fragments of 1930s Shanghai.

Shanghai. A heaven built on hell!8

West Shanghai, bright moon climbing the sky, illuminating the wasted sprawl. Ashen sprawl, blanketed with silver-gray moonlight, then inlaid with deep gray tree shadows and heaps of village shadows. On top of the sprawl, iron rails draw an arc that reaches along the sky and goes down there beneath the horizon.

Lincoln Street. (Here morality gets trampled underfoot, crime gets praised to the skies.)

Carrying a grocery basket, he walks alone there, a hand in his pocket, watches the steam of his breath slowly roil into the blue of the night's sky.

Shadows flash of three men dressed in black silk jackets and wrapped in long black robes.9 Three faces beneath fedoras that only show noses and chins block his way.

"Take it easy, friend!"

"Say what you got to say. Friend."

"Every crime has its criminal, every debt its debtor. It's not like we got anything against you, everybody's got a master, we got to eat too, don't say later we weren't good friends. Remember, this day next year will be the anniversary of your death!"10

"You're joking! Come on guys, we're friends—" Tossing his basket, his hand grabs the speaker's gun, his fist lashes out.

Bang! His hand goes limp, he falls, clutching his stomach. Bang! Another shot.

"This guy's got spunk!"

"See you in the next life, friend!"

The "black silk jackets" push back the fedoras to the napes of their necks, walk over train tracks and disappear.

"Help!" Crawls a few steps.

"Help!" Crawls another few steps.

The roar of a whistle sounds, from beneath water level a trail of arc-light beam reaches up. Rumble of iron rails, railroad ties centipede-like creep forward in the beam, telephone poles appear and disappear in the pitch black, the belly of the "Shanghai Express" shoots out, da da da, to the beat of a foxtrot,11 bearing the night's pearl, dragon-like whipping by, rounding that arc. Lips open again with another whistle blast, a trail of smoke trails down the tail there, the arc-light beam penetrates the horizon, then disappears.

Silence again.

At the railroad crossing, the criss-crossing arc-light beams of cars, the signaler holds downturned red and green banners, pulls up that white-faced red-lipped, red-gem-earring-wearing railroad barrier. Immediately at the crossing's signal the cars fly by, a long line.

A car pulls up in front of a villa-style small foreign house, the car horn beeps. The bead on Mr. Virtuous Liu's skullcap shoots out.13 The tinkling of the links of a gold watch hanging from the two small pockets on his black hemp vest accompanies him out of the car and into the house. He tosses half his cigar outside the entrance, walks into the living room, and, just as he sits down, from the carpet on the stairs the dada sound of the light steps of shoe heels.

"You're back?" a laughing voice asks, as his spouse, by age his daughter-in-law, by law his wife, runs in and tweaks his nose. "Hurry up! Write me a check for three thousand dollars."

"Last week's money already spent?"

Without speaking she hands him a handful of bills, yanks the sleeve of his blue satin jacket, leads him into the study, and puts a pen into his hand.14

"Hey . . ."

"What?" She pouts.

He gives her a look and signs the check. She simply bows her head and gives him a kiss on the lips. "You'll have to eat supper alone tonight, me and Little Virtue are going out!" She smiles and runs out, slamming the door. He takes out a handkerchief with the word TANGEE printed on it and wipes his lips. It's as if I had a daughter, all she ever wants from me is money.15

"Dad!"

He looks up, Little Virtue for who knows how long had been there beside him, staring at him like a rat who'd just seen a cat.

"You back again?"

"Stepmother called me and told me to come back."16

"What for?"

"Money."

Mr. Virtuous Liu thought this was pretty amusing, they really had him over a barrel.

"Why would she ask you to ask me for money? Why doesn't she ask me herself?"

"I want the money. Stepmother asked me to take her out tonight."

Suddenly the door opens, "Don't you have any cash?" His wife Liu Yan Rongzhu runs in again.

"I've only got . . ."

A just-nail-polished little hand reaches into his pocket and takes out a leather wallet! Ruddy nails count out the bills: five, ten, twenty . . . three hundred. "You get fifty. If I give you more you'll stay out all night again." She gives him a cute look, takes her legal son's arm and out they go.

The son, a clotheshorse day in and day out reading magazines for the GIGOLO, slips on a creased, shiny, freshly pressed robe, necktie whorled in the middle, takes his mother by the arm and gets into the car.

Onto a street lined with legs of white-painted trees, legs of telephone poles, all still-life legs . . .REVUE-like, heavily powdered thighs of young women intersecting and extending . . . white-painted legs in formation. Along that quiet still boulevard, from within the windows of houses, like the eyeballs of the city, piercing curtains, stealing out muted red, purple, green, virginal lights.

They're driving a brand-new 1932 Buick, but at heart they're having a 1980 love affair. A late autumn night wind blows, blows the son's collar, the mother's hair, everything feels a little chilly. The legal mother nuzzles into her son's chest and says:

"What a shame you're my son." Teehee giggling.

The son plants a kiss on his mother's tiny just-kissed-by-his-father lips and almost swerves onto the sidewalk.

NEON LIGHT reaches colored fingers writing large words in the blue ink of night's void. An English gentleman stands there, wearing coattails, carrying a cane under his arm, walking with a lively stride. Written underneath his feet: "JOHNNY WALKER STILL GOING STRONG." At the roadside the real-estate utopia of a small grass lot, above it a Chesterfield-smoking American looks on as if to say "What a shame this lilliputian utopia; is that big lot too small for me to put my foot down in?"

A re appears in front of the car, the horn honks, the pedestrian turns back to look and slips out from the front of the car and onto the sidewalk.

"Rongzhu, where do you want to go?"

"Any CABARET will do. Let's try someplace new. Licha, Dahua, I'm already bored with them."

On top of the Shanghai Racing Club track, the weather vane's golden horse kicks out four hooves toward the red moon. The grass racing track surrounded by an overflowing sea of light, waves of sin, Moore Memorial Church penetrates the darkness, kneels, prays for these men and women descended to hell. Radiating circles of light, the Great World spire, refusing all repentance, glances down condescendingly at the pedantic missionary.17

Azure dusk envelopes the space completely, a SAXOPHONE reaches its neck, opens up a pair of lips, whoowhoo pounds them shouting. The smell of alcohol, smell of perfume, smell of English ham and eggs, smell of cigarettes . . . singles sit in corners taking hits of black coffee to shock their nerves.

Dancing: a waltz melody spins their legs, their feet stand atop a waltz melody floating, floating.

The son leans into his mother's ear: "Some things can only be said when dancing a waltz, you're such a great waltz partner—but, Rongzhu, I love you!"

Feeling a gentle kiss on the temple, the mother shyly cuddles into her son's chest, laughing low.

A fake French gentleman Belgian jewel broker leans into movie star Yin Furong's ear: "The smile on your lips would make all the women in the world jealous—but, I love you!"

Feeling a gentle kiss on the temple, she shyly cuddles into his chest, laughing low, suddenly notices a new diamond ring on her finger.

The jewel broker sees Liu Yan Rongzhu and nods to her over Yin Furong's shoulder, gives her a smile. Little Virtue turns back and notices Yin Furong and GIGOLO-like raises an eyebrow in her direction.

Dancing: a waltz melody spins their legs, their feet stand atop a waltz melody floating, floating.

Blue-eyed girls in tight skirts, dark-eyed girls in cheongsams, between their thighs the same servile charm.

Beside the street, in an empty lot, a wooden pyramid-like structure stands vertically, thick wooden legs in mud, an arc light fitted in the top, light falling, illuminating the people on the wooden platform below. They shout "Aiya!" From atop a wooden frame several hundred meters high a wooden pile driver falls straight down, bang! Three arm's-length-thick wooden posts hammered into the mud, all four corners fitted with arc lights, violently expanding light illuminating the empty lot. A new dance hall arises! A new hotel arises! Take his strength, take his blood, take his life crushed underneath, just like other hotels, just like the Huadong hotel Mr. Virtuous Liu is striding into.

26. The "zhong tile" is a tile from the Dragon series in mah-jongg. The character zhong roughly translates as "center" or "middle." Since there are only four of these tiles, Liu would have difficulty finding a match.

27. Sullivan refers to a Shanghai bakery/coffee shop that often appears in the prose of the period.

28. The publications enumerated here, and a few lines later, were well-known journals of the period.

29. "Humanitarianism" (Rendao zhuyi), sometimes translated as "humanism." The history of this term in modern China is quite complex. Mu Shiying's use refers to the ideologically charged debates occurring in the 1930s in which leftist critics attacked the (as they saw it) inherently bourgeois implications of particular forms of writing.
