Ornament, Gender, and Interiority in Viennese Expressionism

Sherwin Simmons

[res]

In January 1914 Arthur Roeßler's article "Women and Art" appeared in the Stickerei-Zeitung und Spitzen Revue, a journal devoted to textile art in Germany and Austria. While the embroidery of Emmy Zweybrück ( 1), a leading textile artist in Vienna, was ostensibly his subject, Roeßler used the opportunity to secure women's creative activities firmly within Nadelkunst and the domestic sphere. He concluded that her embroideries deserve more praise than any "square meter of painted canvas that any woman aspiring to 'high' art places on view." 1 Stating that the best women's painting could only be an inadequate reflection of a male model, he argued that female talents lay instead in decoration, creations that were part of women's being, only hinting at an unknowable mystery beneath the ornamented surface. This characterization of ornament's superficiality contrasts with those artistic values he praised in the work of young male expressionist painters who were "able to turn the interior of people inside out" during the same period. 2 Artists such as Egon Schiele defined an approach to painting that surpassed nature and became the apex of human achievement. Roeßler suggested that these artists recovered art's high calling, which had been lost through a recent preoccupation with applied art: "They want to move away from applied art, which Klimt approached happily and frequently, sometimes even at the cost of the psychological achievement of an aesthetic creation. All works for them are true works of new art, are storage batteries of psychological forces." 3

The contrast between ornament and psychological expression, between surface and depth, used by art critics of the [Begin Page 247] period to characterize the stylistic shift from Gustav Klimt's aestheticism to Schiele's expressionism, continues to be of use for thinking about Viennese modernism. 4 The discursive interplay between ornamentalism and asceticism yields insights about gender's role in the divide between these attitudes towards form. 5 Studies of the writings of Karl Kraus and Adolf Loos show how their work contributed to a critique of ornament grounded, in part, in responses to women's changing roles in the public sphere. 6 Fashion's expansion stimulated new perceptions about women's increasing participation in the modern world. These perceptions provoked male anxieties about fashion's impact on culture and the arts at the same time as female artists in Vienna began to organize and assert their presence in both the fine and applied arts. 7

Roeßler's celebrations of a new textile art and a new painting were intertwined, both being part of an effort to draw gender distinctions in a world where boundaries were becoming blurred. Too little critical attention has been afforded the emergence of an artistic style, linked to folk art and characterized by terms such as "primitive" and "expressionist," within the Wiener Werkstätte between 1905 and 1908. Women artists played a role at least equal to that of men in the development of this style, since its impetus came from textiles and it was quickly applied to Vienna's emerging fashion industry. The discourse surrounding that ornamental style infiltrated discussions about the art produced by women. Young male artists, such as Kokoschka and Schiele, had early ties to this initial "primitive" style and its application to fashion, but distanced themselves during 1909 and 1910 by developing a competing discourse about interiority that found expression primarily in their treatment of skin.

Ornament, the focus of many debates about historical styles during the nineteenth century in Vienna, continued to be an important issue during the years before World War I. Otto Wagner had questioned the suitability of using historical architectural ornament to dress contemporary buildings during the 1890s, calling for a "raiment of modernity" equally effective in expressing both the structural and symbolic character of the new architecture. 8 Similar desires for a modern style within painting and sculpture led to the formation of the Vienna Secession in 1897, whose frequent exhibitions gave the new artistic movement a significant public forum. Appointments of Secession members to teaching positions in the School of Applied Arts and the creation of the Wiener Werkstätte in 1903 signaled that the battle to replace historical ornament with a modern style in Vienna had been won and efforts to extend its influence and reap the financial rewards of international trade were well under way. Writing in Die Flache, a luxurious new journal devoted to ornament, Joseph August Lux claimed that "[t]he growing understanding of decorative form and color is the result of the Viennese School. People, who have been accustomed for decades to obtain their models from Paris, already today demand the 'Viennese Style.'" 9 This style initially mixed a study of nature and artistic materials, typical of other currents of "art nouveau," with elements selected from the ancient cultures of Greece and Byzantium. Between 1905 and 1908, however, decorative elements from the folk cultures within Austria-Hungary became decisive elements of the style. Exhibitions at the Museum of Art and Industry had drawn attention to folk art during the 1880s, leading to the founding of the Association of Austrian Folklore in 1894. The Association envisioned the study of folk art as an important contribution to the creation of an Austrian political identity based on cosmopolitan multiculturalism. 10 Built on ideas advanced by Crown Prince Rudolph in the 1880s, this political ideal hoped to demonstrate the benevolently unifying role that the state could play vis à vis the multitude of nationalities that made up the Empire.

Two events contributed significantly to folk art's impact on modern Viennese ornamental style--the Exhibition of Austrian Domestic Crafts and Folk Art of 1905 and the Imperial Jubilee Procession of 1908. The Museum of Art and Industry spent ten years organizing the exhibition, which ran from November 1905 to February 1906 and assembled over 4,000 examples of popular handicraft. An extensive catalogue contained essays describing regional traditions of costume and art. Looking at the province of Bukowina on the Empire's distant eastern border, the catalogue provided a telling description of clothing decoration:

Nature, with its infinite riches of leaves, flowers and blooms, birds, beetles, butterflies, and small animals, which we find more or less stylized in embroideries or weavings, offers the subjects, some of them copied from old embroideries that mother and grandmother completed sometime before, however, others also newly created from individual feelings. The seven or eight year old girl, who is already trusted with the care of geese and sheep in the pasture, sits there, linen and needle in hand, embroidering without model or pattern. 11

Ludwig Hevesi, a supporter of the Secession, wrote that he found "dialects of the pure language" in the exhibition catalogue and praised the colors "at the disposal of these primitives: the peasant wives of Czechoslovakia, Ruthenia, Rumania, Croatia. . . ." 12

The exhibition's examples of folk costume and ornament were useful in designing the Imperial Jubilee Procession. Held on 12 June 1908 to mark the sixtieth anniversary of Emperor Franz Joseph I's reign, the procession was witnessed by upwards of 500,000 citizens and tourists. It was divided into one section composed of a series of groups, costumed by prominent Viennese artists, representing historical periods of Hapsburg rule, and another in which the Empire's diverse cultures, resplendent in their traditional costumes, paraded before the public. 13 The latter was a great hit with Lux, who contrasted it to the historical section by describing the costume parade as being

no longer history, no longer mere masquerade, but folk art, which still in large part at least serves life. A peasant woman with her grand finery, laces, embroideries, metal jewelry, stands there like an inimitable work of art, in a colorfulness like a small peasant garden. . . . We must be thankful for this experience. It was a beauty that one can experience nowhere but in Austria. 14

Like other supporters of the modern Viennese style, however, Lux took particular pleasure in one group in the historical section. Bertold Löffler designed Group XIII to represent a harvest festival during the reign of Joseph II (1765-1790). Löffler, who had been appointed to teach a master class for decorative painting and graphic art at the School of Applied Arts in Fall 1907, involved his students in the design of the costumes and paraphernalia. Although well researched, the costumes were seen anew with modern eyes and commentaries remarked on similarities to the Secession's aesthetic. Löffler and his students, many of whom acted the parts of eighteenth-century peasants in the parade, created an idyllic vision of folk art under a benevolent and enlightened monarch. Group XIII reified the ideological construct of the Jubilee Procession--an optimistic vision of a multicultural nation under the beneficent stewardship of Emperor Franz Joseph. Costume designs done by Löffler and his students for the procession were later printed in the series of Wiener Werkstätte postcards.

Taken together, the exhibition and procession encouraged a shift of style from "tectonic simplicity" to "elaborate, dense surface decoration" within the Wiener Werkstätte. 15 Folk pottery certainly influenced Wiener Keramik, the ceramics firm that Löffler and Michael Powolny formed in 1905 that produced the colorful tiles proclaiming the new stylistic direction at the Cabaret Fledermaus. Folk textiles, however, had the greatest impact on the Werkstätte's new decorative style, a point made by Paul Westheim in response to an exhibition of Hungarian textiles:

These works of the people are the root for a good part of all the textiles and graphics of the young artists in Vienna. The surface patterns of Margold, Wimmer, Zainer, etc. can be traced back without difficulty to such stimuli from Bosnia. Only this folk art was stripped of its naïveté, of the original liveliness, the entirety pervaded by a shot of fashionable sensuality, cultivated perversity, the unbroken colors sacrificed to a feminine, elegant, perfumed sense of color and finished with that gracefulness that pleases the delicate nerves of Europa. 16

The artists Westheim mentions are men, but he describes their style in fully feminine terms. Emmanual Margold and Eduard Josef Wimmer-Wisgrill did, in fact, work closely with the large numbers of women who sought careers in the applied arts after the turn of the century. Graduates of the School of Applied Arts, the Art School for Women and Girls, and the Royal Art Embroidery Schools responded enthusiastically to folk art and helped define the new ornamental style.

Zweybrück, who had studied with Joseph Hoffmann and Franz Cizek, was only one of many who joined the Wiener Werkstätte, regularly showed embroideries at the Museum of Art and Industry's exhibitions, formed co-operatives to advance sales, and became known outside Austria-Hungary. 17 Using thread and cloth produced by modern industry, their artistic imaginations and highly-skilled hands sought to invest the objects they produced with older as well as newer values. While remaining within the modern fashion system, artists and consumers were drawn to the values of community and social reproduction that folk art embodied. The popularity of the printed silks, beaded bags, and embroidered blouses and shawls that they created contributed to the opening of the Wiener Werkstätte fashion department in March 1911, headed by Wimmer-Wisgrill. Women were the majority of designers and assistants. Through the course of the century's second decade, it became the Werkstätte's most economically successful and well-known unit; its products were celebrated at the 1914 Werkbund Exhibition in Cologne and in exhibitions devoted to Viennese fashion in Berlin.

Wimmer-Wisgrill designed the interior of the Werkstätte's room in the Austrian pavilion at the exhibition in Cologne, covering an entire lower wall with a grid of fashion illustrations. Over the next year, a series of fashion illustrations was published in twelve porfolios of twelve prints each as Mode Wien 1914/15. 18 Female artists produced 111 of the 144 original prints, while the inclusion of seven prints by Dagobert Peche rendered him the best represented male artist. 19 His hand-colored linoleum cut ( 2), which appeared in the first portfolio, emphasized the block printed fabrics that had developed from folk textiles and become one of the Werkstätte's most popular products before World War I. The dress's fabric is covered with large clusters of flowers and leaves, a vegetative motif that extends to the re's frame which includes branches of laurel. Similar leaves sprout from the model's head, her fingers resemble tendrils, and branches grow from the dress's sleeves, motifs that recall the myth of Apollo and Daphne.

The mythic suggestion of Peche's image differs from representations of fashionable women in the prints by other artists, such as by Maria Likarz or Lotte Calm, which depict modern scenes of athletic competition and shopping. Since Peche became the artist most closely associated with the Werkstätte's ornamental style during the 1910s and 1920s, his repeated use of Daphne's image bears investigation. 20 His most likely source was Ovid's Metamorphoses, wherein Apollo, the heroic warrior-diety, is struck by Cupid's golden arrow and falls in love with Daphne, a beautiful daughter of the river god Peneus and Gaea, the Earth goddess. 21 His love rejected by the chaste nymph, Apollo turns into a hound inflamed by passion and chases after Daphne to rape her. She escapes by praying to her father, who causes her to metamorphose into a laurel tree: "Her hair was changed to leaves, her arms to branches. Her feet, but now so swift, grew fast into sluggish roots, and her head was now but a tree's top. Her gleaming beauty alone remained" (MAD 35). Embracing and kissing the laurel tree, Apollo then proclaims, "Since you cannot be my bride, you shall at least be my tree. . . . My hair, my lyre, my quiver shall always be entwined with you, O laurel. With you shall Roman generals wreathe their heads, when shouts of joy shall acclaim their triumph, and long processions climb the Capitol" (MAD 41). In designs, sculpture, drawings, and five letters written in 1915-6, Peche treated this metamorphosis. 22 His repeated association of the female body with vegetative life unites women, nature, and ornament, in some cases even inscribing leaves and flowers on drawn female nudes as if they were tattooed on the skin. 23 Such images came to embody the Viennese culture of fashion and the discursive association of ornament and femininity during the 1910s.

Klimt's portraits of members of the Werkstätte's clientele, as in his painting of Eugenie Primavesi ( 3), captures well the culture inspired by the Werkstätte's fashion department. Eugenie and her husband Otto, who came from a prominent Moravian family of bankers and industrialists, were among the most active customers and even acquired a 30 percent interest in the company when it underwent economic reorganization in 1914. In 1913 Josef Hoffmann began designing their country villa near [Begin Page 252] Winkelsdorf in Czechoslovakia. Constructed of logs on a rubble stone socle and capped by a thickly thatched roof, the villa utilized materials of local vernacular architecture. Its plan and loggia with gigantic oak columns, however, were Palladian; and the inclusion of a water turbine to generate electricity, tennis courts, and swimming pool made the villa an exemplary modern building in folk style. 24 The interiors were richly painted with Moravian folk motifs, while the wallpapers, curtains, and upholstery were hand-printed designs from the Wiener Werkstätte. Visiting guests were provided with loose gowns to wear during their stay, each selecting from a stock of garments sewn from variously patterned Werkstätte silk fabrics. Eugenie is clothed in such a gown in Klimt's portrait, its colorful pattern spilling into the painting's field. The villa and painting embody what the initial enthusiasm for folk art had become in the hands of Wiener Werkstätte artists and patrons by the 1910s.

The fashion department had an audience far beyond such wealthy patrons. Over 70,000 visitors attended a fashion exhibition in Vienna that opened on 16 December 1915 and focused primarily on the Wiener Werkstätte. 25 Max Eisler believed that this exhibition confirmed fashion's special character in Vienna, its emphasis on unique artistry both in the dresses' forms and in details such as hand embroidery. These qualities linked it, he said, to the Werkstätte's previous applied art. 26 The exhibition also prompted ten female artists within the fashion department to create a portfolio of twenty colored linoleum cuts entitled Life of a Lady which emphasized fashion's role in modern life by following a fashionable woman from hat shop to bath to the sales rooms of the Austrian Werkbund. Such images strengthened the perception, already expressed by Westheim, that Viennese ornament and applied arts were intensely feminine.

This perception was so strong that when Johanna Meier-Michel designed a poster to advertise "Art of the Woman" ( 4) for the first exhibition of paintings and sculptures organized by the Association of Austrian Female Visual Artists, her image referred to the bright stylized flowers of the new graphic design and embroidery rather than the "fine arts." 27 The exhibition's 315 works constituted the first major survey of both past women artists and contemporary production in Europe. Visitors were numerous and sales strong at this and the group's second exhibition, indications of women's growing participation in the world of visual culture. Martha Hofrichter's cover for the second catalogue, an image of female wooden dolls with painted aprons simulating embroidery, added children to the exhibition's frame of reference. 28 Indeed, children's art was closely linked with folk art, a fact made clear by the following comment about the children's art room at the 1908 Kunstschau--"Primitive folk art, lamented by many as lost, blooms here ever anew." 29 Contemporary embroideries, described as "expressionist orgies of color," were seen as being equally primitive in feeling. 30 Helene Funke's painting Hungarian Girl ( 5), shown at the association's 1911 exhibition, joined youth, femaleness, and folk embroidery in a single work which one critic termed "ghastly Van Goghism" connected with the new trends of Neoprimitivism and Expressionism. 31 Roeßler declared Funke to be one of the exhibition's stronger painters, but added that such painting by a woman was always second-hand creation. Young male artists, such as Kokoschka and Schiele, were in his estimation redefining painting as a creative [Begin Page 254] activity surpassing nature. But women were excluded from this level of achievement, because, he concluded, "as long as art arises from stress and compulsion, which are specific to a male spirit and disposition," fine art would remain "'a necessary product of male particularism,' the effort for universal synthesis." 32

Roeßler's comment about Kokoschka's and Schiele's efforts to distance themselves from applied art was an observation about their origins. Kokoschka first came to public attention as a student in Löffler's class at the School of Applied Arts where he participated in graphic design projects for the Wiener Werkstätte. Dancer in a Meadow ( 6), one of his postcard designs from fall 1907 anticipated Löffler's contributions to the Jubilee Procession. 33 The girl's beflowered, wide-brimmed straw hat and her skirt trimmed with lace and embroidery are similar to the idyllic peasant world evoked by Löffler's group's designs. 34 While other of Kokoschka's postcards represent aspects of the new textile arts, three large cartoons called Bearers of Dreams (one major project shown at the 1908 Kunstschau) were designed to be executed as textiles. Several sources suggest that he considered the cartoons to be studies toward six embroidered panels or tapestries for the Palais Stoclet's ladies' sitting room. In the end, however, these designs, described as "having the character of naive folk art," were replaced by Carl Czeschka's wall decorations. 35

While Kokoschka's early work was related to the new decorative style, when his cartoons and children's book called Dreaming Boys--both published by the Wiener Werkstätte--were shown at the 1908 Kunstschau, their highly personal psychological characters were quickly remarked. 36 Unlike those whose books were based on existing fairy-tales and stories, illustrated by Secession members, and published by Martin Gerlach in a series for children, Kokoschka composed his own text and produced images better described as evocative than illustrative. Max Mell, whom Kokoschka consulted about the book, noted its chaotic quality and described it as a serious artistic probing of puberty. About the isolated adolescent girl and boy in the final plate, he wrote, "Between these poles of puberty, the holy lack of desire and the lust for life, lie anxiety, horrible secrecy, lust for adventure, the idyllic." 37 In his autobiography, Kokoschka refers to the book as his first love letter to Lilith, a young Swede who had already disappeared from his life when the book appeared. 38 But Li, the girl associated with "the bird-woods of the north" in the story, was, in fact, Lilith Lang, a sixteen-year-old Austrian student at the School of Applied Arts (DjK 260). She appears in the story after the narrator, growing aware of his sexuality and approaching adulthood, dreams of adventures in exotic worlds, of becoming a werewolf who invades the sites and bodies of conventional society. While the narrator dances "the desires of the sexes" in a garden, Li steps "from the festoons of vermilion flowers and sulfurous yellow stars." 39 Meeting her, he feels that he has already known her body's angular gesture and has expected her arrival at his silver bedspread. The pleasures of sexual desire are evoked, along with unanswered declarations of love and feelings of possessiveness-- [Begin Page 256]

After the relationship cools, the story ends with a reflection about his changed state: "I was a stumbler / when I discovered my flesh / and a lover of everything / when I spoke with a girl" (OKS 120).

Kokoschka's letters to Erwin Lang, Lilith's brother, express his bitterness over the relationship's breakup during the winter of 1907-8 (OKL 16). Speculation about this breakup and its impact is difficult, but it seems likely that a marriage proposal coupled with intense possessiveness might have been resisted by a young woman with close connections to the feminist movement in Austria. 40 Li's association with embroidery and lace in the story is related to the narrator's ability to see her as no one else could, his revelation of her form and character understood as a demonstration of love and entitlement to her. This passage recalls Kokoschka's sketches of Lang's naked body in angular poses and in motion, drawings that contributed to the book's final plate and were probably shown at the 1908 Kunstschau. These drawings are arguably his first works that focus on psychological expression, a direction that Mell encouraged, urging him to consider the "noble task" of portraiture, "to uncover the essence of a person's face" ("CK" 252).

During 1908 and 1909, after meeting Karl Kraus and Adolf Loos, Kokoschka began to distance himself from the production of applied art. Loos, impressed by his pieces in the 1908 Kunstschau, recalled that

he was employed by the Wiener Werkstätte and was occupied with the painting of fans, drawing postcards and similar things in the German fashion of art in the service of the businessman. It was immediately clear to me that one of the greatest crimes against the Holy Spirit was being committed here. 41

This clarity formed as part of a development of ideas eventually expressed in "Ornament and Crime," a lecture of 1910. 42 Ideas that originated in his writings at the turn of the century became publicity-seeking polemic as he condemned the Jubilee Procession for its display of ornament, calling its participants "tribes" of a pagan past who made manifest the Empire's backwardness. America and England, where efficient and economical functionalism dictated the abandonment of ornament, were his models of modernity, while Papuans with tattooed faces and elaborately ornamented articles of daily use represented modernism's Other. Drawing from the writings of Cesare Lombroso and various ethnographers, Loos rendered the tattoo a topos of everything uncivilized and backward, an embodiment of erotic and violent origins that modern society was compelled to suppress. In this, he revived a trope employed previously in an 1898 essay on women's fashion in which he declared, "The lower the culture, the more apparent the ornament. Ornament is something that must be overcome. The Papuan and the criminal ornament their skin. . . . But the bicycle and the steam engine are free of ornament. The march of civilization systematically liberates object after object from ornamentation." 43 The article decried fashion as outdated ornament that bore witness to women's subjugated condition. While men had largely abandoned ornamental dress in the nineteenth century, Loos asserted that women's fashion began to change rapidly as more novelty became necessary to arouse desire in men and draw them into the marriages that give women social status.

This article, as well as others by Loos, extolled the functionality and quality of English men's clothing, opposing that restrained and seemingly timeless style both to current Parisian fashion and efforts by artists to reform fashion. Rather than dress becoming expressive of individuality, he believed that modern individuals should adopt a neutral mask to protect the self. He addressed his ideas to the "aristocrats of the spirit" who would allow "the Slovak farmer's wife who embroiders her lace" to continue to derive pleasure from her work, for she knew no better culture. Yet judging from his own interiors, such an aristocrat could nevertheless enjoy a quality Persian carpet in his modern apartment. In light of this seeming contradiction Loos concludes that "he who goes to listen to the Ninth Symphony and who then sits down to draw up a wallpaper pattern, is either a rogue or a degenerate."

Kokoschka's suspicions about fashion's inauthenticity in the modern world are elaborated in a wry letter sent to Erwin Lang in the winter of 1907-8, in which he explains,

175-186 cm above the ground, hats go for walks, and clothes 15 cm lower, falling in folds as if they were alive, and everywhere all the folds are governed by the same monotonous theatrical law. . . . I shall buy myself a false red beard to put between the hat and clothes, so as to disrupt this theatrical pageant of folds. [OKL 16] 44

His use of ornament certainly changed with his increased contact with Loos, as is demonstrated by an analysis of the mother and child motif that he obsessively drew during 1908-9. While some fantastic scenes do employ heavy gestural marks across the paper's surface deriving from van Gogh's reed pen drawings, others are studies from models. The res' embroidered garments in several drawings seem worn, like the bodies of their wearers, peasants drawn away from their rural past and proletarianized by the modern city. Simultaneously, however, another type of ornament, the tattoo, began to fascinate Kokoschka. 45 Speaking of his initial preference for the Museum of Natural History over the Museum of Art History, he claimed he could grasp the import of "a Polynesian mask with its incised tattooing . . . at once, because I could feel my own facial nerves reacting to cold and hunger in the same way," but concludes that "For all my sympathy with primitive art, it would not have occurred to me to imitate it. I was not a savage. I would have had to live like them for my imitation to be genuine" (ML 19-20). Yet a connection of such material to his art was noted by several critics at the time, as when one described the sea and cliffs represented in The Bearers of Dreams as looking as if they were "tattooed with strange plants and animals" ("CK" 250). Another described Warrior (6, a painted clay self-portrait shown at the 1909 Kunstschau and purchased by Loos, as having the "grimace of a New Zealand fetish." 46 Indeed Kokoschka would have been familiar with the Museum of Natural History's rich collection of Oceanic art, including numerous Polynesian masks. 47 However, human skulls, overmodelled with clay to restore facial forms and then painted with decorative patterns, were also an important component of the collection. An extensive group of these had been collected by the Mission House St. Gabriel and Dr. Rudolf Pöch from the Sepik River area of Papua-New Guinea. 48 Dr. Pöch lectured about his expedition and the objects collected after they were given to the museum in 1907. 49 He stressed that Papuans believed that the head was rich in mana (spirit), and this was why skulls taken in warfare and those of important clan members were preserved. The taking of the first head was an important step in a boy's rise to manhood, for skulls were tokens of bravery and transmitted power to the victorious warrior.

Kokokschka's Warrior was likely inspired by such material. Its unfired clay surface is heavily worked, with raised and incised lines tinted blue and red suggesting the coursing of blood beneath the skin. The eyes, with blue irises, framed by the socket's reddish ochre surface, appear frozen in an eternal stare. The mouth gapes like a wound below. Supported on a long neck and tilted slightly upwards as if in awe or startled reaction, the head resembles a type of overmodeled skull ( 8) often mounted atop a woven basket mask, called the big-Abwan. 50 These skulls, overmodeled to make the face long and thin with a huge nose, also rise as if on a long neck above the basket's body, which conceals the wearer's torso and head. Painted patterns swirl across the face's surface and help to emphasize the eyes, nostrils, and mouth. Kokoschka appears to have imitated the abstract patterns in indices of skin wrinkles, blood vessels, and nerves. While the form was converted to a more naturalistic Western idiom, he clearly hoped to retain a sense of ritual and myth. Many years later, in the context of a story about the horrors of World War I, he reacted with anger when a lover showed him the death mask of her former husband. "It may be that this," he wrote,

was why from very ancient times people have modeled the faces of their dead--to keep death away. Perhaps it began with painting the chief's dead face with red ochre, the color of life. Later they covered the skull with gold leaf, which was more resistant to the passage of time. There was the mask of Agamemnon from the tomb at Mycenae. 51

In the same story he also discusses his own fascination with prehistoric skulls found in natural history museums. He attributed the holes that are often bored in them to early man's practice of headhunting to gain nourishment from the brain of his victim, explaining that the practice's cessation was an important step in "man's evolution from a cannibal to a paterfamilias" (SR 35). But for modern man, Kokoschka claims, "the terror has crawled away down into our subconscious. It is in the unconscious realm that we work off our bad conscience, like our pussy-cat decently covering up her excrement in the sand-box provided" (ibid.). With the title Warrior, Kokoschka sought to associate his self-portrait with the terrors of combat and rituals that instilled and maintained gendered power differences. 52 Through his effort to turn ancient ornament into blood vessels and nerves, he simultaneously declares his modernity and critiques fine art's emphasis on an intense interior life, a quality that many critics maintained was lacking in contemporary, woman-centered ornament and applied art.

The exhibition of Warrior under a glass cover at the Kunstschau imbued it with an exotic otherness, leading critics to invoke the ethnographic museum and Paul Gauguin in their responses to it. 53 A large exhibition of Gauguin's work at the Miethke Gallery in 1907 played a role in Kokoschka's growing desire to escape to other worlds. 54 Since European longing for escape had been constructed first by Daniel Defoe's Robinson Crusoe , it is perhaps not surprising that during 1908-9 Kokoschka undertook a set of illustrations for that novel. When they were exhibited at the 1909 Kunstschau, Ludwig Erik Tesar, an art critic who had previously published a German translation of the novel, responded enthusiastically,

it's real illustration, not the type that chatters tactlessly about the story, but the type that picks out and emphasizes crucial sentences and words, actions and feelings. The personal note of the illustrator lies in the selection of significant passages, his greatness in the artistic independence with which he strengthens the poetic through a corresponding artistic symbolism. 55

Comparisons with previous illustrations for Crusoe confirm the originality of Kokoschka's approach and suggest why Karl Kraus felt compelled to advertise--without success--for publication funds in the pages of Die Fackel. While a contemporary study of the novel's place in the history of children's literature recommended its aesthetic and pedagogical value as a counter to the sensationalism and sensuality of pulp adventure stories, one doubts that the author would have found the same values in Kokoschka's illustrations. 56 Gratuitous violence, a quality that educational reformers decried in colportage literature and films of the period, seems to break out, for instance, in Kokoschka's Man Running Amok ( 9). The drawing depicts an episode in Madagascar after Crusoe was rescued from the Caribbean island where he had been marooned while sailing to Africa to buy slaves for his Brazilian plantation. After a sailor raped a native girl, the natives attacked the trading party, killing some and forcing the remainder back to ship. A group of sailors, against Crusoe's advice, then armed themselves for reprisal and proceeded to torch all the huts and slaughter over 150 inhabitants. Crusoe, however, recounts that he mastered his rage and refrained from slaughter, and it is, perhaps, his silhouette at lower right, a passive spectator before the blazing spectacle of something like a cinema screen. Tattoos on his arm indicate the murderous sailor: amidst traces of veins are an anchor, pierced heart, and the initials O. K. and L. L., which effectively insert Kokoschka's failed relationship with Lilith Lang into the image. The motifs are typical of sailors' tattoos, while the use of initials points to the practice's rebirth in European culture when sailors on James Cook's voyages had their initials tattooed on their arms in Polynesia. 57

Polynesian tattoos may have played some role in Kokoschka's thinking about the costumes and drawings for his play Murderer, Hope of Women. 58 The graphic marks demand attention in his image of "Man" subduing "Woman," whose "graphic spontaneity may be read as an analog for the violence of the play itself ( 10)." 59 The marks originate in observation of anatomy and drapery, but clearly exceed that representational code to become signs in themselves. As seen in a drawing from Augustin Krämer's 1902 study of Samoan culture ( 11), tattoos often had a mimetic relationship not only to surface anatomy, but revealed the interior on the skin surface. But these relationships were exceeded by development of meanings within the cultural system of marking. While Kokoschka's marks do evoke "nerve lines, muscles and tendons," as he says, they are also loaded with meanings related to the discourses about the tattoo in 'primitive' cultures. The initials O. K. on the murderous sailor's arm--like his shaved head--also secure an identification with European sub-groups such as imprisoned criminals, an interest that is also briefly visible in his drawings for Der Sturm during 1910 of circus and variety performers, who are also tattooed.

Egon Schiele, although educated at the Academy of Fine Art rather than the School of Applied Art, began his career with a close relationship to the Wiener Werkstätte. A group of four early works, including Portrait of a Woman with Black Hat (Gertrude Schiele) ( 12), that he exhibited at the 1909 Kunstschau were strongly influenced by Klimt's decorative portraits of women. 60 Klimt personally invited his participation in the 1909 Kunstschau and, at the end of the year, secured a position for him in the Wiener Werkstätte where he briefly designed men's and women's fashions as well as postcards that treated the theme of fashion. 61 Schiele's sister Melanie, who designed many of her own clothes, supplied the hat and ermine neck wrap worn by their sister Gertrude in the 1909 portrait, along with the decorative coverlet that she holds in her hands. The portrait's background, like Schiele's other early works, was originally executed in metallic paint in an effort cheaply to emulate Klimt's application of gold and silver leaf to his paintings' surfaces. However, at some point he painted this background over with white paint, lending greater emphasis to the re's psychological presence. The image of a young woman fashionably dressed for the street, seeming to show a decoratively patterned fabric to the viewer, nevertheless remains strongly related to the Werkstätte--an observation reinforced by the fact that Schiele subsequently obtained a modeling job for Gertrude in the Werkstätte's fashion department.

It was thus predominantly issues of fashion and clothing (rather than ornamental pattern and Polynesian tattooing as with Kokoschka) that helped to frame Schiele's preoccupation with issues of interiority during 1909-11. The sole exception from that period is found in a re study of a dancer who partnered with Erwin Osen, a painter friend of Schiele's. Why she assumed the stage name "Moa"--also the name of New Zealand's large, extinct, flightless bird--is unknown, but her thick mane of black hair certainly resembled that of Polynesian women in photographs taken for anthropological and colonialist texts. A painting on paper of Moa from 1911 ( 13) demonstrates how Schiele's re studies revolve around the tension between clothing and body. If a nude is defined as an idealized set of artistic conventions--harmonious pose, adjusted proportions, excision of body hair and imperfections, lack of clothes constructed as a "natural" state--then Schiele's res are better described as naked. This was a recognition on his part akin to J. C. Flügel's observation in 1930 that "Clothes, though seemingly mere extraneous appendages, have entered into the very core of our existence as social beings," and further "of all the motives for the wearing of clothes, those connected with the sexual life have an altogether predominant position." 62

One of the most interesting aspects relating Schiele's work to fashion is its role in increasing sexual allusion in fashion illustration. In the initial Wiener Werkstätte fashion postcards of 1911 Mela Köhler stylized fashion's forms, but the illustration remained essentially functional, concerned with showing the dress well in an environment that lent appropriate prestige. 63 Otto Lendecke had learned a similar approach through his work for Paul Poiret in Paris, but a drawing done in Vienna around 1912 evidences a new quality. 64 The re strikes a pose of sexual solicitation against a void in order to highlight the slits in the blue overdress. It is difficult to explain other than as a response to Schiele's works, which were known in the fashion department since Wimmer-Wisgrill had become one of the artist's collectors. 65 In many of Schiele's works similar poses are struck which also stretch to the field's edge. Raised elbow and revealed axillar hair in Lendecke's drawing suggest the exposed pubes in Schiele's explicitly erotic drawings, the trajectory of both images pointing to the future sexual dance of fashion photography that Michelangelo Antonioni staged so memorably in the English film Blow Up . 66

Clothing's role in social construction and its motivation in sexual expression are seen not only in Schiele's studies of women and himself, but also in those of the painter Max Oppenheimer. Between 1908 and 1911 Oppenheimer moved his work in a direction similar to that of Kokoschka and Schiele; his anti-ornamental sentiment is reflected in a statement made to Roeßler that "The decorative is the most primitive form of painterly expression. It will always be the hobby horse of those who enjoy ordinary coarse sensations and are unable to have a feeling for anything but the 'applied.'" 67 Roeßler's description of Oppenheimer's appearance as making "him stand out at the first glance with his somewhat idiosyncratic but always distinguished clothing" could also be applied to other writers and artists--like Kokoschka and Schiele--who were turning from aestheticism to asceticism. 68 Recognizing that their life and art needed to be on display in the contemporary art world, but striving to deny any relationship to that world's commerce, they cultivated an overall look of aristocratic elegance touched with subtly unique detail. 69 The result is seen in a drawing by Schiele ( 14) in which Oppenheimer seems simultaneously to retreat and reveal himself through the cut of his black suit. The relationship of dress to the male subject's sexuality is suggested by the placement of his hands, an issue also raised by a drawing in Die Muskete that satirized a self-portrait shown by Oppenheimer in the 1908 Kunstschau. It represents him in a men's room and is captioned: "It is requested to arrange one's clothing before leaving." 70 The drawing in the satire magazine likely pointed to his homosexuality through his clothing, an issue also raised by Schiele's drawings of him. These show him lying, both prone and supine, on a bed with his legs extending toward the viewer. In the first case, the foreshortening leads the eye to the buttocks, in the second ( 15) to the groin which is indicated by his hand, a gesture made telling by the model's languid face which returns the gaze. 71 The hand and face attract the eye by their colors, because the chalk outlines of Oppenheimer's suit remain unfilled. The touches of watercolor run the spectrum from green to red and seem to reveal both the surface of the skin and a condition beneath it, an effect seen most vividly in the re's right hand where only the bones are picked out by the brush.

While Schiele's approach to re drawing undoubtedly derived in large part from August Rodin, he challenged one convention of the nude--inviolate skin--that Rodin had retained. Clothing would often become the immaterial, unexamined surface that skin had once been, as in a self-portrait ( 16) once owned by Wimmer-Wisgrill where the paper is untouched within the contours of the shirt's vest, while the arms are glimpsed through the transparent sleeves. Short hairs growing from the neck's nape declare the body's life and primacy over social conventions such as clothing. Oil's viscosity adds to this quality in Portrait of Dr. Erwin Graff ( 17), a gynecologist who permitted Schiele to draw his patients at the Vienna Medical School. Graff's skin's imperfect materiality, suggested particularly through the bandaged finger, stands at odds with the antiseptic white of his scrubs. This materiality in Schiele's and Kokoschka's works always gives access to an interiority ultimately alone and moving towards death, as indicated in the following lines written by Roeßler about the skin of paint in Kokoschka's portraits: "He smears it like ointment and lets it cake into a scab and heal [Begin Page 269] into scar tissue. . . . He paints their mite infested skin, their ulcerating flesh, which stews in inner heat, is worn down by debauchery, eaten away by sickness." 72

Alfred Gell, in his study of Polynesian tattooing, considers skin's cross-cultural role as symbolic form. While some societies, such as those in Polynesia, have viewed the skin as an appropriate support and vehicle for expression of social relations, others, including most in modern Europe, have developed prohibitions on the skin's marking. 73 Individuals in the New Guinean highlands may view body decoration as a means to display their true selves' links to ancestors and cultural myths, but Europeans at the turn of the century believed that the true self, the interior one, remained hidden from public view by the skin. 74 In the final lines of "Ornament and Crime" Loos expressed that "Primitive men had to differentiate themselves by various colors, modern man needs his clothes as a mask. His individuality is so strong that it can no longer be expressed in terms of items of clothing." 75 This emphasis on a rich interior existence grew from an altered concept of childhood that had developed in Europe during the eighteenth century and coalesced in Freud's theory of the unconscious. 76 Psychic identity came to be experienced as an inner space rooted in childhood's lost history. This conceptual framework helps to link the Viennese expressionists' extraordinary focus on children to their equally unusual effort to convey unseen psychic life. The depiction of skin's material condition, like the use of expressive dance movements and auratic emanations, provided a means to allude to a hidden condition that originated in the separations and conflicts of childhood.

Schiele's The Birth of Genius ( 18), a painting of 1911 since destroyed, imagined and reenacted an infant's severance from its initial fused state with the mother's body. A startled face looks out from a transparent womb, the infant's hand tests the space of culture that his skin now meets without mediation. The motif, according to Roeßler, had its origin in December 1910 when he, in response to Schiele's complaints about his own mother, suggested that the artist execute a series on the theme of motherhood. 77 Schiele was immediately drawn to the idea of a dead mother and painted an initial version that he presented as a gift to Roeßler on Christmas Eve 1910. In the first version he clearly wanted to encourage contemplation of the painting's relationship to traditional representations of mother and child in genre and religious painting, for the heads incline toward each other, suggesting that the infant is enclosed by a blanket and both are asleep. Only the mother's ashen color challenges this interpretation and, upon reflection, forces the realization that this is a view into the womb, eliciting shock in response to the macabre transformation of the theme. In the second version, however, the mother's head and hand are more clearly cadaverous and the tender touching of heads was removed, changes that brutally call attention to the severings of parturition and the later individuation of consciousness.

Roeßler located the work's impetus solely in Schiele's personal history, never mentioning a possible relationship to the paintings on view at the December 1910 The Art of the Woman exhibition. Julie Johnson has shown how the exhibition's organizers deliberately "selected works which would represent aspects of domestic femininity. 78 Images of motives and children were plentiful, perhaps to reaffirm traditional female roles to a public who could have been disturbed by the exhibition's bold assertion of women's presence in the history and current practice of visual arts. In his review of that exhibition, Roeßler stressed that woman's natural role was that of mother rather than of painter or sculptor. 79 He continued to espouse this view in his article about Zweybrück, arguing that women were more suited to those arts that allowed them to remain a riddle, singing, dancing, and decorating. This was woman's natural role, because, as he explained,

Each man was only wholly inside woman's being once, as an unborn child; but in his later life he retains only an uncertain, dream-like memory about that internalized existence. And precisely because of that, because woman by her mere existence alone already has a strong impact, man values her as an artist only if it is a matter of a particularly feminine art. 80

Roeßler valued Zweybrück principally as a craftswoman, for embroidery had been construed as a highly gendered art since the nineteenth century. This conformed to textile's gendered status in European folk cultures where cloth was an important means of social reproduction. Celebratory gifts of textiles bound groups together and the transmission of techniques connected women to both their ancestors and progeny, encouraging the analogizing of textile production with human reproduction.

It was exactly such feminized ties that Schiele denied in his painting. In a letter written to his mother two years after The Birth of Genius, he made an extraordinary claim that says much about his understanding of the artist's imagination and psychological state. "This is the great separation," he told her:

Without doubt I will be the greatest, the most beautiful, the most valuable, the purest, and the most precious fruit--through my independent will all beautiful and noble results are united in me--this also, no doubt, because of the man. I will be the fruit which after its decay will still leave behind eternal life: therefore how great must be your joy--to have borne me? 81

This imagined separation involved much more than personal psychological history. It was, in effect, one of expressionism's myths of origin, a fantasy about the relative values of fine and applied art, about painting's eternal life as opposed to textile's decay. Through such myths, male expressionist artists struggled to separate themselves from the growing presence of women in the Viennese art world as well as the discourse about ornament and fashion that accompanied women's expanding roles in the public sphere.
