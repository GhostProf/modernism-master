Cinema Regained: Godard Between Proust and Benjamin

Alessia Ricciardi

1. Godard's Trauerspiel of Cinema

In recent years, several screen adaptations of Proust's À la recherche du temps perdu have been produced in defiance of the legendary difficulty of an enterprise that discouraged earlier directors such as Luchino Visconti and Joseph Losey. 1 In 1999, Raoul Ruiz completed Le temps retrouvé and, in 2000, Chantal Akerman's La captive enriched the growing Proustian filmography. Whereas Akerman successfully adapts just one section of the Recherche, Ruiz's movie more ambitiously dramatizes the entire last volume, thus attempting a synoptic recapitulation of the entire oeuvre. To make an adaptation of the Recherche may look like an act of hubris, yet, as Walter Benjamin remarks apropos of an imagined film production of Goethe's Faust, "the indestructibility of the highest life in all things" compels critical intelligence to regard the quality, rather than the principle, of a work. 2

Although Ruiz's effort shows many clever intuitions, such as its canny usage of superimposition to represent the intricate effects of involuntary memory, his film, in the final analysis, fails to convince. The reason for his failure has less to do with the a priori impossibility of filming the Recherche than with a literal-minded fidelity to his narrative model and a concern for décor d'époque, concern which unfortunately conveys the impression of a Merchant-Ivory period piece, or perhaps a minor Visconti costume drama. Moreover, the Chilean director's insistence on a surrealist manipulation of the image within a single frame (for example the scenes carpeted with upturned hats or populated by mutiple Marcels) rather than on the organizing work of montage mistakenly results in an adaptation that reduces the Recherche--a novel whose very title suggests an operation of some epistemological importance--to a trivial dramaturgical puzzle. A better translation of the novel into the cinematic idiom would aim at a more ral or "involuntary" relation to the original, a reinterpretation through which the very temporal ground of interpretation would reveal itself allegorically. 3

Along these lines, a work that ought to be regarded as an "involuntary" adaptation of La recherche is Jean-Luc Godard's Histoire(s) du cinéma. The four-hour film, released simultaneously in France with a four-volume book interweaving text and stills, ambitiously attempts to reconstruct twentieth-century history and the very development of the movies through a collage of film traces. Godard worked upon this singular enterprise over a span of ten years from 1988 to 1998, and it at once constitutes a sort of archeology of the medium film and an act of recalling the twentieth century. 4 The result is a work divided into eight segments or, as Godard puts it, "constellations," bearing such evocative titles as "Toutes les histoires," "Une histoire seule," "Seul le cinéma," "Fatale beauté," "La monnaie de l'absolu," "Une vague nouvelle," "Le contrôle de l'univers," "Les signes parmi nous" (ACMS, 9). The different units vary in length and bear an often inorganic relationship to the central idea of the film. The titles and subtitles reappear as if refrains in a litany. 5 The final work consists of a dense montage of fragmentary movie sequences which is punctuated by the superimposition of flashing text, color schemes, paintings, and readings of literary works. Playing the role of Virgil, Godard guides us on our meandering way through an inferno of images while commenting in voice-over on the past as if from beyond the grave.

Melancholic in tone, Godard's Histoire(s) ought to be understood as a highly subjective response to the increasing obsolescence of a certain idea of cinema, call it "art cinema," the cinema of montage and mise-en-scène. Although computerized cinematography may in some sense represent the future of the form, it is evident that the digital image, as distinct from its celluloid variant, enacts a temporality of the perpetual present tense. Indeed, in so far as the digital image is removed from any ontological grounding or referent, it may even be regarded as a photograph that is atemporal in nature, without temporality. What the camera produces is no longer necessarily a historical record, because digital techniques enable the creation of the image ex nihilo. Although the flexibility of the new technology opens a space for a certain type of creative experimentation, its divorce from historical reality deprives film of its function as a mode of memory, and hence as a framework for critical inquiry. In this light, Godard's search for lost cinema represents a potential corrective to indiscriminate acceptance of the status quo.

Implicit throughout this search is the recognition that film has furnished the twentieth century with new resources of memory, chief of which are the reorganizing procedures of montage. Montage defines the peculiar manner in which we remember movies, the mode in which consciousness incorporates the fragmentary, involuntary, and defamiliarizing experience of time. Yet as powerfully as Godard's mélange of film quotes affirms the idea of cinema as an artistic means of access to the past, it also mourns this untimely and imperiled notion. Histoire(s) du cinéma thus performs an especially pointed, if allegorized, reinterpretation of À la recherche du temps perdu. Speculating on the fate of a medium that seems about to disappear before the completion of its perceived end, Godard draws a parallel between the task of cinema and that of the narrator at the end of Proust's opus.

Appropriately, the most daring of film editors has achieved the most Proustian of films. Histoire(s) du cinéma recounts the camera's pursuit of what the film alternately calls a "monde perdu" and a "temps perdu." 6 The very "matière et mémoire" of Godard's history thus derive from Proust's Recherche. If, early in his career, the director's bold experiments helped establish the "amnesic" style of the New Wave, they now permit him to rehabilitate the tatters of our past. In Histoire(s) Godard is intent on rescuing past images from banality and on restoring their strangeness by refusing to supply the narrative context from which they are taken, as Alain Bergala points out: "Godard systematically refuses the present of the shots he shows us. He recuperates these shots from the past and if he tries to resuscitate them, it is by trying to allow them to return in a different way, disconnected from the script within which they were caught." 7

Godard's approach renounces the "objective" logic of collective history and memory in favor of the subjective contingency of Proustian mémoire involontaire. His quest for a lost cinematic paradise, however, is motivated by an ambition that exceeds the strictly "autobiographical." Godard strives to express the physiognomy of the twentieth century through its remains. In this sense, if his aesthetic inspiration is mainly Proustian, his ideology and methodology betray a close proximity to Benjamin's last works, The Arcades Project and the "Theses on the Philosophy of History." Indeed, as I will argue, it is by developing a cinematic language capable of encompassing the Benjaminian antipodes of materialist critique and messianic hope that Godard ultimately arrives at a truly Proustian relation to the past, at a model of remembrance that is neither mechanical nor nostalgic. Histoire(s) du cinéma imagines the resurrection of history through the recuperation of an idea of film that has been eradicated by the ascendancy of mass entertainment, a recovery that can only be accomplished in a Proustian spirit of receptivity to the involuntary.

2. Cinematic Proust

If memory is the broad, manifest theme of Histoire(s), Godard in particular wants to show how cinema alters our very conception of memory, transforming it from the faculty of willed recall into a ghostly element of involuntary reminiscences. On this score, he demonstrates to what extent the medium film possesses a Proustian aspect: movies return to haunt us according to the logic of the involuntary. Indeed, he argues cogently that our memory of film consists of the emotional shape of certain images, not their phenomenological content. 8

This position dovetails neatly with a view held by the French philosopher Deleuze. He believes that, had Proust lived longer, he would have written in cinematic terms; but he also regards him as the proper originator of the most complicated of film theories, that of the time-image. 9

Godard enlarges upon Deleuze's intuition to compose a history of cinema that enacts the rhythm of the time-image, instead of adopting a conventionally chronological approach. Histoire(s) du cinéma thus pertinently reframes the question of the relationship between Proust's novel and cinematic memory. While many critics, from Brassaï to Mieke Bal, agree that there is a photographic logic at work in the Recherche, the cinematic subtext of the novel is more rarely and obliquely considered by readers (Deleuze and Benjamin being the only two critics who readily come to mind). 10 As will be contended, however, the language of the novel powerfully deploys a range of cinematic techniques from montage to superimposition.

Did Proust ever go to the movies? According to his latest biographer, William C. Carter, the answer is yes, but probably only once. The event took place in 1908 during a stay at the hotel in Cabourg, which later served as the model for Balbec in the novel: on the last day of his visit he went to see a Punch-and-Judy show. 11 "This was apparently Proust's first movie," Carter writes in a footnote, "it may also have been his last. The cinema was one of the modern inventions that interested him little" (MP, 866). According to the biographer, Proust presented his friend Marcel Plantevignes with a copy of his French translation of Ruskin's The Bible of Amiens just before the film began. 12 The choice of gift seems strangely apt. The gesture brings Proust's devotion to the art of vitraux exemplified in Amiens cathedral back to mind, and suggests his fascination--in spite of himself--with that twentieth-century art form that most powerfully renovates the optical phenomenology of stained glass. Morever, the juxtaposition of religious scenes characteristic of medieval, stained-glass window-making anticipates the modernist emphasis of art cinema on montage and mise-en-scène. In this regard, it is interesting to recall that Godard has often maintained that Christianity represents a type of cinema, precisely on account of its obsession with the spectacularized image: "I invoke Christianity as the original film; it is there for every painter, it is something literature has not done" (ACMS, 77).

In his book The Proustian Quest, Carter remarks that in the first two decades of the twentieth century, motion pictures offered Proust and others (for example, Mirabeau, Léger) an ideal model for describing the sensation of movement in the mechanized modern era. 13 Carter cites a section of the Recherche in which Proust arrestingly likens the passage of a group on horseback to its own cinematic image. The tableau is that of Odette being greeted by the Prince de Sagan and his team of riders who advance past her as though "filmed at a gallop in the blinding glare of the avenue." 14 Although it is rare, as Anne Henry notes, that film so plainly exerts its hold over the Proustian imagination, the novelist doubtless knew that his ocular preoccupations were ahead of their time. 15 For example, Proust persistently indulged an interest in visual perspectivism; such fluidity of outlook enabled him to resist the finality of psychological judgments upon his characters: "Nothing is farther away from the analysis of a character than this succession of points of view which defines them. Now, by the same token, nothing is more modern in our century of images than such an approach" (TMP, 223).

Even if it may be involuntary, the filmic subtext of Proust's article "Impressions de route en automobile," published in Le ro on 19 November, 1907, is nonetheless unmistakable. Reworked in the Recherche in the famous description of Martinville steeples, the essay details his drives with Alfred Agostinelli to visit Gothic cathedrals in Normandy. In the novel, he conveys the exhilaration of kinetic sensations associated with riding in an automobile in terms of a heightened experience of perspectival shift. In the original essay, however, his account of the continuously mutating point of view resulting from the car ride climaxes with the startling epiphany of a motion picture. At night in the town of Lisieux, while straining to see or, as he adds, at least to touch the stony foliage of the cathedral of Notre Dame, he is suddenly confronted by the spectacle of an overtly cinematic nativity scene:

But at the very moment when I was approaching it groping blindly, a sudden radiance inundated it; trunk by trunk, the pillars emerged from the night, vividly detaching, in full light against a background of shadow, the large pattern of their leaves of stone. It was my driver, the resourceful Agostinelli, who, sending to the ancient sculptures the greeting of a present whose light served all the better to read the lessons of the past, directed the fires of his headlights successively over all the parts of the portico in such measure as I wanted to see them. And when I returned to the car I saw a group of children whom curiosity had drawn close and who,bending toward the lights heads whose curls fluttered in that supernatural luminousity, reconstituted here, as if projected from the cathedral in a ray of light, the angelic ration of a nativity scene. 16

The darkness and the mechanical blaze of the headlights seem to reproduce the phenomenological conditions of a movie theater (or perhaps more precisely a drive-in), opening up a space for the "projection" of the improvised nativity scene. The scene is all the more dramatic for its juxtaposition of the mythical past in the shape of the cathedral to the new myths of modernity in the shape of the car and, we might want to add, the cinema.

In the Recherche, however, motion pictures do not receive a flattering representation. Besides the description of the team of riders greeting Odette, we find only four other references to the "cinématographique," all in Le temps retrouvé and all reaffirming the superiority of literary or metaphoric representation over the cinematic. 17

If reality were indeed a sort of waste product of experience, more or less identical for each one of us, since when we speak of bad weather, a war, a taxi rank, a brightly lit restaurant, a garden full of flowers, everybody knows what we mean, if reality were no more than this, no doubt a sort of cinematograph film of these things would be sufficient and the "style," the"literature" that departed from the simple data that they provide would be superfluous and artificial. But was it true that reality was no more than this? ( ROTP , III: 925; RDTP , IV; 468.)

Proust clearly means this last question to be understood rhetorically, since he had already observed in a preceding passage that "nothing is further from what we have really perceived than the vision that the cinematograph presents" (ROTP, III: 917; RDTP, IV: 461). In such passages, Proust shows little awareness that the impact of film resides not in the ability of the camera to capture images, but in the potential of montage to incorporate time.

On the other hand, Proust firmly defines the novel as a whole as the "optical instrument" with which the writer furnishes the reader to refine his or her perception of the world (ROTP, III: 949; RDTP, IV: 489-90). If we surmise that the model for this optical instrument may be film, we do so not simply because, ever since his childhood in Combray, the narrator has been beguiled by the mysterious "magic lantern" that projects the endlessly mesmerizing story of Golo and Geneviève de Brabante. 18 As Anne Henry has noted, we arrive at this intuition, because the narrative mechanism that organizes the Recherche is nothing other than montage. Rather than a serial succession of events such as can be found in the more linear plots of Balzac and Zola, we encounter in Proust's chef d'oeuvre a circuitous conjunction of heterogeneous moments and concerns. The discrete events comprising the Recherche unfold according to a variety of different perspectives and narrative detours which achieve synthesis only by means of the work of montage:

Proust is therefore driven to inaugurate this modern technique: montage, that is to say an artificial continuity obtained by adjusting sequences built independently from one another, belonging to different domains and yet receiving a superior unity from the general project. . . . The strength of this kind of montage is to be found not in the effort to produce aesthetic effects, alternation, symmetry, etc., but in the fact that it is placed at the service of a demonstration. As one says in cinematic language, it is an ideological montage. ( TMP , 200).

Henry's vision of a "superior unity from the general project" which holistically emerges from the novel's independently built sequences is consistent with Proust's revelation that he composed the Recherche non-sequentially, writing the first chapter of the first volume and the last chapter of the last volume in succession and then filling in the intervening episodes. Regarding this authorial method we should also recall the novel's vivid depiction of the narrator's work routine. Like a director, the narrator Marcel constructs his manuscript through the procedures of collage and montage by having Françoise, the domestic, cut and paste the text into a new arrangement every day (ROTP, III: 1090; RDTP, IV: 610). At the level of narrative macroeconomics, the montage technique permits the simultaneous development of three divergent main themes, which, following Deleuze's suggestion, we might classify as the narrator and his social life, the narrator and his sentimental education, and the narrator and his artistic vocation. 19

Yet even at the level of its linguistic microeconomics, the Recherche relies on an inherently cinematic device. The novel's well-known predilection for metaphorical expression is not only manifested through the mere choice of the appropriate, poetic image; rather, the Recherche develops a sort of mise-en-scène of metaphor through an insistence on the technique of superimposition. That is to say, the Recherche aims to capture in writing how consciousness sustains moments of optical equivocation, how the mind blends together one image with another, a practice compounded by photographic technology. Indeed, the experience of la mémoire involontaire--the novel's philosophical nucleus--functions as a superimposition of two images, a process that in its psychological, temporal, and visual aspects ultimately achieves the effect of film. 20 When Marcel reaches the Guermantes courtyard late in the novel and trips on the uneven paving stones, he is reminded of an earlier visit to Venice and expresses his happiness in terms of "evoked images": "A profound azure intoxicated my eyes, impressions of coolness, of dazzling light, swirled round me and in my desire to seize them . . . I continued, ignoring the evident amusement of the great crowd of chauffeurs, to stagger as I had staggered a few seconds ago, with one foot on the higher paving-stone and the other on the lower" (ROTP, III: 899; RDTP, IV: 445-46). Not only are these sensations provoked by involuntary memory described in visual terms; the moment of epiphany itself is presented as a sort of visual ralenti in which the narrator's image is nearly frozen as he strikes a fateful pose. The cinematic logic pursued by involuntary memory defamiliarizes the very itinerary of recollection from the slow-motion unwinding of the epiphany through its culminating freeze-frame of the narrator to the ultimate superimposition of two images--one of Venice, the other of the "Guermantes courtyard"--which have no organic connection to one another. This logic delivers a shock to the narrator precisely because no clear hermeneutic connection can be established between the two images conjoined within involuntary memory: "Owing to the work of oblivion, the returning memory can throw no bridge, form no connecting link between itself and the present minute" (ROTP, III: 903; RDTP, IV: 449). As there is no apparent rational link between the two images, the operation of involuntary memory finally must be assessed in terms of a cinematic "superimposition." 21

The critic who has been most closely attuned to Proust's cinematic sensibility is Walter Benjamin, and it is no accident that he, in his major essay on Proust, focuses on the central role of the image within the Recherche. He observes that Proust treats the relation of resemblance between two things according to the code of the dream world, through the intervention of a third element: "the image." 22 Rather than providing a smooth synthetic integrity, the image jars the reader of Proust through its "beatings of wings" and "small shocks" ("IOP," 208). 23 "The beatings of wings" aptly typifies the Proustian mise-en-scène of superimposition, a poetic that also corresponds to the crucial notion of "intermittences" ("IOP," 203). Given this understanding of the image as the presentiment of an uncanny similarity between disparate things, we should not be surprised that Benjamin, in the "Theses on the Philosophy of History," also identifies the image as the proper means of access to the historical past.

The "Theses" conceive of history as a stream of visual "monads" that, by virtue of their perpetual recombination, fleetingly suggest a larger pattern of correlation. At the center of this current of fragments, we encounter the most elusive image of all: "The true image of the past flits by. The Past can be seized only as an image which flashes up at the instant when it can be recognized and is never seen again." 24 Benjamin's definition of history in the "Theses" never strays very far from the language with which he discusses memory in Proust. His insistence that history's authentic image vanishes at the moment it "flashes up" bears a strong resemblance to his exposition of mémoire involontaire in "The Image of Proust": "When the past is reflected in the dewy fresh 'instant,' a painful shock of rejuvenation pulls it together ... But this very concentration, in which things that normally just fade and slumber consume themselves in a flash is called rejuvenation" ("IOP," 211). 25 It seems particularly fitting to stress the latency or dormancy of the material expended by involuntary memory when we consider the dream-like suddenness with which the apparition of the narrator's dead grandmother in the novel is superimposed upon the empty room where Marcel is about to take off his boots (ROTP, II: 783; RDTP, III: 152).

Indeed, the fictive and linguistic practices of the Recherche could be said to exemplify the operation of a cinematic unconscious. 26 In the novel, both superimposition and montage help to establish a larger tropology according to which existence is defined as a function of ocular perception. And in consonance with this tropology, the novel presents the approach of death as the moment when we see the moving pictures of our lives unreeling before our eyes. Within a certain vein of modernist discourse this conceit has become familiar, crucially animating essays by both Benjamin and Pier Paolo Pasolini; in Proust's novel the trope provides the allegorical horizon within which the Recherche reveals its ideal form as a film. Benjamin's celebrated essay "The Storyteller" portrays the mythical moment preceding death as the occasion when "a sequence of images is set in motion inside a man as his life comes to an end," and meaning thereby becomes transmissible. 27 In his article "Observations on the Sequence Shot," Pasolini argues that death instantaneously effects the final cut of one's life, putting significant moments into perspective by arranging them in sequence. 28 Both these writers may be viewed as performing variations on the theme established by Proust at the end of the Recherche when Marcel, contemplating himself on the verge of death, seems to experience the sudden transformation of his consciousness into a kinetic visual projection of his entire past. The very circular structure of the Recherche enforces the presentation of the narrative as a cinematic après-coup, a print that will outlive the narrator. 29

The trope of life as cinematic spectacle elaborates the novel's central concept of involuntary memory, which, as Benjamin points out, is comparable in effect to the rapid screening of an emotionally charged or significant image. In a handwritten text found among the critic's paperwork after his death and entitled "From a Brief Speech on Proust on the Occasion of My Fortieth Birthday," he explicitly compares la mémoire involontaire to a movie trailer or review of our death:

Concerning involuntary memory . . . one might say that a tiny image, a photo of ourselves, is appended to our deepest moments exactly as on certain packs of cigarettes. And that "whole life" that, as we often hear, passes before the dying or before those people who are in danger of dying, is composed precisely of such tiny images. They present a rapid streaming like in those notebooks, the precursors of cinematographs, in which, when we were children, we could admire a boxer, a swimmer, or tennis player at his art. 30

According to Benjamin, then, Proust's "notebooks" are cinematic as a consequence of the narrator's existential point of view, since it is only his imminent death that endows the fragmentary layers of images, the passing of involuntary memories, with that elusive fluency of meaning characteristic of film. If the usual gratifications of narrative terminus seem dubious at the end of the Recherche, they are so not only because of the unlikeliness of Marcel's having a lifetime of sufficient remaining length to write the book we have just read. The novel seems open-ended rather because its meaning consists in an eternal return of the same, no longer as a text but as a film. This is to say that, in the end, the Recherche provides an anticipatory glimpse of its own afterlife. The task which the narrator must shoulder, as he recognizes, is to give birth not just to a new work of art, but altogether to a new type of art. It is the drive to achieve not merely the latest in the expected output of artistic products, but a change in the very methods of production that gives À la recherche du temps perdu its radical force.

To come back to Godard, what makes his Histoire(s) du cinéma such a suggestive adaptation of the Recherche is in large part the film's successful assimilation and refinement of a Benjaminian view of Proust's novel. 31 Godard's and Benjamin's interpretations of the novel coincide in so far as both privilege an understanding of mémoire involontaire in terms of an expressly visual dialectic of recollection and forgetfulness. However, Benjamin's work provides Godard not only with an aesthetics of reminiscence, but also with an ideal of the politically redemptive value of history. In this sense, Godard composes his Histoire(s) through a contamination of Proust's doctrine of involuntary memory with Benjamin's problematic of history. As the French director himself puts it, "the future bursts amid remembrances" (HDC, III: 12). Godard's ambiguous description of the arrival of the future as a violent or shocking occurrence already hints at the political and cultural fatalism that will frame his unveiling of a Proustian history of cinema. Such an attitude represents an extension of Benjamin's melancholy position with regard to history, capitalism, and the epoch of the commodity.

3. Histoire(s) du cinéma: The Future of the Past

One essay crucial to an understanding of Histoire(s) du cinéma is Benjamin's "Theses on the Philosophy of History." In The Arcades Project, Benjamin had already defined history not as an objective science but as a form of subjective remembrance [Eingedenken] (AP, 471). On this score, it is in Benjamin's wake that we should position Godard's resistance to linear narrative and preference for constellations of images. 32 His affinity for the critic's view of history embraces not only a common imagistic method, but also a similar preoccupation with the end of time in its political and religious dimensions. Godard shares a belief with the later Benjamin, albeit ambivalent or "weak" in the philosophical sense, that history provides the messianic horizon of a possible resurrection of art as cinema. 33 Indeed, the image for Godard inherently carries a promise of the redemption of reality: "The image is first of all of the order of redemption, look here, that of reality" (HDC, III: 149). In the age of commodification nothing speaks to the contemporary Zeitgeist with more iconoclastic candor, nor offers greater hope of cultural salvation. We should not forget that, for Godard, Christianity represents the ideal origin of cinema, precisely because it constructs an allegorical iconology on the foundation of a revolutionary historiography. It is hardly accidental that one of the epigrammatic refrains of the film, inspired by Saint Paul, avows that "the image will return at the time of resurrection" (HDC, IV: 75).

For Godard as for Benjamin, the messianic horizon is linked to a "moment of danger." Benjamin writes: "To articulate the past historically does not mean to recognize it 'the way it really was' (Ranke); it means to seize hold of a memory as it flashes up at a moment of danger. . . . In every era the attempt must be made anew to wrest tradition away from a conformism that is about to overpower it" ("TPH," 255). There is no doubt that it is to the mindless conformism of Hollywood and the totalizing domination of mass-market consumerism that Godard opposes his Histoire(s):

Yes, it is of our times that I am the fugitive enemy, yes, the totalitarianism of the present as it is applied mechanically every day more oppressively at the planetary level, this tyranny without a face that overwhelms everything for the exclusive profit of the systematic organization of the unified time of the instant . . . because I try in my works to show an ear that listens to time. ( HDC , IV: 289)

Elsewhere, Godard reiterates this point when, adopting a pastiche of Hölderlin's and Heidegger's metaphysical terminology, he imagines the world as a realm deserted by gods where the function of the artist is to rediscover the necessity of the sacred: "Not only have the gods and god fled, but the splendor of divinity is extinguished in the history of the world" (HDC, I: 245).

If Godard and Benjamin share similar notions of memory and of history, their respective approaches to cinema might seem at first sight very different. Certainly, Godard would reject the German critic's initial appraisal of cinema. In his 1936 essay "The Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction," Benjamin links the emergence of cinema to the destruction of the aura of the work of art. However, Benjamin also labors to elucidate the politically liberating consequences of the disconnection of film from traditional culture. 34 Yet, by the time of his 1939 article, "Some Motifs in Baudelaire," Benjamin proceeds to explore the question of aura in a different frame of mind, complicating his first "political" definition with nostalgic overtones.

In his accompanying commentary upon the Histoire(s), Godard clearly articulates his nostalgia for the aura of lost cinema. We should bear in mind here that he defines film neither as an art nor a science, but rather as a "mystery," a word that invites interpretation in terms of the auratic. 35 According to Miriam Hansen, the auratic in Benjamin never coincides with the unveiling of a symbolic integrity, but on the contrary with a "catastrophic and dislocating impact." 36 The same is true of Godard's auratic cinema. In Godard's eyes, film is mysterious because it performs an allegorical movement, an alternating dispersion and concentration of signification. Histoire(s) du cinéma aims to witness just that movement, in the process bringing to light the messianic possibility of a catastrophic change. Whereas for Benjamin history must attend to the experience of the vanquished, for Godard film must be given attention in so far as it has been vanquished by video, by digital technology, and more generally by what he defines as the "totalitarianism of the present" (HDC, IV: 286).

In the most celebrated passage of the "Theses on the Philosophy of History," Benjamin imagined history as the allegorical subject of Klee's painting "Angelus Novus," a divine spectator who looks back on the past while being propelled into the future amid a hail of debris by a wind blowing from paradise ("TPH," 257-58). Strikingly, Benjamin revives the metaphor at a pivotal juncture in "The Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction" when he imagines the advent of film: "Then came the film and burst this prison-world asunder by the dynamite of the tenth of second, so that now, in the midst of its far-flung ruins and debris, we calmly and adventurously go traveling" ("WOA," 236). The resemblance of the two passages suggests that Benjamin at some level associated cinema with the paradise from which the storm of the future originates. Godard quite consciously appropriates and redeploys this association. In the first, televised version of Histoire(s) du cinéma, at the end of Episode 1B, we thus encounter the image of Klee's angel dramatically (and perhaps a bit self-aggrandizingly) superimposed upon that of Godard himself (NMQG, 222). Behind Godard's enterprise stands a Benjaminian insistence on the critical recuperation of history, a retrieval of ephemeral images "flashing up" to demand analysis in the present, because, as Benjamin puts it, "every image of the past that is not recognized by the present as one of its own concerns threatens to disappear irretrievably" ("TPH," 255). Film offers the ideal reservoir of such images, a reservoir that offers the advantage of presenting historical events without adding causal explanations from outside the events themselves.

If the philosophical standpoint of Godard's Histoire(s) is the same as that of Benjamin's "Theses," its methodology is closer to that of The Arcades Project. Indeed, as an attempt to delineate the specific physiognomy of the twentieth century from its fragmentary cultural traces, through a collage of images and texts, Histoire(s) du cinéma reveals a genealogical relation to The Arcades Project. As has been documented, Benjamin until the end of his life pursued the project of a history of the "minor" or marginal, a project applying the practices of historical materialism and articulated around the emblematic res of the nineteenth century: arcades, whores, gamblers, gilded mirrors, mechanical dolls. The resulting epigrammatic sketches, organized into a kind of patchwork of quotations, contribute to what Benjamin defined as a "dialectic at a standstill," a sort of intransitive unveiling of the truth that avoids the illusion of linear narrative progress. But as in the case of the "Theses," the theory of collective memory developed in the Arcades also owes its inspiration to the Proustian notion of involuntary memory, of which it represents a sort of "objective," material aspect. 37

Godard's Histoire(s) du cinéma identifies the twentieth-century analogue of the arcade in the movie theater. Like the arcade, film is able to provide a centrifugal mode of access to meaning and history. Not only do Benjamin and Godard both espouse a poetics of montage or quotation, they also share a determination to escape the strictures of conventional historicism in order to unveil the allegorical res that arise from the ruins of history. Even Godard's written commentary upon Histoire(s) mimics the visual procedures of montage, linking as it does citations or pastiches of a variety of authors--philosophers, film critics, songwriters, poets, and directors--who are listed at the end of each volume, but not individually attributed. Film clearly represents for Godard a sort of private or personal historical arcade, a spectacular space that provides us with an "involuntary memory" of the century. In this light, Benjamin and Godard may be seen to share much the same project; both aim to perform a materialist critique of culture by means of Proustian acts of remembrance, to expose the hidden costs of production by means of a poetic recovery of the past. Such a critique, far from enforcing an objectively verifiable or scientific picture of events, provides access to history via the digressive, subjective paths of allegory. Godard underlines this emphasis when he writes, "why simplify when one can complicate?" (HDC, II: 78). If history becomes legible for Benjamin by alternation between the mystical perspective of the "Theses" and the materialist approach of the Arcades, we find in Godard the possibility of bringing the two modes into convergence through the language of cinema, a language that according to Godard expresses the "egality and fraternity between real and fiction" (HDC, III: 126-27).

In Histoire(s) du cinéma, however, Godard intends to superimpose his personal experience upon the collective, public face of cinema. Histoire(s) du cinéma is an autobiographical enterprise in spirit, since the material traces of film are also reflections of Godard's own personal life story. 38 As he himself phrases it, "without cinema I would not have any history" (HDC, II: 39). Proclaiming the visual language of film as the mother tongue of the twentieth century, Godard refuses to provide an ostensibly objective, chronological overview of the medium. To the question of historical origin he responds instead by taking the invention of motion pictures as the revolutionary new beginning of a certain way of looking at the world, the emergence of cinema in this sense standing for the birth of a peculiarly modern consciousness of estrangement. We thus find three historical levels "superimposed" on one another in the Histoire(s): the personal, the communal, and the cinematic. The titles of the first two segments--"Toutes les histoires" and "Une histoire seule"--imply the constantly viable possibility in Histoire(s) du cinéma of a Gestalt switch between two ways of thinking about the medium: in terms of its social currency as spectacle or in terms of its psychical value as allegory.

It is well known that Deleuze's theory of cinema divides the history of the medium into two periods: that of the "movement-image" and that of the "time-image." Coinciding with Italian Neorealism and the French New Wave, the second period represents the equivalent of a Kantian revolution of cinema in as much as it overturns the silent era's subordination of temporality to movement and instead comes to enact what Deleuze, employing a very Proustian formulation, calls "temps à l'état pur." 39 We might surmise that it is because the most recent productions of the movie industry threaten to eradicate art cinema between Hollywood's bloated overcommodification of the medium and the atemporality of digital technology that Godard resolves to resurrect the time-image. Histoire(s) du cinéma should be regarded as an exploration of the autobiographical value of the time-image, an effort advanced in a Proustian mood to distill from the fractured remnants of the past a "pure history." If Godard's Histoire(s) shares with Proust's Recherche a similar inspiration we should also note that it runs a similar risk of appealing to a regressive or at least nostalgic mentality. Since film (as opposed to television or video) represents for Godard a sort of idealized childhood of the arts, his enterprise in Histoire(s) du cinéma may be regarded, like Proust's in À la recherche du temps perdu, as an attempt to regain infancy (HDC, I: 238).

According to Godard, cinema is best defined not in terms of ontological presence à la Bazin, but rather in terms of metaphorical and historical representation (ACMS, 67). And like Proust, Godard affirms a poetics of shock or paradox by cultivating visual superimposition in order to sustain epistemelogical instabilities, by performing a "battéments" of images, a deployment of signs that we might describe in Benjaminian accents as a "beating of wings." 40 At stake in this agitated beating of images is the "fatal beauty" of film, its uniquely sublime quality. As Godard declares elsewhere in Histoire(s), "only cinema" mobilizes itself with such unnerving ostentation. To better highlight the metaphorical function of film, Godard, like Proust, actively experiments with the condensation and displacement of images, confronting us with a visual archive of the century that exemplifies the paradox of superimposition: "That which is above all at the base, it's always two, always presenting from the start two images instead of one; it's what I define as the image, this image made of two, that is to say the third image" (ACMS, 27). As a strategy of visualization, superimposition corresponds to a complicated and enigmatic philosophy of history within whose terms the past manifests itself as a palimpsest that is always in the process of being revised. Godard, the narrator, orients our cinematic excursion through the labyrinth of history by means of a sardonic, frequently melancholic commentary that propounds a poetics of quotation.

Jacques Aumont interprets the melancholic tone as the lament of an artist-critic for his lost or defunct art (AMNÉ, 168). By way of an allegorical recapitulation of the project of the Recherche, Godard aspires to recapture his ideal of the "film perdu," once a certain type of cinema has run its course:

We can say, in general, that a certain idea of cinema, not Lumière's but that which was perhaps pursued by Feuillade and continued by Delluc, Vigo, and from which I do not consider myself to be far, this idea of cinema is over, as the Fontainebleau school is over, as Italian painting is over. . . . One can say that a certain cinema has now been achieved. As Hegel put it, an epoch is finished. Afterward it is different. One feels sad because childhood has been lost. ( ACMS , 87)

Just like literature in the Recherche, cinema in Histoire(s) is not "sheltered from time," but simply "the shelter of time" (HDC, IV: 299). This is another way of saying that Godard's Histoire(s) does not embrace the machinery of archival history. To the order of the archive, Godard opposes the aura of involuntary memory.

Indeed, Godard's intuitive, dream-like layering of images resembles involuntary memory more than it does organized montage. As if to drive the point home, during the four-hour course of the Histoire(s) we are repeatedly shown a self-referential scene of the director gazing with somewhat lax attention at the film as it passes by on his editing table. Although we might regard such an image as a way of thematizing the hermeneutical problematic of the film, that problematic is framed by Godard not in analytic terms, as Bergala has remarked, but in emotional terms, in terms of distress and consolation: "We should most of all refrain from thinking, before the authority of the completed work, that Godard has always been sure about which shots he was looking for, and that he knew in which films and in which place in those films to find them. The most troubling shots are those regained when they were inaccessible to the convocation of his voluntary memory" (NMQG, 241).

In the choice of images that constitute the Histoire(s) we should recognize not so much Godard's logical ordering of experience as his spontaneous election to an intensified state of consciousness by his memories of forgotten films. The obsessive return of certain images suggests that Godard has been chosen by his memories in so far as he allows them to resurface. 41 We might say that Godard unveils the Proustian unconsciousness of cinema to the extent that he reveals even montage in its ostensible identity as a visual technology to obey the involuntary rhythms of the subjective. A sly illustration of this point may be found in Episode 3B (AMNÉ, 17), where, after an eruption of fragments from Rossellini's Paisà and Carné's La belle et la bête, we abruptly encounter the legend, "Hardly Working," which ironically raises the question of the psychic labor behind the editing. Although, on the one hand, montage may be work, its result resembles the effortless arbitrariness of the involuntary. It is on this ground that Aumont rightly considers Godard to be Proust's "brother" (AMNÉ, 225).

Aumont's judgment is to some extent invited by the director; in Episode 2B of the Histoire(s), which is entitled "Fatale beauté," references to the Recherche are made openly and at some length. Godard emphatically repeats the name of Marcel's vanished and mysterious love, Albertine. On the screen the phrase "le temps perdu" flashes out several times. This evocation of Proust's enigmatic female protagonist occurs not accidentally during a meditation on the nature of cinema, which Godard equates with its function neither as art nor as technique, but as mystery: "I said: neither an art, nor a technique, but a mystery (voice of Godard). Time regained. Only cinema. All alone. Mystery" (HDC, II: 154-55). Yet his very insistence on the value of film as the embodiment of regained time may also be read as an admission of the medium's untimely status or condition. If the Recherche is written at the dawn of cinema, the Histoire(s) awakens to its sunset, a distance that Godard captures by ironically rewriting Proust's famous first sentence, "for a long time I used to go to sleep early," as "for a long time I used to wake up unhappily." The punning conversion of "early" ("bonne heure" in the original version, which itself echoes "bonheur"--happiness) to "unhappily" ("malheur" in the original) or unhappiness brings to light at once the proximate, but also mutated, condition of Godard's present relative to Proust's past. The director's mise-en-scène must dispense with elegiac niceties, and his own search for lost cinema will be conducted, according to Aumont, as the potential final chapter in the history of art (AMNÉ, 149). Indeed, immediately after airing his revision of Proust's incipit, Godard indulges in a bit of wordplay on the phrase "le temps retrouvé" (time regained) by typographically highlighting the "trou," the hole or empty space, at the heart of the adjective "retrouvé."

Godard's opus is Proustian in spirit because it manages to evoke the involuntary intermittence of our memories of film in a first-person narrative voice: "All that remains of films are a few images in our memory" (ACMS, 85). Moreover, for the French director the history of cinema does not contribute to any larger optimism regarding the revolutionary triumph of the social collective. Instead, Godard's historical outlook derives from an anxious sense of the singularity and frailty of personal memory, and it is in this light that his aims can be considered Proustian. His effort is not to construct a systematic catalog of film memories that provide immediate access to history, but to retrieve a few fragmentary touchstones that, like Proust's involuntary memories, redeem the past by means of indication or suggestion. Paradoxically, these touchstones can be recovered only through a process of forgetting. It is precisely in the readiness of consciousness to brave the threat of erasure that he locates the possibility of recollection as an aesthetic act: "Whoever wishes to remember must trust to oblivion, to that risk which is absolute oblivion, and to the beautiful accident that might then become the remembrance" (HDC, IV: 128-29). Whereas the visual composition of Histoire(s) du cinéma demonstrates that films may provide the material analogue par excellence of memory, Godard's assertion implies that their evocative power rests on what Aumont defines as "amnésie." 42 It is Godard's intention to save a select few auratic images and thus the essence of art cinema at the very moment when the cybernetic practice of the information archive or database seemingly replaces that of a living cultural memory (NMQG, 216).

Godard's cultivation of a sort of involuntary memory of cinema shows to what degree cinema as a medium is well suited, probably better than literature, to elaborate the phenomenology of remembrance. 43 He suggests that, by virtue of its nonchalance of retrospection, film makes it possible to rewrite the myth of Orpheus in a less tragic key than literature permits: "Cinema authorizes Orpheus to turn back without killing" (HDC, II: 96-97). However, if the history of cinema revises the ideology of involuntary memory in a compelling way, Histoire(s) du cinéma at the same time bears witness to the approaching terminus of that history, to the disappearance of film's Orphic potential. In this light, the Proust who is a true brother to Godard is not only a disenchanted, Deleuzian semiotician, but also an elegiac spirit for whom "the true paradises are the paradises that we have lost" (ROTP, III: 903; RDTP, IV: 449). Histoire(s) ends on a freeze-frame of a rose superimposed over a still image of Godard himself. Over this literally arresting vision, the voiceover intones "if a man crossing paradise in a dream received a flower as proof of his transit and, on awakening, found the flower in his hands, what else would he say but I was this man" (HDC, IV: 306-11).

It is at the intersection of the Proustian poetic of involuntary memory with the Benjaminian doctrine of messianic history, then, that we should position Godard's faith in the resurrection of cinema. After all, at its invention the medium of film was taken so seriously as a technological means of resuscitating the dead that, as Christian-Marc Bosséno reminds us, somebody in 1905 went about providing cemeteries with projectors to show movies of the dearly departed. 44 In this light, Godard's redemptive valediction to art cinema looks back through a messianic conception of the medium to the very origin of film itself, so completing a circular motion that is a quintessentially Proustian authorial gesture.
