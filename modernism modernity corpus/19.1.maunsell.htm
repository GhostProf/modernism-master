The Hauntings of Fernando Pessoa

Jerome Boyd Maunsell

The Portuguese poet Fernando Pessoa (1888–1935) was inhabited by so many conflicting voices that his only solution was to turn himself into an entire school of different writers. One of the most individual of writers, he also contained a host of individuals within himself. Of his time and place—mostly Lisbon in the early decades of the 20th century, after a crucially formative education in Durban, South Africa—but also eerily beyond it, he was a one-man Modernist movement or series of movements, who aimed not merely to write individual books, but to “convert himself into a literature,”1 creating an ever-increasing constellation of personas that is said to number at least 75 different imaginary writers or “heteronyms.” Pessoa also helped invent at least three movements—Paulismo, Sensationism, and Intersectionism—taken up by other writer friends in Lisbon, all of which, as his editor and translator Richard Zenith writes, became “the instruments by which Pessoa and his compeers brought Modernism to Portugal.”2 Unusually, Pessoa’s heteronyms also commented on each others’ work, and engaged in promoting and criticising each other in ways reminiscent of other Modernist movements. But Pessoa also wanted to engage with the wider sphere of Modernism outside Portugal, and despite his relative isolation from the currents of Modernism elsewhere in Europe and America, his work resounds with extraordinarily close unconscious echoes and prefigurings of other contemporary writers. In sum, his oeuvre reconfigures the given narratives of international Modernism, as well as providing ghost or shadow Modernisms of its own.

The relatively sparse corpus of English-language criticism of his work continually notes this neglect. In a 2007 collection of English-language essays on Pessoa, Anna M. Klobucka and Mark Sabine remark on how Pessoa’s body of work has been well covered by Portuguese and Brazilian scholars, and has an internationally acclaimed standing, “even as it remains relatively underrecognized in English-speaking countries, where the limited scope of Pessoa’s work available in English translation could, until recently, be blamed for this lack of acknowledgment.”3 Klobucka and Sabine refer to Pessoa’s “ghost-like presence on the contemporary canonical scene of modern Western literature,”4 and the image of Pessoa as a phantom presence is indeed apt. The moments they refer to when Pessoa took “occasional centre-stage prominence on the Anglo-American scene”5—a New Yorker piece by George Steiner of 8 January 1996, and Harold Bloom’s inclusion of Pessoa in The Western Canon (1994) and Genius (2002) —offer glimpses rather than full-bodied assessments of his work. In her much more sustained English-language monograph on Pessoa, from 2003, Irene Ramalho Santos traces Pessoa’s international presence in English in more depth, beginning with a 1955 essay about him in Poetry, while noting that “substantial Pessoan scholarship and critical interpretation in English are not yet abundant.”6 “This is surprising,” Santos writes, because “the Anglo-American poetic tradition could actually claim Pessoa as one of its finest poets… Furthermore, the roots of his poetic theory and practice reach almost equally into Portuguese literature and the Anglo-American literary tradition.”7 Santos argues that an understanding of Pessoa’s work is essential for a wider understanding of Anglo-American Modernism in general, and that Pessoa’s strongest links are with Walt Whitman, Hart Crane, and Emily Dickinson in particular. George Monteiro, meanwhile, has traced many of Pessoa’s earlier influences up to the 20th century, persuasively situating him within an English poetic tradition leading from Shakespeare and the metaphysicals through to the Romantics and Robert Browning,8 all of whom Pessoa read in English and often studied in great depth. Darlene Sadlier has analyzed the heteronymic poetry in terms of Barthes’s and Foucault’s ideas of authorship.9 A recent study of Pessoa from 2010 by Patrícia Silva McNeill traces Pessoa’s affinities with and differences from W. B. Yeats10—a strong resemblance also noted by the poet and Pessoan critic Jorge de Sena.11 In this essay, I aim to continue and extend the line of English-language criticism of Pessoa, taking up Santos’s challenge and attempting to further reclaim Pessoa, not only for the Anglo-American poetic tradition, but ultimately as the quintessentially international writer that he always wanted to be. As K. David Jackson writes in his 2010 study Adverse Genres in Fernando Pessoa, “Pessoa has not taken up his deserved position” alongside Pound, Eliot, Duchamp, Cavafy, Mandelstam, Kafka, Rilke, Borges, “and other foundational figures” of Modernism.12 Here I want to align him with some of these canonical figures (and others) internationally, showing how he came—independently—to many of the same artistic conclusions, above all in his prose book, O livro do desassossego (The Book of Disquiet), the collection of fragments on which he worked intermittently from 1912 or 1913 until his death. For it is arguably

in prose rather than in poetry that Pessoa contributes most effectively to international 117 Modernism. Resituating Pessoa among his international contemporaries in this way is, necessarily, also to assess the extent to which his prose, rather than his poetry, was built for—and even benefits from—translation into other tongues, and for the posthumous reassembly which has been its fate, as Pessoa himself intended. The Book of Disquiet, as I argue, echoes elements of Modernism in different ways at the level of each individual fragment, and in the form of the book as a whole. Where the individual fragments of Disquiet recall many other writers’—especially poets’—voices, the book in its entirety also specifically illuminates a tradition of Modernist literary fragments and ruins which remain intrinsically unfinished and unfinishable, in a way which is linked to the entire wider project of literary Modernism: from Franz Kafka’s three unfinished novels, Robert Musil’s The Man Without Qualities and Walter Benjamin’s The Arcades Project to various incomplete posthumous edifices of Modernist life-writing, including collections of letters. Yet this is only one aspect of Pessoa’s multifarious achievement, which touches most of the other canonical American and European Modernists along the way, and finally goes beyond them too, pointing the way to Postmodernism.

To resituate Pessoa in this way is also in line with his own wishes. For from the first, Pessoa emphatically did not want to be restricted to Portugal in his work. Indeed, he sporadically wanted to be an English writer, and saw himself as contributing to the wider sphere of literature outside Portugal. In one of his many instances of self-publicity, for example, Pessoa wrote an advertisement to be sent to a list of British publications for the new volume of poems by one of his most prominent heteronyms, Alberto Caeiro. Pessoa tellingly refers to Coleridge—while breathlessly asserting Caeiro’s extraordinary originality.

The Rime of the Ancient Mariner in relation to its time is, if anything, less original than Alberto Caeiro’s astonishing volume—The Keeper of Sheep (O Guardador de Rebanhos)—which has just appeared in Lisbon.

No one in Portugal’s literary milieu had ever heard of him. He appeared suddenly. And his contribution to Portuguese and European literature breaks away (…) from all traditions and currents that were valid in the past or are active today.13

The reference to the contribution to both Portuguese and European literature is crucial here, as is the contradictory, simultaneous denial of tradition and comparison with Coleridge. The very writing of the advertisement can be seen as an attempt to break out of a merely Portuguese frame of reference, just as it shows Pessoa also attempting to break out from the weight of the traditions he knew so well. Perhaps more strikingly, when it came to the question of what language Pessoa would choose to write in were the Iberian Peninsula to be split into different nations, such as Castilian and Catalan, Pessoa sides with English. “Unamuno put the case: why not write in Castilian? If it comes to that, I prefer to write in English, which will give me a wider public than Castilian; and I am as much Castilian as I am English in blood and much more English since my education is English.”14 Yet Pessoa’s genuine sense of biculturalism

118 and Englishness was thwarted, as he remained in Lisbon: he became, as Jorge de Sena put it, “a fictitious Englishman, with no basis in reality.”15 Consciously, however, Pessoa sought to transcend the Portuguese literary milieu, and sought this “wider public.” And he often did write in English. Much of his literary critical output is written in English, and deals with the English canon.16 He also produced chapbooks of poetry written in English—35 Sonnets (1918), Antinous (1918), and English Poems I–II and III (1921) —which he sent to English journals to be reviewed. The reception of these books points to the problems he faced when writing in English, since these chapbooks were archaic in form and language, and provoked only short notices (the Glasgow Herald noted their “Shakespearean” register and “a certain crabbedness of speech”; the Times Literary Supplement their “ultra-Shakespearian Shakespearianisms”17). Santos agrees: “To be sure, Pessoa’s heavily convoluted English poems, especially his Shakespearean sonnets, sound somewhat old-fashioned and stilted and convey little of the freshness and innovativeness of his best Portuguese poems”18; yet she also notes that Jorge de Sena, in his book Fernando Pessoa & Ca Heterónima, “is the only Pessoan critic who considers the English poems as comparing not unfavorably with poems written by English poets… of the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries.”19 As Sena argued, these English poems were proto-Modernist in their rediscovery of metaphysical poetry, and in that sense, were underappreciated. Yet to some extent at least, one can acknowledge that Pessoa’s distance from the register of spoken English meant that his diction and his phraseology in his poetry also belonged uncomfortably and unintentionally to another era. Pessoa was to fare much better in English with the help of translators, years after his death. After his initial English venture, and a failed attempt to be published in England, he turned back to Portugal. The influence of other literatures always remained strong, however, perhaps due to Pessoa’s ambivalence about his own Portuguese literary inheritance. Portugal, he asserted, “has never yet had a great literature, just a small and insignificant one, so that everything in this field still needs to be done.”20 Pessoa absorbed literatures from across the world, finding special affinities with Wordsworth, Coleridge, Keats, Browning, Poe, and Whitman, among others. In Europe, Pessoa was also well aware of Cubism, as well as Futurism: he once wrote a long, detailed letter to Marinetti21, and the poems of his heteronym Álvaro de Campos, such as the deck-clearing “Ultimatum” and “Ode Marítima”, are very Futurist in style—almost consciously, in a mode of satirical pastiche. Yet much of the interest of Pessoa’s absorption of other writers lies in the confluence of his relative isolation and the many echoes of other Modernists in his work. Pessoa offers a unique opportunity as a case study of European and American Modernism, in that he had imbibed many of the same influences as other writers, and came to different, if intriguingly related, artistic conclusions, styles, and forms. It is in this aspect of his work that we can sense shadow Modernisms, even what could be called imaginary Modernisms—as Pessoa follows paths which other writers might have taken, but did not; or, just as intriguingly, finds himself taking the same paths unconsciously. Occasionally, when reading The Book of Disquiet, in particular, the momentary frisson of recognising another writer’s voice is so strong as to seem like ventriloquism or mimicry. There are

too many such instances, and there are too many writers echoed, for this to be a case 119 of imitation. Rather, Pessoa, or the many different writers in Pessoa, came to some of the same key junctures and issues as other writers. Or perhaps there were so many different writers within Pessoa that the spectral haunting of other styles was inevitable.

In The Book of Disquiet, which Pessoa ultimately attributed to his semi-heteronym Bernardo Soares, an “assistant bookkeeper in the city of Lisbon,” he sometimes seems to write, in turn, like Eliot, Pound, Proust, and Stevens, to name only a few voices caught within these pages. He was born the same year as Eliot, but there is no evidence that he knew his work.22 Like Eliot, he was profoundly influenced by Shakespeare, Dante, and the French Symbolists. He too read Poe via Baudelaire, and rejected Milton (Paradise Lost, for all its “metrical science,” is “dull, and we must not lie to our souls by denying it”23). Like Pound, he strongly resembles both Walt Whitman (Pessoa was enormously influenced by Leaves of Grass, and his heteronym Álvaro de Campos wrote an outrageous “Salutation to Walt Whitman”), and Robert Browning, whose dramatic monologues were formative in Pessoa’s thinking about his “drama in souls.”24 His resemblances with Wallace Stevens go beyond a similar concern with writing as an attempt to fill the void left by the departure of Christianity, converging in uncannily similar patterns of imagery25. At several points, Pessoa especially recalls the Stevens of “Sunday Morning,” when he notes that “fictitious comfort, if I don’t think about it, is real enough. And there are so many things that comfort! There’s the blue sky above, clear and calm… There’s warmth when it’s warm, and coolness when it’s cool.”26 At other moments, Pessoa revels in memory like Proust, as with the smell of bread from a bakery, “my childhood rises up from a distant neighbourhood, and another bakery emerges from that fairyland which is everything we ever had that has died” (BD, 233–234).

And yet Pessoa always transcends these echoes, and his scope seems almost omnivorous. At one point in his English literary criticism, which was often obsessively concerned with calibrating different levels of talent, he makes the admittedly odd comparison between Edgar Wallace and Shakespeare, before deciding that the reason the latter writer was the greater was because “There is an Edgar Wallace in Shakespeare.”27 The same inclusion of other writers marks Pessoa’s best work. Whereas Browning invented voices for each of his poems, Pessoa invented poets, all of whom were granted an ongoing existence outside the poem, inside himself. These various voices came and went throughout his life, like friends, and Pessoa did his best to identify each one accurately, although he sometimes wavered as to attributions, more and more as time went on. One chilling concern of Pessoa’s late ”orthonymic” poetry of the 1930s (i.e., written under his own name) is whether anything is left of Pessoa himself, and if so, who that self might be. Amidst the chaos of all the 27,543 largely unpublished papers Pessoa left at his death in a trunk, this question is the one that still seems most haunting, and never more so than in The Book of Disquiet, which often strikes a Prufrockian note, before going one step further. “We’re something that goes on during the show’s intermission,” (BD, 63) Soares writes, in this Prufrockian vain, until his more extreme sense of paralysis makes Prufrock’s indecision appear somehow tame.

I suddenly felt like one of those damp rags used for house-cleaning that are taken to the window to dry but are forgotten, balled up, on the sill where they slowly leave a stain (BD, 32) … I didn’t even act anything out. I was the role that got acted. At most, I was the actor’s motions. (BD, 39)

The Book of Disquiet can be seen as Pessoa’s masterpiece, with its collection of fragments that tell no story other than its author’s constantly shifting states of mind and feeling over a very long stretch of mostly undated time. The autobiography of a man who never really lived, or a diary without dates, Disquiet recounts, in thousands of moods, the eventless stretches of its assistant bookkeeper’s existence. It is an imagined book of the soul; or the soul turned inside-out into a book. Sometimes so intensely static and claustrophobic as to be unbearable, it also contains passages of great beauty and cold truth.

As Jorge de Sena was the first to suggest, there were three key phases in the writing of Disquiet. In his seminal “Introdução ao Livro do Desassossego,” later collected in Fernando Pessoa & Ca Heterónima, Sena notes how the first phase was written between 1912 and 191728, leaving the work more or less in suspension in the second phase throughout most of the 1920s, while the final phase was written from 1929 to 1934.29 Alternating in modes and styles that range from Decadence and post-Symbolism through to a voice that seems at times to anticipate Postmodernism, the book is a radically uneven work that spans most of Pessoa’s mature creative life. Unpublished during Pessoa’s lifetime, and, as he wrote in one of his many notes to himself about possible editions of his work, probably “unpublishable” (BD, 471), it was first projected for publication by Sena in the 1960s, although this edition was abandoned after ten years of work. The first Portuguese edition was not to appear until 1982, and since then the book has surfaced in various Portuguese permutations.30 Almost another decade passed before an English version, when four translations appeared at once in 1991: by Margaret Jull Costa, Alfred MacAdam, Iain Watson, and Richard Zenith. Each English version of this fragmentary prose work has a different sequence and number of texts, as Pessoa never left strict instructions as to what order the loose pages he grouped together for the book should go in, and seemed rather unsure himself. There are also many texts that are not labelled “B of D” and yet seem—to an editor, that is—to belong to it. The default version I refer to here is the fifth translation so far into English—itself an expanded and revised version of one of the 1991 translations—Richard Zenith’s 2001 edition, which looks likely to prove the most definitive English version of Disquiet for some time yet. The whole Disquiet project can be seen, in its notebook/diary-like entries, to be a logbook wherein Pessoa registers a lifetime’s worth of reflections on his own mutability, and Zenith’s editions allow the book to be its fragmentary and uneven self, always on the point of disintegrating, rather than a smooth, finished product. They are also the only English editions to offer direct references to the Archive envelopes in the notes.

Each of all these five editions of Disquiet in English creates a very different overall

book, showing the extent to which the selection and sequence of texts imposes different

readings of the same material. Zenith’s 1991 edition has a different title, The Book of Disquietude, contains 532 texts, and includes the titled passages, which are removed 121 from the main text and placed in a separate appendix in his 2001 version, which apart from its appendices contains 481 texts. Margaret Jull Costa’s translation, based on Maria José de Lancastre’s edition, contains 259 texts, and is more fluent and quotidian in tone, smoothing over some of the more Symbolist passages. Meanwhile, Iain Watson’s translation contains 141 texts, and brings out the more Decadent flavour, placing many titled passages in the centre of the book. Even in their layout, these latter two versions make the book seem perhaps more coherent than it is. And yet, there can be no edition that does not impose a certain thematic reading on what is otherwise a loose group of disordered pages. Zenith’s two editions, for example, thread the late passages (the only dated ones) from 1929 to 1934 in chronological order throughout the book, creating an insidious sense of drama. Even the opening passage that Zenith favours as Text 1 places Pessoa in the context of godlessness, a theme which is enlarged upon as the book goes on, instantly making its ”disquiet” seem like an era-defining breaking down of systems and traditions. This wider sense of ”political disquiet” is perhaps best expressed in Zenith’s Text 175; and the disorder it describes and diagnoses seems like the perfect subject for a book which is consequently so open-ended, and as we shall see, such a ruin itself.

Our fathers blithely wreaked destruction, for they lived in a time that was still informed by the solidity of the past. The very thing they destroyed was what gave strength to society and enabled them to destroy without noticing that the building was cracking. We inherited the destruction and its aftermath. (BD, 157)

II

In an essay on Pessoa that predated the first publication of The Book of Disquiet, Gabriel Josipovici writes of “five grey-suited gentlemen: Constantin Cavafy, Franz Kafka,

T.S. Eliot, Fernando Pessoa, Jorge Luis Borges,” all of whom embody “the essential spirit of modernism.”31 Josipovici hints at a worldwide, cosmopolitan Modernism in this comparison of these “grey-suited” writers, noting how “each belongs to a city and has made that city his own: Alexandria, Prague, London, Lisbon, Buenos Aires.”32 Each of these figures can be seen as a typical Modernist everyman, reminiscent of Leopold Bloom in Ulysses—a copy of which Pessoa had in his library—in their ordinariness, their often bureaucratic existences, their concealment of their infinite selves beneath a plain, drab suit. They are all, Josipovici writes, “men whose lives contain little that is dramatic or extraordinary,” and yet “they are the true revolutionaries of our era.”33 This vision of Modernism is necessarily retrospective, since all of these writers’ major works, except those of Eliot, were published relatively late, or even posthumously. Yet it is curious to think of the global correspondences between their work, as they developed it in their different cities. Pessoa, especially in his desk-bound Disquiet persona as Bernardo Soares, joins this cosmopolitan group of poetic and novelistic everymen with extraordinary ease.

122 But as Jorge de Sena noted in Fernando Pessoa & Ca Heterónima, the different phases of the construction of Disquiet, over more than two decades, showed very different aspects of Pessoa, and the more Modernist persona of Soares in Disquiet only emerged in the later phases. Richard Zenith credits Sena with having produced “the best critical introduction” to Disquiet, and writes that “virtually every subsequent introduction, including mine, has borrowed directly or indirectly from what Sena wrote.”34 Sena’s pioneering work on Disquiet for the edition which never came to fruition in the 1960s makes him, in a sense, the book’s most ghostly editor: his introduction for an unmade edition, as Zenith suggests, has permeated all subsequent editions and readings, even though Sena died before the first Portuguese version of Disquiet ever appeared. Perhaps Sena’s unmade edition is also, ironically, the most appropriate arrangement of Disquiet: where other editors have often noted that the book, ideally, would take the form of a loose-leaf, unstructured edition, none have been able to resist the challenge to impose some structure on it. Sena’s influence, in particular, has highlighted the way in which it took some time for Pessoa to shake off being something of a Decadent, if he did so at all, and the way in which this change, like all of his personal oscillations, is registered in the microscopically self-obsessed fluctuations of Disquiet. During its long gestation, Disquiet changed authorship in Pessoa’s conception from a certain Vicente Guedes to Soares, who Pessoa describes not so much as a fully-fledged heteronym as “a mere mutilation” of his own personality.35 Sena was the first to recognize that Soares is deliberately, as Ellen Sapega notes, “less than his creator, not only in the sense that he inhabits a lower social station and has benefited from fewer educational or cultural opportunities, but also because he incarnates a different range of emotions.”36 Sena’s position on Disquiet thus emphasizes Pessoa’s eventual transformation throughout the book, via Soares, into a great Modernist, valuing the Soares fragments over the earlier ones by Guedes. Through his specific influence on Zenith’s editions, Sena’s reading has meant that the English versions of Disquiet often prioritize its Modernist elements, even while revealing its split identity. The Portuguese versions have often made more of Guedes (even sometimes granting him co-authorship along with Soares37); and the attribution not only of individual fragments, but of the final book, remains a great point of contention. Yet Disquiet in English, to date, has tended to belong more unequivocally to Soares. Both Guedes and Soares are Modernist everyman types in terms of their nondescript business careers, but in changing Guedes to Soares, Pessoa tones down the Decadent flavour of his initial portrait. Guedes, like Paul Valéry’s Monsieur Teste, is a marvel of nature, almost superhuman: “I doubt that any outwardly human creature has lived in their consciousness of self in a more complex fashion” (BD, 466). In the portrait of Soares, however, the semi-heteronym is presented as solitary, but more ordinary, with a “pale, uninteresting face” which brings to mind “the indifference that comes from having already suffered alot” (BD, 5). Guedes is explicitly a “dandy,” whereas Soares’s only affectation is implied by his awareness of Orpheu, the literary journal Pessoa helped found. Like Eliot, Soares/Pessoa is unable to maintain a Romantic conception of himself, since such a self-image is always undermined by “a hearty snicker from the

other I that’s always nearby, like a downtown street” (BD, 53). Yet, for all his dailiness 123 and the many “grey,” superbly uneventful passages in the office, Soares sometimes still resembles Monsieur Teste. His project, and the drab rooms in which he lives, retain a hint of the diabolical aura that Valéry’s narrator finds in Teste, when he has

a glimpse of feelings in him that made me shudder, a terrible obstinacy in his delirious experiments. He was a man absorbed in his own variations, one who becomes his own system... I have never had a stronger impression of the ordinary... I was terrified by the infinite dreariness possible in that abstract and banal place.38

The shift from Guedes to Soares was crucial for Pessoa, and a very similar shift was made by Eliot—who, we recall, shared many of the same literary influences as Pes-soa—in order to become ”modern.” In an introduction to Valéry’s “The Art of Poetry,” Eliot refers back to an 1889 essay by Valéry called “On Literary Technique,” which interests him because, as Eliot writes, “what it announces is no less than a new style for poets, as well as a new style for poetry.”39 The decadent roles of “the satanist, the dandy, the poète maudit”40 are shaken off by Valéry for another conception, that of the “cool scientist, almost an algebraist.”41 This “new conception of the poet,” Eliot writes, turns the tower of ivory into a laboratory, with the writer like “the austere, bespectacled man in a white coat, whose portrait appears in advertisements, weighing out or testing the drugs of which is compounded some medicine with an impressive name.”42 Yet even here, Eliot’s pharmaceutical image bears traces of its metamorphosis from the remnants of Decadence—and the final development for many Modernists was to replace this white lab coat for Josipovici’s grey business suit.

Likewise, Soares/Pessoa sometimes sees his project in quasi-scientific terms, although unlike Valéry’s Monsieur Teste, he is concerned almost exclusively with feelings, rather than thoughts, paying “special attention to his own inner life, subjecting it to analysis with a precision instrument created out of himself” (BD, 74). And again, there is a strong trace of Decadence left in Soares’s conception of “the future historian of his own sensations” who “may be able to make a precise science out of the attitude he takes towards his self-awareness.” “We’re only in the beginnings of this difficult art,” Soares writes, “—at this point just an art: the chemistry of sensations in its as yet alchemical stage” (BD, 74).

One typical movement of a Disquiet passage tends to begin with the solitary I attempting to capture his feelings with scientific precision, “to photograph what he feels” (BD, 81) in words, or describing the weather, before conflating the weather outside and the internal weather of his emotions. The image of photography recurs explicitly very often, although unlike Isherwood, Pessoa is not a camera but “an ultrasensitive photographic plate” (BD, 436). The plate, like the page, “fills up with nothing but me” (BD, 436). The problem that Pessoa soon discovers with trying to capture these states in writing, however, is that as the image develops on the page, as it were, the initial feeling that he is trying to trap undergoes metamorphosis, becoming something else entirely. And so all that he can record are the ruins of his emotions, “the morbid

124 dribbling of sensations when they crumble and rot” (BD, 399). Robert Musil makes a similar observation in the mass of posthumous papers that form a part of his unfinished Modernist novel, The Man Without Qualities:

Because they are constantly fluid, emotions cannot be stopped; nor can they be looked at “under the microscope”. This means that the more closely we observe them, the less we know what it is we feel. Attention is already a change in the emotion.43

Pessoa, of course, means ”person” or ”persona” in Portuguese, and there is much wordplay in the original revolving around this meaning in his book,44 which seems to enact, almost in slow motion, the fragmentation of the self and the corresponding breakdown of the narrative voice: the sense that, as Valéry puts it, “The I, perhaps, is no more than a conventional notion.”45 Pessoa often fuses and compares the pages of his book with his self, so that the two come to mean almost the same thing, and in Portuguese this conflation comes out even more when he writes, for instance, as Irene Ramalho Santos points out, how he hates “the badly written page itself, as if it were a proper person [como pessoa própria].”46 In a similar way to Stevens, Pessoa’s pages in Disquiet are close to being a constant reinvestigation of his aesthetic intentions—a statement of what the function of the work will be and therein a kind of fulfilment, merely by the stating, of this function. But Pessoa also keeps on shifting his position, at the same time shuffling his bank of symbols, ideas, descriptions and images—suits, souls, selves, the sun, the wind, the office, the city streets, the liminal states between waking and sleeping, dreaming and reality—in different combinations. An extended reading of the book immerses the reader in this world to a point where all the different motifs start to exchange values, becoming interchangeable. Although all the passages are written in prose, this is a prose poetry, marked by a stasis that is almost the opposite of narrative. And each passage starts the process of self-analysis and reflection all over again, often coming to different conclusions. “I soar as someone else—that’s all,” Pessoa wrote in a studiedly careless letter to an editor from 1931, where he tries to explain the nature of his entire heteronymic project:

As soon as the critic understands that I’m essentially a dramatic poet, he will have the key to my personality... Armed with this key, he can slowly open all the doors to my self-expression. He knows that as a poet I feel; that as a dramatic poet I feel with complete detachment from my feeling self; that as a dramatist (without the poet) I automatically transform what I feel into an expression far removed from what I felt, and I create, in my emotions, a nonexistent person who truly felt that feeling and, in feeling it, felt yet other, related emotions that I, purely I, forgot to feel.47

The final twist of that process, in the Disquiet prose passages, is the rereading of these pieces to start the cycle of feeling all over again. The cycle is a kind of verbal machine or system that turns the human heart into a “clockwork train”48 that Josipovici places alongside “the bachelor machines of Duchamp and the twittering machine of Klee.”49 Disquiet is the most extended display of that system at work, pushing it until it breaks down. It is a very self-consciously unfinished whole, an intensely self-aware wreck of 125 a book; and throughout its pages Pessoa argues constantly with himself as to whether there is any chance of his managing to engage in literary “construction,” when his self is so inconsistent that he cannot even agree on its inconsistency. Sometimes he blames the “present-day milieu [which] makes it impossible for constructive qualities to appear in the human spirit” (BD, 214). Whether because of this “milieu” or his own personality, as his self shifts ever more unnervingly from page to page, it becomes impossible to finish a coherent piece of work, perhaps even a short poem or prose passage. Eliot makes an interesting comment in relation to this, again in his introduction to Valéry’s “The Art of Poetry”:

I think I understand what Valéry means when he says that a poem is never finished: at least, his words to this effect have a meaning for me. To me they mean that a poem is “finished”, or that I will never touch it again, when I am sure that I have exhausted my own resources, that the poem is as good as I can make that poem. It may be a bad poem: but nothing that I can do will make it better. Yet I cannot help thinking that, even if it is a good poem, I could have made a better poem of it—the same poem, but better—if I were a better poet.50

Pessoa is a better poet than himself, frequently, and this is part of his dilemma, enacted as his book goes on. It is a dilemma he encounters both at the level of each individual prose passage in Disquiet, but also to a much greater extent at the scale of the book as a whole. Deciding that “the creators of metaphysical systems and [blank] of psychological explanations are still in the primary stage of suffering” (BD, 341)—and deriding all religions and philosophies “that the drowsy children of the abyss invented for sport” (BD, 178), Pessoa builds the notion of his book’s unfinishability into its plan.

III

In its self-contradictions and broken chains of logic, as well as its combined scope and fragmentariness, Disquiet aligns startlingly well with a Modernist tradition of crumbling monuments: most notably, as I have already suggested, Kafka’s three unfinished novels, Musil’s The Man Without Qualities, Pound’s Cantos, and Benjamin’s Arcades Project. Kafka and Musil were both struggling with the very form of the novel—Musil to the extent that even after 20 years his efforts to finish his book simply undermined the foundations he had laid for it. Part of Musil’s problem—again, one shared by Pessoa but to a lesser extent, in that he was not writing a novel—was that he worked on The Man Without Qualities for so long that, as he noted, “During my work on it and under my hand this book has become a historical novel; it takes place twenty-five years ago!”51 The very long stretch of time elapsed in its creation pulled against its ultimate coherence. Likewise Pound, in Canto CXVI, finds himself surrounded by his “errors and wrecks” and “cannot make it cohere.”52

But in his specific obsession with the form of ruins, Pessoa’s book most resembles Benjamin’s Arcades Project. Both books remain unfinished and to a large extent un

126 sequenced, while showing clear signs that their component parts have been refined to a rare degree. Benjamin revised “earlier drafts, notes, or letters,” and even “transferred masses of quotations from actual notebooks to the manuscript of the convolutes,”53 thereby wishing to “assemble large-scale constructions out of the smallest and most precisely cut components.”54 Pessoa, to the despair of his editors, obsessed over the individual passages marked for Disquiet, and seems especially to have been concerned with polishing their endings, to the point where alternate words are often left forever hovering in the margins. Both writers share an interest in decay and refuse, Benjamin wanting only “the rags, the refuse”55 of history to pin and mount in his verbal museum, Pessoa often identifying with “all the junk on the outskirts, sold by weight among the postscripts of the broken” (BD, 54). Of a potential edition of the poems that he eventually removed from Disquiet, Pessoa wrote that it “should have a title indicating that it contains something like refuse or marginalia” and could “form part of a definitive collection of dregs” (BD, 471). Both the Arcades Project and Disquiet are books with a hole in their construction in another sense, Benjamin working on his project from 1928–29 and taking it up again in 1934, Pessoa stopping work on his for most of the 1920s. Both books were eventually published posthumously for the first time in 1982, and were eventually assembled by editors. But while Benjamin’s aim was to shatter the dream of commodity fetishism and “destroy the mythic immediacy of the present,”56 Pessoa shatters and destroys the illusion of the continuity and coherence of his self—which, like his book, is doomed to remain eternally unfinished:

I’m the ruins of buildings that were never more than ruins, whose builder, halfway through, got tired of thinking about what he was building. (BD, 61)

At numerous points throughout Disquiet, Pessoa asserts that the only possible perfection lies in imperfection. His book is an intentional ruin, since “the only great and perfect works are the ones we never dream of realising” (BD, 248). And yet there is also the unmistakable sense that the unfinishability of Pessoa’s project haunts him as a compromise, the only solution left open to a quicksilver cast of mind that is unable to construct anything more solid or complete. In a slightly shrill voice, Pessoa frequently calls Shakespeare a “failure,” and imagines the remorse he would feel if he had written King Lear, because “the sheer greatness of this work grossly magnifies its defects” (BD, 247). The strain of self-persuasion in passages such as this seems even stronger in the light of the very conscious striving for literary greatness that seems to have come upon Pessoa around 1929, the year he wrote to his one-time sweetheart, Ophelia Queiroz (perhaps just to shake her off), that “It’s time for me to consolidate my literary work, finishing up certain things, compiling others, and writing some things that are still in my head.”57 His “Erostratus” passages, dating from 1930, are filled with reflections on literary greatness and calculating schemes as to how to achieve it, given the spasmodic nature of his talent, and the way in which “whatever can’t be done in a single burst suffers from the unevenness of our spirit” (BD, 247). There is no point, according to Pessoa, in trying to write anything more sustained than a poem or a passage that can be 127 composed in one sitting, since longer forms are scarred by their lack of inner coherence:

Our civilisation, so rich and so complex, has produced extraordinary lyrics, unparalleled in range, depth, and comprehension and subtlety. It has not produced any supreme achievement in constructive poetry and literature.58

This idea that he must write everything “in a single burst” seems to have stayed with Pessoa throughout his writing life, and the “triumphal day” of his life, as he wrote in a letter of 193559 was when three key heteronyms appeared in him at once on March 8th, 1914 and dictated more than 30 poems to him in one go. It is significant that this day was eventually recounted in a letter: to some extent, with their intrinsic transience, letters were an ideal literary form for Pessoa (although he also carefully revised many of his letters, and their dates are often thus suspect). He made his living working as a translator of business letters into English and French, “something I have to do almost every day”60, and all of his fragmentary prose writing owes much to the form of letters. Sometimes, such as in a letter to Mário de Sá-Carneiro from 1916, Pessoa decides that he can incorporate real letters into his book, making a typed copy “so as to include some of its sentences and grimaces in The Book of Disquiet” (BD, 469). And many of Pessoa’s explicatory writings as to how his complete oeuvre should be potentially assembled, interpreted, and published, exist in the form of letters. Though perhaps self-consciously myth-making, such letters have proved invaluable for critics explaining the nature of Pessoa’s heteronymic system. By Pessoa’s standards, they are quite sustained passages of prose, and he claims to have written them

off the cuff, as fast as this typewriter will let me... using whatever expressions come to mind, without regard to their literary content. Imagine—for it’s true—that I’m just talking to you.61

The Book of Disquiet can thus also be seen very productively in the context of Modernist life-writing, in its proximity to the form of letters and diaries, and above all in its recombinant and unfinished nature, to editions of letters and diaries, with the simultaneous juxtaposition of refined individual parts and an incomplete whole. Pessoa realised this similarity, and played on this proximity, writing of the book of poems which he ultimately discluded from Disquiet that it would be “somewhat analogous to a book of unfinished poems by a poet who died young, or the letters of a great writer” (BD, 471). He noted of Henri-Frédéric Amiel’s diary, Fragments d’un journal intime, which had a great influence on the Vicente Guedes entries in Disquiet, that it would have been a far greater work if he hadn’t published anything—“that’s where he falls down” (BD, 109).

Individually, and in the way that they are posthumously assembled in a book, the Disquiet passages are very close to the forms both of the diary entry and the letter. Even Bernardo Soares’s day job as an “assistant bookkeeper” in a fabric warehouse, working on his financial accounts, takes us close to the very origins of the diary: as

128 Philippe Lejeune writes in On Diary (2009), “the form of the account book probably acted as an inspiration or model for the less financial and more personal journals that people began keeping of their other “properties” in the modern era.”62 Soares often very pointedly tells us that he is keeping a kind of account book of the soul, entering debits and credits which fluctuate day by day, and are kept in a similarly habitual way. However, letters and diaries are often both defined by the way in which they are marked with specific dates and times, and one of the ways that Disquiet defies both these forms is in its flouting of chronology and dating (apart from the dated passages from the 1930s). Yet its unfinished nature might be due in part to its similarity to these forms, which are likewise intrinsically unfinishable as wholes—other than by the intervention of death. In his essay “How Do Diaries End?”, Lejeune notes that “an autobiography is virtually finished as soon as it begins, since the story that you begin must end at the moment that you are writing it. You know the end point of the story, because you have reached it, and everything you write will lead up to this point, explaining how you got there.”63 The diary, meanwhile, “is virtually unfinishable from the beginning, because there is always a time lived beyond the writing, making it necessary to write anew, and one day, this time beyond will take the shape of death.”64 For Lejeune, “all journal writing assumes the intention to write at least one more time, an entry that will call for yet another one, and so on without end.”65 Letters are perhaps more discrete in their nature than the journal or diary entry, and in this regard are more finishable. Likewise, it is quite common for discrete letters to be highly polished or revised pieces of writing. Yet once one begins to select or collect letters in editions—as so often happens with writers’ letters—such editions can never be said to be completed. Many letters are inevitably lost between the two sides of any epistolary exchange; there is always the chance that more letters might be discovered. A correspondence can never be said to be truly over until one of the correspondents has died. As Lejeune also reveals, “it is the fate of the great majority of journals to be, at some point, destroyed, either by the authors or by their descendants”66 —and letters are equally prone to destruction by their recipients and their authors. This is another quality, at least in aspiration, that Disquiet shares with both these forms. For there is an abiding and disturbing sense that Pessoa quite deliberately mutilated Disquiet. The reflections on the qualities of literary form in “Erostratus” show him meditating on precisely this aspect of his book. Pessoa named the Erostratus passages after the Greek who in 356 BC set fire to the Temple of Diana, one of the Seven Wonders of the World, to make his name immortal.67 In these various texts about literary fame and form, Pessoa writes that literature in the future must be concise, and his forecast, generally, is one of ongoing compression and subtraction, a natural distillation of each writer’s essence. As the years pass, each anthology of great poets’ works will be gradually reduced in volume, worn down to rubble by a kind of temporal erosion.68 Pessoa praises Poe for foreseeing the “necessity of the shorter poems... this was one of his visions of a future, as the detective story was one of his anticipations of it.”69 And beneath these

musings, we can sense him wondering if there is a way to salvage Disquiet, perhaps by 129 an act of literary vandalism akin to that of Erostratus.

Both the fragmentary form of the book, and even the trunk, can be seen as a deliberate, and entirely successful, manufacture of a faux-relic. Pessoa’s mutilated text is replete with the sorts of problems of transcription, conservation, interpretation and selection usually associated with a much older text such as Pascal’s Pensées. In some ways this sense of the book as a faux-relic reinforces its Decadent elements. Even as a young man, Pessoa wrote that “I have always had in consideration... the case of a man becoming immortal under a pseudonym, his real name hidden and unknown.”70 Given Pessoa’s love/hate relationship with Oscar Wilde, and the extent to which he saw his project as posthumous, the Disquiet pages, in their self-destructive bid for immortality, recall a warped inversion of the Dorian Gray story:

Ah, my love, the glory of works which have been lost for ever, of treatises which today are mere titles, of libraries which burned down, of statues which were demolished! How blessed with Absurdity are the artists who set fire to a beautiful work! (BD, 278)

IV

Despite the slightly deathly cast which sometimes tinges Pessoa’s pages, the terrifying sense that Pessoa has sacrificed his life and his self in order to fill out a tomb-like, “immortal” book, the unfinished status of Disquiet—whether premeditated or merely intrinsic to its nature—is also what takes it beyond the realms of many of its Modernist contemporaries. In the last of the lectures on literature written at the end of his life, Italo Calvino talks of “multiplicity” as “the thread that binds together the major works both of what is called modernism and of what goes by the name of the postmodern.”71 He groups together a plethora of unfinished books, including Musil’s novel, Flaubert’s Bouvard et Pécuchet, Gadda’s That Awful Mess on the Via Merulana, and Proust’s À la recherche du temps perdu, tracing their tendency towards “a confluence and clash of a multiplicity of interpretative methods, modes of thought, and styles of expression,”72 and their common failure “to find an ending.” (Proust, in fact, did know his ending, yet his book grew endlessly from the inside out, as Calvino says.) Calvino also mentions Paul Valéry in this context, not for “The Evening with Monsieur Teste,” but for the “prose work composed of essays of only a few pages and notes a few lines long, found in his notebooks,”73 which is much more similar to Disquiet in overall form, where Teste is similar in terms of the central figure of Guedes/Soares. (Notebooks being another form, like letters and diaries, which are often intrinsically unfinishable projects.)

In this company, Pessoa’s unassembled papers seem prescient in their endlessly reconstructable form and potentiality. Writing of Gadda’s unfinished, fragmentary novels, Calvino states how—as we have seen both in Disquiet and Benjamin’s Arcades—their form resembles “the ruins of ambitious projects that nevertheless retain traces of the splendour and meticulous care with which they were conceived.”74 Although he makes

130 no mention of Pessoa, Calvino’s very term ”multiplicity” echoes him. As António Mora, one of Pessoa’s philosopher heteronyms, says, “reality... is multiple,”75 and in the words of Soares/Pessoa:

Each of us is several, is many, is a profusion of selves... In the vast colony of our being there are many species of people who think and feel in different ways. [Zenith’s translation. BD, 327–328]

Each of us is more than one person, many people, a proliferation of our one self... In the vast colony of our being there are many different kinds of people, all thinking and feeling differently. [Text 15, Costa’s translation]

Each one of us is multiple in our unique selves, is myriad, is a proliferation of ourselves... There are beings of many sorts in the vast colony of one being, who think and feel differently. [Page 6, Watson’s translation]

One can also productively compare Pessoa’s multiplicity with that of Proust, as Calvino suggests. Especially towards the end of the Recherche, Proust repeatedly dwells on ideas of the multiplicity of the self, on “those innumerable and humble ‘selves’ that compose our personality,”76 declaring “a truly objective truth… namely that none of us is single, that each of us contains many persons who do not all have the same moral value” (ROTP

V: 605). Proust finds the same problem with the microscopic analysis of character and sensation that Musil (and Pessoa) noted. “It is as difficult,” Proust writes, “to present a fixed image of a character as of societies and passions. For a character alters no less than they do, and if one tries to take a snapshot of what is relatively immutable in it, one finds it presenting a succession of different aspects… to the disconcerted lens” (ROTP V: 373). Passions above all, in Proust, provoke even more mutability—and are rendered problematic by this very multiplicity. “I was not one man only,” Proust writes, “but as it were the march-past of a composite army in which there were passionate men, indifferent men, jealous men—jealous men not one of whom was jealous of the same woman” (ROTP V: 559). In love above all, “we fabricate a person” (ROTP V: 606) out of these many selves, and out of the many selves we perceive in our beloved. Yet in this focus on love and the emotions, we can perhaps sense the ways in which Proust’s multiplicity differs from that of Pessoa, who is much more detached in his self-analysis, and whose book is seldom, if ever, about his relations with others. Proust’s multiplicity is intrinsically social; Pessoa’s is not at all.

Another central difference between these multiplicities is that, for Proust, chronology also plays a large part in his dissimilar selves. If the narrator of the Recherche records his “amazement at having become another person to whom the sufferings of his predecessor are no more than the sufferings of a stranger” (ROTP V: 682), then this is due to the effects of time, on a grander scale, as much as an essential multiplicity of character. For Proust, “one is no more distressed at having become another person, after a lapse of years and in the natural sequence of time, than one is at any given moment by the fact of one being, one after another, the incompatible persons, malicious, sensitive, caddish, disinterested, ambitious which one can be, in turn, every day of 131 one’s life” (ROTP V: 737). Yet Proust is careful to trace both of these developments— over the grand sweep of time and day by day—whereas in Disquiet the sense of time passing has been shuffled irrecoverably into an endless, dateless horizon of eternal mutability which proves much more disorienting. The lack of dating and chronology in the Disquiet fragments, other than in their final phase, has necessitated reconstructive work, beginning with Sena’s outline of the book’s construction; and this lack not only differentiates Pessoa’s book from the forms of the letter and the diary, but from Proust’s finally more gradual sense of the multiplicity of character. The datelessness and achronology of Disquiet prevent any sure sense of temporal development between its dissimilar selves (other than the broad change from Guedes to Soares), which is always so strong in the Recherche—where, ultimately, the effects of ageing completely transfigure the protagonists in the final volume. Proust also traces the inheritance of each of these “incompatible persons” which coexist in everyone, noting that if personality is a “sequence of juxtaposed but distinct ‘I’s which would die one after the other or even come to life alternately” (ROTP VI: 299), then different elements of this personality are handed down through families.

In so many people there are different strata which are not alike: the character of the father, then of the mother; one traverses first one, then the other. But, next day, the order of their superimposition is reversed: and finally one does not know who will decide between the contestants (ROTP VI: 2).

In other words, Proust is finally reluctant to trace this multiplicity, as Pessoa does, to a total rupture with tradition and the past. There is a stronger sense of linearity in Proust that does not really correlate with Pessoa’s vision of the ruins of the self. And of course, where Pessoa’s book, as we have seen, is quite ostentatiously ruinous, Proust’s novel is a monumental achievement of continuous discontinuity, finding a form which much more seamlessly integrates its mass of incongruous data. The Recherche is a prodigious act of “construction” of precisely the type in which Pessoa could never hope to engage: he was always far too busy falling apart.

V

For all these traces, we are still left with the question: who is Fernando Pessoa? Perhaps it is precisely in his endless echoing of other people rather than himself that Pessoa is most himself. He insists that other people are himself, in a way that disrupts still-tenacious notions of literary originality and of literary voice. If I have traced some of the “hauntings” of his style in this essay, and the way that Pessoa echoes a host of other international writers in his work—with the hope that this will encourage the tracing of further links—perhaps there is a danger that this comparative approach slightly denigrates his oeuvre, or reduces it to a series of similarities, rather than anything else. Yet such an approach is also, to some extent, inescapable with Pessoa’s oeuvre, since

132 his various voices have such range that the underlying question raised by them—as in the “orthonymic” work—is whether the author finally has anything of his own left to say, or any distinctive character or personality, after all this mimicry. This may not be a question that one would ask of a dramatic poet such as Browning, or a playwright such as Shakespeare (or indeed many novelists), even though their work is often likewise filled with disparities of voice and tone. Yet the difference in Pessoa’s case is the way that he refused to close off the barriers between his life and his art, granting his personas and heteronyms their ongoing life inside himself, outside their texts, since the heteronyms continued to evolve long after the specific occasion of poetry or prose generated by them. It could be said that through this blurring of the frames within which life and art take place, Pessoa’s project takes us much closer to the inherent fictionality of life and personality. Pessoa seems, if not at ease, then certainly well-acquainted with this fictionality, and his essential self often seems to be located in the realisation that he has no essential self, other than traces, echoes, influences. One could see the fragmentation of voice this entails as a culmination of what Josipovici calls the “crisis of authority” of Modernism, which, as Josipovici writes, “affected every sphere of activity in Western Europe and America, in the late nineteenth century—political, philosophical, scientific and artistic,” and which, at the artistic level, created “a crisis of confidence in the authority of the author or creator.”77 For Josipovici, Eliot’s Prufrock—in ways that resonate again with Pessoa—shows “the failure of the individual voice, no longer a part of a living tradition, to speak at all.”78 Eliot’s solution was not “to speak in his own voice. Rather, he builds up his next poems by juxtaposing blocks of other people’s words, either those of earlier writers or of numerous personae.”79 Pessoa avoided such montage, but seems even more presciently Postmodern or even Post-Postmodern than Eliot, with his infinity of voices, his laments for synthetic memories that he never had in real life, his sadness at not being able to meet fictional characters, his doubts as to whether he exists, or whether he is merely “the character of a novel” (BD, 245) —and with his sudden Borgesian imagining in Disquiet that the “sum total of the world might be… an interconnected series of dramas and novels,” made up of boxes inside boxes, “everything being a story made up of stories” (BD, 245). On the final page of Six Memos for the Next Millennium, Calvino tackles those who “might object that the more the work tends towards the multiplication of possibilities, the further it departs from that unicum which is the self of the writer, his inner sincerity and the discovery of his own truth. But I would answer: Who are we, who is each one of us, if not a combinatoria of experiences, information, books we have read, things imagined? Each life is an encyclopaedia, a library, an inventory of objects, a series of styles, and everything can be constantly shuffled and reordered in every way conceivable.”80 In this context, Pessoa’s shifting styles and selves feel quite at home. Yet it is worth noting that there is, finally, something distinctively Pessoan about Pessoa, other than his echoing of others. Undoubtedly, there is an intrinsic ghostliness to the Pessoan project, as “by thinking so much,” he became “echo and abyss. By delving within, I made myself into many” (BD, 91). For all that, however, Pessoa sometimes rereads “some of the pages that together will form my book of random impressions” (BD,

363) and realises that his work isn’t in fact always so different. In his self-created role 133 as a kind of writing machine, “as if my being were hollow... as if it were nothing more than a typewriter I carry around with me—portable, opened and ready” (BD, 36), he is also sometimes shocked at the lack of personal evolution, at the essential sameness, that he also shows, one which should undermine his entire theory. And, paradoxically, his most repetitive and characteristic refrain is how multiple his personality is. He still turns it all into prose, which, in the way it reaches us in the multiple versions of the English translations, takes us even further away from his initial feeling:

Even while saying that I’m always different, I feel that I’ve always said the same thing; that I resemble myself more than I’d like to admit. [Zenith’s translation, BD, 363].

I feel that in describing all my different moods, I always use the same words; I feel that I am more like myself than I would like to think. [Costa’s translation, Text 171].

I feel, even when saying I am always different, that I have endlessly repeated the same thing; that I am more like myself than I should like to admit. [Watson’s translation, 99].

This realisation of the coherence of his disparate selves is not to diminish his achievement, but to discover that something essential persists in his character and his personality, even if this something is merely his repeated insistence on his own multiplicity. If this makes the whole Pessoan project of multiplicity seem somehow artificial, then with his love of ruins and broken systems, Pessoa would probably have welcomed this. For one can say with confidence that much as he thought he was “no one, no one at all” (BD, 227), only Pessoa would have dreamed up the project of dividing himself into heteronyms, carrying it through with such persistence and deathly thoroughness—and above all, writing it all down. Only Pessoa would have seen all of his various selves in such consistently literary terms, as “the long-winded commentary on a book never written,” and as “the character of an unwritten novel” (BD, 227). And only Pessoa could have created an oeuvre of such quintessential open-endedness and pure possibility, and imbued it with such languor and decay. For Richard Zenith, there is another quality which distinguishes the Pessoa of Disquiet: honesty. “Strangely or not, it was precisely in his faking, in his self-othering,” writes Zenith, “—a profoundly personal process—that Pessoa was astonishingly true and honest to himself. By being so who he was, and so very Portuguese, he succeeded in being the most foreign and universal of writers.”81 Turning himself inside out so effectively, Pessoa becomes all of us.

While I do agree with Lejeune that the diary is more intrinsically unfinishable than any autobiography, this opposition between diary and autobiography is perhaps a little too clear-cut. Many autobiographies continue into the time of the writing—or take many years to write—and in this way become more like diaries once they reach and cross the present, or recount events that took place during the process of writing. The Book of Disquiet has often been seen in terms of autobiography, and Pessoa’s term a “factless autobiography” suggests the relevance of such an approach.
