The Futurist Johannes R. Becher *

Peter Demetz
Translated by Wilhelm Werthern

With Johannes R. Becher the Germans have a gifted and spirited poet whom they have not yet appreciated; neither the poems of his developing period (1912-26) nor the documents of his extraordinary life (1891-1958) have been read enough. Here and there (apart from a few praiseworthy exceptions) 1 people are busy suppressing the memory of his early works as well as his real life story; and thirty years after his death there is still no biography that would show us how the wild young man transformed himself into the Comrade Minister of State who came to censure himself. European literary history has yet to admit that J. R. Becher, the first Communist Minister of Culture in the GDR, in his earlier and poetically most productive years was more thoroughly attracted to the Italian Futurists than any other German poet of the war generation, and enthusiastically seized on Futurist rhetoric, usually considered protofascist in its ideological implications, in the most effective Leninist poems ever written in the German tongue. Becher himself attempts to interpret and bend into shape the years of his youth and early manhood (at the end, he quotes Stalin's essay on language against the poems of his youth); and he is also a victim of the pedantry of a Germanist, which in its exclusive zeal of its interpretive and metatheoretical strivings, on the Left and the Right, has forgotten that poems, especially good ones, are sensual creatures that anarchically pierce the flatness and grayness of our everyday speech.

It is difficult to describe how the idealistic Munich highschool student revolted against his father, the highest judge in the kingdom of Bavaria, and how at age seventeen, in a romantic suicide pact, killed a part-time prostitute and attempted to kill himself (his father rescued him, pleading insanity). He never finished his studies and was repeatedly treated for morphine addiction. Commuting between Munich and Berlin, a bohemian of the gutters, he lived strangely unharmed through the years of the war and his own confusions (he was not called up because his suicide attempt that lacerated his lungs). He longed for salvation by soulful women and beautiful young men, studied the writings of mystical nuns, Gorky, and Lenin, and was readily welcomed in the best salons, or what was left of them. After the war, he made his slow way to the all-forgiving Communist party, the activism of the orthodox functionary (he had his part, together with Georg Lukács, in undoing the Left avant-garde) and exile in Moscow, where he never intervened for anyone accused of treason by the Secret Police. He returned, by special plane of the Soviet Army, to his devastated fatherland; and was later photographed at the massive desk of his ministry, surrounded by joyous schoolgirls who presented bunches of roses to the loyal poet of the new national anthem. It is a German life: strange, unique, and beyond comparison.

Becher's works resist clean divisions, and even Norbert Hopster, in his praiseworthy dissertation, is not altogether happy with his chronicle of its changes and motifs. The two merge, and the literary loyalties, from Richard Dehmel to Walt Whitman and from Rimbaud to Futurism, blur together and transcend the borders of ideological transformations; even in the final Leninist Maschinenrhythmen (Machine rhythms; 1926) Becher works, for the last time, with Futurist ideas and language patterns.

The years 1912-13 and the beginning of the war pushed the schoolboyish admirer of Richard Dehmel and Waldemar Bonsels into radical modernism. In his collection of poems Die Gnade eines Frühlings (The grace of a spring; 1912) he still published neoromantic songs in praise of Mary ("burningly my mouth arms itself / for you, Mary, sweet life"--this, incidentally, also in protest against the Protestant father), 2 or pastoral lyricism ("so childyoung / the air makes me, so soulblossoming"). 3 Already one year later in De Profundis Domine and Verfall und Triumph (Decay and triumph; 1914) the impressionistic and playful elements are gone; the influences of Georg Heym and Jakob van Hoddis, two young expressionists, draw Becher and his poems into urban Berlin, where everything happens in feverish simultaneity. Nightcafés, cinemas, and gigantic train stations are the new sites of experience: "Locomotives! / Steep-faced with the viaducts' momentum of the most rapid whores." 4 He is fascinated by the war, and after paying tribute like so many of his writing contemporaries to the general patriotic enthusiasm with a cycle of sonnets, Becher, who was never a soldier and who later loved to be photographed in the uniform of the Roter Frontkämpferbund, creates his own myth of war; activated masses, brutality, steely, destructive technologies, and mutilated humans or, as he says, "Menschenmüll" (human garbage). 5 War: at first as the possibility of destroying the bourgeois tradition and the tyranny of the fathers; then, a little later, war as the advance of a holy socialist army against the satanic enemies of humanity, and finally the Leninist war as bloody class struggle, in Germany and in the Soviet Union. For a long time, this personal myth of war as "the only true hygiene of the world" 6 is supported by Futurist motifs and rhetoric, 7 at first in the asyntactical parole in libertà and later mainly by Becher's Futuristic analogies, whose intensity and linguistic power far outreaches Marinetti's tests.

A 1915 text published in the Weiße Blätter signals Becher's Futurist rhetoric, as well as the chronic attraction that the technological brutality of modern warfare had for him. Historically, it is not the first signal (that is to be found in the poetry and earlier), but, by emulating the Italian model, has a particularly massive force. Becher calls his text "Vorworte zum Tagebuch italienischen Fähnrichs Georgio Quadro" (Prologues to the diary of the Italian cadet sergeant Georgio Quadro) and refers to the writings of a young Italian officer. The diary was found after Quadro's death in the Austrian trenches at Krn and published in the Berliner Tageblatt on 19 July 1915. 8 Becher sees the experiences of the patriotic officer, previously a civilian employee at a bank, as symbolic of all the young men of Europe who were pushed into the trenches with big words. In hymnic prose he continues the political education of the young Italian and leads him to the brink of political understanding, before the Austrian bomb tears him apart. Becher's realization of the moments in which Quadro takes sweet leave from the world of peach are written according to Futurist rules: "Sweat and tears. Gardens for you summer-shepherded, adria-blue and marble-wilderness, gardens: (most soft, most delicate, most flowery) for you creators in mourning rainbow-colored made up girls bend. Mother-of-pearl little faces long-oval." 9 The syntax is all but destroyed, with Futurist analogies instead of traditional comparisons. Indeed, Becher uses, or rather invents for himself (the only German example that I know of) the "semaphoric" function of adjectives, as Marinetti had demanded in his manifesto of 11 May 1913. 10 Adjectives follow nouns and refer parenthetically to earlier nouns, bypassing the immediate antecedent. The parole in libertà and their distaste for the verbum finitum (which here sometimes ossifies into a participle) dominate the many passages of the "Prologues" in which Becher confronts the reader with the fragmentation of humans and landscapes: "Exploding Colonel Lazzoni of the Fifth Carabinieri; with turned-out face; on it intestines, teeth, a throat all intertwining." Or: "Fever. Thick air. Lungs tear. Dripping horse-corpse. Arms farmerboys hacked to pieces. Hair, intestines, flesh--: one pulp.... Bloodily backed knife whipped. Ditches chlorinechalkpale filled." 11 Such images return in Becher's poetry almost monotonously for years, but not in the spirit of pacificism; rather, they appear with a zealous conviction that the old war would have to change itself into the new, no less aggressive war against everything old before the total dawn of paradise.

If there were a reliable biography, we would have an answer to the questions of when and how Becher first became acquainted with Futurist texts. After the literary projects of his publisher-friend Heinrich Bachmair had failed (including the journal Die Revolution, where Hugo Ball had published his early hymn on Futurist painting), Becher moved on, like Ball, to the circle of writers around the journal Aktion, and it was the translator Else Hadwiger who inspired him, like Ball, to read the Futurists. A letter dated 18 March 1916 from Becher to the publisher Ernst Sulzbach of Berlin confirms, at any rate, that he has read the Futurists Marinetti and Paolo Buzzi--that is, just those authors whom Hadwiger translated for Sturm and Aktion. 12 Personal issues are of some significance here: after the death of her husband, the Prague symbolist poet Victor Hadwiger who had published a great deal in Aktion, Else Hadwiger did not withdraw from social life. She became sympathetic to increasingly radical ideas, and was also often to be seen in the company of the translator Fritz Max Cahén in cafés and literary assemblies, 13 as well as with the Wilmersdorf publisher R. A. Meyer, who had published her translation of Marinetti in a limited edition. They met in Meyer's apartment at the Bayrischer Platz or in her own spacious flat, at that time not far from the Südwestkorso and Ludwig Meidner's studios (Meidner often stayed with her). Becher, I have no doubt, was closely connected to Hadwiger, regardless of his other liaisons. He sought refuge with her, both literally and spiritually, whenever his Munich exhaustions led him to Berlin. Even later, when the hectic affair of 1914-15 was over, Hadwiger, who had already sacrificed part of her fortune for her husband, went on, together with the Princess Mechthilde Lichnowsky and Katharina Kippenberg, the wife of the Leipzig publisher, to support the morphine-dependent poet-vagabond (Harry Graf Keßler did his share too).

Love poems are not reliable biographical documents, but at least they testify to how certain processes, internal and external, articulate the writer's ability to experience; and in this respect Becher's Berlin poems "Ekstasen der Zärtlichkeit" (Ecstacy of tenderness) and "Ein Ende" (An end), both of them explicitly "dedicated to Frau Else Hadwiger," are revealing. The one can be found in the collection Verbrüderung (Fraternization; 1916) and the other, originally dated 1915, in the collection Päan gegen die Zeit (Paean against the time; 1918). The cycle "Ekstasen der Zärtlichkeit" consists of four pieces introduced by the highly suggestive verses by Alfred Mombert: "You are the one who understands me / and who is reconciled / to my burning crimes." 14 The poet professes an absolute feeling of love; Else is mother, Holy Virgin, lover, "unfolded like a flower" and "protectress"; a world lives in the "mirror" of her "oval nails"; "young men boiling / poured dying themselves over the planks of [her] hips"; and the world speaks only of her: "you are meant by the homecoming of noisy soldiers... / the banners shine your name / you, every cinema's loud poster." 15 In the other poem, "Ein Abschied" (A farewell) 16 is constituted of two pieces: the first is agitated and, in spite of all the Futurist analogies ("Nacht-Ozean" [night-ocean]), an unsettled mixture of realism and confessional hymn; the second is more relaxed, simultaneously classical and lyrical, the stanzaic sound reminiscent of the old Goethe. The first part depicts the scandal of a violent farewell: someone else has gained the favor of the woman ("O woman! Chestnutbrown"), and the writer feels abandoned amid alienation and threats: "blessed was he. Sweet. At home. / Now fly bridges...knives...bombs...!" 17 While lost in the streets, he finds only bragging, naive, and evil words for the past: "the other may seize you--... / powerful marches explode like a gun / Bursting apart your bed's mudlake." 18 The second part is cooler and more collected; the writer has found himself again, speaks words of comfort to himself: "Yes --, now he is drifting again, / only for a short while bound to her." 19 This lover was home to him, but the moment of leavetaking is a new beginning:

always returning into you
home, lost you already.
You lifted us from everything difficult--
and yet we hasten away. 20

Becher's acquaintance with the work of the Futurist Paolo Buzzi is, however, of little significance for the development of his own Futurist leanings. Becher refers to Buzzi's "I reclusi," a little imprecisely, as "Gedichte aus den Städten" (Poems from the cities) in a letter to the publisher Ernst Sulzbach. That choice of wording reveals that this cycle of six poems, whose original title Else Hadwiger had translated in the 7 November 1914 edition of Aktion more precisely as "Das Lied der Eingeschlossenen" (The song of those confined) had impressed upon him--contemporaneously with Heym and Hoddis--the dangerous and demonic nature of the metropolis instead of the aspect he chose to emphasize, at least in his Buzzi imitations: the accentuation of new technology. In his "Reclusi" cycle Buzzi himself, in spite of his personal loyalty to Marinetti, was not so much a Futurist in the sense of the early manifesto as a late or ironic romantic or symbolist; the Futurist element lies exclusively, or almost exclusively (as with the early Marinetti) in the free verse, as it was programmatically cultivated in the journal Poesia, which Buzzi helped found. "Das Lied der Eingeschlossenen" consists of elegiac role poems, in which the victims--the forgotten, those who had been forced from their cities into secrecy--speak in chorus of their sufferings, longings, and hopes "Aus den Klöstern" (From the monasteries), "Aus den Bordellen" (From the brothels), "Aus den Hospitälern" (From the hospitals), "Aus den Gefängnissen" (From the jails), "Aus den Irrenhäusern" (From the mental hospitals), and "Von den Friedhöfen" (From the graveyards). (In Else Hadwiger's translation, part 3 of the Italian cycle, the chorus of the soldiers from the barracks, "Dalle Caserne," is missing.) The forgotten speak calmly, movingly, and simply like the monks: "clean the little cell, full of roses the small garden," 21 or the whores: "we write home and stitch fine linen lace / ...don't know why they despise us outside," 22 and only rarely in helpless irony like the sick in the hospitals, who complain about the tedious holiday visitors: "On Sundays the masses stream here like to the graveyard, / knock at the beds and dirty the carpets in the hallway / and bring us unhealthy sweets." 23 Futurist elements, in the sense of the ideological and rhetorical demands of the manifestos, are present only at a distance, for example in the anger of the mad: "we have been procreated by the green anger of the innermost organs" (reminiscent of Marinetti's romantic cult of the creative insane in the second manifesto), or the bizarre images in the litany of the dead, who complain about what the living could have done to them: "head over heals they could have thrown us into the sea, / hung on a balloon, let us drift into the blue!" 24

Becher replies with his own poems to Buzzi's "Das Lied der Eingeschlossenen (I reclusi)," but the noteworthy element of his reply is the fact that he contradicts the Futurist poet by being more Futurist. The later poems of the German poet are well ahead of Buzzi's early poetry in their radical quality. Buzzi's thematic accusations are taken up and reshaped by Becher in his poetry collections Verfall und Triumph and Päan gegen die Zeit (for example in the poems "Krankenhaus" [Hospital] and "Bordell" [Bordello]); and I suspect that even poems like "Die Armen" (The poor), "Die Huren" (The whores), and "Aus den Höfen" (From the courtyards) are in accordance with the suggestions by Buzzi. 25 Becher had been acquainted with Hadwiger's Buzzi translation from the beginning, and his own hospital poems (Becher had had his own experiences, in the Landesanstalt Haar, a psychiatric hospital near Munich) demonstrate how he takes up Buzzi's perspective and tone and then emancipates himself from them only a little later. In "Krankenhaus" , especially in the second of the four pieces, he, like Buzzi, lets the sick speak in chorus; Buzzi's soft and melancholy elements are not wholly absent:

We cry ourselves through prison and ether-hall
Towards each other, experiencing sweet closeness,
When we are given the last supper,
we are anointed with oil's soft snow. 26

In the later version of "Krankenhaus" , to which Becher himself applies the term "variation," Buzzi's melancholy and soft irony are destroyed by the dithyramb; the poem no longer speaks for and with the sick, but instead presumes to change their world through the released word: "I enforce their dissolution into wonderful May creatures." 27 The opening of the poem, line one, partly alludes to Buzzi/ Hadwinger's "qui si muore in fila"/"hier stirbt man reihenweise" (here one dies in rows [WIF, 256]) and turns into "hier wälzt sich's frei" (here it wallows itself free [WIF, 329]) in Becher's text, which then goes on to revel in its usual way in horrifying images of pus and blood. The syntax is cryptically reduced: "pointy steel from temples shrinking thunderstorms"; metaphor takes the form of Futuristic analogies: "slime-seaweed," "clip-skeletons," "shepherd-doctors"; and just as the Futurist manifestos demanded, the cities are "overgrown with electric streams." 28 Here Becher is the real Futurist, not Buzzi.

In his later writings, which contain more than one confession about the creative experiments of his youth or, as he puts it, "special Don Quichotteries," 29 Becher the state minister thinks back nostalgically to the readings and contemporaries of his youth and touches on a basic idea of Futurism, which he ascribes to a friend from that period. In his reminiscences of what he calls expressionism (the minister now shies away from the word "Futurism," part of his vocabulary in 1914) and of his early poems, the poetic, if not even ontological, principle of simultaneity plays an important part. Simultaneity and a modern "Weltgefühl" (world sentiment) were the same for him (and for the Futurists), and when he speaks of "Simultanismus" (simultanism) he quotes a term that did not fail to make its impression, even on him. 30 In Sturm Herwarth Walden had published Umberto Boccioni's essay on the "Simultanéité Futuriste" (Futurist simultaneity) (in which the painter claimed the term for the Futurists and against the Frenchman Robert Delaunay), but the later Becher ascribes the demand for simultaneity to his unhappy friend Jakob van Hoddis, who was in the habit of publishing his poems in Sturm in the years 1911-13. Hoddis claimed, the later Becher writes, that "the sense of simultaneity" 31 is already intact in Homer (and therefore not only with the Futurists or their French colleagues, who articulated simultaneità/simultanéité as a modern program?). Becher adds that he and his friends were willing to realize this sense of simultaneity as an "immeasurable world-wideness," 32 as Hoddis had done in his famous two verses of "Weltende" (End of the world) 33 --"the great is mixed in with the small and vice versa, nothing is able to last separately by itself." 34 The call for simultaneity (which also appears in the diary of Georg Heym) 35 withered soon, according to Becher, to a "stencil," and "bureaucratic verse-products were generated, which were mathematically calculated, so that each sentence had to be followed by an antithesis." 36 Whether entirely calculated or not, in Becher's early praxis this simultaneity is often signalled by a strong Bavarian "Derweil" (meanwhile), which connects even the most contradictory events and experiences "in the spirit of an expressionist pantheism." 37 The minister of state does not, however, remember that in a 1923 letter to the editors of the cultural section of the Frankfurter Zeitung in which he attempted to explain his prosody, as a Marxist he connected even the unusual typography of his verses and his idiosyncratic punctuation (as stopsigns against the flow of syntax) with simultaneità. He wrote at that time to the Frankfurter Zeitung that the antisyntactical precautions transform "the consecutive" "with a dialectical manoeuvre into a parallel" and added that the poem thus is put into "the sphere of simultaneity" and indicates "also externally the rapid speed." 38 It is of less significance whether Becher owed this connection of simultaneity and speed, simultaneità and velocità, to Hadwiger or Boccioni, than the fact that, whether he wanted to or not, as a recently converted Leninist and enthusiastic partisan of communism, Becher was a poetic ally of the Futurists.

All arguments for or against Becher's entanglement in Futurism would be farfetched if they could not be supported, as has been demonstrated in detail by Hopster and also partly by Carmine Chiellino, by shared features of thematics and rhetoric and indeed by their integration. 39 Among the expressionists there were many city and war poets, but only a few who were as attracted by modern technology and its destructive, purifying effects as Becher was. With its reduced syntax in which the noun and simultaneity reign, the way in which he describes the metropolis, as simultaneously horrendous and the paradisiacal site of utopian beginnings, and the ideal war, as an at once violent and hopeful destruction of old worlds, is closer to Italian Futurism than any other German poems of the era. No other German poet wrote poems like "Auf ein Maschinengewehr" (To a machine gun) or "Lokomotiven" (The locomotives) simultaneously with "Die neue Syntax" (The new syntax). 40 Had he written in Italian, Becher would have become a towering poet of European Futurism in the years 1913-19 and even later, with his Leninist Maschinenrhythmen . The decisive elements are not so much the movable parts of the Futuristic technology--the racing cars, airplanes, trains, and the complete war arsenals: battleships, torpedoes, shrapnel, the phallic cannons (as in Marinetti's "Schlacht" [Battle] 41 ) and the spewing bayonets--but rather their linguistic presence in a reduced syntax, in "befreiten Worten" (liberated words), in simultanism and in the analogies and analogy chains, which correspond to Marinetti's programmatic demands. Even if Hadwiger and van Hoddis had not related things Futurist to him, Becher would have invented his own Futurism under the irrepressible compulsion of his explosive imagination.

Each of the German authors who grappled with Futurism accentuated in his own texts a different element of the Futurist rhetoric; and while Alfred Döblin dealt mainly with the parole in libertà, and August Stramm and Ball (to simplify things here) dealt with the structure of the syntax, Becher, unlike anyone else, worked with analogies and chains of analogies. In Marinetti's poetic program, which gives precedence to the image over the sentence, the "analogies," a word he derives from Edgar Allan Poe, number among the significant features of the new rhetoric. He explains in the "Technical Manifesto" and in its "Supplement," as they could be read in Sturm in 1912 and 1913, 42 that the modern "aviatische Schnelligkeit" (aviatic speed) of our "Weltkenntnis" (la conoscenza del mondo [knowledge of the world]) demands its correspondence in a speedy metaphoric; no more leisurely comparisons using "as" or "just as," but instead, lightning-like "doublings" that "pull the object into one characteristic word with the image evoked by it," 43 i.e. man-torpedo-boat (uomotorpediniera), woman-gulf (donna-golfo), crowd-surf (folla-risacca), and so on. 44 Marinetti does not hesitate to distinguish between bad, good, and still better analogies; basically an anticlassical concetto (conceit) is hidden in each analogy, and therefore Marinetti demands "Steigerungen" (increases) of the analogies, which for their highest effect bind together "fernstehende" (distant), "verschiedene" (different), and "Feindliche Dinge" (antagonistic things) in such a way that they replace the merely mechanical and threadbare elements of the language with the unexpected, the surprising, and the effective. When a writer compares a fox terrier with a small thoroughbred (Marinetti's example), he has not yet done much to break through the expected; that only happens when he connects the image of the terrier with the agitated bubble of boiling water. Marinetti's sketch is not content with the single analogy (his text "Schlacht" is the first to show that); the whole world is in rapid motion, and therefore the "aufeinanderfolgenden Bewegungen" (successive movements) require a "Kette" (chain) or a "Netz" (net) of analogies, "which one sinks into the sea of the appearances." 45 Nothing would be more desirable, Marinetti says, at least theoretically and without any concern for the reader, than to leave the first members of these chains of the analogy out and to bind together only the second ones in a mysterious poetic text, in pure images.

Becher's endeavor to create word combinations, which were supposed to enable him to intensify the expression and to lessen the necessities of typical syntactical construction, has a long history that begins with his earliest poems, is in danger of solidifying into a rhetorical mannerism in the last years of World War I, and finds its end in the 1926 Maschinenrhythmen. Those special noun constructions using hyphens, which one may call Futuristic analogies, cannot be found before the end of 1912 / the beginning of 1913, that is, before the publication of Marinetti's "Technical Manifesto" and "Supplement" in Sturm. In Die Gnade eines Frühlings, Becher still writes in the neoromantic mode: "the glow of the evening rose" pushes in, "the evening sky plays completely blue, shinysunkenblue," and when he is really adventurous, Becher also imitates Arno Holz's morphological monsters, and the sunbeams sit on the "skyandcloudglowingdowndrinking" backs of the roofs. 46 In Verfall und Triumph, which contains the poems written between December 1912 and November 1913, the sentiment is expunged once and for all; Becher works with frequent noun combinations, which the German language easily tolerates as neologisms, for example "gold-trombones" and "mould-moon," or he connects adverbial and adjectival notions with hyphens: "reeling-crazy," "deathly-shrill," "drunkenclose," in a pattern he will rely on for a long time. 47 The poem "Die Armen" (The poor) constitutes the obvious exception to this pattern, in which we see signs of Becher's future development; we find the first, awkward attempts to form analogies like "civil servant-snout," more effective examples in the call to Christ as "diseasehoard," and anticipations of the future with "brain-beams." 48 Even the first attempt to unite two analogies in a single verse is here: "when smell-seas softly caress our endurer-bodies." 49

Becher's poems written during World War I (published in various volumes and often suppressed) are characterized by the frequent repetition and boldness of the analogies; some are closely related to Marinetti's standard (for example, "shrapnel thicket," 50 "woman-slough," 51 and "human-ant" 52 ), but in Becher's livelier moments and before the process ossifies into a mannerism, they show an unforgettable poetic spontaneity and mysteriousness, as in "nonsense-litany," "tarantula-Christ," and "places-pimples." 53 Sometimes they lock into incredible chains of analogy that are unparalleled in even the most adventurous expressionist poetry of the era: for example, the snow in its "nail-whip-flight," "O forehead-rabble slipping: snow-accord," or the line "trombone-taiphoo-thunderstorm from lip-coasts rage." 54 This all goes far beyond the poems of Marinetti and his friends.

Becher's analogies might be more closely defined by a process that not only observes the basic patterns of his combinations but also helps to separate spontaneity from mannerism. Most noticeable are those forms of the analogies in which the human body or one of its parts forms a semantic element. Statistically, those forms in which the corporeal functions as a basic term predominate, which suggests that Becher very often only feigns analogies by forming composites according to the grammatical rules of the German language (Marinetti does not have this possibility in his languages) and separating them again with a hyphen, as in "torpedo-bodies," "flag-arms" (or "lasso-arms"), "flag-hairs," "hill-hip," "rock-knee," "moss-lips," "forehead-dam," "pulp-head," "drum-belly," or "bayonet-arm." 55 Statistically more frequent than these pseudo-analogies (which twist the possibilities of German morphologies apart and are reminiscent of baroque composites) are those in which the bodily element appears first and massively increases the imagistic power of the analogy through the inversion, for example in "finger-rake," "neck-towers," "lip-ruin," "eyelash-moon," or "muscle-viaducts." 56 A third pattern, one which tends to degenerate into mannerism, can be found when a physiological aspect, such as blood or a secretion, functions semantically as a basic word, for example "pus-moon" (Becher shares Marinetti's dislike of la luna), "excrement-cloud," "blood-night," "pus-fog," and "excrement-cities." 57 My observations are unfair insofar as they tend to focus on repetition rather than the singular, the mannerism rather than the uniqueness; but in the work of a poet, one cannot be defined without the other, and without being aware of what mannerism Becher develops, I cannot be completely clear as to his originality. An analogy like "craziness-salt," found in Maschinenrhythmen, exceeds all statistical analyses, which inevitably find repeated patterns because they are meant to find them; an interpretation of the unrepeated, the unrepeatable, has the larger claim. 58

I intentionally have not quoted from Becher's Maschinenrhythmen , because I think that it belongs to the key texts of the radical Left German literary avantgarde, just as a little later Brecht's Heilige Johanna der Schlachthöfe (St. Joan of the stockyards; 1929-30) does. Becher's collection of poetry was forgotten because it positions itself against traditional ideological prejudices. One knows only too clearly what is Left and what is Right, rhetorically and politically, and one finds oneself disturbed by poems in which a visionary Leninism articulates itself in a poetic idiom in which a tendency toward Futurist themes and rhetoric once more, and for the last time, goes to the extreme of linguistic self-destruction, stammering, feverish, fragmented. These poems, including the violent "Hymne an Lenin" (Hymn to Lenin), read as if possessed, with the class war as the only power of salvation in the world:

downcrazyshone
bayonet-forest. Triumph-laughter was,
Victory-screaming, drinking of the murder-party...cannon-
wheels, skull-crushing, rolled over us,
braided thunder. 59

Huge power stations are made completely present through language as industrial scenery:

Factory squares: fevercurved
landscape: demon-heaps, iron-
blocked, rose up thrown over, as if
corrosively armored, by neon
smoke-coats; by tube-textures
surrounded: cracked fire-faces;
energy-waves; melting ore floods. 60

Finally the triumph of the revolution is realized: "nation-hymns, thousands of years-- / buried, thunder rising from / hell-caves." 61 The only commentary that does justice to this kind of poetry can be found in Antonio Gramsci's remarks on Anatoli Lunatscharsky's speech to the Italian delegates at the Second Communist International (Lunatscharsky had called Marinetti a revolutionary intellectual, and the "philistines of the workers' movement" were horrified). 62 At that time , Gramsci said very clearly that the early Futurists had been defended by "troops of workers" against their enemies, because they "were in their field, culture, revolutionaries." 63 He articulated the thought that the Futurists had anticipated, in their way, the revolution of the proletarian culture (Proletkult):

they had the pure and clear conception that our epoch, the epoch of large industry, of the large workers' city, of the intensive and tumultuous life, required new forms of art, philosophy, customs, language: they had this completely revolutionary, absolutely Marxist conception, when the socialists did not even remotely concern themselves with a similar question. 64

Gramsci will later judge Futurism differently, but his Lunatscharsky commentary explains and positions Becher's remarkable mixture of Leninist dogma and Futurist rhetoric in their own historical moment. In their respective beginnings, Futurism and fascism were not as inseparably linked as the impatient generalizations of later ideological criticism makes out, and it would be productive to ask the question of whether the national anarchism of the early Futurists does not in fact clearly anticipate elementary features of the German national Bolshevism of the Weimar era. 65
