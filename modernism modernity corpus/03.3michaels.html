Response

Walter Benn Michaels

Our America and Nativist Modernism:
A Panel

Our America has two theses, one historical and one theoretical. The historical thesis describes the emergence of a distinctly modern concept of racial identity and its transformation--already implicit and never complete--into the concept of cultural identity. The transformation is never complete because culture, imagined not as our actual beliefs and practices but as the beliefs and practices that are appropriate for us, can never free itself from race; the transformation is always implicit because race presents itself from the start as a kind of ideal, something we must not only embody but live up to. What Our America calls nativist modernism is the literature of this transformation and of the dramas of identity it makes possible: the betrayal of your culture and the recovery of your culture, the effort to become yourself and the struggle to remain yourself. Our America understands both the characteristic thematic ambitions of American modernism (e. g., for "the family" to maintain itself) and its characteristic formal ambitions (e. g., for the poem to be itself) as expressions of these identitarian commitments. The theoretical thesis is that any notion of cultural identity that goes beyond the description of our actual beliefs and practices must rely on race (or something equivalent, say, sex) in order to determine which culture is actually ours. Hence the idea of cultural identity--despite the fact that in recent years it has customarily been presented as an alternative to racial identity--is in fact, not only historically but logically, an extension of racial identity.

Marjorie Perloff, Robert von Hallberg, and Charles Altieri focus most of their attention on the historical thesis. They argue that it is at best only partially true, which is to say, true only of a few major modernist writers and not entirely true even of them. They also think that modernism as a movement is diminished by my account of its social meaning; indeed they think that the value of literature itself is diminished by my account of modernism's social meaning. Perloff and Altieri also disagree with the theoretical thesis. Perloff defends the idea of racial/cultural identity on the social constructionist grounds that "identity is largely a question of how others perceive us" ; Altieri defends the idea of cultural heritage (racial/cultural identity in its historicist mode) on the grounds that it can function as a purely "educational" concept without a "racial basis" .

Neither of the theoretical objections seems to me to have much force. Perloff apparently thinks that because the critique of identity "cut no ice" with the Nazis we should accept what "the history of our brutal century has taught us, identity is largely a question of how others perceive us" . But, even leaving aside the particular question of why the Nazis' commitment to the primacy of identity should count as an argument in its favor, Perloff's idealism is both theoretically incoherent (the belief that people belong to races is not in itself evidence that people belong to races) and politically disabling (what grounds would we have for criticizing the Nazis if we didn't think that their perceptions of Jewish identity were mistaken). Altieri doesn't think we can save cultural identity simply by validating everyone's mistakes and accepting Perloff's criteria (if somebody thinks you are something, then ["largely"] you are the thing they think you are); he thinks we can save it by understanding it neither as racial entitlement nor as shared beliefs but as "shareable competencies" . Thus where I argue that Shakespeare's belonging to our cultural heritage can only be a consequence of rather than a motive for our reading him (if we do, he will; if we don't, he won't), Altieri says that, "so long as we teach literature," "anyone whose interests are directed toward such materials will profit from trying Shakespeare" ; to encourage people to try Shakespeare, he thinks, is thus to encourage them "to try out identifications with a heritage even if there is no racial basis for the identification" . But how does being interested in a subject (literature) get turned into (or equated with) identifying with a heritage? No one will deny that if you're interested in literature it's useful to know something about Shakespeare, but the point of appeals to cultural heritage is not, of course, to find new words to describe peoples' cultural interests. It is to identify those things that count as part of your culture whether or not you are interested in them.

The historical objections seem to me more serious. For if modernism, as Perloff plausibly says, "does not . . . belong to the United States alone" , any attempt to insist upon its national and even nationalist character may look dubious, 1 and so a certain proportion of these responses is devoted to reminding me that there were important modernist writers who were not American nativists. But the undeniable truth of this assertion does not in itself gainsay my claim that many American modernists were; more interestingly, it does not address the question of the relation between international modernism and the increasing prominence of racial identity as a meaningful social category in, most obviously, France and Germany, but not insignificantly in Britain, Italy, and (with allowances for the dramatic changes produced by the revolution) in Russia as well. Which is just to say that, although my respondents tend to think of the international character of modernism as counting against my insistence on the primacy of race/culture (von Hallberg says that my nativist modernism is "much more nativist than modernist" because the "connections" of my authors to international trends are "faint" [115]), the truth may be just the opposite--what may be most international about international modernism is its involvement with those categories of identity that have played such a central and often dominant role in European history throughout the twentieth century. From this standpoint, it would be precisely my authors' obsession with a racialized Americanness that would mark most clearly their participation in modernism as an international movement.

This does not mean, of course, that international modernism should be understood as a collection of local nativisms on the American model. Modern American nativism, as I describe it, is essentially anti-imperialist, originating in the representation of the resistance to Reconstruction as the resistance to empire and deploying its pluralist racism against the very idea that people of different cultures could be ruled by the same government. Nothing could be less true of racism in England or, indeed, of the racial commitments of writers like Ezra Pound and T. S. Eliot whose literary ambitions, it is traditionally argued, were formed in London before the war. Perloff thinks "a consideration of Pound's or Eliot's nativism" would have usefully complicated my argument but my argument is that Pound and Eliot weren't nativists, and that their anti-pluralism produced racial commitments very different from (if no less racist than) those of F. Scott Fitzgerald and Ernest Hemingway. More generally, although it is true that the emergence of modernism is linked almost everywhere to the rise of race-based modes of social organization, it does not follow that these modes were everywhere the same: anti-Semitism in France, for example, was deployed in the mobilization of a mass politics; in the U. S., it tended to express a certain hostility to the very idea of mass culture (Negrophobia did the work of racializing "the people"). Marcel Proust is not Gertrude Stein, and it would be a mistake to think that what Jewishness meant to the author of The Making of Americans was the same as what it meant to the author of Du côté de chez Swann. But it would also be a mistake to think that the question of identity was irrelevant to either.

Of course, one might still argue, as von Hallberg does, that "literary history is better justified" when "it records some deviance from social, political, and institutional history" , and thus that literary history should focus its attention on those modernists who were critical of the primacy of race/culture. He finds this critical deviance exemplified in William Carlos Williams's commitment to hybridity instead of purity, as in Père Rasles's "will 'TO MARRY . . . to hybridize, to crosspollenize' with the North American continent and its Indian inhabitants" . But, even setting aside for a moment the question of how to understand this commitment to hybridization, it should be clear that the demonstration that a writer like Williams was committed to hybridity instead of purity cannot really count against a description of modernism that insists on the absolute centrality of the categories of a racialized identity. In an important sense, it does not matter for my argument whether an individual writer prefers racial hybridity to racial purity or vice versa. What matters is that these are the relevant choices. Thus when Altieri insists that "the gulf between Faulkner and his characters requires us to postulate within modernist culture more than one attitude toward these racial matters" (108-9), he seems to me (about the "attitude" if not the "gulf") exactly right. There are indeed differences in "attitude" between modernist writers and the point of Our America is not to deny those differences; it is instead to describe the logic according to which it is precisely those differences (differences about "racial matters") that are the ones that matter. They aren't the ones that mattered to Edith Wharton or Henry James. 2

Whether these differences amount to the deviance called for by von Hallberg is another question. Von Hallberg thinks that In the American Grain is distinguished from more "romanticized" texts by Père Rasles's will "TO MARRY" the Indians but--from Hart Crane to D. H. Lawrence to Robinson Jeffers to Zane Grey--no event in the nativist canon was more frequent than sex with an Indian. Although von Hallberg's equation of "miscegenation" with the impulse "'to hybridize'" seems plausible enough, what the fantasy of sleeping with truly native Americans seemed in fact to offer was hybridization without miscegenation, a way of legitimizing rather than problematizing one's Americanness. To see the point here, one has only to note the relative absence of romantic fantasies about crosspollenizing with Jews. Tom Outland, in The Professor's House, imagines himself descended from Indians and so imagines the Anasazi relics he has discovered as belonging to his family--the American family. Roddy Blake, by contrast, sells the relics and thus betrays his country, "like Dreyfus." 3 And Louie Marsellus marries the sister who should have married Tom, thus guaranteeing that the Professor's descendants will be Jewish, not Indian. The desire to "hybridize" with Indians was the desire to make your children as native as their native American parent and thus to make yourself--the parent of a native American--as American as your children.

Needless to say, most of this sex was, as Altieri would say, "of a distinctly metaphoric kind" . Altieri seems to think that this is a problem, that unless these writers "based specific political decisions" on the "identifications" I describe , there is nothing political about them. 4 But it's hard to see why writing books that reimagine American history and reconstruct a national poetic along lines that articulate the principles of Calvin Coolidge (which is, if I am right, what William Carlos Williams was doing between 1923 and 1929) counts as less political than actually voting for Calvin Coolidge. It is, of course, precisely what they see as this reduction of the literary to the political that most concerns all three of my respondents; they think that it makes literature, or at least the study of literature, unnecessary: "For why do we need to study literature in order to learn about the identity politics of the 1920s?" Perloff asks, "Surely there are more informative and efficient ways than to read dozens of what are largely undistinguished novels" . Here their defense of modernism becomes the defense of the literary as such, and we are confronted with an opposition--between literature and history or "cultural studies"--that the New Criticism made foundational for the modern discipline of literary criticism (an opposition that many on the "cultural studies" side would no doubt find just as gratifying--the fact that 's respondents all take the literature side has an easy, but for the issues under discussion here, irrelevant institutional explanation). But, whatever the ultimate theoretical merit of this opposition may be, the texts discussed in Our America make immediately clear certain of its more obvious limitations. Does Faulkner's attempt to imagine the transformation of words into things belong to literature or to history? If, on the one hand, nothing is more characteristic of the literary ambition of modernism than its effort to overcome reference, its effort to make the words be the things rather than refer to them, nothing, on the other hand, is more characteristic of the social history of modernity than the emergence of the identitarian project of being yourself. Indeed, the invention of the various dramas of identity that I describe--betraying one's identity or defending it, repudiating it or recovering it--is unimaginable and probably would have been impossible without literary modernism's various idealizations of its own literariness, which is to say, of the quintessentially identitarian question of the text's relation to itself.

Robert von Hallberg opens this discussion by saying that Our America describes American modernism as "driven by considerations of race and identity more than of form and representation" . But Our America argues that questions of race and identity are questions of form and representation, and, although the converse need not be true--although questions of form and representation need not be understood as questions of race--Our America argues that in modernism they were, that what distinguishes modernism is precisely its understanding of questions of form and representation as questions of race and identity. Maybe we should describe modernism as something like the research-and-development division of identitarianism. In any event, just as I don't think you can understand literary modernism without understanding its participation in the production of identitarianism, I don't think you can understand modern identitarianism without understanding literary modernism. The real reductionism is in thinking that you can somehow separate the two.

In a similar vein, Altieri suggests that the changes I describe inthe meaning of citizenship, changes embodied in my juxtaposition of the Immigration and the Indian Citizenship Acts, cannot be understood simply by a reading of the relevant texts. Indeed, he argues that "the very effort to establish commonness on the level of what these pieces of legislation meant for people requires overlooking the economic factors that are central in establishing different experiences of citizenship" . But the question here is not only about how people experienced citizenship but about what citizenship was: was it still, in the wake of the various technologies that had been invented to reconre it, the sort of thing that could be or should be acquired? And the answer to this question does not depend on the different economic circumstances of the various groups in American society; indeed, it was a crucial aspect of nativist modernism that it substituted the question of what your nationality was for the question of what your socioeconomic status was, that it substituted race and culture for class. The "commonness" between the Johnson Act and the Citizenship Act thus has nothing to do with some presumed commonness between Indians and immigrants; it has everything to do with common commitment of congressmen and writers to imagining the desire to succeed as the desire to pass, to reimagining Clyde Griffiths's desire to have more money as Jimmie Gatz's desire to be Jay Gatsby.
