1. Construction, Silence

"The very existence of psychoanalysis," John Forrester has observed, "is a permanent testimony to the failure of communication." 4 It is a comment which might remind us how keenly the Surrealists sensed that the psychopathology of cognitive failure (the aphasias, the psychoses, as well as the psychoneuroses) could provide models for alternative patterns of attention and communication. Indeed, any adequate appreciation of Surrealist automatism must begin with just this sense of the new possibilities offered by alternative forms of attention, promising access to different perspectives of experience and world-projection. For Artaud, Bataille, Breton, and others, appreciating that the ontological possibilities inherent in the psychopathology of failure could be the precondition for certain forms of creativity was grounded in an awareness of the key role that dialogue plays in psychiatric discourse, whether in the recent field of trauma studies ushered in by the Great War or the older case studies of patients such as Charcot's Augustine X.L. or Blanche Wittman. 5 For Breton and Artaud, in particular, the question of dialogue was linked to a movement of disorientation that always posed the question of the imago as a means of confronting the imbrication of time and sense. As Monique Borie expressed it of Artaud, "Going beyond the question of meaning's closure, Artaud is always concerned with the question of communication." 6 Dialogue, in other words, did not necessarily lead to communication, as one can easily see in the poignant examples of non-communication between doctor and patient which are represented in Charcot's Iconographie photographique de la Salpêtrière. 7 Throughout the existence of the Surrealist movement, forms of dialogue will provide privileged sites for the exploration of this question, especially in its dimensions of negativity, movement, and the informe (formlessness).

Nowhere is this question of non-communication and dialogue better manifested than in Breton's famous narrative Nadja, and in particular in the discussion surrounding the various drawings which Nadja gives to Breton. On the one hand, there is the work which Breton titles A True Shield of Achilles, a drawing charged with archaic imagery (the devil, a siren, serpents) of a sort typical of psychosis, a sort in which the temporality of the preconscious collapses into the Zeitlos/anachronism of the unconscious and so turns place (in this case, the place of the drawing itself) into a functional imbrication of different temporalities. 8 On the other hand, there are Nadja's final sketches, making up a work which, unlike the other drawings by her which are all reproduced in photographs, is known only through Breton's verbal account of it:

In these, on a table, in front of an open book, a cigarette lying on an ashtray and insidiously releasing a serpent of smoke, a globe cut open to hold lilies, between the hands of a beautiful woman--everything was arranged to permit the descent of what she called the human reflector, kept off by forceps, and which she said was "the best of all." [N, 130/155-57]

In this work, the "globe cut open" suggests a complete identification between desire and the ideal in a manner typical of the megalomania of psychosis: the psychotic who thinks he is God the Father, the Son of God, Mary, a saint, or Napoleon. Here, however, the telling point is not megalomania's violence, buts its projection onto the entire world ("a globe" or "mappemonde") and its response to the power of someone ("the human reflector") who is "'the best of all.'" Tellingly, Breton's account of the final drawing is followed by blank space occupying about a line, then a prolonged ellipse of dots that stretch across the page, then another blank space. No other section of the narrative is followed by such a notable silence, one which registers Breton's growing awareness of, and his inability to avoid, the brutal fact of Nadja's borderline condition. Moreover, this account is carefully separated from the discussion which treats the main body of Nadja's drawings, and also from the virulent denunciation of psychiatry which follows. And if this textual silence signals a rueful recognition of Nadja's state--and note how the presence of mourning is secured by the absence of its object, the drawing itself, which is not reproduced--then it will come as no surprise to find a mirror image, as it were, of the same dynamics at work in the siting of the next drawing by Nadja. That work, a drawing titled The Soul of the Wheat and the last by her to be reproduced in the book, has been carefully separated from all her others and inserted into the section denouncing psychiatry, where it immediately follows the photograph of Professor Claude, epitome of the psychiatric profession's blindness. 9 Placed here, the drawing's presence becomes a mournful mark of Nadja's absence, her disappearance into the sanatorium, even as it simultaneously reminds us of the archaic imagery of The True Shield of Achilles, particularly in its understated evocation of the grain associated with Demeter, and acknowledges such imagery's threatened status by means of the scythe sweeping toward the wheat. Abundance and plentifulness (wheat, Demeter) are juxtaposed against death and mourning (the scythe), a movement also recapitulated in other drawings and even in Breton's and Nadja's wanderings through the city. "Pink is better than black, but the two harmonize" ("s'accordent"), says Nadja in a maxim which Breton later recalls (N, 116/138), as if suggesting that such discordances might be harmonized or gathered into some reciprocity, in much the same way that Breton and Nadja's different personalities might potentially complement each other. But over the course of Nadja, what begins as a suggestion of mutual reciprocity, epitomized in the glances that Breton and Nadja exchange at their first meeting, modulates into projective identification and thence into a form of intra-subjectivity--the lack of relation to something beyond one's individual consciousness--which bypasses and yet encloses Breton. It is this movement of failed relationality that I should like to explore in this essay, doing so by considering the role that silence plays in Breton's construction of Nadja, which is to say, in how he elaborates his relation to the temporality of his encounter with Nadja. Construction, here, is used in the sense intended by Freud when he wrote that the task of the analyst is "to make out what has been forgotten from the traces which it has left behind, or, more correctly, to construct it." 10

When Breton senses the psychotic dimensions of Nadja's drawing of the globe, he responds with silence and effectively acknowledges the call of what he has earlier called "our very instinct of self-preservation" (N, 20/21). His response is to narrate--"the desire to tell you," as he later says to X (N,156/184). But to respond to the kind of narrating that will be Nadja, we must consider the nature of his silence and its relation to Nadja's final drawing of "a globe cut open to hold lilies."

Few topics in Breton's thinking are as difficult of treatment as that of silence. Certainly, in the tacit importance of silence in his oeuvre, Breton, who in his early days had been a disciple of Valéry, is part and parcel of that high European modernism, beginning with Symbolism, in which silence is accorded a critical role in the experience of thought and registers deepening doubts about the limits of discursivity. But behind Valéry's fascination with silence was another aspect of his thought which Eliot later noted, one that also fascinated Breton. As Breton later recalled: "But perhaps the most piquante aspect of his [Valéry's] conversation was when his 'profoundly destructive, even nihilistic mind,' which so rightly impressed T. S. Eliot, showed through." 11 It was because of a similar nihilist spirit that Breton's conception of silence, however strongly shaped by Symbolism, never formed part of a broader mysticism. Whence his attempt, in 1936, to distinguish the Surrealist marvelous (merveilleux) from Symbolist mystery (mystère), while still recognizing that both had common roots in a historical moment when "the bridge of communication between humans has been broken." 12

The effort of communication between Breton and Nadja, an effort that is prolonged into the construction of the narrative Nadja, teems with silences: they function as bridges, openings, and reciprocities, and at times they signal the recognition of limits or an admission of failure. As an effort of interpretation, of construction, the narrative Nadja inevitably contains such reflections, however tacit, on what both connects and disconnects, on the formality or measure of the experience that is the narrative. The many intervals, which are marks of difference and a staging of distance, which occur between the events that take place in the story and their construction within the narrative, signal an alternative mode of attention at work, one discernible from the moment that the work comes embedded with marks of silence, that long pause which follows Nadja's mention of "the human reflector . . . 'the best of all.'"

Such silence allows for no movement other than that of circularity. But while Nadja's final drawing leaves Breton no choice but to recognize and acknowledge the demented nature of her experience, the ensuing silence also gathers up and returns to what has been tacitly present from the book's famous opening sentence, "Qui suis-je?" (which can mean either "Who am I?" or "Whom am I following?"). The caesura following this ambiguous interrogative is momentous: it recapitulates the devastating dividedness of the experience which Breton's and Nadja's wanderings have produced, in much the same way as the sentence itself allows the speaker to relive the same fragmentation, an experience that is proleptic of the narrative experience to come. The ambiguous interrogative opens a movement of doubling from which the narrative construction never deviates, one that discloses a crucial aspect of silence: the doubling that is heralded by the opening interrogation is both temporal ("Whom am I following?") and nontemporal ("Who am I?"). But in refusing the non-temporality of anteriority, as the image of the fantôme would seem to imply to him, in favor of a temporality of temps vécu, Breton leaves the construction open to a double temporality and its attendant resonances and disturbances, particularly where such temporality encounters silence in the texture of the feminine, or the feminine in its absence as trace.

Silence, in other words, is one of the means by which embodiment in its prepredicative modality is lived in Nadja and in Surrealist experience. The inscription of silence is not possible apart from the body, which serves as a perceptual mechanism linked to the unconscious in much the same way as Freud describes experience being inscribed on the unconscious through the image of a note pad which retains the impression of writing from the previous leaves which have since vanished. 13 When the passé anachronique of depth, or the timelessness of the unconscious, encounters a lived and transred instant, unconscious memory takes the subject into the depth and disperses, as it were, the surface writing, as mute experience seeks its articulation. 14 Silence may well be a precondition for that articulation, but it can also pose a threat to it. As Rousseau, whose statue adorns the square from which Breton sets out on his journey, understood, silence can be an innocent medium for intimacies beyond language, and hence beyond corruption; but it can also become a mark of absolute separation--not silence as transparence and finely modulated lyric moments, but silence become obstacle. 15 When silence as obstacle is articulated with memory, as occurs at the moment Breton recalls Nadja's drawings, it raises questions about silence's metaphorical or symbolic value. What does silence symbolize? In their disagreement about the phylogenetic aspect of symbolism, Freud and Jung came to crystallize their differences around two issues: the status of the maternal, and silence. Jung wished to abandon the Oedipus complex and place in its stead the re of the eternal mother, but at the price of lacking any worthy account of temporality. 16 Yet his attitude toward silence is a challenge to which Freud never adequately responded, for Jung's challenge merely served to consolidate Freud's mistrust of any attempt to diminish the role of language in shaping the distinctiveness of psychoanalysis. 17 John Forrester conveys the dispute thus: "What the conflict amounted to was the fundamental difference of attitude towards the ineffable. Freud, in absorbing the ineffable into the inexpressible, and in his verbal rationalism, distrusted the symbol insofar as it became pure Image, and was detached from the Word." 18 For Jung silence could open into the archaic and the maternal, while for Freud it was simply a mark of resistance in the transference--and thereby "the danger of proximity." 19 For both, however, and inescapably so, it signalled a failure of symbolization.

Though Breton disavowed the high Symbolist mysticism of silence, his thinking, from the inception of automatism, repeatedly returned to the phenomenology of silence. In approaching the conception of silence at work in the construction of Nadja, we shall see that it takes us back into the heart of the book, into its interior as an act of unfolding, a slow circular opening out that is perhaps the characteristic movement of Breton's writing in Nadja, one which leaves the narrator caught in flux and parallel meanings. It is an unfolding movement that also contains a form of layering, the topology of which is very close to Freud's image of the writing-pad. (This unfolding movement finds its reflex in Breton's habitual recourse to words with floral connotations.) Such unfolding lets the narrative explore the relation between silence as resistence and silence to the maternal. Failures of symbolization, the silences that punctuate Nadja, are not simply the outcome of an attempt to manipulate skilfully the reader's feelings; they are indices of Breton's condition in the act of constructing the work, and they testify to the faltering in Breton's desire for the human being who was Nadja, a failure of emotional investment or cathexis. As Monique Schneider argues, "In that sense, we could consider trauma as a 'failure of inscription,' a failure inseparable from another sort of incapacity: incapacity to invest oneself in what arrives, with the result that the only possible form of investment is concentrated into a reaction of refusal" (Pdl'O, 93). The construction of the narrative Nadja thus becomes an attempt to manage this failure of desire and its various intervals--of silence, temporalities, and understanding-- through new strategies of cathexis that require repetition: for Breton to tell the story, first to "himself" and then ostensibly to X, is to acknowledge and, from his and Nadja's viewpoint, to relive "the irreparable disaster sweeping away a part of herself . . . the disaster of which that day [first] gave me a notion . . ." (N, 114/135).

The disaster which Breton first divines on that day, Sunday, 10 October, 1926, is tacitly signalled through a particular form of the counter-transference: boredom. Though boredom can be thought of as dissolving the psychic processes responsible for the presence of form preparatory to the giving on to exteriority, it nevertheless remains compatible with fascination and even the possibility of participation. 20 Something of this is discernible when Breton recounts his relationship of participation with Nadja, which never allows him to be separate, untouched, or unaffected by "the irreparable disaster." When on Wednesday, 6 October, Nadja opens her copy of Breton's most recent book, The Lost Steps, she turns to the essay "The New Spirit," which tersely records Breton's enigmatic encounters with a charming woman whom he subsequently pursues but cannot locate. Nadja has read it and "is astonished and disappointed by the fact that I had considered commentaries on the account of the day's brief occurrences quite superfluous" (N, 78/89-90). She presses Breton for an explanation and an indication "about the degree of objectivity which I attribute to it" (N, 78/90). His response, reported as indirect speech, bears upon the construction of Nadja and his relation to Nadja:

I am obliged to reply that I know nothing about it, that in such matters the right to bear witness seems to me to be all that is granted, that I have been the first victim of this abuse of confidence, if that is what it is, but I can see that she is not going to let me off, I read first impatience, then consternation in her eyes. Perhaps she imagines I am lying: a certain discomfiture persists between us. [N, 78/90; translation modified]

This is a pivotal instant in the tacit articulation of silences and temporalities: the means of bearing witness ("de constater") make Breton's position of observation into a double one, in which he is both the reporter of an event and "the first victim" of his own report. But Nadja's questions touch on the possibility that this observing double is simply fictional, raising the spectre of deception. Her bodily impatience and consternation register the possibility of distance, while a perceptible tension between the two ("a certain discomfiture") registers another multiplicity in silence. True, that tension may seem to be dissipated or resolved by the ensuing first kiss, which follows shortly. But Nadja's consternation isn't sexual; it concerns Breton's attitude toward "the lack of results of this pursuit" (N, 78/89), pursuit referring to both the woman and the essay that recounts his failure to find her again. It indicates the distinct temporalities that stand between her and Breton: he has the capacity to turn events into discourse and can be a subject in both realms, versus her suspicion of this fruitless pursuit, a discrepancy that anticipates their different fates by the book's end. Her consternation also recalls the earlier moment, on Tuesday, 5 October, when Nadja first receives copies of the "Manifesto of Surrealism" and The Lost Steps and we see the first tacit signs of boredom:

October 5. --Nadja is here first, ahead of time. She no longer looks the way she did yesterday. Rather elegant today, in black and red, with an extremely pretty hat which she takes off, revealing her straw-colored hair which has lost its incredible disorder, silk stockings and shoes which, unlike yesterday's, are quite presentable. The conversation has also come more difficult and starts on her part with a number of hesitations. [N, 72/83]

This barely passable elegance contrasts sharply with Breton's first glimpse of her, when she is a person charged with "that look which is hers alone" (N, 72/82; translation modified). The later consternation of Nadja's body amplifies this earlier discomfiture and hints at her suspicion that she may well become a victim of Breton's genius, of what she terms his "great idea" (N, 70/81). As she surveys the crowds hurrying homeward, all those people who will not be the actors in the revolution ("Everyone is hurrying around us . . ."), she says to Breton: "Hearing you speak, I felt that nothing would hold you back, nothing, not even me" (N, 70/81). Her remark, as reported by Breton, uses language remarkably consonant with the voice of "Subject" ("Subjet"), the reconstructed soliloquy of a shell-shocked soldier which Breton had published in early 1918:

Suited to the greatest social zeal ["dévouement," also self-sacrifice], I was destined to be the experimenter's guinea-pig. Still, today, unless they persuade me otherwise, I shall perhaps make the sacrifice of my reason to the human species. Yet I remain unaware of the point of your manoeuver. Apparently it costs you nothing to succeed. I no longer matter in the scale of your production. 21

It is a chilling echo that alerts a discerning reader to the dangers that will eventually engulf Nadja. The monitory note is struck again when Breton and Nadja meet two days later, on 6 October, with Nadja "looking as she did the first day I saw her" (N, 77/88), exuding that "look" which Breton has so highly esteemed. Just before the episode turns to the occasion of the first kiss, Breton reports: "She seems to be suffering from a certain inner conflict . . ." ("une certaine lutte paraît aussi se poursuivre en elle")(N, 79/92). The passage, in its rhythm and diction, and in evoking a threshold experience, recalls that dramatic moment when the audience recognizes that the child in the play Les Détraquées will be murdered: "The child, abashed, heads for the door. On the threshold, she seems to be torn by a great inner conflict" ("Sur le seuil, un grand combat paraît se livrer en elle")(N,41-42/48). The resonance is reinforced when Nadja later recalls her kiss with Breton, terming it "a kiss with a threat in it" (N, 85/98).

In the "Manifesto of Surrealism," Breton links the beginning of Surrealist automatism, in writing and listening, to his encounter with Freud and his sense of dialogue's importance in psychoanalysis. As he conceptualized the significance of free and spontaneous speech, Breton's attention was drawn to the element of a shadow dialogue. "The forms of Surrealist language adapt themselves best to dialogue. Here two thoughts confront each other; while one is being delivered, the other is busy with it; but how is it busy with it?" 22 Automatic listening requires one to listen intensely to silences that make for the possibility of a shadow dialogue. But there is a danger in such intense opening to dialogue, to listening and to listening through silences. For Nadja, transference turns into utter dependence on Breton, while for Breton transference is soon struggling with counter-transference. The bodily inscription of silence in Nadja finds its counterpart in Breton; what is consternation in her body (and hallucinations recorded through her drawings) becomes exhaustion, emptiness, migraine. Thus, immediately after his long encounter with Nadja of 6 October, ending with the midnight walk in the Tuileries, Breton notes: "October 7. --I have suffered from a violent headache which, perhaps mistakenly, I attribute to last night's emotions and also to the effort of attention, of accommodation which I have had to make. The whole morning, too, I have been bothering myself about Nadja . . ." ("Toute la matinée, pourtant, je me suis ennuyé de Nadja. . .") (N, 90/103-4). On this occasion the pain of his relationship with Nadja does not prevent him from longing for her, a longing subtly conveyed in the expression "Je me suis ennuyé," which in nineteenth-century usage still meant "I long for you." But such acknowledgements of intermingled longing and boredom come with a sense of guilt that prompts him to pose the question of their relationship from Nadja's viewpoint: "How does she regard me, how does she judge me?" (N, 90/104). That question, in turn, leads to another passage of marked ambiguity: "When I am near her I am nearer things which are near her" (N, 90/104). Breton's new relations to "things which are near her" may be essentially unstable, but at times they often seem of more interest to him than she herself. That ambiguity will increasingly be lived through the voice, silence, and intervals of connection and disconnection. Five days later, on 12 October, on the day he has received the first of Nadja's drawings, she and Breton began to walk near the Jardin du Palais Royal; for the first time, Nadja spontaneously composes what is effectively a pageant for Breton, a mime:

After dinner, walking around the garden of the Palais Royal, her dreams seem to have assumed a mythological character I had hitherto not discerned. With great skill, so that she gives the striking illusion of reality, she briefly evokes the elusive character of Melusina. Then she asks me point-blank: "Who killed the Gorgon, tell me, tell." [N, 108/127]

The spontaneity and the character of mime, acting within a texture of silence, are important to this performance, which represents a psychological shift for both participants. True, such scenes occur elsewhere in Nadja, typically when Nadja is being playful. But this is the only one which abruptly gives way to speech, to voice displacing silent mime. Nothing of this sort happens later when Nadja and Breton visit Saint-Germain-en-Laye: "As we walk in front of the château, Nadja says she wants to be Madame de Chevreuse; how gracefully she conceals her face behind the heavy, nonexistent plume of her hat!" (N, 108/127). In the earlier scene, instead, Nadja's insistent voice, "Who killed the Gorgon, tell me, tell," compels Breton to silence. While Nadja often speaks in soliloquies, Breton's soliloquy is Nadja and takes place at the level of the construction. As he wanders through the city, at times paying attention intensely to Nadja, he comes to admit: "I have more and more difficulty following her soliloquy, which long silences begin to make unintelligible" (N, 106/125; translation modified). Such silences cannot be translated into language, and they take place not only between the two protagonists, but also within them. Is Nadja's "soliloquy" a single monologue that is interrupted, or many soliloquies originating in different parts of the psyche? Still, the more pressing problem of translation remains an inter-subjective one, and it remains linked to the question of the different temporalities of story and narrative, experience and discourse, and of how human identity is shaped in language, a question that had fascinated Breton since his experience in composing The Magnetic Fields, his first work of automatic writing, penned with Philippe Soupault in 1919 (published 1920). Silence threatens the possibility of communication, and raises the spectre that a founding and constitutive negativity originating in traumatic experience will make it impossible to construct the traces of events into narrative. Breton is the surviving witness of a traumatic event, one who continues to marvel at the miracle that human beings, however intermittently, can communicate with one another: "How does it happen that thrown together, once and for all, so far from the earth, in those brief intervals which our marvelous stupor grants us, we have been able to exchange a few incredibly concordant views above the smoking debris of old ideas and sempiternal life?" (N, 111/130).

The difficulty of traversing the "brief intervals" that lead from experience to discourse is inherent in human communication. "Perhaps life needs to be deciphered like a cryptogram," he suggests at one point, then proceeds to offer an allegory. Imagine a man in a museum at night who illicitly examines the portrait of a woman: "Secret staircases, frames from which the paintings quickly slip aside and vanish (giving way to an archangel bearing a sword or to those who must forever advance) . . . we may imagine the mind's greatest adventure as a journey of this sort to the paradise of pitfalls" (N, 112/133; my emphasis.) Frames of reference or interpretation, in this account, are emptied out by the slippage and disappearance of their contents, while the spectator experiences a radical, vertiginous passivity, a loss of the self that is a precondition for the intense experience of communicative understanding ("to exchange a few incredibly concordant views").

Recalling the interval of time which has transpired since the day when Nadja had told him a story about her degrading experience of being slapped by a man and the present moment when he is recounting her tale again, Breton comments: "Perhaps, during the same period, the irreparable disaster sweeping away a part of herself--and the most humanly defined part--the disaster of which that day gave me a notion, had gradually separated me from her" (N, 114/136). "The irreparable disaster" plainly refers to Nadja's borderline condition and the degradation it will entail. But Breton's syntax so emphasizes the time that separates the two acts of narration that one might also think that "the disaster" refers to that intervening time itself, the temporality of construction. Such temporality had already been outlined in the earlier "Manifesto of Surrealism" in the section discussing the role that Surrealist dialogue played in the writing of Magnetic Fields:

The forms of Surrealist language adapt themselves best to dialogue. Here, two thoughts confront each other; while one is being delivered, the other is busy with it; but how is it busy with it? To assume that it incorporates it within itself would be tantamount to admitting that there is a time during which it is possible for it to live completely off that other thought, which is highly unlikely. And, in fact, the attention it pays is completely exterior; it has only time enough to approve or reject--generally reject--with all the consideration of which man is capable. This mode of language, moreover, does not allow the heart of the matter to be plumbed. My attention, prey to an entreaty which it cannot in all decency reject, treats the opposing thought as an enemy; . . . it puts me in a position to turn it to good advantage in my reply by distorting them. ["MoS," 34/335-36]

The same time that may yield a breathtaking exchange of "concordant views" can also lead to distance, loss, "disaster."

But, in principle, comprehension cannot be separate from the exteriority of time, the very exteriority that Breton has built into the conceptual and phenomenological foundations of Surrealist dialogue. Recalling his experience in writing "Barriers," the fifth chapter in Magnetic Fields, which is made up of passages written alternately by Breton and Soupault, each in response to the other, Breton writes:

Poetic Surrealism . . . has focused its efforts up to this point on reestablishing dialogue in its absolute truth, by freeing both interlocutors from any obligations of politeness. Each of them simply pursues his soliloquy without trying to derive any special dialectical pleasure from it and without trying to impose anything whatsoever upon his neighbor. . . . The words, the images are only so many springboards for the mind of the listener. ["MoS," 35/336]

This form of communication, in which two styles of thinking confront one other, especially encourages participants to identify with one another through affective mimesis; and, if sufficiently sustained, it resembles the sort of dialogic communication that distinguishes the analytic situation, and in particular the silent dynamics of the transference, as the patient reactivates and reenacts his fundamental conflicts around the re of the analyst. Yet analysis does not conclude with the transference; it continues by analyzing the transference through ongoing dialogue between patient and analyst, carried on in distinct but linked temporalities. Breton had built this temporality of distance, of the interval, into Surrealist dialogue and theory; but it is Nadja who compels him to experience it on the pulses in contexts extending far beyond Surrealist games, or even listening to the victims of war neuroses. His encounter with Nadja impels him to conceive of constructing temporalization, rather than deploying it, when faced with the overwhelming emptiness, the voids, and the echoes left by his passage with Nadja. The transference, which in its most primitive form is mimetic identification, has occurred with Nadja, thus enabling that creative illusion of relationality which is registered in the work's discourse of double meaning. When Breton asks Nadja the "one question which sums up all the rest, a question only I would ever ask, probably," he is startled to hear for the first time "a reply worthy of it." "'Who are you?' And she, without a moment's hesitation: 'I am the soul in limbo'" (N, 71/82). When Breton gives her a copy of his book The Lost Steps, she responds, "Lost steps? But there's no such thing!" (N, 72/83). After reading Jarry's sonnet, "Among the Heaths" ("Parmi les bruyères"), Nadja says, "Oh! That must be death!" (N, 73/84). Each of these moving utterances points to an effect of transference, up to the point where the unconscious would touch upon death. But at the same time, each points to a different movement of comprehension, and so to a different relationship between temporality and memory. In constructing the narrative Nadja, Breton cannot avoid this disjunction, for Nadja is no longer present to enable him to escape the solitary reliving of their passage through the city. Her absence makes the time of Surrealism, from its birth, a time of disaster and negativity.

2. Voice and the Effraction of the City

Silence is linked to various senses of projection in the construction both of the narrative of Nadja and Nadja's experiences. In a Freudian context we can link silence to projection by perceiving interior stimuli (such as hallucinations) as though they have originated from an external source. In a psychopathological context we can link silence to projection as a hypnagogic experience which Maury described as an intermediary state which places one "in the presence of a dissolution or suspension of thought near to death." 23 Silence makes it possible to take in the various slippages between internal apperception and the external stimulation that comprise the encoding dimension of discourse. Freud also notes that silence may function as a form of transference, yet silence always carries with it a close proximity to death: in silence one straddles precipitously between the interior processing of an event and the exteriorizing description of that event in narrative structure. 24 Such is the function of communication: namely, to channel dynamic experience into frozen time. The act of channelling or translating an event into a narrative structure is the act of living, in effect, a life that becomes split from itself: in silence one must confront both the real time of experience and the frozen time of narrative recreation. This bifurcated voice of silence has become one of the prominent concepts in the Surrealist movement, beginning with the inaugural hypnagogic phrase, "There is a man cut in two by the window": once the silence has been broken, the voice of a narrator is disembodied ("MoS," 21/325). In the opening passage of "In 80 Days," a chapter in Magnetic Fields, we can find the concept of the disembodied voice in the iconography of bodily displacement in an urban environment: "The sad Sunday suburbs and the lines of a local interest are a sad setting. The newly equipped traveller who departs with one idea in his brilliant brain surveys with his eyes his house, his chimney and the wide avenues leading to the town halls. . . . His tread resounds; he is walking over his heart." 25 Soupault was not the only artist to depict the doubling hypnotic state as a lyric displacement of the body articulated through the imagery of the banal urban environment. Surrealist painters also used similar allegories in both tragic and lyric treatments of the disembodied voice. We can look to Max Ernst's Pietà, ou la révolution de la nuit , La belle jardinière , and the Victoire de Samothrace ; and Joan Miró's Musique, Seine, Michel, Bataille et moi for examples of this allegory at work. There is also, in the works of Miró and Soupault, an exploration of diacritical engagement as a threat to the creative, spontaneous possibilities that arise when a physical body resides within the space of the city. In Musique, Seine, Michel, Bataille et moi, Miró allows the apperceived voice to float in circular wanderings, as does the body image of the traveller in Soupault's "In 80 Days."

During their first meeting together, Nadja identifies herself as such a wandering spirit when Breton asks her "Who are you?" (N, 71/82). In the English edition Nadja's answer is "I am the soul in limbo," losing in translation the sense of movement and travel that is implicit in the French: "Je suis l'âme errante" ("I am the wandering soul"). In this early sequence, Nadja embodies the possibility of becoming for Breton a kindred spirit, who like Breton roams about the city without distinct purpose or direction: "She was on her way, she claimed, to a hairdresser on the Boulevard Magenta (I say claimed because she later admitted she was going nowhere)" (N, 65/73). Such aimless wanderings echo those Breton describes in the first section of the narrative, where his own excursions in the city unfold as a response to the question he poses as the beginning of his book: "Who am I?" Addressing his readers directly, Breton describes his ambles about the city streets of Paris in search of himself:

Meanwhile, you can be sure of meeting me in Paris, of not spending more than three days without seeing me pass, toward the end of the afternoon, along the Boulevard Bonne-Nouvelle between the Matin printing office and the Boulevard de Strasbourg. I don't know why it should be precisely here that my feet take me, here that I almost invariably go without specific purpose, without anything to induce me but this obscure clue: namely that it will happen here. I cannot see, as I hurry along, what could constitute for me, even without my knowing it, a magnetic pole in either space or time. [N, 32/36-38]

During their first meeting, the city functions as a backdrop for Breton and Nadja as they seem to discover in each other a potential for mutual understanding that defies rational knowledge and logical progression. Breton recollects that "without a moment's hesitation, I spoke to this unknown woman, though I must admit that I expected the worst. She smiled, but quite mysteriously and somehow knowingly, though I had no reason to think so" (N, 64-65/73; emphasis in the original). Breton does not need to deploy rationality to perceive that Nadja possesses an intimate understanding of his "whole manner" of thinking and speaking (N, 71/82). Nadja's knowledge is itself a projection, for what Breton is most fascinated by when he looks into Nadja's eyes is not what those eyes might perceive, but what they reflect when he looks into them: "I took a better look at her. What was so extraordinary about what was happening in those eyes? What was it they reflected . . . ?" (N, 65/73). The question of whether or not the narrator is capable of seeing in Nadja's eyes the capacity for perception even as she functions as an instrument of self-reflection is one question that propels this narrative forward.

Nadja's play in the city evokes, and draws upon, a comparable and varied use of projection in both the gestured slowing down of time in her miming and in her use of comic mimicry: "Nadja, who has thrown a fold of her cape back over her shoulder, assumes with astonishing facility the arrogance of the Devil as he appears in romantic prints" (N, 102/120). These displacements are a function of projection, especially in Nadja's world "where everything so rapidly assumed the appearance of a rise, a fall" (N, 135/159). In such a world as Nadja's, there is a continuity between the pathology of the forms of play and dissolution; Breton calls these projected forms her distractions. There is still a sense that Nadja is able to distinguish between play and displaced absorption when, for example, she "supposing herself alone . . . was singing and dancing as she walked beneath an arcade of the Palais Royal" (N, 101/118). Yet in this scene Nadja begins dangerously to identify herself with a projected hallucination as she moves within the trajectory of a city that pierces her to distraction. 26 In his account of their sixth meeting in the diary portion of the book, Breton recalls one instance of Nadja's dissolution which turns into an accusation that he directs towards himself:

We reach the corner of the Rue de Seine, where we turn, Nadja no longer wishing to continue straight ahead. She is still quite distressed [distraite, distracted], and tells me to follow a line slowly traced across the sky by a hand. "Still that hand." She shows it to me, as a matter of fact, on a poster, a little beyond the Dorbon bookstore. It so happens that there is, high above us, a red hand with its index finger pointing, advertising something or other. Nadja insists on touching this hand . . . which she succeeds in slapping with her own. "The hand of fire, it's all you, you know, it's you." She falls silent for a while, I suspect she has tears in her eyes. Then suddenly . . . virtually stopping me, with that extraordinary way she has of calling me, the way you might call someone from room to room in an empty castle: "André? André? . . . You will write a novel about me. I'm sure you will." (N, 100/116-17; first two ellipses mine, third ellipsis author's]

Through Breton's recollection we learn that Nadja must correlate the hallucinations she projects from inside with the piercing assault of stimulation that comes in from outside. Likewise Breton the author struggles to make sense out of his own projection, the character "Breton," whose life depends upon the movement of the narrative scene. Who does Nadja call by name when she accusingly says "André? André? . . . You will write a novel about me"? Who moves within the narrative and who keeps the narrative moving? A silent voice resides, ghost-like, in the elliptical space that marks the distance between "André" and the narrative he will write about Nadja. The question of narrative form becomes, in the psychopathology of trauma, a question of life and death.

Whether we refer to the psychopathological researches of Henri Ey, inflected by his sympathy for Surrealism, or to the contemporary researches of psychoanalysts such as Fédida or Schneider, there is remarkable continuity in the description of the phenomenology of the experience of effraction and place (lieu) as "the sensible support (insofar as it is 'lived' by my body) of the sense of these exchanges; but it disappears under the sense of its sense." 27 The sense of support, however, is one of the necessary illusions one must conjure to cope with the trauma of bifurcated identity. Such an understanding of voice in relation to place necessarily bypasses a conventional understanding that would confer to voice a phallic, dynamic capacity for penetration into an implicitly passive, fixed place. As the montage of photographs, song snippets, and juxtapositions of recurrent images as both hallucinations and memories suggest, the narrative events in Nadja (and indeed, in Nadja) do not just happen in the city, they occur through them.

It is impossible to articulate the dynamism of place because this dynamism, this active engagement in real time, cannot be contained in narrative structure or identity construction. Absence and presence (in lost real time, in remembered events, in dreams, in projections and hallucinations) must be understood in terms of correspondence, where the presence of one image might presuppose the juxtaposition of other images in all instances of their reoccurrence. The recurring images of waving hands, fixed handrails, closing eyes, open windows, and hidden tunnels suggest an "Originary otherness from which one cannot withdraw oneself, since it is inseparable from the possibility of being alive" (Pdl'O, 143). In her study of "Claire," Monique Schneider claims that the question of presence and absence in place cannot be resolved by positing place as "the invisible posited as the antithetic of the visible." Rather, place is what makes the emergence of recognizable forms possible. Place is where correspondences can be drawn between interior forms and exterior stimuli:

Place could in no way embody the invisible posited as the antithetic of the visible; it participates in the deployment of the visible, but without emerging as recognizable form. It could not moreover be confused with that which is designated as ground; it is situated on the side of the support, and not on the side of that which is in relief--whence the matriciel dimension of transporting space; the earth as transporting place occurs in the prolongation of what can be attributed to shadow understood as an internal place from which emerges whatever is going to be brought into relief, or appear on stage. [Pdl'O, 141]

("Matriciel" is a pyschoanalytic term indicating whatever fulfills for the feminine the function that the phallic fulfils for the masculine; it indicates a form of causality that is non-symbolic and non-paternal, in time that is not linear but cyclical, and is situated at the pre-oedipal level.) 28

According to Monique Schneider, another mark or emplacement of trauma can be found in screams and cries: "The scream is as much in complicity with the effraction of space as with the passionate movement aiming to essay the erection of a bridge capable of binding together the banks which glance at the menace of emptiness. Will the scream arrive at this other bank, in which the gesture awaited on the part of the other consists?" (Pdl'O, 113). The effraction of identity finds its middle, bridging term in silence before and after a cry, an impassioned, pained cry that courses like an arc from beginning to end in Nadja. Cries and screams incise the tragic experience of return and repetition as a confrontation with effraction and proximity to death. Silence is one mark of trauma that cannot be drawn into the logic of narrative structure directly but which can nevertheless convey the sense of vertiginous, horrific confrontation with an effraction of identity that so closely approximates death. Such cries mark events, enclosing them in an interior or psychic (Breton might claim feminine) place. The close of the play Les Détraquées, for example, culminates in an "unforgettable scream" ("le cri inoubliable") (N, 49/53)--an event that draws a correspondence between the non-discursive mark of ineffability in the cry and the head of a "missing" child whose presence is shockingly revealed in the opening of a cabinet door: "The child's bloody corpse appears, head downward, and falls onto the floor" (N, 49/53). 29 The vulnerability of rationality, the fragility of the structures that allow rational, causal correspondences to be drawn, is showcased in the re of the inverted, falling head. In another episode, Breton attempts to embrace Nadja but "suddenly, as I am kissing her, she screams. 'There (pointing to the top of the window), someone's there. I just saw a head upside down--very clearly'" (N, 107/126). One of the saddest, most shocking repetitions of this motif can be found in Breton's anecdote of M. Delouit, "a story that was so stupid, so melancholy, and so moving" (N, 155/183). M. Delouit is partially aphasic, incapable of remembering certain things such as numbers and certain proper names. He comes to rent a room at a hotel and informs the assistant of his condition:

"Excuse me, I have no memory at all. If you please, each time I come in, I'll tell you my name: Monsieur Delouit. And each time you'll tell me the number of my room."-- "Very well, Monsieur." Soon afterwards, he returns, and as he passes the desk says: "Monsieur Delouit."--"Number 35, Monsieur."--"Thank you." A minute later, a man extraordinarily upset, his clothes covered with mud, bleeding, his face almost not a face at all, appears at the desk: "Monsieur Delouit." "What do you mean, Monsieur Delouit? Don't try to put one over on us! Monsieur Delouit has just gone upstairs!"--"I'm sorry, it's me . . . I've just fallen out of the window. What's the number of my room, please?" [N, 155-56/183-84; ellipsis in original)

The fall and presumed screams of M. Delouit are not explicitly described in the contents of the narrative itself, but are implied by Breton's judicious use of narrative techniques both within and outside of this story within a story. The ellipses that frame this account as well as the dashes and ellipses internal to the story mark the ineffable traces of trauma on M. Delouit's body and the subsequent effraction or effacement of his identity.

The tragic effacement of M. Delouit correlates with the thematics of doubling as mime and mimicry (no less than the problem of identity in relation to language); the drawing of correspondences enacts a mechanism of substitution that can ultimately endanger a cohesive sense of identity. When Nadja mimics Mme. de Chevreuse she ". . . gracefully . . . conceals her face behind the heavy, nonexistent plume of her hat!" (N, 108/127). The allure of the face of an actress mimicking another face is, in such a scene, the allure of Melusine, Medusa, or the Siren. The 'killing look' is the reflection that one obsessively seeks out yet must avoid if one is to survive the encounter intact. The mimicked image and the ineffable cry are traces of inscription, the sometimes horrifying evidence of an attempt to forge a direct link to what Fédida and Schneider have called the feminine (jouissance) as place.

The city landscape is such a place, a backdrop that functions much like the screen in the analytic situation. The screen (itself a surface of multiplicities) becomes a protective shield that both the analyst and the analysand need to sustain the therapeutic dialogue. We can find the city allegorized as a screen in Nadja, in the chapter of Magnetic Fields which is titled "In 80 Days," in Louis Aragon's novel Paris Peasant, as well as in Desnos's Liberty or Love. 30 Surrealist painters such as Miró (Musique, Seine, Michel, Battaille et moi) and Man Ray (L'étoile de mer) also allegorize the city as the backdrop that makes it possible for its inhabitants to sustain their necessary illusions. Nadja's body, whether extended to the drawings or situated in the city, navigates along the fault line between dynamic interior stimuli and exterior excitation. The distance between Nadja and Breton, as well as the disjunction between the two characters and the city they inhabit, is the mark of their effraction. Nadja's interactions with the city are increasingly pathological close calls with city emplacement as jouissance; observing "that hand on the Seine" across the sky (N, 85/98), she threatens to tear away the very screen that protects her freedom of movement. She is in danger of immobilizing herself by staring directly into the face of Medusa, that is, by seeing herself as nothing more than a character that has been frozen into the logic of narrative space.

The photograph of the wax model from the Musée Grevin suggests the dangers inherent in the enactment of mimetic substitution by correlating emplacement with jouissance. Breton included this photograph in the 1963 edition of Nadja and described it as ". . . an adorable wax-work re in the Musée Grevin . . . a woman fastening her garter in the shadows, and it is the only statue I know of with eyes, the eyes of provocation" (N, 152/177-79). Importantly, the photograph of the wax re found in Nadja is cropped in such a way that our attention is drawn not to the re's adorable "eyes of provocation" but towards two busy, fastening hands that are fumbling with a garter. The "eyes of provocation" that have gone missing from the photograph of the wax re correlate implicitly and uncannily with Breton's description of Nadja's "fern-colored eyes" (N, 111/130), while the garter directly recalls the character Solange in Les Détraquées as she lifts her skirt, "revealing a beautiful leg . . . a little above the black garter" (N, 45/49), the role played by Blanche Derval, "the only actress of the period" (N, 49/55; emphasis in original).

Nadja's own city play can be correlated to trauma as is red by Pierre Fédida, who provides a phenomenological account of analytic experience in the context of the psychopathology of informe conditions: "It is clear that every effraction of the protective shield--every perforation of the separating membrane protecting the inside from the outside, which brings about a linkage of the two--is, in the first instance, the basic sense of traumatic experience." 31 Breton conveys Nadja's experience of the outside by shrouding the sequence of events in ambiguity. The narrator forges a sense of continuity by presenting events not in chronological order but through repetition and through correlation. For example, Nadja's rejoinder that she "no longer wish[es] to continue straight ahead" ("résistant à aller plus loin") (N, 100/116) echoes an earlier meeting with Breton where Nadja, refuses "to go any further" ("d'aller plus loin"):

As we walk along the quais, I feel that Nadja is trembling all over. . . . She is very abandoned in her behavior, very sure of me. But she is looking for something, she insists that we walk into a courtyard, a courtyard of some police station, which she rapidly explores. "It's not here. . . . Listen, tell me why you have to go to prison? What is it you're going to do? I've been to prison too. Who was I? It was ages ago. And you, then, who were you?" [N, 84/97; first ellipsis mine, second in original]

In this passage Nadja ushers Breton into a courtyard and asks him "tell me why you have to go to prison? What is it you're going to do?" Breton uses Nadja's accusation to foreshadow his responsibility (as author) for her subsequent disappearance. Nadja's question "Who was I?" is anything but rhetorical when we consider the scene as a staged confrontation between Breton the author and Breton as character/projection. The protective screen that allows this confrontation to take place also turns Nadja into an agent who, in turn, becomes a medium for Breton, though each permits this to happen unconsciously. The possibility that Nadja and Breton might forge a reciprocal understanding of each other as co-inhabitants of the same city landscape is thus foreclosed; Breton turns Nadja's insistence that they stop moving forward into an occasion to interrogate, via Nadja's questions of identity, his own success or failure at producing a reliable translation of true events. Thus, the authenticity of Breton's narrative is guaranteed through his disclaimer that Nadja is, or was, a living person whose presence can be felt in those textual aporias that have, in psychoanalytic discourse, been closely correlated with death.

Nadja's close proximity to death is also correlated with her fear of abandonment, red as the threat of free-falling, uncontrolled motion. Before she and Breton visit the courtyard that becomes the scene that foreshadows the crime of her disappearance, Nadja describes the sense of vertigo she felt when surveying a high window from the perspective of another high window. She echoes the sense of dislocation invoked in the hypnogogic phrase, "There is a man cut in two by the window," that features in the "Manifesto of Surrealism" ("MoS," 21/325): "'It was there, from a window in the Hôtel Henri IV, and my friend, the second man I told you about, was about to leave. And there was a voice saying "You're going to die, you're going to die." I didn't want to die, but I felt so dizzy. . . . I'd certainly have fallen if he hadn't held me back'" (N, 84/97; ellipsis in original). The unnamed man who "held her back" is replaced, in the following passage, by Breton, a man with whom Nadja refuses to move forward until she has surveyed, to her satisfaction, a low-lying window that overlooks a moat:

Now we are walking along the iron grill railing again, when suddenly Nadja refuses to go any further. Here on the right, is a low window that overlooks the moat, and she cannot take her eyes off it. It is in front of this window which looks so forlorn that we must wait, she knows that much. It is from here that everything can come. It is here that everything begins. She holds on to the railing with both hands so that I will not pull her away. She virtually stops answering my questions. . . . She has not forgotten about the underground tunnel and probably imagines she is at one of its exits. She wonders who she might have been, in Marie Antoinette's circle. The footsteps of passers-by make her shudder for a long time. [N, 84-85/97-98]

The window overlooking a great divide is a pivotal allegory of bifurcated identity in the narrative that grants Nadja, in Nadja, her identity. But like the dislocating and vertiginous heights of a window, this fragile identity is itself dislocated from its own surroundings. The footsteps of nameless, faceless passers-by invoke for Nadja a horror that time can march on without her. Yet who or what is responsible for Nadja's increasingly disjunctive and fragile state? She implicates Breton himself during their third encounter, where "She seems to be suffering from a certain inner conflict, but suddenly she surrenders, closes her eyes for good, offers me her lips. . . . Now she tells me of my power over her, of my faculty for making her think and do whatever I desire, perhaps more than I think I desire. Because of this, she begs me to do nothing against her. She feels she has never had any secrets from me, long before knowing me" (N, 79/92; ellipsis in original). Breton makes his own authorial anxiety and sense of guilty remorse a feature of these scenes, for it is when Nadja most emphatically implores Breton to stop moving that the narrative drives relentlessly forward. When Nadja stops moving, Breton explores the duality of identity at the core of the question of communication. The questions "Who were you?" and "Who am I?" recriminate a culpable author residing outside of the narrative space. Ironically, the recrimination is like a certificate of authenticity beguiling the reader into believing that the story of Nadja is a story sans artifice. When the story invokes the fluid temporality of dynamic events, the problem of voice becomes not only the problem of translation but the problem of ventriloquism ("She tells me of my power over her").

As Fédida suggested in his analysis of trauma, the successful communicative exchange involves a sacrifice, namely, the fixing of the dynamic movement of events within the frozen time of narrative. And as we have seen earlier, Nadja's talent for mime and mimicry makes clear that her body must necessarily be subordinated to the narrative voice--even as this body, as corpse or as corpus, provides the grounds or place through which this narrative voice might speak. The problem of projection becomes, in the construction of the narrative, a ventriloquist's act. Such ventriloquism shadows the movement of the analytic dialogue, where projection is used to identify (and thereby contain) the trace or memory of trauma. When the narrator claims that the real Nadja once said "You will write a novel about me. . . . You have to," Breton the author is acknowledging the necessity for using Nadja's voice, through Nadja the character or projection, to suture himself back together again. Yet Breton also realizes that in his recollections what he captures or contains is never the biographical person behind the name Nadja, but the complex projection that he needs to maintain his own variety of necessary illusions.

During their meeting of 10 October Nadja identifies André as the man who will have a hand in her undoing (and subsequent literary redoing when she claims that he will one day write a novel about her). The reality of the author's story is confirmed in a broken exchange, but only, as we have seen before, by casting a long shadow of doubt over the stability of the narrator's sanity as well. Breton the author carefully explores the anguish that motivates Breton the character to justify his chronic storytelling, via Nadja's tacit blessing that he must write a novel about her. Breton's anguish continues to register through Nadja long after she is gone; the literary marks of silence (the ellipses, the dashes, the caesuras) voice those questions that Nadja can no longer ask directly: whose story is being told? for whom is this story being told? and what is necessarily missing?

Breton and Nadja begin their romantic exchange full of the possibility offered by the dynamic landscape of the city. But as Breton realizes that he is incapable of loving Nadja the way he believes she needs him to love her, Nadja is increasingly absorbed into the fabric of city space. The temporary fascination that draws Breton to Nadja is no match for the permanent power that yokes Nadja to Breton: "How does she regard me, how does she judge me? It is unforgivable of me to go on seeing her if I do not love her. Don't I love her? . . . In her condition, she is certainly going to need me, one way or another, and suddenly" (N, 90/104). For Breton, the city landscape continues to function as a backdrop and as a screen, providing him with the cover he needs to sustain the necessary illusions that drive the otherwise disjunctive narrative forward. Breton's narrative ends with allusions to the technologies of escape, to planes, trains and automobiles. But as Nadja's fate unfolds, her range of motion is increasingly constricted and finally ends in the confinement of a lunatic asylum. Nadja too is like the landscape of the city, a screen upon which the main players will enact their drama of identification vis à vis her embodiment of that necessary, distancing silence. In the literary space of Nadja, the interaction takes place within the traumatized, bifurcated identity of Breton himself as he faces the repercussions of creating a story sans artifice.

The passivity characteristic of Nadja's construction is a device through which Breton, though now separate from her in time and place and yet still traumatized by the "irreparable disaster" that has overtaken her, seeks to encounter Nadja herself, or Nadja as she might have been. The effects of his experience with her continue to leave their mark on the writing of Nadja, and it is only in the act of writing that he begins to understand her. It is as if Nadja's madness, her borderline state, is her insight into the mechanizations that lie beyond the confines of her own frozen, atemporal world; the narrative must move on, even though she continues to hope that something will remain of her experience shared with Breton.

The anonymous footsteps march ahead; the particulars of time and place are, Nadja recognizes, in themselves immaterial. The French play on "pas," meaning both "footsteps" and "not" is lost in translation in the English edition. In the French, the step that is taken can suggest the foreclosure of all other possibilities of movement: a journey as a narrowing down of possibilities. The anonymous footsteps remind Nadja not only that time moves on without her, but that she herself may one day become a voided and anonymous "not" (or "has-been"). Breton's guilt in this scene is doubly correlated to Nadja's tragicomic mourning ritual: he knows that Nadja is doomed in her "madness" to remain, in Nadja, an embodiment of projection. She may be removed from the narrative scene, but Nadja will never entirely disappear. She is doomed to remain in a perpetual free fall towards death. We can find traces of Nadja in the textual marks of silence that return us to the problem of construction and to the enigma, Qui vive? ("Who lives?") (N, 144/172):

There are sophisms infinitely more significant and far-reaching than the most indisputable truths: to call them into question as sophisms, it must be admitted that they have done more than anything else to make me hurl at myself or at anyone who comes to meet me, the forever pathetic cry of "Who goes there? Is it you, Nadja? Is it true that the beyond, that everything beyond is here in this life? I can't hear you. Who goes there? Is it only me? Is it myself?" [N, 144/177]

By insisting that Breton must write a novel because "'everything fades, everything vanishes. Something must remain of us . . .'" Nadja confirms the narrator's authorial power to reconstruct a journey that begins with the question "Who am I?" But even as the narrator invokes, in the book's final section, technologies that facilitate movement and travel, Breton's journey is suspended by a question that must elude his powers of recollection. In the textual marks of silence, in trauma, there is a persistent enigma "neither static nor dynamic" (N, 160/190) that eludes both death and the freeze of narrative time; namely, the question: "Who was she?"

3. Construction, Trace: Memory and Song Fragments

The life of a city can be represented by a documentary image. That is what I have attempted to do for Paris. Indeed, no city could lend itself better to an effort of this sort.

Marcel Poëte 32

When we experience a transition from consciousness to expression or from perception to consciousness, a radical failure of symbolization such as silence can seemingly turn the body into the only available medium for responding to the world or perceiving it, at least in psychopathological conditions. The unforgettable scream in Les Détraquées, Nadja's trembling, or her sense of being observed from behind (the latter two common schizophrenic symptoms) capture this sense of alienation and emptiness. Both Nadja and Breton experience the dislocating bifurcation of identity that is concomitant with traumatic experience, but they do not share the same experience or sense of temporality. While Nadja straddles between the world of dynamic events and the realm of frozen time characteristic of narrative structure, Breton experiences a temporality that is related to, yet never wholly coincides with, hers. Yet the "concordant views," those rare but breathtaking possibilities of communicative exchange evoked by Breton, occur only in the negativity of just such moments, those intervals that are also the basis of temporality in narrative/identity construction. To better understand Breton's invocation of such possibilities for communicative exchange or dialogue, we can look to his use of photographs and song fragments in Nadja, devices related to a motif of enigmatic traces that is discernible not only in Nadja but throughout Breton's oeuvre, beginning with "The Unsilvered Mirror" and "Seasons," the first two chapters in Magnetic Fields .

The most telling critical responses to Nadja have been those of Maurice Blanchot and Michel Beaujour. 33 Blanchot's essay has been less influential among art historians, while key themes and terms from Beaujour's essay have influenced both art historians and literary critics, especially his arguments about classifying the photographs and more generally the role of photography within Nadja. Breton, in order to construct a story sans artifice, that is, a work intended not to be a novel (romanesque), deploys a procedure of modal patterning that groups smaller anecdotes into larger structures which, as they aggregate, develop into coherent and cohesive patterns. An organizational structure similar to that of retinal memory develops, despite self-evident discontinuities. Such structures suggest the "existence of a network wherein each element, by its proximity to another one, takes up and confirms the authenticity both of the other elements surrounding it and of the system as a whole" ("QqN," 785). As new elements become attached to this montage, the illusion that these fixed images continue to correspond to the lost continuous time of real events is created to "bring about multiple regroupings. This is the role which has devolved to the forty-seven photographs of the revised edition"("QqN," 785). 34

Beaujour's comments have been modified by those of Arrouye and Ades. Jean Arrouye's study of photography in Nadja recognizes certain trans-iconic themes which are disclosed through the photographs: the themes of what he calls "the regard" and "departure," both subsumed in a broader theme of "recommencement." For Arrouye, the photographs function as "an activator of the imaginary" in Nadja. 35 Dawn Ades describes the non-literary function of the photographs which record the irrational nature of events in Nadja. She especially focuses on the almost complete absence of people in the photographs depicting the city. "At the risk of overdetermining these images," she urges, "I think that the avoidance of the bustle of human activity and city life is deliberate and relates to a preoccupation referred to on the cover of the fourth issue of La Révolution surréaliste, which bore the photograph of a mannequin ascending a curved staircase. The caption runs: 'And War on Work.' What was implied there is stated clearly in Nadja." 36 But much earlier than the "War on Work" is Breton's fascination with De Chirico's empty squares, above all The Engima of a Day . We can find a conspicuous nod to the empty squares motif already in the first photograph to appear in the book, a statue of Rousseau standing before the Hôtel des Grands Hommes, its sharp relief accentuating the flat grid of windows behind it. Another model for the diction of emptiness in Nadja's photographs are the haunting pictures of Vieux Paris taken by Atget, some of which were published in the journal La Revolution surréaliste. 37

Breton's notorious distaste for work or labor does indeed find expression in Nadja, as Ades argues, but it is not in the photographs. They, instead, engage the question of absence, and do so in relation to issues of voice and the problem of place as it appears in the narrative construction. There can be no conception of place, whether in metaphysics, phenomenology, psychiatry, psychoanalysis or deconstructive reflection, that is separable from a conception of memory. 38 This is especially true for a notion of psychic or an interior sense of place, which inevitably depends on a notion of the past as the trace of experience, as memories which are lodged in the unconscious. Breton explicitly links memory to the way anachronic traces are structured in psychic or interior space; the fragility of this link between memory and psychic place is one of the organizing themes that give rise to the narrative structure of Nadja as a series of repetitious events and correlating, juxtaposing images.

It was partly as a consequence of his view of memory that Breton shared the skepticism toward photography that was common in the culture of Surrealism. The title of Miró's painting, Photo: ceci est la couleur de mes rêves (Photograph: This is the Color of My Dreams), wittily dismisses photography in a manner which Breton would easily have endorsed. 39 Photography, it says, cannot capture the color of dreams. Similarly, when discussing Man Ray, Breton would reflect: "The photographic print, considered in isolation, is certainly permeated with an emotive value that makes it a supremely precious article of exchange . . . nevertheless, despite the fact that it is endowed with a special power of suggestion, it is not in the final analysis the faithful image that we aim to retain of something that will soon be gone for ever." 40 Photography, in short, cannot fulfill the functions of memory. No photograph could capture, for example, the "low window that overlooks the moat" which so absorbs Nadja's attention that she "cannot take her eyes off" it (N, 84/97). No photograph could reproduce the hallucination she experiences in the Place Dauphine, "Where only two or three couples are at this moment fading into the darkness, she seems to see a crowd. 'And the dead, the dead!'" (N, 83/94-96). Nor did Breton's mistrust of the medium hinge on the difference between straight (or realistic) and experimental photography, at least if we understand the latter to mean adopting procedures that foreground the unmotivated nature of the sign. For such procedures would be irrelevant for Nadja, since movement in its narrative is always motivated, as is any psychic movement that is matriciel. For this reason, signs that mark the transition from interiority to exteriority or vice versa, including voice, cannot be merely a function of a discursive field; instead they mark the very limit of the discursive, even call for its reconration. The motivated nature of the sign entails a relation to memory, however tacit or unconscious, one that conjoins the orders of the audible (voice) and the visual (the photographs) insofar as both negate the discursive and stand on the brink of its reconration. The trace or mark looks forward, one might say, to an object or event that is about to happen (the reconration of discourse), even while it registers the sense of risk experienced and relived in memory, through construction.

Breton was strongly attracted to the idea that dreams epitomized a world of pure continuity, conceiving of them much as Lacan later defined the Real, or that which lies outside the symbolic process, when he wrote that "The Real is without fissures." 41 Memory, in contrast, is a violation of such continuity, a form of negativity not unlike death. As Breton writes in the "Manifesto of Surrealism": "In the limits where they operate (or are thought to operate) dreams give every evidence of being continuous and show signs of organization. Memory alone arrogates to itself the right to excerpt from dreams, to ignore the transitions, and to depict for us rather a series of dreams than the dream itself" ("MoS," 11/317). Surrealism, instead, introduces its practitioners into the secret society of death: "It will glove your hand, burying therein the profound M with which the word Memory begins" ("MoS," 32/334), alluding to the French word for death, la mort. Earlier in his "Carnets," when speculating on memory, observation, and imagination and their relation to past, present, and future, Breton envisages the possibility that memory might be conceived as a function of imagination and the future, a viewpoint which would turn the present into an evanescent point: "Substitution doesn't even exist, why. Negation of death." 42 Such a notion would effectively dissever time from any sense of direction; the present instead would belong to the order and experience of place (a point), one which would admit no sense of movement either toward or away from. Memory, seen in this light, would become a non-temporal place (lieu anachronique), while death would become a non-temporalizing event (événement anachronique). It is between place and event that the photographs of Nadja occur.

Shortly after Breton raises the possibility that people might "exchange a few incredibly concordant views above the smoking debris of old ideas" (N, 111/130), and shortly after he senses "the irreparable disaster" that is engulfing Nadja (N, 114/136), he resolves that he will persist in his effort of recuperation and reparation vis à vis Nadja, but pursue a different strategy:

It would be futile to multiply here instances of unusual happenings which apparently concern only the two of us and dispose me, for the most part, in favor of a certain finalism which might permit us to explain the particularity of anything, happenings, I say, of which Nadja and I have been the simultaneous witnesses or of which only one of us has been the witness. I no longer wish to remember, as the days go by, any but a few of her sentences, spoken or written spontaneously in my presence, sentences in which I best recapture the tone of her voice and whose resonance remains so great within me. [N, 115/137]

Breton goes on to record nine sentences, but the first two are especially pertinent: "'With the end of my breath, which is the beginning of yours'" (N, 115/137); and "'If you wish, for you I would be nothing, or merely a trace'" (N, 116/137; translation modified). These maxims fall within the understanding of Lacan's la parole fondatrice, that use of dialogue which hinges on the movement of pronouns as the medium of subjective repositionings whereby the participants are changed by virtue of their participation. 43 But they also open up another possibility. Breton, when he recounts the story of his rapport with Nadja, consistently notes that their mutual but distinct temporalities remain in a shadow dialogue with each other. His decision to persist in his pursuit of Nadja, "quite beyond any question of luck" (N, 91/105) and quite beyond anecdote or story into mere transcription of her aphorisms, is a logical extension of the way in which the narrative construction has recorded the effects of negativity. Consider such phrasing as "the tone of her voice," its "resonance . . . within me," "the end of my breath," or "a trace" (N, 115-16/137). These capture a growing sense of the tenuous and insubstantial, while simultaneously offering a promise of life, the hope that people can communicate "concordant views" despite the gaps that divide them. Each phrase, too, captures the sense of an experience in negative ("end of my breath," or to be "nothing, or merely a trace"). Placed in their perlocutionary context, however, where the narrator uses them like a gesture to summon up Nadja's presence, the aphorisms become quasi-silent counterparts to the photographs; in them, memory functions as a non-temporal place (lieu anachronique), just as the song fragments will function as a form of death, a non-temporalizing event (événement anachronique).

There are many moments in Nadja that allude to Breton's earlier prose poem, "Soluble Fish," or which echo its sonorities. The most dramatic occurs on 6 October, a little prior to Nadja's "distractions" or hallucinations. Heading for the Rue Dauphine, Nadja and Breton wander instead into the Place Dauphine, "where," Breton goes on to explain, "curiously enough, another episode of 'Soluble Fish' occurs: 'A kiss is so quickly forgotten'" (N, 80/93). 44 Breton is alluding to section 24 of "Soluble Fish," in which lyrical evocations of a day are woven round the refrain to a forgotten popular song, "A Kiss Is So Quickly Forgotten." The allusion creates a vertiginous montage of memories. Breton is carried away from Nadja and the Place Dauphine to an episode in "Soluble Fish," but it is never clear whether he has made a simple allusion to a literary work or whether, indeed, one experience has connected with another, with the memory of the "lady friend" ("mon amie") in "Soluble Fish" who "speaks in aphorisms" like those of the later Nadja. 45 Section 24 of "Soluble Fish" contains several registers of fragmentation, and each is linked with a mode of presenting the feminine in a way that recalls the presentation of song fragments in Picasso's hermetic Cubism: the speech of the beloved in sententious form; a song fragment that is the refrain for a song which is never named; or the dissolution of song into water, "so that in the morning all that was left of us was that song . . . that lapped a bit of night water in the center of the square" ("SF," 87/380). The dissolution of song into water turns into the movement of water in defined forms: "We passed by unnoticed by the curves down there, sinusoids, parabolas, geysers, rains" ("SF," 88/380), leading to the final presentation of the body via metonymy, faces and hands that persist amid the disappearance or effacement of interacting bodies since: "We in turn put on the garments of pure air" ("SF," 88/381). The movement of memory begun cannot stop here, not least because it opens onto another dimension of experiencing the feminine. The recounting voice of "Soluble Fish" declares: "My eyes were the flower of a hazel tree, my right eye the male flower, my left the female flower" ("SF," 87/381). That passage is plainly echoed in Nadja when Breton discusses The Lovers' Flower, Nadja's drawing which depicts an intertwining pair of eyes: it was "essentially under this sign that the time we spent together should be placed" (N, 116/140). Such imagery and diction is overdetermined in a way that would have disposed Breton to feel such transpositions as significant; after all, at the close of "The Unsilvered Mirror" (chapter one of Magnetic Fields) he had envisaged "plants of the greatest beauty, male rather than female and often both at once" (MF, 30/33). Elsewhere in Magnetic Fields moments of plenitude are rendered in terms that link the register of the audible with a sense of the body being suffused, as in the following passage from "Seasons": "The elephant tusks lean on the star-rise steps so that the princess can descend and the bands of musicians step out of the sea. There is nobody but me now on this sonorous scale-platform, the equivocal wavering of which is my harmony. Ah! to descend with one's hair down and limbs in disarray in the whiteness of the rapids" (MF, 36/39-40). The sustained development of the register of the audible--of intensive focal listening, a passivity in which sound approaches its own spatiality as touching upon, becoming one with space--goes together with the kind of fragmentary movement in image clusters which characterizes automatic writing. 46 Thus, again in "Seasons," we are told: "I have so much sand in my ears, moreover, that I do not know how I shall learn your language" (MF, 37). In such listening (l'écoute automatique), in which attention hovers uneasily between inside and outside and objects of thought are rendered indistinct, the tendency to fragmentation not only preempts the presentation of whole objects, it tends to make the object pulse, blink, fold, peek, and dematerialize into waves, rhythms, or intervals which are encircled by sonorous auras. The subject of attention, meanwhile, is returned to the matriciel dimension of place in which time is essentially cyclical, so questioning the phallic dimension of spatiality. Or as Rousseau put it, recalling the dear old nurse who had attended his birth, "The hands which opened my eyes at birth will be able to close them for me at death" ("Les mains qui m'ouvrirent les yeux. . ."). 47

Rousseau's thought bears on the song fragment and the status of recollection in Nadja. Breton begins one of the unpublished divisions of "Soluble Fish": "A memory doubtless the first and the one that prompted the stars to flower, to open their hands" (". . . fleurir les astres, ouvrir les mains)." 48 The text evokes tones that recall Lacan's Imaginary order, one of mirror-images, identifications, and reciprocities, with images of shadowed deflections that make up the elements and part-objects of fantasy and instinct, especially in the juxtaposition of delight and threat. Deploying diction that echoes the ending of "The Unsilvered Mirror" in Magnetic Fields, a scene in which children's frenzied activity is calmed by gentle erotic hypnotism, the text of "Soluble Fish [II]" describes a psychic state in which: "The appetites are calmed by struggles, the anxieties the doors under which slips a serpent. This memory, pacified, bruised, worked like an iron grill, sometimes cedes its place to a pellet of poison" ("SF [II]," 515). Those who solicit this memory "are big. But the garden of persecutions closes its gates on them and they remain trapped by hosts with spider feet" ("SF [II]," 515). The texture of the work of memory--an iron grill--calls to and transposes the presence of water emblematic of the interior life: "Sheet of water that the interior life, devoted to traces and to calls of moving sand" ("SF [II]," 516). It is in this register that the most telling aspect of the realm of imaginary identifications in Breton's sensibility is presented. For the ensuing equivocations not only dramatize the anachronic or timeless aspect of the unconscious and make clear the implicit relation between the emergence of temporality and the feminine in experience; they stage thinking in relation to the feminine as something inescapable, a state of enchantment and hypnotic fascination. Thus, in a scene that works under the form of a metonymic presentation of tears and joyous melancholy, we come upon: "But a female silhouette inscribed in heart-rain, an encirclement of butterflies in a thought, there it is, which detours the forked time of our pedestals of white frost" ("SF [II]," 516). The obliquity of the "female silhouette" ("silhouette de femme") presages a structure of shadows. In another section of the unpublished "Soluble Fish," one which presents a Judgment of Paris scene and begins, "You are a shadow, nothing but a shadow that is descending from a bell tower" ("SF [II]," 552) the text urges that "there is also a strange rain that resembles death" ("SF [II]," 553). Time, in this passage, is encompassed along with memory and light and the shapes identified in Breton's conception with thinking, above all, the curve such as in the earlier poem "Sunflower": "Had I business with the ambassadress of saltpeter / Or the white curve on the black background we call thought." 49 In the following passage from "Soluble Fish," it is the movement of light, water, and shadow in relation to memory and temporality that is telling: "Through pierced towers, under the wing of vegetal flags which signal nature's barrack to the humans in transit, subsequent to spaces and by the force of things, the shadow regains somewhat the terrain that we had made it yield" ("SF [II]," 516). The prepositional structure of this passage ("Through . . . under . . . subsequent to . . ."; "Au travers . . . sous . . . par la suite . . .") not only textures the movement, but also shapes the structure of shadow extending from the "female silhouette" to the "vegetal flags," from "spaces" into "the shadow [that] regains somewhat the terrain that we had made it yield." The prose poem closes by personifying memory, which now appears partly as projective identification, partly as waiting, and partly as desire and anticipated loss: "It is in this wasteland that I await memory maddened from all the rendez-vous to be had on the surface of the earth. A beam dances on the crystal molehill and from this molehill memory issues like a sheaf of irises, for the greatest satisfaction of the senses crumpled before opening up like gravestones" ("SF [II]," 516). Memory, in this view, comes from a mixture of light ("a beam") and darkness ("a molehill"), a mixture which is given texture and volume ("des sens chifonnés," or "some rumpled meanings") ("SF [II]," 516) and hence an element of the timelessness of the unconscious (anachronique). In another section of "Soluble Fish [II]," the relationship between memory and death which Breton perceived is suggested by evoking ephemerality: "You have not the sombre air of reason in your brilliant closing, beautiful as the root of passions, you do not know why ardor comes to light. You are the only bird for each one of us, the bird which throws itself into the assault of the precipice and which must live but for an hour" ("SF [II]," 553).

If Breton, as I have argued, conceived of memory as a place, and place as feminine, then it should come as no surprise that certain places, especially those most connected with Nadja or most evocative of "Soluble Fish" (the work which most directly explores the texture of the maternal), should prompt ambivalent reactions on his part. When he accidentally wanders into the Place Dauphine he comments: "The Place Dauphine is certainly one of the most profoundly secluded places I know of, one of the worst wastelands in Paris. Whenever I happen to be there, I feel the desire to go somewhere else gradually ebbing out of me, I have to struggle against myself to get free from a gentle, over-insistent, and, finally, crushing embrace" (N, 80/93). Here we recognise the register of panic prompted by the texture of the maternal, a texture that subtly evokes the threat associated with the Sirens, that of immobilization.

The intricate patterns of metonymy and synechdoche in Nadja point to a notion of the feminine which is linked to cyclical temporality. We have explored several forms of encirclement, cyclic return, and frozen time in Nadja, including the tragic repetition compulsion of Solange, a circular form psychosis; M. Delouit's more pathetic compulsion to enact repeatedly his own effacement; the many song fragments (nursery rhymes or chansons); the urban wandering of Nadja and Breton, or Breton's own return to the places haunted by Nadja; and, at the comic level, Brenton's account of the film The Grip of the Octopus, in which "a Chinese who had found some way to multiply himself invaded New York by means of several million self-reproductions," a work "which has affected me far more than any other" (N, 32, 37/38). The image of Solange's skirt "revealing a beautiful leg" (N, 45/49) in a frozen time is but one expression of the jouissance which is linked to psychosis, the troubles de la pensée ("mental troubles") of Solange, and the "crushing embrace" of the Place Dauphine. The grounding support for these encirclings is the city, but the city conceived as maternal imago. The city as feminine place acts as a protective or supporting screen, enabling Breton to reconstruct an identity of sorts in his confrontation with the effracting voice of trauma. Throughout "Soluble Fish," the relationship between thinking and the substance of the feminine is imagined as the interplay of light and shadow, while death is linked to the primitive, archaic memory of the womb. The material is nearly always linked to emergence and to place. 50 It is well known that Breton did not care for music; but he shared with Valéry a profound sense of the music of poetry, for what we hear in Nadja and its fragments is a highly chromatic music of the pathos of place.

The kind of memory which is triggered by the song fragments moves uneasily between the reorganization of a past characteristic of après-coup, that sort of retrospective interpretation which stabilizes the past, and the unorganized timelessness which is the passé anachronique of the unconscious. That oscillation continues all the way to the end of the book, crystalized in the image of the falling bridge and the nursery rhyme "Sur le pont d'Avignon" to which Breton makes oblique reference (N, 154/182). 51 When we see the extent to which identity, effraction, temporality, and death have been situated within la substance du corp féminin (the substance of the female body), we can more fully appreciate the degree to which the canonic definition of Surrealism as "automatisme psychique pur" ("pure psychic automatism," "MoS," 26/328) has been shaped by that key category of nineteenth-century psychiatry in France, troubles de la pensée, a term that covered general failures of attention. The pathology of troubles de la pensée was always gendered feminine. In part of that move in which essentially Symbolist concerns are reinscribed within Freudian terms, Surrealism rejects the ideology behind the category troubles de la pensée. But with Freud's meta-psychology it takes up, consciously and unconsciously, a way of exploring the experience of thought that could not evade either the matriciel dimension of place or the internal relations holding together movement, thought, temporality, and death. This is the level of the archaic which is glimpsed in the haunting silence of the construction in Nadja, in that radical experience of failure.
