The Liberation of Gertrude Stein: War and Writing

John Whittier-Ferguson

[res]

"I was just thinking of a good title for an art book. From Bismark to Hitler." 1

It is an unlikely title for a history of art, and yet, if we had read it when and where it first appeared, in England, in mid-June of 1940, in a little volume by Gertrude Stein called Paris France, we would perhaps have given it our grim approval. 2 Paris had just fallen to the Germans, who now occupied virtually all of Northern and Central Europe. A couple of weeks earlier, a quarter of a million British soldiers had been evacuated from Dunkirk, and Germany's designs on England were obvious. German militarism and the ambitions of its leaders had done much to twist the century into its misshapen, modern form. We could have agreed that "From Bismark to Hitler" was an apt title for a great many books--even those concerning subjects that writers of an earlier era might not have thought belonged under the aegis of a chancellor. Nevertheless, we should pause over the deliberate and forceful incongruity of Stein's wry suggestion. Particularly coming from this writer who had lived so fully, for so much of her life, in contemptuous disregard of explicitly political matters and the business of governments, who openly maintained her scorn for the "gross têtes," the "big heads [who] are ambitious that is the reason they are big heads and so they are at the head of the government and the result is misery for the people," the idea that Bismarck and Hitler might preside, not only over millions of people, but over her discussion of art, seems to constitute a terrible concession by Stein of her aesthetic domain (PF, 28).

But over the course of her life, Stein comes to understand how the story of art is also the story of armies, how accounts of artists and "gross têtes" belong together on the same vexed page. She spends the last years of her life living in wartime, musing on the causes and effects and experience of war, and working war into her art and her theories about art. 3 I focus in this essay on three selections from her extensive writings about the second world war of her lifetime, conscious all the while that leaving any of Stein's war writings out of my discussion means that I have not addressed the full complexity of her response to that war. 4 My selections, nevertheless, are Paris France, written before the German invasion of her adopted country, and two pieces she wrote after the defeat of Germany: an essay for Life magazine titled "Off We All Went to See Germany" , and her final composition, published posthumously in the Yale Poetry Review, "Reflection on the Atomic Bomb" . Although the works are written for different audiences, and although they bracket the war's beginning and ending, they all display Stein's understanding of war as an especially vehement and large-scale manifestation of difference. Literally, of course, those differences are geopolitical and military, and they occur between and within nations. But when Stein writes about war, other differences become crucial as well--between private and public spheres, between past and present, between childhood and adulthood, between her own and others' points of view. Words themselves take on different meanings.

I frame my discussion of Stein's World War II writing by first looking briefly at concepts she develops before that second war, in texts written during the 1920s and 1930s. The peculiar view of World War II through Stein's eyes cannot be understood without first understanding her often anguished meditations--composed for the most part following the publication of The Autobiography of Alice B. Toklas in 1933--on audience and intimacy, on self-possession and the self dispersed, on writing with and without reference, on being read by a necessarily alien public as well as by the few who know her. Insofar as Stein's war writings have received attention at all (and that attention has, with a few exceptions, only begun within the past few years), this late work has been insufficiently linked to her earlier texts. There has also been an understandable tendency to read Stein's war work in terms that, while of great importance, are ultimately limiting. Recent discussions of Stein in France have rested on attempts to decide and evaluate what political position (or positions) she held during the war. Readers in our own time generally want first to determine which side she took (or why she refused to take sides), and how extensively she benefited from the protection of the Vichy government. They ask whether aspects of her own theory and artistic practice can be said to carry political meaning. And how fully does she reflect the continually shifting attitudes of the French themselves--as collaborators, resistors, and the majority of those who stood equivocally somewhere in between? 5 I by no means want to foreclose reading Stein's wartime writing as part of an important contemporary exploration of the problems of allegiance in occupied France, but I want also to expand the terms of the discussion of these texts beyond specific, historical questions of collaboration. For Stein, the phenomenon of war is a manifestation and crucial cause of the excitements and horrors of modernity. These excitements and horrors are as much aesthetic and ontological as they are sociopolitical and military. And in her intimate conjoining of the realms of war and art, Stein displays her modernity every bit as fully as she does in her gnostic, avant-garde writings.

War emerges with startling centrality in "Composition as Explanation"--Stein's lecture delivered in 1926 to undergraduates at Oxford and Cambridge. There is little in Stein's preceding work, or in immediate, contemporary circumstances to explain the pivotal presence of war in this speech. Ulla Dydo suggests that Stein makes her analogy between aesthetic and martial innovation in an effort to close "a generation gap between her audience and herself," but I do not find this explanation convincing. 6 It is more plausible to understand the introduction of the Great War not as a bid for the attention of the young so much as a carefully thought-out explanation for the shape of society and the domain of art in the years after 1919. For Stein, modernity and the war that brings the twentieth century wholly into "the modern composition" derives from conflict, when a peaceful, homogeneous state is broken by the introduction of something different: "Everything alike naturally everything was simply different and this is and was romanticism and this is and was war" ("CE," 501). "Romanticism," in addition to referring somewhat loosely to early-nineteenth-century English poetry, names the fact of division, the introduction of difference into any system--aesthetic, political, psychological. It is important to understand that she connects these systems. The writer who would name her art history "From Bismark to Hitler" is the writer who insists that changes in one sphere mean changes in others.

Her Lectures in America will show Stein developing at great length a geographical history--the phrase is hers--that ties the characteristics of a nation's art closely to the exposure of that nation to difference. 7 In her explanatory narrative, most extensively laid out in the lecture titled "What is English Literature," England grows less and less self-contained as time progresses, beginning to enter its modern state of being with the conclusion of the Napoleonic Wars. By 1815, "England" no longer designates an island and the comparatively homogeneous group of people living within that island's limited bounds but points instead, disconcertingly, to an empire either being built, or maintained, or falling apart far beyond the island's shores. English writing consequently suffers, because it now needs to answer to so many different demands.

But Stein is nominally an American, and she tours her native country with a lecture on the problems of English literature in part so that she can offer to her audiences the answer to modernity provided by their own writers, their own culture. Since America is not an island and since, in Stein's telling of history, it is not a country engaged in the exhausting, impossible labor of maintaining an empire, it has never had to deal with the traumatic violation of inside by outside. Immigration leads to incorporation; it does not result in a nation divided against itself. Clearly, Stein is telling her lettered audience of students, patrons of art, intellectuals, and members of the social elite a comforting story about unity across classes and cultures at a time still cast in the shadow of the Great Depression in a country ferociously divided by difference. 8 Her description of America as having been always and fundamentally alone, disconnected from the rest of the world, also suits the isolationist temper of the decade. 9 Here is a nation that cannot but be uninvolved in the world's increasingly troubling affairs. And for Stein herself, whose anxieties surrounding exposure and connectedness to a general public are so powerful as to bring her writing career to a crisis following the widespread success of The Autobiography of Alice B. Toklas, 10 a claim of her American heritage stands as a bid for remaining unassailably beyond everything:

the disembodied way of disconnecting something from anything and anything from something was the American one. . . . Some say that it is repression but no it is not repression it is a lack of connection, of there being no connection with living and daily living because there is none, that makes American writing what it always has been and what it will continue to become. 11

Ironically, of course, the very fact that Stein gives this talk and the other five that she assembles under the heading Lectures in America to audiences nationwide--audiences that subject her to nonstop, if largely adoring scrutiny during her tour--demonstrates how extensively she is connected to her public. Her overwhelming interest in the issues attending embodiment makes perfect sense. Her second autobiography, Everybody's Autobiography , centrally concerned with "What Happened After The Autobiography of Alice B. Toklas" (the title of the book's opening chapter) and with the American tour, reveals on virtually every page Stein's sense of exposure, particularly as she traveled in her native land. 12 She remains always on display, in texts that can be read and reread by strangers, and in her own person as she stands before those strangers. The fact and conditions of her lecture tour create a principal subject for those lectures. 13

We cannot forget, further, that Stein lives in France rather than England or America--on the European continent, not on an island or a landmass effectively protected by oceans. If we return to her discussion of war and modernity in "Composition as Explanation," we realize that, far from living composed in an "American" state of removal from connections of all kinds, Stein has experienced most of this century in what she calls her "romantic" country rather than the one where she "belongs"--center stage in the theater of world war--and war remains the most appallingly powerful means by which difference is made violently manifest on a large scale (PF, 2). The "romanticism" of 1914-8 has brought her brutally into modernity. There is much in "Composition as Explanation" that might be read simply as aesthetic theory, but we can feel the sharp edge of Stein's satirical characterization of her century as she describes how war dragged millions of people to an unarguably modern state:

And so there was the natural phenomenon that was war . . . [which itself] became completely contemporary and so created the completed recognition of the contemporary composition. Every one but one may say every one became consciously became aware of the existence of the authenticity of the modern composition. . . . And so war may be said to have advanced a general recognition of the expression of the contemporary composition by almost thirty years. ["CE," 501]

Stein utters these sentences to men of ting age, in a community decimated by the Great War, in a climate filled with the most scathing reflections on the pointlessness of the global conflict of a decade earlier, started and scandalously mismanaged by old men from another era altogether. We recognize here the same dark wit that would lodge Bismark and Hitler in the title of a book on art. What has been "advanced" to "every one"--notice the understated comedy of the emphasis Stein places on "every one" ("but one may say every one")--is the violent understanding of difference as it manifests itself in invasion, attack, conflict ending in death, the redrawing of maps, cultural and political revolution, and social and aesthetic change of unparalleled rapidity and scope.

The painful challenge that wartime conditions force on Stein is in important respects a challenge that she has been struggling with ever since the publication of The Autobiography of Alice B. Toklas. Does she choose to stand before an audience, uttering explanations and opinions and answers, exposing herself to queries, engaging in conversations, using language primarily as an instrument for communication? Or does she refuse to debase her medium and to violate her privacy, writing instead purely with the aim of putting words in lively combinations on the page, purging her mind--and, ideally, the minds of her readers--of thoughts about what these words mean, whether they make a story, how they relate to our ordinary world? She pursues both courses, often in dizzying alternations on a single page. And the stakes attending this double choice are extraordinarily high. By the summer of 1940, invasion and occupation are no longer only psychologically inflected res for the experiences of writing and being read. Outside breaks in; Germany overruns France. Although Stein continues to believe that her highest calling is the writing she does without reference to daily living, from 1939 through 1945 she works under the inevitable pressures placed upon a writer to respond to the threat and then the actuality of invasion and the newly importunate facts of daily existence that follow. She now feels compelled to bear some kind of witness, to tell her story about the war she sees, and to satisfy an American public--brought largely into being by The Autobiography--with accounts of life in occupied France. 14

Stein's vacillation between reference and antireferential writing constitutes one of her idiosyncratic strengths as a writer about war, even as it leaves her vulnerable to charges of carelessness in the face of urgent, demonstrable human suffering. All of her texts written during World War II are marked by an aesthetically, morally, and emotionally volatile instability. Examples are everywhere; I offer one from Paris France:

I like words of one syllable, and it works out very well in the French order for general mobilization. The printed thing gives all the detail and then it says the army de terre, de mer et de l'air. That is very impressive when you read it in every village. 15

It "works out very well" if one reads this proclamation of nationwide conscription purely as a collection of words on a government notice (and if the reader is not a man of conscriptable age). Here, as is so often the case, Stein courts furious accusations from readers who are more directly caught up either in the machinery of war itself or who will resent as unpatriotic this aesthetic attention to words instead of a more direct embrace of their stirring reference to battle. She sustains criticism from both groups throughout the war. 16 But she deliberately refuses to be "mobilized" herself by the government's martial rhetoric. She maintains a skepticism about the act of making war and the ways language can be used to promote war. She recognizes facts about life in wartime that an ardent defender of any particular side or cause might not see or might not allow him or herself to register in writing. Her careful observation of the details of life in occupied France constitutes an unusual and important act of witnessing, without the obtrusive presence of the author guiding us through an illustrated argument. As she stares at this "printed thing," she is not attempting to explain, shape opinion, appeal to familiar systems of ethics, influence political or military choices. In this particular form of disengagement, her work is unusual among war memoirs.

Stein's body of work on the war matters in part because it explores, in the most unpropitious circumstances imaginable, a late and especially explicit iteration of a modernist aesthetic dream: the creation in language, by an author who has effectively vanished--inviolate behind the words on the page--of a system of relations that does not tell a story, of words moving but never falling into narrative and never, therefore, pulling us into the human, all-too-human world where cause and effect brings with it responsibility and anxiety and hope and, ultimately, mortal ends. Stein's war writing also matters for the opposite but profoundly allied reason that we can discover on her pages powerfully realized portraits of life in wartime. And the final irony pertaining to Stein's divided aims and her writing that is often so utterly at odds with itself lies in the fact that every writer's attempt to narrate purposefully experience during war founders as it confronts events that cannot be told, experiences that refuse grammatical parsing and the shapes of narration. 17 Her reasons for joining this numerous company of necessary failures are idiosyncratic in the extreme. And yet there on her pages are the rhetorical effects she regularly achieves in her misshapen, interrupted, jarringly framed and therefore deeply expressive sentences about World War II.

Peace and Quiet on the Western Front: Paris France and the Phony War

Written in the bizarre months of late 1939 and early 1940, during which her adopted country declares war in September and then waits for an attack that does not come until the following May, Paris France stands in many respects as the war book of Stein's that captures the most recognizably widespread sense of the times in France. 18 Once France falls to the Germans, Stein's perspective on events grows increasingly personal, though it remains accurate to the peculiar experience of life for the French after armistice, under occupation. Following the Allies' declaration of war against Germany on 3 September, the fall and winter in both England and France--a period journalists soon named "the phony war"--was marked by a surreal combination of normalcy and frantic preparations for battle. Expecting immediate German air raids, both countries placed themselves under blackout, pursued plans for evacuations of women and children from major cities, restricted civilian movement and issued gas masks. They changed radio programming to music interrupted only by occasional announcements, set up home defense squadrons, had city inhabitants digging trenches for shelter, and made numerous other provisions for self-defense. 19 But the expected attack failed to materialize and restrictions relaxed. Life shifted, as the fall progressed, to a strange mixture of waiting in anxious preparedness and resuming something like ordinary daily living. Though a great many sentences of Paris France take an ironic inflection and can even seem grotesquely off the mark when the book is read after its publication date in June of 1940--the Nazis having entered Paris on the fourteenth of June--Stein writes her book when France is living in this indeterminate time before the arrival of the Germans. War is a fact, but it has not yet fully arrived. Poland falls by October, Finland ts the Soviets through the winter, the German army invades Denmark and Norway in April, but France remains untouched. "Paris, France," she tells us in her first sentence, "is exciting and peaceful" (PF, 1). Ever since Three Lives, Stein has been composing sentences around the copula that splinter when we arrive at their predicates. Briefly, now, she is just describing the way things are. She has spent much of her writing life considering how often a single subject comprises contradictory states of being, reflecting on the plural and uncertain ways we occupy any temporally located moment. She proves astonishingly well prepared to describe the limbo of the phony war as many lived it in France.

The country that takes shape on the pages of Paris France seems virtually immune to the motions of time. Stein writes to assure herself and her readers that little has happened or can ever happen in this place, as though she were performing a comforting stroll through an undamaged garden between dramatic but ultimately inconsequential storms. Hers is a pre-industrial, agrarian France that follows rhythms too deeply ingrained to be affected by mere politics or the martial doings of certain men. "[W]hat is was and what was is," she insists: "the season and fashions that exist with the seasons are the things France lives by, the earth has its seasons and the people who live on that earth have fashions and that is all" (PF, 24, 28-9). Imbued with the rhythms of Ecclesiastes, Stein's sentences inflexibly connect the French with the earth and with the least instrumental, least necessary human pursuit imaginable: fashion. French people in this book are either absorbed in attending to their most basic physical requirements--procuring food, clothing, and shelter--or they are deliberately preoccupied by the most frivolous concerns--the design of hats, say, or the ways of their dogs. Fashion, the behavior of animals, and the needs of the body matter enormously to Stein and to the French because these things are still disconnected from the emergencies of the time (though this disconnection will not be sustainable once the invasion begins). "Poetry makes nothing happen," Auden wrote in February of this same year 20 --a time when the laws of cause and effect themselves at times seem weirdly suspended--and Stein celebrates a disengagement that has long been considered the province of the aesthetic: "Now all these dogs being of no use can be made fashionable, because fashion must never be useful, must very often be exotic, and must always be made to be french. That is what fashion is and it must change" (PF, 35). For the first half of Paris France, fashion seems to be the only thing that changes--or the only change that is worth noticing. When those changes come, furthermore, they arise essentially without agency. Like all of the productions of genius in Stein's scheme of the world's operations, fashions cannot properly be attributed to conscious design. 21 They happen almost by accident, as natural phenomena occur, regular as the seasons--predictable, mildly surprising in their minute variations, and consoling: "And so there is no use going on, except that the summers follow one after the other and the fashions go with the seasons" (PF, 28). "Fashion" comes to mean a great deal more, too, than the way clothes hang on a body. Stein increases the domain of fashion, of perceived form, so radically in Paris France that wars themselves somehow seem like hemlines or the cut of a man's jacket--cyclically linked, following a design, echoing one another in a way that brings a dark kind of comfort to the stolid, stoical French. The fall days of 1939 are shadowed by those of 1914. Resigned, the French find pattern, not progress, repetition rather than evolution: "And now it is once more an August and September and there is once more a crisis and once more the farmers the gentle farmers talk about life as it is" (PF, 27). This is a land that cannot be understood in historical terms. It is instead a place of the perpetual present tense: "And so France cannot change it can always have its fashions but it cannot change" (PF, 33).

But a realm locked this completely into timelessness can become difficult to distinguish from a land of the dead. Paris France is haunted by death, which exists as an imminent possibility and an immanent fact of life in wartime. French life exhibits much of the changelessness of a work of art or, more particularly, a monument. Its people move as though choreographed, caught in the earth's ritual embrace. We find ourselves pondering a country that has already lost the war because resisting seems beside the point. France has resigned itself to invasion, given itself over to a life that exhibits few vital signs:

And then the way they feel about the dead, it is so friendly so simply friendly and though inevitable not a sadness and though occurring not a shock. There is no difference between death and life in France and that too made it inevitable that they were the background of the twentieth century. [PF, 13]

In this eternally ancient nation from which the twentieth century grows, among the fatalistic citizens who will pass mildly away, children become the locus for Stein's characterization of the costs and the effects of war. When Paris France shifts from descriptions of the country and its place in history to an attempt to address the experience of wartime more directly, Stein turns our attention to a little girl who lives near her in the village of Bilignin. 22 She makes the shift to childhood for several reasons. Her understanding of space and time has long deliberately retained something of the flexibility, the synchronicity of a child's. 23 Those who live in the state of temporal suspension that characterizes late 1939 and early 1940 in France live to some degree outside of time's ordinary progress, as children do. They seem to be equally helpless, as well, unable to control whatever is about to happen. This village girl also allows Stein to examine her own earlier days as a writer. Using the girl's name as a trigger for her backward glance, Stein returns, in a textual representation of memory, to a prior form of art--what has become, by 1939, one of her most famous instances of avant-garde, noninstrumental writing--now measured against words differently employed. One of Stein's strategies for assessing the meaning of the coming of war is to see what happens when a word slips from a wholly linguistic field of play into a time, a place, a body.

The word is "Button." It has gone from part of a title--Tender Buttons--to a girl's last name: Helen Button. But before we listen to Helen's wartime experience, we must first recall the sketch that famously opens Tender Buttons, under the heading "Objects." Stein presents us with the portrait of an object that is comparatively impervious to time:

A CARAFE, THAT IS A BLIND GLASS

A kind in glass and a cousin, a spectacle and nothing strange in a single hurt color and an arrangement in a system to pointing. All this and not ordinary, not unordered in not resembling. The difference is spreading. 24

The tensions between the familiar and the strange in this passage will return to haunt Helen Button. Even the ordinary attributes of this carafe have turned unsettling: the adjective "blind" disconcertingly suggests the possibility of the object becoming a subject capable of returning our scrutiny, just as "hurt" may point ratively to color but gestures also toward a body. Though this object is "not unordered in not resembling," though it has discernible form, is an "arrangement in a system to pointing," we are not initiated into the design of that system, the category in which its lack of resemblance would seem merely another instance of order. Different from expectations, familiar as kin and cousin might seem, but also, if looked at again or awry, less than kind, less known than novel, this carafe is a spectacle whose hard-edged indeterminacies threaten to overwhelm the observer and the observer's methods for making sense: "The difference is spreading."

Before we watch Helen Button staring at a kindred object in Paris France--as though she were looking at one of the items from Tender Buttons transposed, twenty-five years later, on the eve of the next war, to a village in Southeastern France--we witness her transformation from a little girl to a character in a story by Stein: "We have had here in the village a little girl and her name is Hélène Bouton, Helen Button" (PF, 80). 25 Hélène Bouton's imperfect reflection in Helen Button signals the difference between life and narrative and inaugurates a double act of recollection--of a French girl and of a highly charged English word from Stein's prior text that shares Helen's last name. Stein undertakes the complex act of recollection emphasizing that all portraits should take the measure of and unfold self-consciously in time, particularly when time carries such heavy freight: "because after all this war is not war," she adds in her opening, "it is war-time" (PF, 80). This war is "not war" because it has commenced without fully, violently beginning in earnest for the French. All of Stein's writings about war will explore the myriad ways in which the state of wartime itself can at once seem all-encompassing and wholly irrelevant to anyone living during war. Every sentence in her work that measures the full effect of living under war's terrible dispensations is followed shortly by a companion sentence that shows the subject virtually untouched by circumstances we associate with conflict. And not only does Stein underscore her subject's divided modes of being in time. She also encourages us to look beyond particulars, so that the local Hélène becomes the more general, more textual Helen, who lives not simply in late 1939 but in a widespread historical condition we call "war-time," stretching back even to the Iliad, where we will also find this girl's name. "She lived somewhere," Stein impatiently assures us, knowing that we will naturally gravitate toward the pathos of a specific case history and thereby be likely to miss the even more sobering truth that there are millions of Helens existing in time and space, their fates echoing across the centuries like the ticking of a clock. "[B]ut the thing that is important is that she lived during war-time. There is a great deal of war-time in history and Helen Button lived in it" (PF, 81). Stein introduces her portrait as a story about "what a child's feeling about war-time is" (PF, 80). Helen Button, we find, has spent many of her days and nights anxiously immersed in history.

Stein's story of Helen occupies ten enormously complex pages of Paris France, and yet it is with the first, deliberately framed narration of an event in Helen Button's life that her last name and the event together recall Tender Buttons. Stein now restages the opening of her earlier work, providing us with an object and a perceiving subject:

Helen Button started out with her dog William. As they were walking along suddenly William stopped and was very nervous. He saw something on the road and so did Helen. They neither of them knew what it was at first and at last as they approached very carefully they saw it was a bottle, a bottle standing up right in the middle of the road. There had been something in the bottle but what, it looked dark green or may be blue or black, and the bottle was standing up in the middle of the road, not lying on its side the way a bottle on the road usually is.
William the dog and Helen the little girl went on. They did not look back at the bottle. But of course it was still there because they had not touched it.
That is war-time. [PF, 82]

The passage resists reading as forcefully as did its antecedent in Tender Buttons. And this kindred moment is explicitly concerned with the effects of that resistance on two emblematical interpreters who share nervousness though their differences from each other extend even to their species. The familiar open vista of the road suddenly contracts around an object needing to be deciphered, threatening in its pure opacity. That mere object, the bottle of indeterminate color, demands interpretation because Helen and William are searching for what Stein, three paragraphs earlier, has called "signs" that will orient them in their ominously unfolding presents. The somehow crucial difference--that this bottle stands up rather than lying on its side--seems fraught with meaning in part because there is so little to interpret, so little to hold on to in this scene that epitomizes Helen and William's continually menacing, largely uneventful lives in wartime.

The difference, too, between this nonincident and "A CARAFE THAT IS A BLIND GLASS" augments its already considerable power. When Stein's object returns on this road, changed but related to a precedent object in Tender Buttons, and when we watch this girl and her dog equally though differently afraid of its possible significance, we confront a representative anxiety about failing to make meaning. Circumstances demand interpretation but leave us with insufficient points of reference by which we might venture to make sense of things. Tender Buttons does not stand as a key in relation to this later scene; the echoes do not help us read this different world. Our own acts of recollection simplify nothing. Brooding over history is as existentially mystifying to Stein as is being in the present, but those twin mystifications are most upsetting and most obvious during war. The comforting appositives, "the dog" and "the little girl" try, with only partial success, to reassemble William and Helen after their misadventure. But the bottle, Stein carefully insists, opening a space in her narrative for memory's continued, hysterical workings, "was still there because they had not touched it." Helen and William do not look over their shoulders, but the narrative glances back, forcing us to acknowledge that the impervious object remains to be recalled. Being in wartime exacerbates the tendency for the subject to fall into a seemingly infinite variety of disorders, leaving the inner and outer interpretable worlds incoherent, filled only with difference and unspeakable absences. The faculty of memory allows (or is condemns a better word?) the repeated return to these moments of difference, just as this brief scene circles back on itself. Stein's sketch of the displaced Helen and William holds out to them and to her readers the possibility, at once terrible and promising, of recollection. Not looking at the bottle but seeing it all the same, they know that there is something--a world--still there "because they had not touched it."

As the story of Helen Button in wartime continues, Stein displays, in the compressed narrative of her character's life, experiences and patterns of thought that will characterize her subsequent writing about the war. Helen's is a world where no relation--between people, with animals, with objects themselves, and even with the ostensible enemy--can be relied upon; where the urge to embrace prophecies, or hold to any system of signs that will allow the comforting sense that the future is foreknown and follows a plan is continually offset by even the most ordinary, expected events going wrong; where the laws of cause and effect seem suspended; where the familiar turns horrific and the anticipated nightmare does not materialize. Helen watches her friend Emil's dog, Ellen, "changing and her fur turned into large baggy trousers and her head turned into a large shaggy head and it was a man, an enemy man" (PF, 86). 26 She clings to her Aunt Pauline, who knows what the future holds--an extremely consequential gift of sight in these days, since "in war-time anything might happen so it was very necessary to know it" (PF, 87). 27 When the unexpected does occur (Aunt Pauline's lucidity has no observable effect on this story), consequences remain puzzling. Helen's dog is hit by a car: "The automobile came on, it went over William and Helen screamed and William was down and then he was up and then away he went, oh how he did go" (PF, 89). Shortly after this misadventure, a wagon comes by, carrying an unidentified "animal": "nobody had ever seen any animal like it before, it was enormous and it was dead. . . . The enormous animal did not have a tail and it did not have any ears. It was an enormous animal and it was war-time. Helen did not really see it but she told herself about it" (PF, 89-90). The last event in the story of Helen Button's life is, fittingly, her encounter with the enemy. Modernity and life within "the modern composition," to use Stein's phrase, means living exiled from the quiet confines of an imagined and secure self, without an island haven, lacking words alone. To be modern is to be forced, perhaps as one writes for a general public or gives lectures before anonymous audiences, often by the frightfully effective mechanism of war, into contact with the irreducible mystery of others. It is to live always in the state of wondering "who goes there?" Helen and Emil, her French friend with the German name, see a young man on a nearby hill with a spy glass. They decide on a patriotic course of action:

Emil went up to the man. He said to him. You are an enemy. I am not, said the man. If you are not what is your name said Emil. My name is Henry said the man.

There they stood and the snow in the fields and the water splashing and the man in wooden shoes, looking through his spy-glass at something.

What should they do.

It really was war-time.

They all went away always looking behind.

Very soon then there was nobody anywhere.

This is war-time.

Helen did not know why but this was the first thing in war-time that had made her cry. [PF, 91-2]

She grieves for herself and her precarious condition, for the vacuity of a crowded world. She knows that looking back will no more resolve the question of this stranger's allegiance than it will establish the meaning of the bottle in the road, and that going away makes as much sense as any other choice she and Emil might have taken, though it accomplishes nothing. She is still young, and the war continues, but she now understands all that can be known of her condition, "So for her," Stein explains, "war-time was over" (PF, 92). In the same bleak but resolved frame of mind, Stein casts a glance back over what she has just written and concludes, "So this is the twentieth century" (ibid.).

Stein in Life: Victory and the Problems of Naming

One of the most terrible aspects of Stein's portrait of Helen Button is that the girl vanishes in tears from Stein's narrative, never to reappear. Triumph over the enemy does not form part of her story. Stein, on the other hand, experiences the complex euphoria of liberation. I turn now to two pieces she wrote after V-E Day, one describing a trip she takes to Germany with the United States Army, the other containing her thoughts about the atomic bomb. Jean Gallagher who, in The World Wars Through the Female Gaze, discusses a number of Stein's World War II writings, argues that, "In Stein's postliberation writing, not only do we see a marked turn toward more detailed visual description and a willingness to interpret visual data for readers, but we also see how Stein participates in a militarized gaze." 28 It is certainly true that Stein's Wars I Have Seen records the coming of the Americans in greatest detail and vehemently celebrates the Allied victory. I do not agree, however, that she allows herself to see more clearly or with increased specificity--in this text or in others written in 1945-6--than she did before the arrival of her countrymen. Nor, I think, does Stein consistently write with an interpretive certainty she lacked before the defeat of the Germans. Rather, I suggest, the United States press catches her up in its representations for the American public of the liberation of France. Her writing about the war and her attitude toward the coming of the Americans make national news. So powerful are the public narratives of victory, and so compelling is the story of this artist's liberation, that Stein finds herself speaking (and being quoted) in public venues that discourage ambiguity, even though she herself continues to interrogate the war and its legacies. 29 The war correspondents, Eric Sevareid and Frank Gervasi, report Stein's "liberation" to the American press; stories about her are carried in many American periodicals; Stein herself makes a broadcast to America. 30 A continual stream of American officers and enlisted men come to her house in Culoz for visits. 31 She appears in a film, A G.I. Sees Paris. 32 Coming out of her necessary retreat in the French countryside during the war, Stein is thrust back into the public eye as fully as she was after the Autobiography was published. And she measures that publicity and the consequences of victory every bit as thoughtfully as she weighed the success and the effects of her own prose in the mid-1930s. She lives now, at the end of the European war, in a time of resolutions, when so many are busily providing answers and explanations and retrospective interpretations. Her own project in this last year of the war becomes a questioning of conclusions. What she demonstrates again, in this next phase of her writing about the war, is her ability to capture the essential oddity of war and its aftermath. Few writers can speak with such peculiar authority about the surreal in the ordinary and the ordinary in what we thought was apocalyptic.

Stein's most explicit return to her mass audience occurs in her appearance as an official witness of the Allied victory before the readers of Life magazine in August of 1945. 33 She begins her essay, "'Off We All Went to See Germany,'" with the insistent invitation of a corporal:

I think you'd better come and make a trip over Germany said Bob Sweet. Bob Sweet is a corporal in the 441st Troup Carrier Group and he is full of ideas and I usually do whatever he tells me to do. [Life, 54]

There is a great deal of humor in this essay, and it is humor of a kind that is likely to meet with the approval of Stein's American readers: "But Bob, I said, I don't like Germans, I saw enough of them in France I don't want to see them at home" (ibid.). She assures us, by the end of the first paragraph, that we need not be concerned that we will hear too much about Hitler's defeated nation: "And I really pretty well forgot about Germany and the Germans in the enormous pleasure of living intimately with the American Army" (Life, 54). The essay already begins to complicate its ostensible purpose. This will be a visit to see Germany in which not many Germans and not much of the country needs to be seen. She reduces that country to a nonentity in prose that is startling in its simplicity, as innocent of martial fervor or vengeful pleasures as the musings of Helen Button or her friend Emil. The editors of Life smile at her throughout, adding a section heading, for example, to her observations about the effects of Allied bombs on Cologne. "Roofs are important, yes," the editor of Stein's essay agrees with Stein, in pleased condescension at her own description:

Cologne was the most destroyed city we had seen yet, it is natural, of course it is natural to speak of one's roof, roofs are in a way the most important thing in a house, between four walls, under a roof, and here was a whole spread out city without a roof. There was the cathedral but it looked very fragile as if you pushed it hard with your finger your finger either would go through it or it would fall over. [Life, 56]

But Stein maintains her own integrity, her distinction from the editorial point of view, simply by recording her observations in her own style--a style that continues to demonstrate how tentative is the act of witnessing, how little we can harvest from our gleaning of the world. With the victory of the Allies comes an endless string of documentary accounts and photographs and films that promise to bring us closer to the facts of the war in Europe. Stein is a cautious, canny member of this documentary assembly. The photograph that introduces her article weirdly illustrates the questions, at once visual and ethical, raised by much of her writing about the war ( 1). How much does this witness see? How much does she know about the surrounding world, and how fully does she respond to that world? Stein and Toklas "see the Rhine," the picture's caption assures us, but due to an accident of lighting, we see not the view from a window but a framed, black square. The image provides an unsettling introduction to a piece built around observations of a foreign country. In a furious response to the predominantly unperturbed tone of Wars I Have Seen, a reviewer in The New York Times wonders, "Didn't Miss Stein ever see the harried, beaten, ragged refugees in Paris from 1933 on? . . . Where are Miss Stein's eyes, those eyes that can so unerringly discern the value of a Picasso?" 34 It is precisely these canny aesthete's eyes, however, through which Life proposes we glimpse Germany. Reinforcing in the strongest imaginable terms a divide between the terrifying world of men in destructive action and these two older women who have given their lives to art and to a sheltered, somewhat unorthodox but fundamentally bourgeois domesticity, this article presents us with a deliberate respite from anguished journalism. In its place we are given a small collection of impressions and disarmingly direct commentary on the causes of and antidotes to war from one of the very first postwar tourists in Germany. Stein herself comes to understand her unique status shortly after her arrival in Frankfurt, as she discovers the Germans looking not at the American soldiers but at "Miss Toklas and myself":

First I was puzzled and then I realized that we were probably the very first ordinary civilian women with American soldiers, not looking official, just looking like American women . . . and [the Germans] realized for the first time that there were going to be thousands of civilians coming there just to look as we were looking. [Life, 54]

And while she is "looking," her special abilities as a collector of art are by no means to be replaced by more predictable capacities for moral outrage. Her aesthetic judgments are exactly what Life requires:

we soon came to the house where Göring's works of art were temporarily housed. . . . It was exciting to see all those pictures but it had nothing to do with Göring, there was no personal taste, he had excellent advice apparently. [Life, 56]

In an eerie, war-inflected reprise of her return to Oakland in April 1935, when she found "no there there," the central purpose of Stein's trip to Germany is to discover emptiness, to find no record of titanic personalities, to see only surfaces and hollow shells of bombed-out buildings, to take a tour, yes, but to find virtually nothing on the trip. 35

Modris Eksteins has recently published an essay on war tourism, focusing primarily on tour guides and accounts of travel after the First World War. Eksteins documents the phenomenon of postwar tourists and tour promoters, arguing that "the sight of civilization in ruin produces . . . an interesting mix of piety and titillation, of rumination and excitement." 36 Stein's quick trip to Germany cannot offer such sublime and suspect emotional satisfactions, since she finds little in the faculty of memory worth attending to--of what use, then, would ruins be? Whatever titillating horror this journey does have to offer must come when Stein reaches her journey's destination--Hitler's mountain retreat in the Alpine town of Berchtesgaden. 37 The photograph of her in this spot comes at the close of the Life essay ( 2). To understand this photograph and the account Stein gives of the occasion it commemorates, we should remember that, in 1945, there were a number of senior Allied officers and intelligence staff who feared that Hitler and his remaining followers would make a last, bloody stand in the "Mountain Redout" of Berchtesgaden. The town and Hitler's sanctuary had become saturated, in the imaginations of soldiers and civilians, with the horrors of the Third Reich.

It is the aura of this place that allows Stein her metonymic description of the next stage of her journey: "off we went to Hitler" (Life, 57). But those five words, which ring with the declarative simplicity of a nightmare, introduce us instead to the most surprising scene of whimsy:

there we were in that big window where Hitler dominated the world a bunch of GIs just gay and happy. It really was the first time I saw our boys really gay and careless, really forgetting their burdens and just being foolish kids, climbing up and around and on top, while Miss Toklas and I sat comfortably and at home on garden chairs on Hitler's balcony. It was funny it was completely funny, it was more than funny it was absurd and yet so natural. [Ibid.]

Stein's long-famous simplicity of diction now serves as a measure of the Allied victory. So completely has Hitler been defeated that where he once ruled soldiers can become children again, and aging women find ordinary comfort. The prepositions, "up and around and on top," speak of liberation and the license to be in places formerly forbidden, places grown-ups aren't usually allowed to go. Stein cannot avoid calling attention to the wonder of this scene--Mother Goose and her gay charges in the now-domesticated heart of darkness--"it was more than funny it was absurd."

This self-consciousness becomes literally manifest in the moment we see frozen in the photograph. As though the pressure of absurdity strikes the whole company simultaneously, Stein reports that "we all got together and pointed as Hitler had pointed" (Life, 57). Their collective pose not only mimes the commanding gesture Hitler might have made as he surveyed his country (and, by implication, the world far beyond his field of vision). The angle of their raised arms and outstretched hands also suggests the Sieg Heil salute itself--one of the most powerfully iconographic gestures of the past century--now turned into the merest assumption of an attitude. As Gallagher points out, this gesture displays "an impulse toward mimetic and iconographic outrage," but she adds that "Stein allies herself visually not only with the victorious American soldiers but also with the stance of victory itself," and thus "the female observer's vision is effectively absorbed into the trajectory of the military gaze." 38 I suggest that Stein by no means fits so easily into the soldiers' company, much as she takes pleasure in horsing around with them in the photograph. Her very next gesture sets her apart again, as she continues to skew the signs of war in the souvenir hunting that follows this anti-salute:

What I wanted was a radiator, Hitler did have splendid radiators, and there was one all alone which nobody seemed to notice, but a radiator a large radiator, what could I do with it, they asked put it on a terrace and grow flowers over it, I said. [Life, 57]

The elegiac associations that might cluster around these future flowers are largely negated by the strikingly odd choice of an object for them to adorn. A radiator remains, however, just the thing that the author of Tender Buttons might be expected to try to carry away from this place. But it is not June of 1914, not the summer when Tender Buttons made its fairly muted debut on the stage of the avant-garde. This short excursion in Life, undertaken in a profoundly different time, before millions rather than hundreds of readers, depends for its success on Stein managing to retain her identity as a famously incomprehensible artist, while also giving a convincing performance as an unremarkable, middle-class American tourist. The critical, satirical edge to the piece--evident in the poses Stein assumes before the Germans and the GIs alike--derives primarily from the odd fact that these two identities exist together in this one radical, ordinary woman.

Near the end of her essay, when she and Alice Toklas are flying back to France with the GIs, Stein comments wryly on her plane's new cargo:

the boys showed us all the things they had acquired. Where they had acquired, what they had acquired, better not know. There are three million American soldiers there and each one of them has to have at least six souvenirs. Dear me. They call these objects liberated. This is a liberated camera. Liberated they are. [Life, 58]

As is the case in all her writings about American soldiers, Stein is entranced by the GI's English usage, the way her native language is changing under the pressures of war. For the victors, "liberated" is a humorously dishonest word, a euphemism, an adjective that evades an ugly truth about the spoils of victory. It is a word that exemplifies and characterizes Stein's lifelong project to shake words loose from their referents, to pry words apart from things. Like "exciting and peaceful" in Paris France, or dozens of other words and moments in this body of her writing, "liberated" belongs in Stein's lexicon of words that reference wartime while also refusing to be only about the war. Helen Button might cry at the end of this piece too, in spite of its cheeriness, since it gives us so little to hold on to. It liberates its readers, finally, from a world that is too strange, too filled with the evidence of past trauma but without the cause and effect, the inquiry into motivation that might make that trauma fit into an explanatory narrative. We see Stein in the photograph from Berchtesgaden liberating a sign associated with Hitler. 39 Whether this liberation is successful we will have to determine for ourselves. While the meanings of that sign may ultimately be as resistant to moving as Hitler's radiators, Stein is conducting an experiment, in the image and the prose she attaches to that image, to determine how far a public moment and an historical gesture can be dislocated. Gallagher, writing about this photograph in particular, and about Stein's post-liberation point of view, finds that "the image is a visual representation of the 'liberation' of Gertrude Stein from a set of wartime political loyalties and representations riven by contradictions, doubts, ambivalences, and from the continual deferral of political interpretation of visual and other evidence." 40 But for Stein, victory does not bring unambiguous clarity, reassertions of meaning, an end to confusions and problems of reference. It brings only the unsettling facts of liberation.

The End of Difference: "Reflection on the Atomic Bomb"

"Off We All Went to See Germany" appeared in Life on 6 August 1945, the day that the United States dropped the atom bomb on Hiroshima. This coincidence serves as well as any single event might to characterize the state of the world that receives Stein's work. Photographs of the devastation in Japan will appear within two weeks in Life. 41 It is difficult to find a newspaper or magazine published in the United States in the years immediately following the end of the Second World War that does not run a series of essays on the meanings of having entered what is already being called the "atomic age." Stein dies on 27 July 1946, but shortly before her death she, too, privately joins this conversation about the Bomb, with a three-paragraph essay, "Reflection on the Atomic Bomb," published in December of 1947 in the Yale Poetry Review. The editors give the piece its title and a gloss: "This is the very last piece that Miss Stein wrote before her death in 1946." 42 It is quintessential Stein:

They asked me what I thought of the atomic bomb. I said I had not been able to take any interest in it.

I like to read detective and mystery stories, I never get enough of them but whenever one of them is or was about death rays and atomic bombs I never could read them. What is the use, if they are really as destructive as all that there is nothing left and if there is nothing there is nobody to be interested and nothing to be interested about. 43

Romanticism and its sociopolitical agent, war, find their apotheosis and their conclusion in this new invention--so completely destructive that it lacks meaning. With her phrase "what is the use," she signals the collapse of the poles of her art: poetry and history, pure and instrumental language, private and public worlds. One cannot measure difference or weigh winner and loser when there is "nothing left." Who can discern dichotomies in this obliteration? Who will there be to recognize this next form of the modern composition, she wonders: "Sure it will destroy and kill a lot, but it's the living that are interesting not the way of killing them, because if there were not a lot left living how could there be any interest in destruction." There are, of course, two enormous "lots" of "living" who would have taken issue with Stein's assessment, however philosophically apt we might find it from our own temporal distance: the wounded, dying, and defeated Japanese and the enormously relieved Americans who had been granted a reprieve from the nightmares of island invasions.

But Stein herself realizes that, in the wake of this second world war, she has very little to say. It is essentially a problem of scale, of calibrating words and things. If the Great War, with its ten million dead, brought us into modernity, what do the fifty million dead in its sequel bring? Why should she continue her demanding, risky search for an appropriate register in which to write about this new form of modernity? The history of her own art ends not with Hitler after all, but with Harry Truman and the invention he authorizes for use. Her last reflection on writing is almost as simple as saying nothing. Here is the whole of her final paragraph:

Everybody gets so much information all day long that they lose their common sense.
They listen so much that they forget to be natural. This is a nice story.

Three weeks before his own death in 1939, W. B. Yeats wrote to Lady Elizabeth Pelham, "Man can embody truth but he cannot know it. . . . You can refute Hegel but not the Saint or the Song of Sixpence." 44 Stein was probably not aware that she had cancer when she wrote her final sentence, and had she lived she would no doubt have found a way to continue using language. And yet this paragraph stands as an end of writing, not a beginning. Like the patient farmers in Paris France, like Yeats in his final days, Stein imagines living in a way that renders words as incidental as the weather. There are no winners and no losers. Only the simplest forms of narrative, the least ambitious kinds of naming (if the fraught activity of naming is even necessary at all), and the last liberation offered by nature.
