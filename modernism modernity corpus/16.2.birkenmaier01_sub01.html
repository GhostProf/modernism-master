Paul Deharme: "Proposition for a Radiophonic Art"

La Nouvelle Revue Française, vol. XXX : 413–23.

Since the appearance of the wireless, everyone has predicted, albeit in fairly vague terms, the rise of a truly radiophonic literature and dramatic art. And so, in every country, radio stations have invited us to listen to the story of the lighthouse keeper driven crazy by a tempest where, alone and powerless in his lighthouse he can only report to the listener what he sees, accompanied by elaborate noise-effects and the sirens of ships in distress; or else there is the story of the scholar in need of silence for his research, who is constantly interrupted by the piano of his neighbor, the trumpet of the faucet-seller, and the cries of the new-born.1xi Today, however, it is no longer enough to shoot a pistol in the air in order to scare the audience or to pretend to be deaf, mad, or a stutterer in order to make them laugh. Why have we, in this new domain and public sphere, not yet come up with truly new forms of expression?

I am convinced that today's minds have a need for an imagination and lyrical transformation that cannot be satisfied by any conventional or even recent art forms, except by a radiophonic art. The taste for the unreal is part of this need, and it announces itself, among other examples, in the pleasure that an average audience, one not necessarily uneducated but young, takes in a film replete with special effects where human beings float in the air or turn into smoke, objects become animated and interfere in the action, and things refuse to abide by the rules that normally govern them.2 It seems to me that the waves of the wireless, remote and mysterious like the sources of our thought, can and should feed our imagination with the new inspiration that it deserves.

The interior theater.

Under the conditions that I am going to explain, the wireless will be enabled to evoke images in the spirit of the listener analogous to those of dreams.3

The psychological importance of dreams can no longer be disputed. We can, however, make one observation easy to grasp for the reader: many times our memory of a dream seems to our mind so plausible and real that we ask ourselves: "Have I really dreamt?" In those cases, we have to make an effort to remember everything correctly and free ourselves from the preoccupation or the joy provoked by that dream, exactly as if we had really experienced it. Similarly, after a slip of tongue or an involuntary gesture, prompted, as everyone knows, by a process similar to that of dreams, we exclaim: "Am I dreaming?" This familiar occurrence expresses an undisputable scientific truth.

It is therefore not absurd to hope that we are able to create inside ourselves a theater analogous to the theater of dreams.4xii We will categorically reject those who claim that these projects are related to hypnotism.xiii Rather, we believe that tales, readings, landscapes, and music instruments can "cast a spell" on us. There is a spontaneous instinctual reaction in us when confronted with nature or with a work of art that makes us "close our eyes" and drift into a state of "absence," as we know. This is the tendency that interests us here. We won't listen either to those who object that this is an acoustic phenomenon whereas dreams are made of visual hallucinations. The hallucinations we have in mind can in fact be recreated by means other than optical ones. All of us have experienced the abyss that separates the impression produced by an anticipated performance from that of the same performance when we see it.

In order to move comfortably inside his imaginary world, the listeners will most likely have to undergo an education of the senses. But this should be an easy thing, as the wireless offers us the means to neutralize a given reality and balance it between the source of suggestions and the state of mind of the listener who plays his inner film, ideally with the aid of headphones.5 Thus, in front of the loudspeaker or perhaps even wearing his headphones, the listener will close his eyes and not think of anything.

We have known silent art, now we have blind art. This project, in its spirit close to surrealism, should not be received by the public as unfavorably as the early literary manifestations of that doctrine were.

Surrealism owes its beginnings and really its life to the subconscious, as it is called today. And it is this same subconscious that we aspire to touch with the help of the wireless, but in a direct fashion, thus avoiding to awake the conscious mind and its disturbing actions.6 The first surrealist experiments, in contrast, were of an exclusively subjective nature; they focused on exploring the realm of dreams, yet their goal was to lend "literary" expression to these experiences. To make out of a highly personal experience the objective origin of art, which is always public, was as vain a project as it would have been to take a picture of oneself in a mirror.7 In a mirror, even if it is a magic one, one can only discover oneself.

In the future it must become possible, thanks to the collaboration and experience of the community of wireless amateurs, to allow the listener to decide the moment when he or she can "see" a suggested image. The listener will in fact have created for himself a mental balance, perhaps as difficult to obtain for some as to keep one's attention focused on external and visible objects, but with some exercise it will become mechanical.

Attempt at a technique

Here are twelve rules that follow immediately from the preceding. They constitute a theoretical summary, a preliminary system. Experience will rule out some elements, correct others, and confirm perhaps a few of them: this list is only meant to open up a path of inquiry.

  1. 1.   When prompted, each listener shall imagine that he is the principal protagonist of the comedy that he is going to hear. Likewise, we will introduce into our objective narrative prompts for subjective sensations such as, for example, the following: "It is cold.—You are hungry.—Here are the ones you love.—Far away, there is a house; this is where you were born.—A car passes by; it is the one that you noticed the other day, etc…" This innovation is essential for our purpose.

  2. 2.   The "reciter" shall forget the art of reading aloud.8 It is necessary that the listener take in the text as if he were reading it by himself in silence (with closed eyes and his ears stopped, eliminating in this way all the risks of distraction from the outside that are run by the solitary reader). Therefore, grey diction is recommended, arguably a difficult task for the speaker, but after a while he will be a specialist at it, a kind of phonograph.9 The idea is to obtain a narrative "chopped up" into separate images that are presented in succession.10

  3. 3.   Broaching now the issue of the background story (as if these were the words in brackets of a novel), instances of direct speech will be rare. These few instances then should produce great effect and should be pronounced by voices other than that of the speaker; for example: "Hands up!" shouts a new voice . . . and that of the speaker will continue tonelessly: "a man appears to the left . . .", and give a description of how the character looks, what kind of personality it is, etc… When they appear for the first time in the comedy, these characters will be "introduced" in as much detail as desired in order to become similar to those who move in our dreams and who originate together with the imaginary memories related to them. In other words, each character will, as if in a dream, come with his or her particular description attached. But the words of a given character can be spoken at times by one individual voice, at times by the speaker, as well as the character's deeds and gestures. The particular voice that, for a moment is at the character's service (see next section), will then become available to be used for other characters or on another occasion (see section 8).11 This should not pose a problem for the balanced listener; does it not also happen in our dreams that a sentence begun by one person is finished by another person, or even by us? We are therefore led to create:

  4. 4.   Vocal masks as the elements of a simple repertoire, like in the classical or Italian comedy—the young lead, a heavenly voice; the noble father, of short stature, etc… The nuances that are almost always prohibited for the reciter will be reserved for the characters.

  5. 5.   Background music. Dream music, as easy as possible, the music of the old melodrama: this kind of music will "warn" the listener through tremolos or ritornellos; it will draw a character in short phrases, and his or her return on scene will then be announced by the repetition of that motif. This will make it possible to bend and complicate the action without endangering its clarity; in short, by using expressive phrases music will "illustrate" the comedy.

  6. 6.   Obviously, the present indicative should be used at all times, just like in the legends of Épinal images.14 One exception can be made for the introduction of the characters (see section 3).

  7. 7.   Chronological order should be adopted when recounting the action: in order not to interrupt the course of events, the listener should always be "on location;" one might want to lead him up to the heavens or cross a continent, but there shall not be leaps in place or in time. We believe that one must avoid saying: "Twenty years ago there was born in New York…" or "ten kilometers away from there, two travelers…"

  8. 8.   The repetition of certain parts of sentences by different voices serves to emphasize an image and make it unforgettable. Important passages will be rhymed or set to music. The most subtle nuances of a text shall thus be rendered palpable to the audience.

  9. 9.   The noises of an action will not bear any trace of reality. Will a gun-shot appear as such? Most certainly not; the reciter may just slightly hit his finger against the table. Our ability to perfectly "translate" noises is revealed to us in our dreams.

  10. 10.   Often when reading a book, one relieves tension by glancing three lines or even three pages ahead. This is impossible in radio broadcasting and as a consequence, one has to "perform" time, that is, one has to plan for moments of complete silence on behalf of the speaker, possibly "furnished" or not with incidental music. For example: An explosion occurs, the voice of the speaker says: "One of the two enemies drops dead." Silence, then a slow resurfacing of the musical motif that is attached to the surviving character. (See section 5)

  11. 11.   Don't precipitate the action. The imagination, in fact, is known for not retaining any notion of time when maintained in a dream-state by the voice of the reciter or by the music.

  12. 12.   Chronometrics of representation. This is what in film is called the montage technique. Use of the metronome: one has to determine the number of pulses of each silent, musical, or spoken passage.

Keeping in mind the observations and the advice of listeners, we should be able to set on its feet, in relatively short a time, a technique as rigorous as the one that helps filmmakers to make marginal indications in the pages of their scripts. A radio script, when prepared for the microphone will bear, just like a film script, the personal imprint of the script writer or the technician.12

While waiting to see original radiophonic works make their appearance, here is a selection of tales, all of them appropriate for adaptation according to the rules above.

One will find that fantastic literature, because of its relationship with our dreams, for now seems best suited for the microphone. But the experience of those who write adaptations for the radio is progressing fast, and an ever vaster domain will open up to writers.

R. Kipling, The City of Dreams, and ten others.

Gobineau, The Illustrious Magician.xv

Brahm Stoker (sic): Dracula, the Man of the Night.

Children's fairy tales: Perrault, Andersen, Peter Pan, and Alice in Wonderland…

J.-K. Jerome: Three Men in a Boat (the scene of the shirt, played by the listener who

evokes two comrades)xvi

Edgar Poe's novellas, Hoffmann's tales, Balzac's philosophical tales, etc.

To conclude, here is a short example of a scenario where we won't have to change or add anything: Ibsen has given it to us with his Peer Gynt.xvii Aase finds herself to be exactly in the position of someone listening to the wireless —her role is limited to expressing sensations suggested to her by the story, by the noises that she thinks she hears, and by the incidental music.

Peer Gynt is Radiolo!xviii

[Incidental music by Grieg.]

PEER
We'll while the evening away
With ballads and nursery-rhymes.
In Soria-Moria Castle
The King is going to give a party.
Come up, go.
We are going to leave—are you ready?

(AASE
But, Peer, am I invited?)

PEER
Why yes, they've asked us both. [He pretends to tether a sleigh, to sway the reins, andto crack a whip.]

PEER
You're not feeling cold, are you?

(AASE
Listen! I hear ringing.) [Bell sounds.]

PEER
Those are bells, mother!
We're going over a fjord.

(AASE
I hear the rushing wind.) [Wind sounds.]

PEER
The windows of the castle glisten.
Can you hear them dancing?

(AASE Yes.)

PEER
St. Peter is standing outside
Waiting to ask you in.
He greets us graciously,
And pours out sweet wine.

(AASE
Wine? Are there cakes as well?)

PEER
There are. A piled up plateful.
And your old friend
Is preparing the coffee and dessert.
[Cracking his whip]. Gk, gk! Get a move on, Black Beauty!

(AASE
Are you sure you're on the right road?)

PEER
[Cracking his whip again]. We are on the high road.
I can see the castle ahead;
We'll be there in an instant.

(AASE
That's good. I'll lie back and close my eyes, then,
And leave it to you, my son!)

PEER
Come up, Greycoat, my beast!
There's a huge crowd. They are swarming
Up to the gate of the castle.
Here comes Peer Gynt with his mother!
What's that, Mr. St. Peter?
You won't let mother in?
……………………………… [ Time]
Aha, so here's God the Father!
Now you're for it, St. Peter!
[In a deep voice]
Stop playing the heavy official:
Mother Aase will be my guest.
[He laughs loudly and turns to his mother.]
You see, just as I thought.
That's made him change his tune.
That's it!—You can rest now, Greycoat;
We have arrived.

Translator's Notes

i. Pierre Cusy and Germinet Gabriel, Théâtre radiophonique. Mode nouveau d'expression artistique. (Paris: Etienne Chiron, 1926).

ii. Marie-Claire Dumas has commented on some of the more interesting advertising spots produced by Deharme together with Robert Desnos. Marie-Claire Dumas, Robert Desnos ou L'exploration des limites, Bibliothèque du XXe siècle (Paris: Librairie Klincksieck, 1980), 193–219.

iii. Pierre Descaves, La cité des voix. Création Radiophonique en un Prologue et 4 Périodes Verbales (Paris: Editions Denoël, 1938). For an analysis of this play, see Denis Hollier, «The Death of Paper: A Radio Play,» October 78 : 3–20.

iv. Pierre Schaeffer, «L'art radiophonique.» Recording date: February 22, 1944. Unknown radio station. Program producer: Le Studio d'Essai and Pierre Schaeffer (INA Archive).

v. For example, Pierre Miquel's reference work of 1984 on French radio and television makes no mention of Deharme. Pierre Miquel, Histoire de la radio et de la télévision (Paris: O.R.T.F., Les Productions de Paris, 1984). See also Pierre Schaeffer, «Au temps perdu de la recherche,» in Un siècle de radio et de télévision, ed. Pierre Descaves and A.V.J. Martin (Paris: O.R.T.F. et Les Productions de Paris, 1965). In that same work, Pierre Descaves and A.V.J. Martin reprint Deharme's article "Proposition d'un art radiophonique," but without explaining who Deharme was nor what other works he had produced.

vi. Paul Deharme, "Où en est la publicité radiophonique en France?," Vendre 20, no. 123 : 68–74. Deharme's radio play Un incident au Pont du Hibou was first transmitted on May 8, 1928 (Radio-Paris) and then rebroadcast on February 26, 1929 (Radio Juan-les-Pins) and again on January 13, 1936 (Radio Luxembourg); it is mentioned on the website of France's Institut National de l'Audiovisuel as the oldest preserved radio recording, but is not accessible to the public.

vii. Guy Robert, "Paul Deharme (1898–1934)," Cahiers d'Histoire de la Radiodiffusion 80 : 180–89.

viii. See Marie-Claire Barnet, "To Lise Deharme's Lighthouse: Le Phare de Neuilly, a Forgotten Surrealist Review," French Studies 57, no. 3 : 323–334.

ix. Deharme, "Où en est la publicité radiophonique en France?," 74.

x. Robert Desnos, Oeuvres, Marie-Claire Dumas ed. (Paris: Gallimard, 1999), 792.

xi. Christopher Todd's list of important radio-plays in France in the time between 1923 and 1935 mentions two plays that use the "ship in distress" master plot. Maremoto, written by Gabriel Germinet (real name: Maurice Vinot) and Pierre Cusy in 1924, is the most famous one, but there is also Le Paquebot Mostaganem by Jean d'Agraives. See Christopher Todd, "Gabriel Germinet and the 'Livre d'or du théâtre radiophonique français' (1923–1935)," Modern and Contemporary France 10, no. 2 . I have not been able to find instances of the other master plot mentioned by Deharme, that of the scholar in distress. Sturm über dem Pacific, referred to by Deharme in his footnote, is a German radio play by Oskar Möhring. It received indeed a special mention at the competition of the German Reichs-Rundfunks-gesellschaft in 1927 and was transmitted over German radio in that year. See Helmut Kreuzer, "Zu frühen deutschen Hörspielen und Hörspielkonzeptionen (1924–1927/28): Hans Flesch, Alfred Auerbach, Rudolf Leonhard, Oskar Moehring," Siegener Periodicum zur Internationalen Empirischen Literaturwissenschaft (SPIEL) 19, no. 1 : 36–48.

xii. Gustav Theodor Fechner (1801–1887), a pioneer in the fields of psychophysics and experimental psychology. He devised the Weber-Fechner law that allowed to measure sensations in relation to the physical quantity of stimuli.

xiii. Deharme might allude here to Cusy and Gabriel, Théâtre radiophonique. The authors had compared the radio listener's experience to that of telepathy.

xiv. "Images d'Épinal": naïve, popular 19th-century cliché scenes that were sold on street fairs; their subjects usually are taken from religion, history, or from best-seller novels. They owe their name to Jean-Charles Pellerin, who first printed this type of images in Épinal and named his print-shop after it, Imagerie d'Épinal.

xv. "L'illustre magicien", novella by Count Arthur de Gobineau originally published in 1920 (Paris: Pichon). The composer Jean-Richard Bloch put it to music and staged it in 1928 in Geneva (Text printed in: Jean-Richard Bloch, Offrande a la musique. Paris: Gallimard, 1930).

xvi. Popular children's book by the English writer Jerome K. Jerome. Two different translations into French were published in 1924 and in 1927.

xvii. The following translation is adapted from James Walter McFarlane and Christopher Fry, eds., The Oxford Ibsen. Brand. Peer Gynt, vol. III (London: Oxford University Press, 1972), 321–23. While it is true that Deharme did not add on to the original publication by Ibsen, his omissions are noteworthy: he eliminates local references and names to make it easier for the listener to imagine a scene of his own invention.

xviii. Radiolo: Pseudonym of one of the first speakers on French radio, Marcel Laporte, who spoke on the French radio station Radiola between 1922 and 1924, until it was transformed into Radio Paris. He was one of the first radio "stars," an object of veneration, mass letters, and finally curses, when he refused to return to the radio after 1926. See Maurice Hamel, "Radiolo, speaker et martyr," Cahiers d'Histoire de la Radiodiffusion, no. 60 .

1. Recently in Germany there was a competition of radiophonic dramas. The jury received 1,200 responses, but refrained from awarding a prize to anyone. Only one work titled Storm over the Pacific received the recommendation to be aired (Le journal, December 21, 1927).

2. Does not the success of film generally demonstrate the astonishing force of our unconscious power of transposition? We have immediately adapted to sequences of images without color, projected over a screen without a profile. A few fantastic films such as Max Fleischer's Clown Koko films, where real life characters mix with cartoon res, are quite admirably executed.

3. After listening to the radio version of The Blue Bird by Maeterlinck in London, an English critic declared never having listened to anything so perfect. The microphone, he said, reinforced the "unreal" character of the work (Echo de Paris, January 23, 1920). According to another English critic, the music of the 17th - and 18th-century harpsichord is especially appropriate for the microphone. He attributes this fact to a certain kind of immateriality rendered especially well in radiophonic art, as it lends a kind of faerie charm to the works of Rameau, Scarlatti, and Couperin (Radio Times, February 3, 1928).

4. The place in the soul where dreams unfold is not the same as that of the representations of waking life (Fechner).

5. Is it not true that the darkness of the movie theater, greater than that of the theater hall, suppresses the distractions to which the spectator is prone, and encourages his receptivity? In Bayreuth, the orchestra is invisible. We should expect much from the illusionary power of a theater where the spectators are at home and alone!

6. These two terms of the conscious and the unconscious have served, as we know, to create a rudimentary spatial notion of our psychic domain, according to which they refer to two separate zones. Here is a typical example of this classic theory: musical motifs are sometimes very clearly articulated on the bottom of our memory, yet we are incapable of warbling them because we are often inhibited by the remembrance of other tunes that we know better and that lie, so to speak, on the surface of our memory. It is this "border patrol" of the unconscious inhibiting, it seems, the exchanges between the outside and the inside that we hope to "cheat" and overcome by passing from the outside to the inside.

7. In contrast, music and the plastic arts are means that can suggest new surrealist dreams.

8. Reading aloud is a rudimentary instrument, as is a certain kind of theater, in clear contradiction with the art that we imagine. A sublime way to achieve this effect would be to use painting or music.

9. In order to touch someone's heart one usually speaks in a low voice and without bursts. Perhaps this is not the case in theater, but certainly in real life.

10. Theater itself has sometimes made use of such techniques. It is, in fact, a proceeding close to magic incantation or litanies, close also to the methods recommended by mystics to make the soul disposed for the gift of supernatural "raptures."

11. "That terrible and clear voice of those people seen when one is asleep." (Kipling, The City of Dreams).
