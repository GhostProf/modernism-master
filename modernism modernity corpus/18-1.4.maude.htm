Beckett and the Laws of Habit

Ulrika Maude

Habit presents a challenge to our received understanding of what it is to be human, because it seems to entail an absence of reflection, thought, and rational intentionality, and appears instead to be closer to a form of automatism. Habit is therefore antithetical to what we value in ourselves: critical reflection, intentional actions, and an Aristotelian capacity for wonder. For this reason, habit figures prominently in the work of a number of philosophers, many of whom view it with suspicion. However, there is an alternative line of thinking that can be traced back at least to the work of the nineteenth-century French philosopher Félix Ravaisson, whose essay Of Habit, from 1838, influenced writers and thinkers such as Henri Bergson, Marcel Proust, Maurice Merleau-Ponty, Paul Ricoeur, and Gilles Deleuze, all of whom are of course anti-rationalist thinkers. In this paper I will argue that Beckett’s work, too, gives habit some very serious consideration. In fact, it might even be argued that an appreciation of its functions and mechanism is crucial to our understanding of Beckett’s oeuvre.

The word “habit” can be understood in two different ways:

  1. In the straightforwardly mechanical sense, as an objective fact and consisting in the regular repetition of an event: for instance, Krapp in Krapp’s Last Tape (1958) eats too many bananas, drinks too much, and records a new tape each year on his birthday.
  2. As a subjective disposition or “the construction of a project of life.”1 This is not merely a question of mechanical repetition, but rather involves the will and originates in an intentional act.

814 I shall argue that although they are seemingly very different, these two senses of habit can be seen to “proceed from the same principle.”2 Ravaisson’s (1813–1900) extended essay advances one of the most sophisticated and detailed analyses of habit available, and, crucially steers a path between materialism and idealism. In all forms of existence, Ravaisson argues, “the following are found together: permanence; change; and, in change itself, a tendency towards permanence” (OH, 27). The higher we move up in the “hierarchy of beings,” the more multiple become the conditions of permanence and change, partly because higher beings exercise will rather than instinct and therefore simply have more freedom of choice. “Permanence and change,” Ravaisson argues, are hence “the first conditions of habit” (OH, 33). “Habit is at first an effect, a way of being that results from change, but it gradually becomes a cause of change itself,” initiating and maintaining repetition.3 As Catherine Malabou rather elegantly puts it, “change generates habit, but in return habit is actualised as a habit of changing.”4 Deleuze calls this “repetition productive of difference”—the very paradox of repetition. Beckett, in his essay on Proust, published in 1931, perhaps says the same thing thus: “There is no escape from yesterday because yesterday has deformed us, or been deformed by us. The mood is of no importance. Deformation has taken place.”5 In his analysis of motivation, Ravaisson makes a distinction between action and passion, which for him are “inversely related to each other”: while action is triggered by the intentional will, passions are initially prompted by receptivity or, what for him amounts to the same thing, passivity. The two are also diametrically opposed because “The continuity or the repetition of passion weakens it; [while] the continuity or repetition of action exalts and strengthens it.” For Ravaisson, “Prolonged or repeated movement becomes gradually easier, quicker and more assured. Perception, which is linked to movement, similarly becomes clearer, swifter, and more certain” (OH, 49). Action leads to habit, and habit to grace, ease and elegance. Prolonged or repeated sensation, on the other hand, diminishes gradually and through the reversibility of energies prompts action and a desire for a strengthened stimulus in the subject (this would for instance be the case in addictions). What began as passivity is therefore transformed into action. Effort, fatigue, and pain initially insert themselves between the will and the action, but they recede along with effort, and as the pain disappears, pleasure develops and increases. Continuity or repetition therefore “weakens passivity and excites activity,” while “the action itself becomes more of a tendency, an inclination that no longer awaits the commandments of the will but rather anticipates them, and which even escapes entirely and irremediably both will and consciousness” (OH, 51). We are therefore faced with an “obscure and unreflective tendency, which goes further down into the organism [than will], increasingly concentrating itself there” (OH, 53). This “unreflective spontaneity . . . beyond, beneath the region of will, personality and consciousness” is what Ravaisson defines as habit—habit as second nature (OH, 53). Habit, in other words, bridges the gap between will or consciousness on the one hand and matter and the automated functions of the body on the other, forming a kind of complex ontology: virtually everything integral to

the subject can be traced back to habit. Here Ravaission’s thinking parts company with 815 the received notion in philosophy that habit is “an obstacle to knowledge.”6 Instead, his theory provides a link between will and habit, will and nature. And he argues, following Aristotle, that “Habit is an acquired nature, a second nature that has its ultimate ground in primitive nature” (OH, 59). So habit, he suggests, is constitutive of the self. For Ravaission, however, as I have already implied, habit is a source of both grace and addiction: it produces ease, facility, and power, but also machinic and at times pathological repetition, such as in “inflammations, spasms, convulsions [which] make regular appearances without any appearance of a determining cause in the material of the organism.” Here, Raviasson argues, “A fever that has come by chance to manifest itself in regular bouts tends to convert itself into periodic affection; the periodicity becomes essential to it” (OH, 35). So illness for Ravaission is a habit, and his essay draws substantially from various medical textbooks of his time.

Beckett’s most direct treatment of habit appears in his essay on Proust, from 1931, in which he declares that “Memory and Habit . . . control the most simple Proustian episode, and an understanding of their mechanism must precede any particular analysis of their application” (P, 18). Something very similar could be said about much of Beckett’s own work. Proust’s ideas on habit come largely from Bergson, who in turn was a student of Ravaisson’s at the École Normale Supérieure in Paris; Bergson graduated from the École Normale in 1881. Beckett would therefore have had indirect access to Ravaisson’s thinking on habit through his knowledge of the work of both Proust and Bergson.

Bergson is far more scathing about habit than Ravaisson. For him, habit constitutes a form of memory, but the acquisition of memories through repetition is not “true” or proper memory, but mere habit-memory, “the main office of our nervous system.”7 Habit therefore functions as a low-order memory: “Habits formed by repeated actions are amassed in the body; these do not represent the past, they merely act it” (MM, 92). In this way, Bergson writes, “is ensured the appropriate reaction, the correspondence to environment—adaptation, in a word—which is the general aim of life. And a living being which did nothing but live would need no more than this” (MM, 96). Where for Ravaisson habit is developed into an ontology, a key to all human existence, for Bergson it is insufficient and more appropriate to animal life. Bergson gives the example of a dog greeting its owner as a prime instance of habit-memory. In Bergson’s thinking, habit must be combined with the memory-images conjured up by spontaneous memory for it to constitute a veritable representation of the past, something that, incidentally, is parodied in Beckett’s Murphy (1938)—in Murphy’s attempt to recreate the image of “his father, his mother, Celia, Wylie, Neary, Cooper, Miss Dew, Miss Carridge, Nelly, the sheep, the chandlers, even Bom and Co., even Bim,” and his inability to do so.8 For Bergson, too, however, the observation of pathological cases reveals that the automatism of motor memory extends far further than might at first meet the eye. Bergson writes in Matter and Memory, from 1896 (trans. 1911):

In cases of dementia, we sometimes find that intelligent answers are given to a succession of questions which are not understood: language here works after the manner of a reflex. Aphasics, incapable of uttering a word spontaneously, can recollect without a mistake the words of an air which they sing. Or again, they will fluently repeat a prayer, a series of numbers, the days of the week, the months of the year. (MM, 99)

Language too can be a mere reflex or a habit, then, something that begins to bring us closer to Beckett’s work.

It is not clear when exactly Beckett became familiar with Bergson’s work, but we do know that he read the philosopher for his lectures at Trinity College, Dublin, delivered in the academic year 1930 to 1931, as Beckett’s student, Rachel Burrows’s lecture notes, and her 1988 interview with Gontarski, McMillan, and Fehsenfeld make clear.9 The original lecture notes, which have gone missing, are available on microfilm at Trinity College, Dublin, Library. They reveal that Beckett makes mention of Bergson not only in his lectures on Proust, but also in his discussion of such writers as Gide, Rimbaud, and Dostoyevsky.10 Although there are notes on such philosophers as Kant in Beckett’s personal copy of À la recherche du temps perdu, the volume contains no marginal references to Bergson.11 However, Proust himself was friendly with Bergson and familiar with his philosophy, and the Bergsonian ideas in Proust may therefore have been acquired second-hand through Beckett’s reading of À la recherche. In his essay, which was submitted to Chatto and Windus a month before he began to lecture at Trinity College, Dublin, Beckett reformulates Bergson’s statements in his own idiosyncratic manner: “Habit is a compromise effected between the individual and his environment, or between the individual and his own organic eccentricities, the guarantee of a dull inviolability, the lightning-conductor of his existence” (P, 18–19). Like Bergson, and unlike Ravaisson, Beckett seems here to suggest that habit is an impediment to knowledge and understanding: “the pernicious devotion of habit paralyses our attention, drugs those handmaidens of perception whose co-operation is not absolutely essential” (P, 20). And, Beckett argues that “Breathing is habit. Life is habit,” which though scathing and damning, could be seen to anticipate the affinities detectable in Beckett’s late work with Ravaisson’s thinking (P, 19). For although Beckett views habit in negative terms in Proust, he also recognizes its necessity: “The fundamental duty of Habit, about which it describes the futile and stupefying arabesques of its supererogations, consists in a perpetual adjustment and readjustment of our organic sensibility to the conditions of its worlds” (P, 28).

Habit figures prominently in Beckett’s own oeuvre, both in its positive and negative manifestations, and it is perhaps habit in its more purely mechanical and pathological sense that dominates in Beckett’s early and mature work. Examples might include the habitual racing of Murphy’s “irrational heart that no physician could get to the root of . . . One moment in such labour that it seemed on the point of seizing, the next in such ebullition that it seemed on the point of bursting.”12 Watt’s walk offers another prominent example of what Ravaisson called “those movements, initially more or less voluntary, that gradually degenerate into convulsive ones, which we call tics,” as do the trembling, spasms and contortions of the trilogy (OH, 51). Habit is also present in 817 The Unnamable’s compulsive, convulsive prose, where language seems to speak itself: “It issues from me, it fills me, it clamours against my walls, it is not mine, I can’t stop it, I can’t prevent it, from tearing me, racking me, assailing me. . . . I have no voice and must speak.”13 Language, in other words, becomes a pathological habit, beyond intentional control. Mouth in Not I (1972) suddenly feels “her lips moving . . . imagine! . . . her lips moving! . . . as of course till then she had not . . . and not alone the lips . . . the cheeks . . . the jaws . . . the whole face . . . all those—. . . what? . . . the tongue? . . . yes . . . the tongue in the mouth . . . all those contortions without which . . . no speech possible.”14 This “sudden urge . . . once or twice a year . . . always winter some strange reason” appropriately takes place in the “nearest lavatory.”15 Beckett wrote to Alan Schneider on 16 October, 1972, that Mouth’s speech was “a purely buccal phenomenon, without mental control or understanding, only half heard. Function running away with organ.”16 Of Mouth’s voice, he stated: “I hear it breathless, urgent, feverish, rhythmic, panting along, without undue concern with intelligibility. Addressed less to the understanding than to the nerves of the audience, which should in a sense share her bewilderment” (NABS, 283). This is Beckett staging language as a parasitic perturbation: as a pathological habit.

A play that focuses centrally on the various manifestations of habit is Happy Days, which Beckett wrote in English between 1960 and 1961. It features two protagonists, Winnie and Willie, “about fifty” and “about sixty” respectively, fixed in a desert landscape, under a blazing light.17 In the first act of the play, Winnie is stuck up to her waist in a mound; in the second act, she is buried up to her neck, virtually devoured by the earth. Her husband, Willie, while hardly agile, is still able to crawl.

Like so much of Beckett, the play is remarkable for the fact that in it, very little happens. As Daniel Albright puts it, “Happy Days is to some degree post-theatrical in the manner of Endgame, in that the concept of event seems to pertain far more to the past than the present.”18 A bell rings six times either to wake Winnie up or to prevent her from falling asleep. Winnie brushes her teeth and hair and resorts to eleven banal objects in her handbag: her lipstick, a hairbrush, a toothbrush, spectacles, a nail file, a mirror, a hat with a feather, a magnifying glass, a revolver, a musical box, and a bottle of elixir. She also repeatedly misremembers and misquotes her classics: Shakespeare, Thomas Gray, Omar Khayyam, Robert Browning, Milton, Keats, Charles Wolfe, Yeats, and Herrick.

Among Winnie’s first lines are an “inaudible prayer” and its audible closing words, “For Jesus Christ sake Amen” and “World without end Amen” (HD, 5). As S. E. Gontarski argues, “Winnie never really hears her prayer. It is simply part of her morning ritual, her habit, which Beckett has reminded us is a great deadener.”19 Ruby Cohn describes Winnie’s prayer as “automatic,” and goes on to add that “soon she invokes deity as a mere expletive.”20 The prayer and Winnie’s expletives, such as “God” at the sight of an emmet carrying an egg, function in the manner of a reflex, mouthed habitually, devoid of semantic content (HD, 18). One of Beckett’s major problems in writing the play, as he put it in a letter to Alan Schneider of December, 1960, was “how to have [Winnie]

818 speak alone on the stage all that time without speaking to herself or to the audience” (NABS, 77). The striking effect of the play comes precisely from the disparity between the stage spectacle and the words conveyed: “This calculated irrelevance of language to the visual images is mutually discrediting; this jangle, this inconsistency, cannot be palliated by any strategy of interpretation” (BA, 66). Something rather similar applies to Winnie’s numerous literary (mis)quotations. When, in 1971, Beckett was directing Eva Katharina Schultz as Winnie in Glückliche Tage, the German-language translation of the play, at the Schiller Theater in Berlin, “he wanted something close to a chant for the literary quotations.”21 Winnie’s (mis) quotations, such as the repeated line from Cymbeline, “Fear no more the heat o’ the sun” or the reference to Gray’s “Ode on a Distant Prospect of Eton College,” “laughing wild . . . something something laughing wild amid severest woe,” function here precisely in the manner of culturally coded chants or mantras: they are key lines from Western literature, but their signification, for Winnie, has long since been obliterated (HD, 15, 18). The misquotations function as an example of the change generated by habit through repetition, which gradually erodes the lines of verse and prose and alters their form. But they are also an example of the habitual, mechanical nature of language itself, ossified into chant and mere sound pattern. In his final essay, dedicated to Beckett’s work, Deleuze writes that words “are so burdened with calculations and significations, with intentions and personal memories, with old habits that cement them, that their surface, barely broken, heals over again. It sticks together. It imprisons and suffocates us.”22 It was important for Beckett that this habitual, cemented quality of language be conveyed in stage productions. In August, 1961, Beckett instructed Alan Schneider, who was directing the American première of the play, to maintain “same tone throughout, polishing mechanically, no emotion . . . In a word I am asking here for vocal monotony and relying on speech rhythms and speech-gesture complexes” (NABS, 95). Later, in the same letter, Beckett referred to Winnie’s “speech abnormality,” and when directing Eva Katharina Schultz ten years later, he advised her: “Speak normally, but be curiously uninvolved, impersonal.”23 Winnie’s classics constitute mere verbal debris, differing, in this sense, very little from the objects in her bag, which serve the function of acting as relics of “the old style.” In order to pass the time, Winnie contemplates listing her belongings: “The bag. . . . Could I enumerate its contents?” bringing to mind Malone’s “inventory” in the trilogy of novels (HD, 18). Here, as in Winnie’s numerous misquotations, listing and stocktaking, the most primitive forms of literacy, constitute Beckett’s aesthetics of impoverishment. S. E. Gontarski argues that words ultimately “have little meaning for [Winnie], other than as sustaining sound.”24 They function in the manner of Bergson’s aphasic utterances: as alliteration. Beckett wanted a similar “monotony of symmetry” to be reflected in the stage set: “the kind of tawdriness you get in 3rd rate musical or pantomime, that quality of pompier, laughably earnest bad imitation” (NABS, 94). But grace, the other side of habit, also forms a central part of Beckett’s work, particularly his writing for the stage. The grace that Ravaisson identifies habit as affording comes from its unselfconscious quality: movement and behavior become automatic

rather than premeditated, something that for Bergson merely constitutes “a habit of 819 the body,” but which nonetheless produces exceptional grace (MM, 90). Beckett, as we know from his admiration of Kleist’s essay, “Über das Marionetten Theater,” from 1810, had an appreciation for this kind of grace. Kleist argued in the essay that self-consciousness destroys gracefulness in humans, for it produces “affectation.” Kleist’s account of the marionettes and the fencing bear exemplify how, “in the organic world, as reflection grows darker and weaker, the natural graces within emerge ever more radiant and supreme.”25 When Beckett was rehearsing Eva Katharina Schultz for the role of Winnie in Happy Days in Berlin, he focused on “the rhythm, pace, pitch, and volume of her voice and the rhythm and timing of her movements . . . He argued that precision and economy would produce the maximum of grace, quoting Kleist’s essay on the Marionette theatre to reinforce his argument.”26 Similarly, some years later, in 1976, when Beckett was rehearsing the BBC production of the television play Ghost Trio at the Ealing film studios in London, he referred both the actor, Ronald Pickup, and his biographer, James Knowlson, to Kleist’s essay, in order “to illustrate what he [Beckett] said about the relations between economy and the grace and harmony that he wanted to see in the movements of the protagonist.”27 This economy, grace, and harmony arise precisely from the implication that F, the male figure in Ghost Trio (1975), has been performing the same actions for so long they have become automated, second nature, or, to use a different term, a habit. Ghost Trio also functions as an example of the compulsive, addictive nature of habit: of those actions initially triggered by a passively received stimulus which gradually develops into a need or addiction and whose pursuit thereafter involves intentionality, until it, too, develops into a habit that settles beneath the realm of conscious control and ultimately escapes it. In Ghost Trio and its companion piece, . . . but the clouds . . . (1976), the protagonists, F and M, are plagued by the desire to be haunted by a deceased lover, and this desire drives each meticulous movement we witness on screen. V, M’s voice in . . . but the clouds . . ., utters: “crouching there, in my little sanctum, in the dark, where none could see me, I began to beg, of her, to appear, to me. Such had long been my use and wont. No sound, a begging of the mind, to her, to appear, to me.”28 Grief itself here has become a parasitic presence, the “obscure and unreflective tendency” Ravaisson identifies with the disease of habit. For, V adds, “had she never once appeared, all that time, would I have, could I have, gone on begging, all that time?”29

The choreography of . . . but the clouds . . . displays its own peculiar grace: the exact number and precise direction of M’s movements, five steps to and from north, east, south and west shadow, is meticulously prescribed. The Süddeutsche Rundfunk production, . . . nur noch Gewölk . . ., which Beckett himself directed in Stuttgart in 1977, resembles, in its uncanny traipsing, a clownish ballet or vaudeville repertoire, always however with Beckett’s characteristic brand of disquietingly dark humor. A similar emphasis on habitual, mechanical movement can be seen in a number of other plays. In Footfalls (1975), M’s nine steps and reel appear as if they were controlled by a curious clockwork mechanism, while in Quad (1982), four players, who for preference have “some ballet training,” complete a strictly choreographed set of permutations

820 always avoiding the “danger zone” at the center of a quadrangle.30 In the play-script, Beckett’s diagram for the choreography resembles instructions for origami, while the finished performance is something akin to “hopscotch” or the maneuvers of an ancient ritual (BA, 137). What Where (1983), Beckett’s final play, is marionette theatre, albeit with real actors on stage. Its players’ machinic enunciations—Beckett no longer called them characters or even actors—bring to mind the permutations of computer code. The emphasis on habit in Beckett’s theatre work can also be detected in his repeated instruction to Billie Whitelaw not to “act,” because “acting,” which implies self-consciousness, would take away from the graceful gesture and posture of habitual, mechanical action and enunciation that Beckett trained his actors to achieve. Daniel Albright has remarked that Beckett’s characters “sometimes seem to be losing species, regressing to the subhuman, trying to rehearse the figures of instinct but botching the job” (BA, 69). Another way to put this might be to suggest that habit in Beckett plays has taken the place of instinct, and that very little, in late Beckett, escapes its influence. In short, the “laws of habit,” as Beckett calls them in Proust, only grow stronger in Beckett’s late work. Flo, Vi and Ru in Come and Go or Bim, Bam, Bem, and Bom in What Where or the four players of Quad are hardly characters any more, but all of these plays nonetheless seem to suggest that what is being represented in their formal permutations is, if anything at all, a compulsive disposition, an organic eccentricity—in other words, a habit. In the non-coincidence of self with self that Beckett’s work repeatedly stages, residual subjectivity emerges and is tenuously articulated through acts of habit. The paradox of habit therefore resides in its diminution of consciousness and will, conventionally associated with ideas of self, but also in its simultaneous suggestion that any sense of self resides precisely in and emerges out of these mechanical, near-automated actions. After all else has been pared down, Beckett seems to be suggesting in his late work, habit is what remains. For Ravaission and for the late Beckett, habit replaces instinct, which in higher beings has been lost but is nonetheless retained as a memory trace— just as there is a memory of the mineral state in organic being, as Freud would later argue, to Beckett’s delight.31 Although Beckett perhaps begins his publishing life with a Bergsonian take on habit as “the ballast that chains the dog to its vomit,” something that impedes knowledge and distracts us from experience, the late Beckett, albeit less affirmative about habit than Ravaission, is nonetheless much closer to his thinking (P, 19). For in Beckett’s late work, habit seems to constitute a tenuous, fraught, and primitive ontology, which is the most, perhaps, that we can hope for.
