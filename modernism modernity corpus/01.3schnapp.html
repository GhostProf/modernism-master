Propeller Talk *

Jeffrey T. Schnapp **

res

It pleases me to scrutinize and ascertain that which I truly possess in the propeller myth, and thus also the nature of the propeller's impulsion or propulsion. One thing seems clear: that the propeller cannot be just "any old myth."

--Carlo Emilio Gadda 1

Driven by the propeller myth of Carlo Emilio Gadda, this essay derives its title from the opening paragraph of Marinetti's 1912 "Technical Manifesto of Futurist Literature": to be precise, from its famous description of how the poet, seated on the fuel tank of a soaring biplane, first felt the need to free language from the prison house of traditional syntactical form. You will recall that this exordium is pronounced by a re well known to readers of La Conquête des étoiles , Mafarka le futuriste , and the early Futurist manifestos: Marinetti's boastful and boisterous first-person narrator/orator persona. The phrase "io sentii l'inanità ridicola della vecchia sintassi ereditata da Omero" (I felt the ridiculous inanity of the old syntax inherited from Homer) thus has a familiar ring to it, linked as it is with Marinetti's usual claims of clairvoyance and prophetic urgency. By the paragraph's end, however, this "I" has been recast in a less customary role, that of taking dictation...from a propeller.

"Ecco che cosa mi disse l'elica turbinante, mentre filavo a duecento metri sopra i possenti fumaiuoli di Milano. E l'elica soggiunse...." (This is what the swirling propeller told me as I sped along at two hundred meters above the powerful smokestacks of Milan. And the propeller added....) 2 Dictation taken from an object. A cutting-edge object, to be sure, but a mere object, all the same, and one that is infringing on the place usually reserved for Marinetti's blunt-edged egocentric muse. Yet the manifesto is unambiguous: a propeller, not a poet, will do the talking; a propeller will dictate from on high the laws that are to govern modern poetic discourse and define the desublimated forms of individuality and subjectivity attached thereto.

It might be objected that, despite this opening gambit, the poet-scribe's silence does not last for very long. By article 7 of the manifesto his voice is cutting back into the propeller's chatter, merging with its modernist whir as if to enact the manifesto's closing dream of a fusion of motor and man, metal and flesh. 3 The takeover is complete once the properly "technical" portion of the argument has concluded, whereupon dictation ceases and the poet reasserts total control: "looking at objects from a new point of view," he proclaims, "I have been able to demolish the old logical fetters and the leaden wires of ancient understanding." 4 So, the "Technical Manifesto" animates the voice of a propeller only in order to absorb it within the poet's own voice, and to remake that voice in the image of the new mechanical kingdom that Futurist poetry is called upon at once to presage and reproduce. The object world represented by the propeller is only a pretext, the true goal being the modernization and expansion of the poet's subjectivity. But if such is an adequate account of the overall trajectory of the manifesto, I would like to return to and interrogate Marinetti's opening fiction a bit further. For example, why is it desirable that this particular mechanical muse should propel the discourse of the "Technical Manifesto"? Why should a propeller stand in for the animated objects, courageous metals, and capricious motors that make up the manifesto's redeemed material world? In what ways does Marinetti's propeller prosopopeia regulate the manifesto's argument and form? In the following remarks I will be addressing questions such as these through close scrutiny of the manifesto itself and by means of a lateral sectioning of the airspace in and around the manifesto's propeller screw.

What I am especially interested in is the multiplicity of simultaneous semiotic chains, institutions, technologies, and historical circumstances whose determinations interconnect in the 1912 "Technical Manifesto". Of particular concern to me, for example, will be emerging links among various apparently unrelated technologies developed during the second industrial revolution, the cognitive possibilities opened up by the experience of flight, and the way in which certain modes of organization and coordination that first arose during the early years of aviation (and, especially, military aviation) rendered "thinkable" forms of literary expression like those prophesied and practiced by Marinetti. I am therefore unconcerned with establishing an argument founded primarily on notions of causal explanation or genealogical development, although I remain committed to the philological reconstruction of certain aspects of Marinetti's text, and take for granted the inaugural role that the "Technical Manifesto" played with respect to Futurism's later aerial formulations of fascist modernism. My deeper concern, rather, lies with sampling the vintage 1912 air breathed by Marinetti's mechanical muse. And, in so doing, I will be operating in a speculative middle zone somewhere in between the sharply polarized assessments of Marinetti offered by, among others. Edoardo Sanguineti and Luciano De Maria: that is, between the former's version of Marinetti as the hack recycler of contemporary commonplaces, and the latter's depiction of Marinetti the revolutionary and founding father of cultural modernity. 5

I begin with a grammatical detail that is easily overlooked--the passati remoti (past absolute) 6 mentioned above, featured in the preface to the manifesto: "sentii l'inanità ridicola" and "ecco che cosa mi disse l'elica." Although a standard rhetorical feature of the manifesto form, the temporal disjunction signaled here corresponds to an actual chronological gap of nearly two years. The "Technical Manifesto" was composed between January and May 1912, in the period after Marinetti's return from the Battle of Tripoli where he participated "in all the most violent, most virile, and most heroic actions" of that colonial war. 7 The months in question were times of relative glory for Futurist art, but of crisis for Futurist poetry. In the words of the poet Aldo Palazzeschi, author of L'incendiario : "Do you [Marinetti] know that no one talks about Futurist poetry any longer? About painting yes, but poetry seems to have drowned in everyone's eyes. Hardly surprising given that the poets are dead silent! Foremost among them the chief!" 8 In response to pleadings of this sort, Marinetti had promised "to dedicate himself exclusively to [the triumph of] Futurist literature" and the "Technical Manifesto" was the device by means of which he hoped to relaunch the movement's literary wing. 9 The Futurist leader had first climbed aboard an aircraft some two and one-half years before sitting down to pen the 1912 manifesto: in September 1909, to be precise, during the first international air meet at Brescia ( 1). 10 The Brescia event was of momentous importance; not only did it launch the aviation craze in Italy, but it also introduced the muses into an Italian aerodrome for the first time. During ten days of accident-prone races, which included a competition for slancio--a key Futurist term (best translated as élan)--Marinetti had been only one among a number of poets to hitch rides, however brief, around the field before an audience that included the young Franz Kafka. 11 Foremost among the aspiring poet-aviators was Gabriele D'Annunzio, who alone was honored with a full eight-minute flight by the aerial luminary of the moment, the American pilot Glenn Curtiss ( 2). D'Annunzio's ascent was, like every act of aviation first accomplished during this era, copiously chronicled in the contemporary press by means of a combination of image and industrial print technologies (multiple typefaces, parallel columns, boldfacing, etc.), as well by means of wireless telegraphy. A photograph of and full-length interview with Luigi Barzini were, for instance, featured on page 3 of the Corriere della Sera on the following day, in the course of which D'Annunzio had proclaimed the birth of no less than "a new sensation...a new necessity, a new passion." 12 The terza pagina ("third page"--the standard cultural/intellectual page in Italian newspapers) was (and remains) the standard forum for cultural debate within the new public sphere constituted by daily mass circulation newspapers; this specifically "cultural" collocation of aviation was the rule from 1909 through the early 1930s. Aviation poems; the stories of feats accomplished by individual flyers, their accidents and falls, their biographies and photographs; accounts of rival nations' attempts to gain aerial supremacy; international flight meets; flight trophies; and even technical developments in flight technology were featured on terza pagine under the rubric of "The Conquest of the Air" alongside more conventional cultural fare and in addition to front-page headlines trumpeting such media events as Louis Blériot's English Channel crossing and the not infrequent breaking of altitude and distance records. 13

Nor was the relation between daily newspapers and aviation one of mere reflection, for the medium itself had assumed an active role right from the start. Aviation was one of the era's defining forms of spectacle so, like its British and French counterparts, the Corriere had provided the prize monies for the Brescia air meet in return for special coverage rights, as well as offering third-page coverage of the event's cultural ramifications. Among the latter would be D'Annuncio's Forse che sì, forse che no (Perhaps yes, perhaps no), the first true aviation novel, as well as an influential oration, also dating from the following year, entitled "Per il dominio dei cieli" (For the dominance of the skies). 14 Less well known, however, is the flood of other literary artifacts with para-Futurist resonances produced in the immediate wake of the Brescia meet by minor poets such as Alberto Alberti and Giovanni Bertacchi. 15 In the former's All'età ventura (For the coming era), for instance, within the arc of a poetic cycle that leads its reader through a sequence of chapters entitled "La macchina" (The machine), "Il passato" (The past), "L'avvenire" (The future), "Gli uomini nuovi" (The new men), and "Il pensiero e l'infinito" (Thought and the infinite), Alberti sings of a winged contraption that with its "leading edge exposed / plunges into battle / with machine-gun fire." 16 Bertacchi's "Canto d'aerea vita" (The song of aerial life) similarly celebrates the ability of the new ethos of flight to descend from the supreme hero of modern times--the pilot/athlete--down into the hearts of the masses "where the new conquest will live again translated into life's everyday rhythms." 17 And this new aerial, quotidian reality is then identified with the familiar Marinettian theme of the passage via technology out of legend into a transred daily life: "I see history peopled with heroes and new exploits in continents of air; I see the city of Icaria with its free children, moving from myth into reality." 18

Many other examples could be cited in order to show just how widespread was Bertacchi's conviction that in the age of industry and the masses--an era, in the words of Edmond Rostand, "without inebriation and where the soul lay idle"--aviation alone could resuscitate art, the individual, and an ethos of heroism. (Rostand's own 1911 Le Cantique de l'aile, for instance, sings of how, thanks to the discovery of flight, "Daring is reborn as well as Exploit!" and how "the Hero, long retired beneath his tent like the Greek hero, has now ripped down the pulsing cloth and flown away with it!") 19 But beyond wishing to point to aviation's key place in the early twentieth-century imagination, my concern here is to underscore one of the 1912 manifesto's essential frame conditions: namely, the existence of a network binding aviation to contemporary literary forms, to the news media, to industrial typography and image technologies, and to new technologically inflected redefinitions of both heroism and everyday life--a network that was already solidly in place when Marinetti embarked on his first true flight nearly one year after the events of Brescia. In other words, Futurism was less ahead of the trend line than alongside it when, in September 1910 at the Milan International Air Week, Marinetti stepped up onto the wing of Giovanni Bielovucic's biplane to set off on the overflight of industrial Milan described a full year and a half later in the opening paragraph of the "Technical Manifesto". 20 The flight in question, "two hundred meters above Milan's powerful smokestacks," was one of the earliest such passenger flights in northern Italy, since only one week earlier Germano Ruggerone had been the first Italian to overfly the Madonnina of the Milanese Duomo ( 3). 21 It occurred, moreover, under the shadow cast by the era's most recent aerial exploit, Juan Chavez's crossing of the Alps, which ended in a fatal crash landing on 23 September--an event coordinated by wireless telegraphy, greeted by daily banner headlines, mourned by millions, and commemorated in terza pagina poems by Giovanni Pascoli ("Ode a Chavez" [Ode to Chavez]), Cesco Tomaselli ("Il prodigio fatale" [The fatal feat]), and E. A. Butti ("L'elegia del volo mortale" [Elegy on a mortal flight]). 22 If such background considerations may help to explain the "Technical Manifesto's" definition of art as "a [heroic] need for self-destruction and dispersal" through innovation, and its promise to "free [mechanical] man from the idea of death," it is only by turning to certain technical features of Marinetti's flight that the opening paragraph locks into focus. 23

The Voisin biplane on which the poet-scribe took his dictation--apparently the same as that imagined back in April 1909 in the manifesto in which he had first imagined himself as an aviator-bombadier, "Uccidiamo il chiaro di luna" (Let's kill the moonshine)--was already of outmoded design ( 4). 24 Its pilot, a young Peruvian like Chavez, was seated on the lower wing with his feet extending forward into a short cockpit equipped with lateral ailerons. To his sides extended a double set of wings (around six feet apart, six feet deep, and forty feet in total wingspan) joined together by vertical struts and by four vertical panels, which combined to form symmetrical cells on each end of the wing with a broad rectangular "window" at their center. The tail took the form of a box kite. Above and behind the pilot, atop the fuel tank, there was space enough for a single passenger, whose lower body, as indicated in the manifesto, 25 would have been partially shielded from the airflow by the aviator's head. Directly behind the passenger was a noisy fifty-horsepower E.N.V. engine with the propeller screw facing backwards, pushing rather than pulling the airship. (Bielovucic's airplane was, in other words, what contemporaries called a "pusher.")

This design bears a number of perceptual-symbolic consequences that may help account for the "Technical Manifesto's" constant oscillation between a poetics of agitated matter associated with aviation's links to the more "intuitive" senses of touch, smell, and hearing, and a poetics of image streams associated instead with aviation's impact on the faculty of sight. I begin with the first. The Voisin's design is such that, as was typical of early aircraft, the poet-passenger's body, rather than that of the pilot, forms a single physical unit with the engine, fuel system, and propeller chassis ( 5). Unable to communicate with the pilot because of the deafening din, he participates instead in the mechanical rhythms and physical vibrations emanating from the engine/propeller complex--the noises, weights, and odors exalted at the manifesto's midpoint. 26 But most of all what he hears is the roar that constitutes the propeller screw's "dictation." 27 Reaching him from behind, "roared" into his ear and relayed directly to his body by the pulsating motor and chassis, the propeller's talk is identified with the principal mystery of flight as then conceived: the fact that masses of metal, wood, and cloth loaded down with human bodies could become airborne, which is to say "dematerialized" and "spiritualized," simply by means of the acceleration produced by an internal combustion engine. Advances in engine design such as the advent of rotary cylinder layouts had more than doubled the speed and duration of flights by 1911. But with increased power came increased danger and unreliability.

All three attributes--power, danger, and unreliability--are advantageous from Marinetti's viewpoint, since they permit the development of a body/machine complex founded on notions of struggle, sacrifice, feverish effort, and expenditure: an aesthetic (i.e. non-productivist) body/machine complex that runs counter to the complexes being devised within the domains of scientific management (Taylor)and the "science of work," which were founded instead on notions of body/machine harmony, energy conservation, and freedom from fatigue. 28 Let me clarify. If motors were to become fully reliable and peril-free, as was the dream of turn-of-the-century mechanical and social engineers, they would embody earthbound and library-bound rationalism and promote the attenuation of instinct in the name of predictability, standardization, and the leveling of human differences. For Marinetti, to the contrary, it was imperative that motors remain quite literal motori a scatto (internal combustion motors), mechanisms that, no matter how technically perfect, are always capable of lunging, sputtering, or exploding due to their capricious, excessive, individualistic nature. This because motors were meant to function as adversaries, as enemy doubles of a human subject who, in the process of taming the always menacing machine, was called upon to metallize himself and to transform his own distinctive vital scatti, his own "breathing, sensibility, and instincts," into those of "metals, minerals, and wood." 29 Hence, the new visionary will be a man like Arturo Mercanti, the organizer of the 1910 Transalpine crossing and of other celebrated aerial exploits; a man who, according to one reporter from the Corriere della Sera, generates not a stable output, but an always-about-to-double horsepower:

He knows that when his ideas strike, they sting and stun; and his first task is to shatter this initial mental wall of dark diffidence. He propagates his ideas with brutal candor: brusquely demolishing, discussing, dismantling. Someone resists him? He attacks furiously, convinces him, enthralls him....There exist, evidently, men cast in the same mold as internal combustion motors. In ordinary circumstances they can produce fifty horsepower, but when push comes to shove they can yield even one hundred. 30

Within the setting of this "surplus-only" model of production, the engine of Bielovucic's biplane may be said to contribute in a twofold manner to the "Technical Manifesto's" own distinctively Futurist (and, in most regards, protofascist) body/ machine complex. 31 In the first place, it is a producer of power, noise, and rapid mechanical rhythms, which are themselves source materials for the words-in-freedom poet. In the second place, this motor is also necessarily a producer of surprises, surprises that, in turn, generate acts of heroism, improvisation, and innovation on the part of its human counterpart, who, abhorring earthbound logic and laws, is obliged to exercise his "higher" instinctual faculties while faced with the imminent prospect of injury or death. 32 "Without poetry's uninterrupted succession of new images," the "Technical Manifesto" proclaims, "there is nothing but anemia and chlorosis," and, in keeping with this polarity, the Futurist body/machine complex is designed to produce an infinite proliferation of novelties and to forestall the onset of repetition (identified with a return to the "dark diffidence," effeminacy, and decay of the fin de siècle). 33

The motor jutting out behind the poet-passenger's back on Bielovucic's Voisin would of course be grounded were it not for the presence of a propeller screw. And whereas the former was made of metal, the latter had to be made of laminated lacquered wood. At the time of Marinetti's 1910 flight, experiments were underway with other surface treatments (copper, lacquer-impregnated linen) and technologies (cast metal) in order to reduce propeller torsion and skin friction. But the problem with metal was excessive weight, so in order to balance the need for lightness and strength, wood became the material of choice. The wood propeller's intermediate position between the weightlessness associated with the airplane's sail-like wings 34 and the weight associated instead with the engine (in other words, between the aerial and metallic aspects of an aircraft's identity), corresponded to its location at a symbolic crossroads: right at the center of the cross formed by the airplane's wings and its perpendicular fuselage. 35 Symbolic considerations such as these, bolstered by the propeller's crucial role as the translator of an engine's power into actual physical thrust, led the contemporary imagination to transform it into the very emblem of that material dematerialization of matter in which the miracle of flight was thought to consist. I employ the word "miracle" because it suggests just how thoroughly the already secularized romantic language of transcendence and sublimity was grafted onto the technology of flight during these early years, even within the domains of aeronautic science and engineering. 36 The airplane, or "aeroplane" as it was oftentimes denoted, was thus imagined as a crucifix, and the pilot as a self-sacrificing god; its propeller or wings became the "wingèd oarage" employed by Virgil in the remigium alarum topos to describe Daedalus's flight, 37 by Church Fathers such as Ambrose and Augustine to represent the human soul's flight from the body's fallen materiality, and by Dante to deepen the tragedy of Ulysses's folle volo (mad flight) in the pursuit of secular knowledge. 38 In the specific case of the propeller screw I offer as a first example of Futurism's contribution an unpublished Marinettian text entitled "L'elica e il paesaggio" (probably dating from the 1920s), in which the female protagonist, a "propeller, halo of the new divinity, the machine," vanquishes the universe by streaming triumphantly up into the sky in five-hundred-meter leaps and bounds. 39 Similarly, throughout the "Technical Manifesto's" immediate predecessor, Le Monoplan du Pape , the propeller is at once delicate and fierce. A fearsome borer of the heavens driven by the poet's pulsing motor/heart, it slaps the aviator in the face with indefatigable gusts of wind. 40 Nonetheless, Le Monoplan's propeller is less a shrew than a floral screw: a "purring" or "swirling rose"; a "vast African rose, drunk with flirtatious insects" that can be offered up as a gift to the rising sun by the poet's motor/heart. 41

Such conceits are ubiquitous not just in Marinetti's writings, but also within the period as a whole. To cite but one example, a technical aeronautics text from the immediate post-World War I ear does not hesitate to describe the motor as the airplane's heart and the propeller as its soul:

Isn't [the propeller] soul-like in its infinite smallness with respect to the whole airplane, in its imponderability when twirling at high speeds, in its invisibility as it traverses the ether's azure? A soul in its makeup, since even a slight tap can cause it to exhale its last breath; so often a mere caress will prompt it to expire. 42

Here, as in the prior propeller-halo equation and in Le Monoplan's propeller-rose, the screw is unambiguously female. So too, I would submit, in the propeller prologue to the "Technical Manifesto" and in numerous other Futurist aviatory works extending from Marinetti's Danza dell'aviatrice (Dance of the aviatrix; 1916) 43 through aeropoems like Giovanni Rotiroti's Eliche sul cuore (Propellers on the heart; 1939) and aeropaintings like Sante Monachesi's Desiderio di eliche (Propeller desire) and Tullio d'Albissola's ceramic designs (both from the late 1930s) (. 6 and 7). (I note in passing the names of Giacomo Balla's two daughters: Elica and Ala ("Propeller" and "Wing"). Whether she assumes the guise of an aureole forming over the motor or of a fragile soul attached to a masculine body/motor, or merely functions as a mechanical muse, the propeller behind the motor is always the little woman behind the big man: a thrust device just like the woman celebrated decades later by Benedetta as "apex and propeller of the spiritual-aesthetic expansion of the great fascist Italy" led by il Duce. 44 In the specific case of the "Technical Manifesto," the woman in question--be she mistress or muse--is implicitly identified with the materiality of wood. 45 Not unlike the linguistic material available to the words-in-freedom poet nor unlike the cloth that makes up the airplane's wings, this wood can be freed from the chains that keep it bound to Mother Earth--the chains that bind matter to death and to the "fatally corrosive effects of time" (la fatale corrosione del tempo [TIF, 42]), the chains that shackle words within the laws of logic, syntax, punctuation, spelling, verb tense, and decorum. All of these life-sapping, leaden fetters constitute a tragic reality that can be momentarily overcome through the activation of a higher potentiality that lies dormant within matter (but is nonetheless an integral component): that inner agitation and drive toward "greater ardor, greater movement, a greater subdivision of itself sung in the 1912 manifesto. 46 What is needed to effect this transformation, to get matter airborne, to recover its secret life, and release it into the plenitude of the eternal present, is a masculine force: a motor and a pilot, a poet-flyer endowed with a wireless imagination, an aerodynamic phallus.

There would be much to be said here about the virile subject over whose head looms the propeller-aureole and out of whose body the aircraft's wings obtrude. Identified at the close of the manifesto with Futurism's ideal "mechanical man composed of interchangeable parts," this cyborg-like creature takes to the air in the pursuit of freedom from "death itself, [the] supreme definition of logical intelligence." 47 And, as was earlier implied with regard to the Futurist body/machine complex, his flight from the logic of death often entails a flight from those aspects of female anatomy and sexuality that seem too closely allied with earthbound forms of reproduction. Hence, for instance, in Le Monoplan du Pape, the poet-aviator's claim that "flying at full speed beyond history and anatomy, / on vacation, on vacation, / far from the vulva, gloomy mandatory school!" he becomes "the artist," a "great and abundant being" because endowed with limitless self-generative powers. 48 Likewise, in the conclusion of "Against Love and Parliamentarianism," the dream of creating "a mechanical son, engendered by willpower alone" is invoked as the inebriating spectacle of a Blériot's takeoff causes "strong Futurists" to "feel detached from woman, who has suddenly become...the symbol of the earth that must be left behind." 49 Given the importance of the propeller-myth analyzed above, the larger meaning of these fantasies of self-creation and of escape from the routines imposed by history, woman, work, and school cannot be detachment from the world. Rather it is reattachment, but reattachment under conditions wherein the aviator is free to dictate his law of infinite novelty (against monogamy, monotony, and other principles of repetition and decay). For Marinetti, in other words, flight is less an evasion of sexuality or labor, than a form of hypersexuality that removes prohibitions and multiplies desires. When the Futurist flyer touches down, and touch down he must, he is a changed man. Revirilized, still propped up by his propeller and wings (which have now become an integral part of his body), he springs from his craft "with an ultralight elastic leap" because he has conquered Mother Earth and overturned the iron-clad law that once "obliged man to crawl." 50

It is under the aegis of this mechanized human type, a subject whose anticontemplative mode of apperception Futurist words-in-freedom poetry would aim to transcribe, that I now wish to turn away from the 1912 manifesto's musings on the transration of matter towards what was earlier defined as their visual counterpart, a poetics of image-streams associated with aviation's impact on the faculty of sight. Once again, I take as my point of departure the physical conration of Bielovucic's Voisin. The poet-passenger on such a biplane, elevated as he is above the pilot and his cockpit, has before him an entirely unencumbered, "transparent" visual field, a visual field unavailable in propellerforward monoplane designs even after 1911 (when monoplane wings were set back and the observer's seat raised for purposes of military reconnaissance). The resulting sense of unrestricted vision and direct bodily exposure to the airstream surely must have strengthened in Marinetti the conviction that aerial vision constituted an unmediated, accelerated, and synthetic mode of apprehending the real that would infinitely expand the poet's powers of vision and permit "literature to enter directly into the universe and to form but a single body with it." 51 The single universal body thereby constituted is conceived within the "Technical Manifesto" as an aesthetic totality emanating from and under the control of a poet who is also a conqueror, bombardier, orchestra conductor, and campaign coordinator, much like the re of Arturo Mercanti. But whereas the latter's "totality" consisted of the staging of unprecedented aerial feats over ever new landscapes, of what, one might ask, might the Futurist poet's emanations consist; and just how might the Futurist poet achieve his territorial conquests and acts of coordination? Typically, Marinetti's answer mixes technical-scientific notions with metaphors of late symbolist derivation. The poet's body, by virtue of its being airborne, is hollowed out:

When I flew for the first time with the aviator Bielovucic, I felt my chest open up like a great hole through which, smooth, fresh, and torrential, all the blue of the sky plunged exquisitely. Instead of the slow watered-down sensuality of walks under the sun and amidst flowers, one ought to prefer the ferocious and blood-tingling massage of the raging wind. 52

The "great hole" stands for a momentary effemination whose overall effect is virilizing. It marks the site where earthbound forms of sentimentality and sensuality have been evacuated or vomited forth so as to make room for aerial passions and sensations. 53 Opened up, reinvigorated by the wind's fierce massage, the poet's body is unchained. It can flow out and take possession of the universe by means of networks of perceptual and/or intuitive strings that, woven together, form invisible aerial nets, "invisible" either because they are mental constructs or are envisaged according to a double analogy with flight paths and wireless telegrams, "aerial" because no longer weighed down by the "leaden" ballast of logic or syntax. So light and rapid that the poet's "expansionist gesture can cast them afar...out over a vast ocean," "the mysterious sea of phenomena," these nets yield an ever more bountiful and renewable catch of images that would be unavailable, or so Marinetti implies, were it not for the advent of an imagination that, freed from wires or syntactical strings, is endowed with wings: "words in freedom, the imagination with outspread wings, analogical synthesis of the earth embraced by a single gaze and wholly gathered together in essential words." 54

Several points need to be made with regard to the rhizome that informs the imagist poetics dependent upon airborne sight and according to which the airplane functions as a mechanical intensifier of poetry's "work of analogy." 55 First of all, the link established in the 1912 manifesto between the visual perspectives made available by flight, the aerial communication of synthetic or mechanized messages, and the conquest of abundant "oceans" by a sovereign subject was not of Marinetti's devising. On the contrary, it might be said that between 1910 and 1915 it was very much "in the air." Two examples from quite different contexts, both involving the use of aerial bombardment, come to mind. In November 1911, the Cinzano company began its first experiments with aerial publicity campaigns, launching disk-shaped tricolor flyers bearing an image of the earth surrounded and embraced by a ring representing its world-famous brand of vermouth. 56 In March 1912, this form of intrusion into the everyday lives of city dwellers was extended into the domain of political propaganda by the newly founded Lega Aerea Nazionale, an ultranationalist aviator association that counted D'Annunzio among its staunchest supporters. In order to spread its nationalist gospel of the air, the Lega scattered tricolor manifestos over numerous Italian cities, calling upon the masses to found "the legion of eagles of the skies dedicated to defending borders, wherever there is danger, wherever Italy's flag and name are at stake." 57

Such uses of the airways for purposes of mass persuasion and national mobilization corresponded in turn to two key areas of experimentation within the domain of military aviation with which Marinetti was well familiar: first, the ongoing race among industrialized nations to forge wireless links between aircraft and the ground (Guglielmo Marconi was pursuing such research) and second, the contemporaneous effort to develop aircraft for scouting, gunning, and bombardment purposes. As early as 1910, wireless telegraphy and aviation had become indissociable not just due to the prominence granted to flight by mass circulation newspapers, but also in the minds of contemporary scientists and their sponsors in various European ministries of defense ( 8). Ground-to-air links, it was rightly believed, would greatly enhance the reconnaissance value of aircraft. They would facilitate the rapid deployment of troops and artillery and disperse the "fog of war," giving rise to a battlefield upon which face-to-face combat would be replaced by increasingly "clean" forms of mechanized firepower arriving via the air and coordinated by a central command located far from the theater of battle. 58 It may be worth noting that on a battlefield of this kind there exists an absolute split between the earthbound perspective afforded the ordinary foot soldier and the aerial or overhead perspective available to the commander, a split that may, in turn, help to explain how, in the "Technical Manifesto," the proposed "destruction of the literary I" 59 might engender an even more sovereign literary subject. For the foot soldier, war's violence is experienced as agentless, random, and catastrophic; for the commander (and his Futurist poet counterpart) it is simply the expression of a greatly expanded sense of individual agency: "greatly expanded" because, through the eyes and bodies of his aviators, he can now see the enemy from the air, penetrate his airspace, and be simultaneously present at every point along the front. 60

Now it just so happens that the first testing ground for these concepts was the 1911 Battle of Tripoli, the conflict between the Italian and Turkish armies that Marinetti later described as "the most beautiful aesthetic spectacle of my existence." 61 The spectacle in question involved aerial surveillance, aerial photography, aerial gunning, and even crude experiments with bombardment. (Air-to-ground communications were not yet in use.) And its aesthetic potential would have seemed all the greater due to the presence on the battle front of the documentary filmmaker Luca Comerio. Comerio was perhaps the earliest Italian film journalist; he can be credited with inaugurating the transformation, recently examined by Paul Virilio, of the camera eye into modern warfare's most conspicuous weapon:

On board an airplane, the camera's peep-hole served as an indirect sighting device complementing those attached to the weapons of mass destruction. It thus prered a symptomatic shift in target-location and a growing derealization of military engagement. For in industrialized warfare, where the representation of events outstripped the presentation of facts, the image was starting to gain sway over the object, time over space. 62

Comerio experimented with footage shot from the air, from moving vehicles, and after dark, in coverage that he hoped would both permit civilians to participate in the war's most glorious actions and "remap the world and uncover new visual and representational rhythms and meters." 63 His efforts made the Battle of Tripoli a doubly appropriate incubator for Futurism's new poetics. It permitted words in freedom to be hatched as a literary analog both of the newly mechanized, transparent battlefield and of a montage-based aesthetic or reportage emerging from within the primitive documentary cinema.

Marinetti was the war correspondent in Libya for the French daily L'Intransigeant. The war dispatches that he contributed from the front were soon reshaped into La battaglia di Tripoli, the reportage that provided, in turn, the raw material for his first words-in-freedom poem and the first implementation of the 1912 manifesto's laws: "Battaglia Peso + Odore" (Battle weight + smell) (printed as a supplement to the August 1912 "Risposta alle obiezioni" [Response to objections]). 64 From his earliest accounts, it was the battlefield's aerial dimension that most attracted Marinetti's interest (though in this regard he was no different from the rest of the international press corps). 65 Accordingly, the climactic chapters of La battaglia propel the poet's palpitating but earthbound heart up into the air alongside that of the aerial scout Carlo Piazza, who from this point on becomes the sole narrator. As is the case with all of Marinetti's airplanes, Piazza's Blériot monoplane functions as a kind of celestial writing table from which the aviator can skywrite and leave his mark on the territory to be surveyed and subdued. 66 Carving "at will circles of death through the dangerous waters of the enemy army," Piazza emits a rapid-fire sequence of discontinuous paragraphs variously addressed to comrades, the enemy, the landscape, the reader, all within the framework of what amounts to a crude allegory of arousal, followed by violent penetration and renewal. 67 Saharan Africa's barren womb, plowed by artillery shells, bombs, and airpower--Marinetti's principal alter egos--is delivered from savagery and rendered fruitful through the civilizing violence of Italy's new war technologies. The closing paragraph speaks for itself:

It was in order that lovely salad greens and rose-bushes be born that a grenade carved out that gaping pit...and we will carve out many, many others. We will plant more palm trees, advance sentinels, arranged strategically, to shield new barleys, new cloverbeds, against the swirling sands of the torrid ghibli. 68

The rape of Africa was needed, deserved, and overdue. Every bomb is a smart bomb that, by destroying, plants the seeds of renewal.

I have lingered over La battaglia di Tripoli not because it somehow reveals the darker, "truer" nature of the "Technical Manifesto," but rather because it conjoins two themes that crisscross within the 1912 airspace and with them this essay reaches its close: that of the conquest of the air and the conquest of colonial ground. By the beginning of the second decade of our century, just as a certain secularized rhetoric of transcendence had been grafted onto the technology of flight, so too the related discourses of exploration and colonization had been fully projected onto what was referred to as the "aerial continent." 69 For reasons of brevity, I cite only two illustrations: one from Walter Wellman's The Aerial Age and another from an early debate on aerial law. First Wellman: "For a century or more man has had two great aspirations in the field of progress--one to conquer the unknown parts of the earth, and other to conquer the air. He had already possessed himself of most of the surface of the globe, and now he wished to take possession of the remainder." 70 Irrespective of whether the conqueror was an individual, a collectivity, or a nation, the remainder of the globe here described was to be vanquished through a combination of technological intelligence and reckless adventurism. (Hence Wellman's self-designation as a scientific adventurer.) Instinct and science, violence and civilization, technical progress and the return to primordial forces and values, nationalism and internationalism...all of these binary oppositions could be harmonized within an airspace that remained, for the moment, at once law-bound and above the law. I quote from the jurist Edouard Rollin: "The air is still something unknown to us, and the drawing up of a body of rules in regard to it is of yet premature....[It is like] the center of the African continent some fifty years ago, when the maps indicated it by a great white space vaguely described by geographers as terra incognita." 71 Pronounced in 1906 during the Institut de Droit International's drafting of the legal corpus that would govern flight and wireless telegraphy, these remarks testify to the sometimes willful hesitancy with which international organizations undertook the task of fully regulating the air. Even four years later, years during which the law of the air had been far more exhaustively codified, the inaugural 1910 issue of the Revue Juridique Internationale de la Locomotion Aérienne would still show traces of this reluctance, defining aerial law as "an act of faith in the glorious destiny of aviation" and defining its purpose as that of "favoring the soaring impetus of the individual emancipated from the earth" in "a century of scientific and industrial miracles." 72 If the lyrical tone here seems out of keeping with the prosaic occasion, it points, as do the cases of Rollin and Wellman, to a deeper paradox which resonates within the 1912 manifesto. Whether viewed by those who would regulate and rationalize it, or by those who would survey and conquer it by technological means, the continent of the air was seen by all as humankind's final frontier: a last remaining source of mystery, miracles, novelty, and the unknown; a last refuge for the individual from the humdrum world of work; a last place of transcendence in a resolutely secular age. Something to be preserved, therefore, in its "savage" natural state even as it is conquered and absorbed within earthbound laws and institutions. This is precisely the same paradoxical airspace traversed in the "Technical Manifesto of Futurist Literature." An airspace where literature's future can be legislated through a body of laws above and against all prior literary laws. An airspace where new sovereign forms of individuality can be forged and outdated ones discarded in the face of physical groundlessness and peril. An airspace where nations can compete in the name of international progress. But most of all, an airspace in which metal and flesh, technology and instinct, mind and matter can be pressed together in the pursuit of that vertiginous feeling of outer fullness and inner emptiness that, Marinetti tells us, results when an inspired authorial hand takes to the skies:

[The hand that] separates from the body as it writes, and freely leaves behind a brain that, itself detached and airborne, looks down from on high with terrible lucidity upon the unforeseen phrases emitted by the pen.
Does this domineering brain look on passively or does it instead direct the leaps of fantasy that excite the hand? Impossible to know. In such moments I have observed, from a physiological standpoint, little more than a great void in the stomach. 73

This is what the propeller said.
