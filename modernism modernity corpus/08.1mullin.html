Typhoid Turnips and Crooked Cucumbers: Theosophy in Ulysses

Katherine Mullin

I got hold of the first two volumes of The Secret Doctrine and read steadily through it, so many pages a day, in the most favourable conditions for such study . . . . Though I understood so little yet, the bigness, strangeness, newness of the subject matter, the virility of the style, the curiosity it awoke in me, held my interest without flagging. 1

Gretta Cousins's memoirs give a disconcerting account of her honeymoon. She confesses how she found "bigness, strangeness, newness" and even "virility" from Helena Petrovna Blavatsky's best-selling introduction to theosophy, The Secret Doctrine, rather than from a more conventional source. As though anticipating readerly raised eyebrows at this substitution of that most fashionable of Edwardian religious faiths for more usual nuptial pleasures, Gretta explained how "providential" it was that spiritual matters preoccupied her during a time of crisis. She grew "white and thin" during her first year of marriage, not, as friends suspected, on account of her vegetarianism, but rather due to "problems of adjustment to the revelation that marriage had brought me as to the physical basis of sex":

Every child I looked at called to my mind the shocking circumstance that brought about its existence. My new knowledge, though I was lovingly safeguarded from it, made me ashamed of humanity, and ashamed for it. I found myself looking on men and women as degraded by this demand of nature. Something in me revolted then, and has ever since protested against certain of the techniques of nature concerned with sex. Nor will I, and many men and women of like nature, including my husband, be sanctified, purified and redeemed, life after life, until the evolution of form has substituted some more artistic way of continuance of the race. [WTT, 108-09]

On June 15th 1904, and at several other occasions during that significant month, James Joyce stayed with Gretta Cousins and her husband James, interspersing evenings with them with his courtship of Nora Barnacle. The couple were established friends of Joyce, Gretta a talented pianist and graduate of the Royal university and James a poet and playwright active in the Literary Revival. To Gretta, Joyce was "a favourite of mine though he was reputed to be a 'bad boy,'" and she "delighted in his lovely tenor voice especially when I accompanied some of his Irish songs with nobody but ourselves to hear in our little drawing room" (WTT, 106). The affection was only partly reciprocated. Joyce was unable to stomach the couple's proselytising of theosophy, chastity, and vegetarianism and fled, grumbling of indigestion resulting from "a typhoid turnip." 2 However, Joyce's discontent with the Cousins was grounded in more than culinary differences. Gretta Cousins's revulsion from "certain of the techniques of nature concerned with sex" exemplifed a theosophical somatophobia viewed with predictable ridicule and suspicion by the young Joyce, a self-proclaimed sexual radical. "Scylla and Charybdis," the ninth episode of Ulysses, performs a deeply subversive assault upon these sexual ideologies. "Scylla" assembles before Stephen a captive audience of Dublin theosophists, yet the chapter also groans with innuendo and sexual excess. This essay will explore this tension, documenting how Joyce discloses the fault lines permeating theosophy's celebration of chastity through a network of intertextual references which recover a buried, latent sexuality.

Theosophy was in tune with contemporary social purity campaigns to impose a single moral standard of sexual continence and self-restraint. 3 Converts were implored to "obtain the power to control their desires," to resist the surrender of "the divine nature to the animal nature": celibacy was recommended as having "superior advantages for meditation and study," whilst "marital continence" permitted "the union of the male spirit with the female soul." 4 Disciples, known as "chelas," were expected to remain celibate whilst seeking the path to enlightenment. 5 Many theosophists were also dedicated social purists. For Charlotte Despard, the pioneering purity feminist Josephine Butler introduced "the close connection between the women's movement and the spiritual awakening which marked the closing decades of the last century." 6 Helena Blavatsky's companion Isabel Cooper-Oakley was a prominent campaigner for the Social Purity Alliance. 7 W. T. Stead, whose "Maiden Tribute of Modern Babylon" articles exposing child prostitution in London had initiated and funded the National Vigilance Association, was another celebrity convert. 8 The Theosophical Society frequently published material which more commonly issued from social purity presses. Two theosophy pamphlets marketed as "of special interest to women" were Lucy Re-Bartlett's Sex and Sanctity, which recommended less of the former and more of the latter, and Edith Ward's The Vital Question: An Address on Social Purity, a millennial polemic against "marital incontinence." 9 This dialogue between theosophy and social purity is particularly significant for Joyce, since social purity had failed to achieve the success in Ireland that it enjoyed in Britain. Irish social purity groups such as the Dublin White Cross Vigilance Association were mistrusted by Catholic nationalists as Protestant proselytising organisations, a mistrust encouraged by social purity's enthusiastic Unionism and imperialist agenda. 10 However, theosophy was an important aspect of the Irish Literary Revival. John Eglinton asserted that the Celtic Revival was precipitated by "the ferment caused in the minds of a group of young men by the early activities of the Theosophy Movement in Dublin," and according to him W. B. Yeats once declared: "Theosophy has done more for Irish literature in three years than Trinity College in its three centuries." 11 Theosophy was thus a conduit for social purity ideology into an Ireland sympathetic to its sexual politics, yet suspicious of its political affiliations.

"Aeolus," the seventh episode in Ulysses, notes theosophy's close links with Dublin's literary establishment when J. J. O'Molloy asks Stephen:

What do you really think of that hermetic crowd, the opal hush poets: A. E. the mastermystic? That Blavatsky woman started it. She was a nice old bag of tricks. A. E. has been telling some yankee interviewer that you came to him in the small hours of the morning to ask him about planes of consciousness" 12

The Dublin Hermetist Society was founded by Yeats and George Russell in 1886. 13 Hermetists included Revivalist poets James Cousins and Susan Mitchell, the journalist Charles Johnstone, the publisher George Roberts and Harry Norman, editor of The Irish Homestead. 14 Many were Joyce's friends or mentors, and, initially caught up in the enthusiasm, Joyce acquired one of theosophy's key texts, Henry Olcott's A Buddhist Catechism, which he signed "J. A. Joyce, May 7, 1901." 15 Consequently, as Ralph Jenkins notes, Joyce's "knowledge of theosophical doctrines is far greater than any commentator has previously pointed out." 16 This knowledge was extended with later purchases of Annie Besant's Une Introduction à la Théosophie and The Path of Discipleship, and comes into play in "Scylla," where Stephen finally responds to O'Molloy's question. 17 Stephen's Shakespeare theory has long been discredited as contradictory, irrelevant, historically inaccurate, a mere theatrical facade and of little literary value, readings all pre-empted by Stephen's own declaration that he does not believe in it himself. 18 Desmond McCarthy offers one way out of the puzzle, noting how trying to discern Shakespeare's personality from the paucity of information about him which remains is "like looking at a very dark glazed picture in the National Portrait Gallery: at first you see nothing, then you begin to recognise features, then you realise that they are your own." 19 Rather than reflecting himself, however, the darkened glass of Stephen's Shakespeare portrait ostentatiously holds the mirror up to his listeners. As Len Platt notes, Stephen's remark "khaki Hamlets don't hesitate to shoot" and his reference to Swinburne's infamous defence of the British concentration camps during the Boer War together introduce this allegorising drive, as Stephen's Shakespeare is soiled by an imperial complicity his Ascendancy listeners defensively disavow (U, 9:133). 20 The allegory is abruptly personalised with "Elizabethan London lay as far from Stratford as corrupt Paris lies from virgin Dublin" (U, 9:149-50). "Virgin Dublin" is political provocation comparable to "khaki Hamlets don't hesitate to shoot," since again Stephen uses the supposedly safe domain of literary criticism to mask a more loaded critique of the contemporary.

Stephen's pointed parallel between Shakespeare's Stratford and "virgin Dublin" thus hits below the belt, announcing the focus of his critique to be the occluded and privatized domain of sex. Individual recollections of the Dublin Hermetists indicate how earnestly "the sex question" was pondered:

In those early days we unmarried ones all hoped to become Chelas and expected to remain celibate, and were rather shocked to hear of Charles Johnstone's marriage, even to a niece of Madame Blavatsky! The urges of sex, natural to youngsters in their twenties, were hardly ever spoken of, except in the disguise of "Karma," and were sternly suppressed. [MAE, 17]

George Russell found theosophical conversion an escape from adolescent "desires of body and heart with which we so soon learn to taint our youth" (MAE, 12). The predictable serpent was only vanquished when "I became aware of a mysterious life quickening within my life, carrying with it unsated desires, base and august, and as I divined it, myriads of memories and secret wisdom" (MAE, 13). The monthly journal The Irish Theosophist was similarly voluble. Charles Johnstone inveighed against "man's animal part," elaborating on the many ways in which "lust is but a disappointment, to be overcome." 21 Russell concurred, arguing that "the lower part of man, the body and its attachments, be conquered and purified." 22 An unsigned article on "The Sex Problem" similarly asked "why cannot love be noble?" since "the soul is sexless and passionless." 23 These individual reminiscences indicate how "virgin Dublin" was part of an international theosophical insistence upon asexuality. Celibacy was a condition of enlightenment, and "chelas" were urged to live in quasi-monastic communes like that at Ely Place. Blavatsky shared her London home with several prominent theosophists, and conspicuously led by example, responding to allegations of bigamy and illegitimate children with the characteristically bizarre claim that "I could never have connection with any man because I am lacking something and the place is filled up with some crooked cucumber." 24 Annie Besant was only admitted into the movement on recanting her notorious teachings on contraception. She destroyed unsold copies of The Law of Population, writing Theosophy and the Law of Population in replacement, which advocated complete abstinence, since "the sexual instinct that man has in common with the brute is the most fruitful source of human misery." 25 These attempts to eradicate desire were summarised in the movement's motto, "A Universal Brotherhood of Humanity without distinction of race, creed, sex, caste or colour."

1. Universal Brotherhood and "the Brother Motive"

The celebration of fraternity as an ideal state of sexless companionship is subverted from the opening pages of "Scylla and Charybdis." "Formless spiritual," Stephen muses, "father, Word and Holy Breath. Allfather, the heavenly man," but his memories of theosophical jargon swiftly turn from paternity to fraternity:

Dunlop, Judge, the noblest Roman of them all, A. E., Arval, the Name Ineffable, in heaven hight: K. H., their master, whose identity is no secret to adepts. Brothers of the great white lodge always watching to see if they can help. The Christ with the bridesister, moisture of light, born of an ensouled virgin, repentant sophia, departed to the plane of buddhi. The life esoteric is not for ordinary person. O. P. must work off bad karma first. Mrs Cooper Oakley once glimpsed our very illustrious sister H. P. B.'s elemental. O, fie! Out on't! Pfuiteufel! You naughtn't to look, missus, so you naughtn't when a lady's ashowing of her elemental. [U, 9:61-74].

Stephen pointedly segregates the sexes, listing the men--Daniel Dunlop, editor of The Irish Theosophist, William Quan Judge, the Irish-American co-founder of the Theosophical Society, A.E., Koot Hoomi, the Tibetan "master" or "mahatma" who supposedly precipitated spirit-messages to Blavatsky--before the women, Blavatsky and Isabel Cooper-Oakley. This gender segregation is summarized with "brotherhood of the great white lodge," the association of benign Masters who watched over the faithful from a higher spiritual plane. 26 Stephen mentally maps out an ideologically loaded modulation of the "family of man" metaphor. Theosophy's ideal of "Universal Brotherhood" reassessed the family of man not as a reproductive unit, but as the terminal stage of biological and social development. By displacing "allfather, the heavenly man" with "brothers of the great white lodge," Stephen draws attention to how theosophical egalitarianism censored the dangerous dynamism of sexual reproduction from a familiar metaphor. The process of this displacement is emphasized with "Christ with the bridesister," where Stephen linguistically demarcates the point of transformation, as "bride" mutates into chaste "sister." As Stephen suggests, the sibling relationship offered theosophists a safely celibate paradigm for the containment of potentially transgressive desire.

Stephen builds up, however, to an imagined scenario which provocatively retrieves the fissures implicit in theosophy's version of siblinghood. He recalls Madame Blavatsky's insistence that her "elemental" or ghost would appear to her followers after her death, and Isabel Cooper-Oakley's much-publicised claim to have been so visited. While Blavatsky is "our very illustrious sister," she is also a perverse sexual exhibitionist, flaunting her "elemental" to her friend, and Stephen's pun on "elemental" / "genital" here transforms sisterhood into a predictable dirty joke, disclosing a repressed lesbian longing behind the celibate facade. Stephen suggests that theosophy's conspicuous disdain for heterosexuality or "straight sex" in favour of an asexual sibling bond in fact masks a sexuality that contemporary standards would have judged perverted or corrupt. 27 It is therefore significant that Stephen's internal monologue is induced by George Russell's theory that "art has to reveal to us ideas, formless spiritual essences" (U, 9: 48-9). Stephen notes how Russell's aesthetics stem from his theosophy and retaliates by placing that connection under scrutiny. Russell "impatiently" objects to Stephen's Shakespeare theory with "all this prying into the family life of a great man" (U, 9:181). His objection is revealingly defensive, since Stephen's unfolding of "the brother-motive" radically challenges theosophy's view of brotherhood as egalitarian and asexual (U, 9:956). If theosophy presented brotherhood as a benignly "spiritualized" alternative to the disruptive dynamism of sexuality, then Stephen reappraises the relationship, building upon his insinuation about Blavatsky and Cooper-Oakley to present the sibling bond not as an alternative to sex, but as a bizarre perversion of it. His theory that Ann Hathaway's "misconduct with the brothers" so obsessed Shakespeare that "the theme of the false or the usurping or the adulterous brother or all three in one is what the poor is not, always with him" disconcertingly sexualises fraternity (U, 9:963, 998). Stephen's detection of the brothers' names in Richard III and King Lear, together with his reading of Hamlet as an autobiographical account of sexual rivalry between brothers, locks the Shakespeare siblings into a stifling homosocial bond, where sexuality is not directed towards the outside world, but is instead turned inwards, mediated through Ann Hathaway and thereby towards each other.

This provocative portrait of brotherhood as sexual claustrophobia is intensified with the spectre of incest. While Stephen's reference to "a bodily shame so steadfast that the criminal annals of the world, stained with all other incests and bestialities, hardly record its breach" alludes to incestuous acts between fathers and sons, the question of homosexual desire within the family it raises contributes towards Stephen's investigation of another form of "bodily shame" (U, 9: 850-2). Stephen's "love that dare not speak its name" (U, 9: 659) is incest between brothers, a taboo desire which Stephen archly presents as barely contained through the brothers' sexual sharing of Ann Hathaway. This development of the incest theme is evident in Stephen's initial insistence on the relationship between male members of a family as one of jealousy, fear and revulsion: "the images of other males of his blood will repel him. He will see in them grotesque attempts of nature to foretell or to repeat himself" (U, 9:433-5). However, Stephen soon revises this vision of repulsion into one of ambivalent desire: one of the selves Stephen insists we encounter as "we walk through ourselves" is "brothers-in-love" (U, 9:1046). The ambiguous re of the brother-in-love collapses the distinction between the homosocial and the homosexual, implying how a brother having sex with a brother's wife is thereby engaging in indirect incest. These hints of this shocking sexual taboo are confirmed and compounded, as Susan Sutliff Brown has fascinatingly shown, by Joyce's perplexing concealment of the names of his own brothers within Ulysses. Following the autobiographical invitation extended in "Scylla" to search for the names of Joyce's three brothers George, Charles, and Stanislaus, Brown notes that all appear in "sexually explicit or suggestive connection with either Stephen or Bloom or both." Thus, George appears in "Scylla" and elsewhere as "Georgina Johnson," or Stephen's favourite prostitute, Charles appears as "Charles Alberta Marsh," the voyeur who contemplates sex with the "bisexually abnormal" Bloom in "Circe," while a Madame Stanislaus is coupled with the cross-dressing Gerald, the performer who Bloom claims "converted me to be a true corsetlover when I was a female impersonator in the High School Play." 28 These disconcerting visions of the Joyce brothers in real or fantasised sexual encounters with Joyce's fictional counterparts thus ironically substantiate Stephen's Shakespeare theory.

Ann Hathaway's "misconduct with the brothers" thus boldly challenges theosophy's ideological attachment to brotherhood as safely celibate. The impact of Stephen's insinuations is indicated when Stephen responds to Eglinton's "severe" rebuke, "the doctor can tell us what those words mean" by declining his interpretation of incest as requiring the psychoanalysis of "the new Viennese school" (U, 9:738, 780). Instead, Stephen displaces Freud with Aquinas to elaborate upon his vision of Shakespeare as enthusiastic and exploitative capitalist, a "moneylender with ten tons of corn hoarded in the famine riots" (U, 9:743). He asserts that incest is "an avarice of the emotions," since "the love so given to one near in blood is covetously withheld from some stranger who, it may be, hungers for it" (U, 9:781-3). Incest is here presented as a perversely obsessional form of self-absorption, where sex, like gold, is hoarded from the outside world. The onanistic ramifications of this suggestion are elaborated: "we walk through ourselves, meeting robbers, ghosts, giants, old men, young men, wives, widows, brothers-in-love, but always meeting ourselves" (U, 9:1044-6).

"Brothers-in-love" moves seamlessly on to "always meeting ourselves," a juxtaposition which refers back to Stephen's earlier insistence that one's male relatives are "grotesque" shadowings of the self to assert a continuity between brother-love and self-love. Brotherhood is envisioned as a turning away from life, an unhealthy repression of or internalization of sexual energies, rather than a utopian state. Stephen's sustained hypothesis of a correspondence between brotherhood and a timid, self-absorbed sexuality implies the sterile frustrations behind theosophy's emphasis upon platonic restraint. Elsewhere in "Scylla," Stephen expands upon these hints that theosophy conceals an insular, claustrophobic and obsessive sexual solipsism.

2. "In the darkness spirit hands were felt to flutter": Sexuality on the Astral Plane

Stephen's hypothesis of "brotherhood" as a form of latent, displaced sexuality, rather than the utopian asexual state which theosophy celebrated, exemplifies a sustained rhetorical strategy. Elsewhere in Ulysses, Joyce subverts theosophy from within, performing highly ironic interrogations of its enthusiasm for chastity by eroticizing its ideology and language. If theosophy's interpretation of "brotherhood" as a sanitised alternative to sexuality is wilfully undercut in Stephen's reading of "the brother motive" in Shakespeare, then so too is another central aspect of its belief and practice. While theosophy was committed to "brotherhood," it was more notoriously concerned not with relationships between the living, but with those between the living and the dead. It developed from the late-Victorian enthusiasm for spiritualism and its materialisation seances and automatic writings which indicated to believers a secret spectral sphere. 29 Blavatsky was an acclaimed spiritualist medium during the late 1870s, and she began to promote spiritualism's faith in the paranormal within the context of wider occult lore. 30 With Isis Unveiled and The Secret Doctrine , she drew heavily on esoteric Buddhism to interpret paranormal events as evidence for a new religious faith. Theosophy's appropriation of Buddhism and other Eastern doctrines focused particularly upon reincarnation, and spirit manifestations were accordingly interpreted as evidence for the durability of the soul after death. Blavatsky argued that this "divine wisdom" was telepathically disclosed to her by the Masters or Mahatmas, Tibetan spirits who communicated their intentions through automatic writings and seance manifestations. 31 When Stephen begins his Shakespeare discourse with the question "what is a ghost?" (U, 9:147) he provocatively names the privileged object of theosophical quest.

One significant premonition of Stephen's strategy of subversive appropriation occurred in 1904, when Joyce and Buck Mulligan's prototype, Oliver St John Gogarty, broke into the Dublin theosophical lodge shortly before a meeting. There, the two discovered what Stephen remembers as a "Yogibogeybox in Dawson Chambers" (U, 9:281), a spirit cabinet usually taking the form of a curtained box or large cupboard accessible only from the front (DR, 46). Next to this "Yogibogeybox" stood a packing case belonging to George Roberts, at the time combining his theosophical investigations with a day-job as a traveling underwear salesman. Joyce purloined a particularly elaborate pair of drawers, stringing them aloft in the centre of the room suggestively balanced on a broom handle, while Gogarty attached a note reading "I never did it" and signed "John Eglinton" as a final flourish (JJ, 174). These high-jinks take on a particularly arch resonance, since billowing Edwardian knickers produced from a packing case reproduce the culturally expected image of ghosthood, a nebulous apparition clothed in white draperies. Joyce and Gogarty's biting visual pun thus placed on show that sexuality which theosophy tried to repress. Their student prank burlesqued theosophy's ostentatious chastity by questioning the nature of the repressed spectres which haunted its members' imaginations.

Stephen's discourse on ghosts may be more intellectually complex than Joyce's 1904 practical joke, but it relies upon similar methods of provoking unease. Stephen similarly manipulates his audiences' expectations and preconceptions about ghosts to draw a parallel between the sexual and the spectral. John Eglinton, the most obvious butt of Joyce and Gogarty's wit, introduces this substitution: "He will have it that Hamlet is a ghost story, John Eglinton said for Mr Best's behoof. Like the fat boy in Pickwick he wants to make our flesh creep" (U, 9:141). Eglinton's commonplace misreading of Dickens is significant. The fat boy refers not to a paranormal occurrence, but to his vision of the middle-aged lovers Miss Wardle and Mr Tupman "a-huggin and a-kissin" in a summerhouse. 32 The following day the fat boy leads Miss Wardle's mother to the summerhouse, announces "I wants to make your flesh creep" and tells her what he has seen. 32 Eglinton's misremembrance of the context of the much-quoted line thus operates in "Scylla" as something of a Freudian slip, as he substitutes a ghost story for a sexual secret. Stephen takes up this confusion, as his initial attempt to define "what is a ghost" as "one who has faded into impalpability through death, through absence, through change of manners" is soon displaced by an erotically charged account of ghosthood (U, 9:147-9). Stephen's argument that Shakespeare should be identified with the ghost of King Hamlet relies upon the two men's shared identity as cuckolds sexually betrayed by their own brothers. King Hamlet's ghost is "endowed with knowledge" of "the poisoning and the beast with two backs that urged it" by his "creator," Shakespeare "weary of the creation he has piled up to hide himself from himself, an old dog licking an old sore" (U, 9:470-2). Stephen thus entangles ghosthood with sexual obsession: his ghosts remain haunted by the body, its desires, its humiliations. These touches are consolidated when Stephen responds to Eglinton's objection to his lascivious portrait of Ann Hathaway. "Carping," Eglinton complains "her ghost at least has been laid for ever." (U, 9:215). For Stephen, Hathaway's ghost is laid in another sense:

Venus and Adonis lay in the bedchamber of every light-of-love in London . . . if others have their will, Ann hath a way. By cock, she was to blame . . . . The greyeyed goddess, stooping to conquer, as prologue to the swelling act, is a boldfaced Stratford wench who tumbles in a cornfield a lover younger than herself. [U, 9:250-60]

The "local color" Stephen provides to "make [his listeners] accomplices" takes the form of a highly sexualized backdrop: Sir Walter Raleigh's "pair of fancy stays," the excessive amount of underlinen hoarded by the "gombeenwoman Eliza Tudor," "the punks of the bankside, a penny a time," "the holy office the ostler does for the stallion" (U, 9:158, 629, 630, 640, 664). Eglinton's ghost "laid for ever" is resuscitated with such lecherous fervour that Russell, disgusted, rises to leave for The Homestead, his departure prompting a flurry of invitations to an evening party from which Stephen is pointedly excluded.

When Stephen resumes, he significantly offers a metaphor which imagines ghosthood as an indelible physical trace. Stephen argues that the "image of the unliving son" bears the traces of "the ghost of the unquiet father," "as the mole on my right breast is where it was when I was born, though all my body has been woven of new stuff time after time" (U, 9:379-81). This provocative confusion between the spectral and the somatic is pointedly sustained elsewhere in Ulysses, where ghosts are inevitably entangled with sexual encounters or imagery. In "Proteus," Stephen's recollection of his dying mother, "the ghostcandle to light her agony. Ghostly light on the tortured face" is disconcertingly spliced to his fantasy of the primal scene of his conception: "the man with my voice and my eyes and a ghostwoman with ashes on her breath. They clasped and sundered, did the coupler's will" (U, 3:46-7). In "Hades," this association between ghosts and the erotic is taken up by Bloom, who rephrases the occult as a form of aphrodisiac: "tell her a ghost story in bed to make her sleep. Have you ever seen a ghost? . . . Still they'd kiss right if properly keyed up. Whores in Turkish graveyards" (U, 6:754-7). Later Bloom expands on the quasi-necrophiliac effects of this scenario:

Even scraping up the earth at night with a lantern like that case I read of to get at fresh buried females or even putrefied with running gravesores. I will appear to you after death. You will see my ghost after death. My ghost will haunt you after death. [U, 6:997-1001]

This connection between ghosts and sex is reprised in "Circe," where Bella commands Bloom to "tell me something to amuse me, smut or a bloody good ghost story," and where the Croppy Boy "gives up the ghost" as "a violent erection of the hanged sends gouts of sperm spouting through his deathclothes" (U, 15:3053, 4548). Such macabre associations enter into dialogue with the kind of anxieties about ghosts and the body which preoccupied contemporary theosophy.

Such anxieties are pointedly addressed in "Cyclops." Alf claims to have seen Paddy Dignam in Capel Street that morning, his companions are superstitiously appalled: "You saw his ghost then, says Joe, God between us and harm" (U, 12:326). Alf's spectral sighting is written up in the jargon of a typical seance report: "in the darkness, spirit hands were felt to flutter," and Dignam's "etheric double" is disclosed through "a faint but increasing luminosity of ruby light . . . . orangefiery and scarlet rays emanating from the sacral region and solar plexus" (U, 12:338-44). The "etheric double" was a specific type of ghost defined in Charles Webster Leadbeater's The Astral Plane as the spectral state closest to the flesh and comparable to purgatory, since the virtuous swiftly moved along into "the devanchic condition where alone his spiritual aspirations can find full fruition." 33 Joyce's "etheric double" of Paddy Dignam, as Leadbeater predicts, is indeed preoccupied with the somatic: he requests buttermilk, worries about funeral costs, and instructs his son to retrieve a boot "under the commode in the return room," while maintaining that the spirit world is equipped with such incongruous modern conveniences as "tãlãfãnã, ãlãvãtãr, hãtãkãldã, wãtãklãsãt" (U, 12:368, 354). Dignam's spirit particularly resembles the "etheric double" of the "victim of sudden death," abruptly severed from an "earth-life low and brutal, selfish and sensual":

Crowds of these unfortunate creatures hang around public houses or other even more disreputable places--wherever the gross influences in which they delight are to be found and where they encounter men and women still in the flesh who are like-minded with themselves. 34

His haunts include Capel Street, a slumland backstreet where disreputable amusements like Mutoscope parlours are to be found, Barney Kiernan's pub, and later Nighttown outside Bella Cohen's brothel, where he addresses Bloom: "I am Paddy Dignam's spirit. List, list, O list!" (U, 15:1218). More strikingly still, Leadbeater insisted that the "auric egg" or spirit mist could be classified through color, and that "a lurid, flaming red" indicated "animal passions"; significantly, not only is Dignam's "auric egg" of the appropriate debased shade, but also the "orangefiery and scarlet rays" shine forth from a suggestive zone of the body, the "sacral region," a coy euphemism for the groin. 35

Joyce's parodic vision of ghosthood as a disconcertingly somatic condition demonstrates the specificity of his knowledge of theosophy's doctrinal nuances. Paddy Dignam's etheric double behaves exactly as Charles Leadbeater predicted the ghost of an "impure" man who met a sudden death would act: he is preoccupied with the banal and the worldly, he manifests in the disreputable places he favoured in life, he even emits the fiery red aura of "animal passions" from his groin. These close coincidences argue for Joyce's willingness to subvert the minutiae of theosophical doctrine for his own purposes. To read "Cyclops" and "Scylla" together is to note how closely Joyce engaged with a fiercely contested issue within theosophy: the problem of unruly spirit manifestations and the affiliated question of supernatural desire. Many converts found comfort in the idea of an asexual paradise awaiting the faithful beyond the grave. For Charlotte Despard, "In the spiritual world there is no sex. 'There', said a Master, 'shall be neither marrying nor giving in marriage; but they shall be as the angels of God in heaven.'" 36 For W. T. Stead "love beyond the borderland" was reassuringly distinct from this life's "temporary passion which is not love, but self-indulgence of the worst shape." 37 For G. R. S. Mead, editor of the occult journal The Quest, the Masters dwelt on "lofty planes of pure morality," while for Gretta Cousins, as we have seen, the future world was one where sex would be transcended. 38 However, Charles Leadbeater provided a controversial and opposing view of an afterlife saturated with taboo sexual desires. The Astral Plane addressed a disconcerting fault line in contemporary occultism: if the afterlife transcended the body's material concerns, how to account for the atmosphere of sexual excess which came to dominate seances?

Within the late-Victorian spiritualist movement, materialization seances like the one Joyce spoofs in "Cyclops" had acquired a certain notoriety. Young, pretty female mediums entered the spirit cabinet as exemplary res of Victorian decorum, then appeared as spirits whose appearance and behaviour was in tantalizing contrast to their conventionally modest demeanor (DR, 219-56). The acclaimed medium Elizabeth D'Esperance produced "Yolande," who was "a beautiful Arab girl, olive-skinned and dark haired," and who appeared in the flimsiest interpretation of Egyptian costume, while her rival Florence Cook's "Katie King," dressed in diaphanous robes, accepted bouquets and gifts, kissed and sat on the laps of male admirers, and invited enthusiasts to "pass their hands over her dress in order that they might satisfy themselves she wore only a robe." 39 Other spectacular evidences of supernatural presences, such as the ectoplasmic extrusions from later mediums' mouths, nipples and, on occasion, genitals, compounded existing suspicions that it was sex, not ghosts, that occult practitioners were really liberating. 40

This seance eroticism was widely publicized since spiritualist enthusiasm was repeatedly linked by a hostile medical establishment to hysteria, a condition with unsavoury sexual connotations (DR, 139). Dr. Forbes Winslow set the tone: "The community of believers contains a large proportion of weak-minded, hysterical women in whom the seeds of disorder are only waiting for a new excitement to ripen into maturity." 41 The British Medical Journal concurred: "Hysteria is doubtless the disease which more than any other furnishes the most abundant crop of fruit to the Spiritualists." 42 For Henry Maudsley, one of the leading Victorian medical psychopathologists, the ecstatic state of mediumship was mere "emotional incontinence" resulting from "irregularities of menstruation and suspected self-abuse" and an excess of "heavily disguised venereal passions." 43 These responses indicate how closely occult practice was linked to diseased sexuality, and place Joyce's own lively responses to seances in sudden relief. Flaunting ladies' bloomers in Ely Place in 1904 seems a loaded allusion to the practices which sullied the reputation of contemporary occultism, an allusion shadowed in Stephen Dedalus's seemingly gratuitous references to Elizabeth Tudor's hoard of underlinen and Walter Raleigh's "fancy stays." Joyce places on show what commonly issued from the spirit cabinet; scantily clad "ghosts," who disconcertingly resembled the smutty postcards, indecent stereoscopes, and tableaux vivants of popular erotic entertainments. Sly allusions to an occultism repeatedly constituted as a displaced form of "venereal passions" are elaborated in Stephen's oppressive stream of innuendo and his discussion of ghosthood as a condition of sexual obsession. Stephen's Shakespeare theory is resonant of the scepticism of the late-Victorian medical establishment which attributed seance manifestations to sexually stigmatised hysteria. His insistence that theosophy is an indirect, evasive and obsessional way of negotiating a sexuality which alternately terrified and fascinated his listeners is rendered particularly specific in the closing pages of "Scylla," where Buck Mulligan effervescently "reads" Stephen's theory to disclose its hidden slur. Mulligan repeatedly constitutes masturbation as the concealed vice of "that hermetic crowd," alluding, as we shall see, to sustained and well-publicised contemporary cultural anxieties.

3. Charles Webster Leadbeater: Theosophy in the "Foul Masturbation Abyss"

In 1906, a Chicago theosophist, Helen Dennis, discovered a letter to her thirteen year old son from Charles Leadbeater, the deputy leader of the London-Adyar branch of the Theosophy Society. Written in a simple code, it instructed her son to masturbate twice weekly, and included the damning line "glad sensation is so pleasant." 44 Leadbeater was called before a disciplinary committee in London dominated by social purity sympathizers, where he argued that he was "clairvoyantly aware" of the "sex problems" tormenting his young pupils, and so advocated masturbation as a "a preventative against unchastity." 45 His "safety-valve" defence radically departed from the social purity orthodoxy that masturbation was a debilitating evil in itself, leading to physical decay, mental instability, emasculation, and further immoralities. It failed to convince the London committee, and Leadbeater was expelled. His influence as "one of the world's foremost scientists in spiritual research," however, meant that the scandal could not be so simply contained (EB, 5). The 1907 trial confirmed rumours prevalent since 1897, when Leadbeater began to urge parents to entrust the spiritual development of their sons to him. 46 His flamboyant chastity further exacerbated suspicions: he pointedly refused to shake hands with women at meetings, and, when a guest of married couples, insisted they occupy separate bedrooms for the duration of his stay, eccentricities which prompted one host to comment "his tastes run mainly to boys and tapioca pudding" (EB, 199). 47 Those who hoped in 1907 for an "end to this wretched controversy" which had for the past decade "threatened to plunge Theosophy into the foul masturbation abyss" were disappointed (LC, 18). Supporters of Leadbeater were numerous enough to force the London committee to reaffirm their decision in 1909. 48 By then, publicity threatened to render "the public propagation of theosophy impossible"; it was vital "to rid the Theosophical Society of this foul blot upon its name and fame," and such fears were not unfounded (LC, 5). Throughout 1909 the London paper John Bull ran a sustained campaign, running headlines such as "A Teacher of Filth" and "Pitiful Degradation of the London Theosophical Lodge," while its editor Horace Bottomley, M. P., threatened to alert the Director of Public Prosecutions that "the Theosophical society is gathering into it ranks an army of morbid moral degenerates whose teachings are calculated to undermine the character and sap the manhood of our race" (LC, 20). 49 Other English papers concurred with John Bull: the New Statesman noted that no other modern adventurer had built up such a police dossier, while the Times and the Daily Telegraph regularly ran articles of protest in a similar vein (EB, 180). The controversy rumbled on well into the 1920s, following Leadbeater to India, where his adoption of two Hindu boys prompted their father to sue for restitution of his children in 1912 in a bizarre lawsuit combining charges of deification and sodomy, and to Australia where a series of articles in the Sydney Daily Telegraph during 1922 prompted a full-scale police investigation (EB, 200).

Given the global and highly public nature of "the Leadbeater affair," one might speculate that Joyce, an avowed sceptic who, I have urged, borrowed from key Leadbeater texts, relished the scandal. Certainly, speculation that Joyce knew of theosophy's seamier side are encouraged in the closing pages of "Scylla." Masturbation is the centrepiece of Buck Mulligan's parody of the Hermetists who listen to Stephen, and its centrality must have been bitingly resonant by 1919 and 1920, when Joyce was composing the chapter and theosophy had already endured more than two decades of persistent scandal over the solitary vice. Mulligan builds upon a theme slyly introduced by Stephen, who draws an implicit parallel between Hamlet, "an androgynous angel, being a wife unto himself," and his listeners, "unwed, unfancied, ware of wiles, they fingerponder nightly each his variorum edition of The Taming of the Shrew" (U, 9:1052, 1062). Stephen presents both Hamlet and his audience as ghosthunters terrified of procreative, interactive sexuality and instead confined to the sexual cul-de-sac of autoeroticism. Hamlet is "a wife unto himself," and the bawdy ramifications of his sexual self-reliance are drawn out with the word "fingerponder," an intrusion of the manual heavily weighted with double entendre. Stephen associates literary Dublin with the kind of forestalled sexuality which Mulligan will later castigate as particularly Irish in "Oxen of the Sun," and these insinuations are voluble in the context of theosophy's associations. Leadbeater's enthusiasm for masturbation was only the most notorious instance of a contraceptive strain running through theosophy, not only through its insistence on chastity, but more problematically through Blavatsky's often-repeated claim that a "crooked cucumber" guaranteed her celibacy, and through Annie Besant's troubling past as a radical campaigner for birth control. Stephen's hints that theosophical sexuality is strangulated, sterile, and secretly self-indulgent are taken up by Mulligan, who offers Eglinton the parting-shot "Monsieur Moore, . . . lecturer on French letters to the youth of Ireland" (U, 9:1001-2). Mulligan makes the associative leap between contraception, or "conjugal onanism" as it was widely known, and masturbation with his parody of Yeats's 1903 "Baile and Aillinn." Yeats's poem would no doubt have delighted the Hermetists, since it celebrated lovers who transcended sexual union in favour of the higher spiritual affinities to be found in the afterlife, thus evading the decay and disappointments of sexuality:

Their love was never drowned in care
Of this or that thing, nor grew cold
Because their bodies had grown old.
Being forbid to marry on earth
They blossomed to immortal mirth. 50

Yeats's vision of love purified is drastically burlesqued by Mulligan, who transforms a virtue into a necessity. Yeats's "curlew cry" becomes a "purlieu cry," the lure of the brothel area Joyce later presented as Nighttown, where British "Tommies" like Privates Carr and Compton patrolled in search of women (U, 9:1043, 1044). Yeats's chaste lovers are displaced by a more incongruous pairing, Eglinton and the minor Revivalist Frederick M'Curdy Atkinson, bound together through a chastity produced not through restraint so much as incapacity. Atkinson's "wooden leg" and Eglinton/Magee's "chinless mouth" "that never dared to slake his drouth" are cruelly visceral metonyms of impotence, in ironic contrast to Yeats's lovers, whose chaste progress to the afterlife spares them somatic decay (U, 9:1147, 1149, 1150). Mulligan ascribes their purity not to spiritual strength, but to mental and corporeal weakness: "Being afraid to marry on earth / They masturbated for all they were worth" (U, 9:1150-1. With his spoof play for "the mummers":

--Everyman His Own Wife
or
A Honeymoon in the Hand
(a national immorality in three orgasms)
[U, 9:1171-6]

and its hero "Toby Tostoff, a ruined Pole," he extends his view of Eglinton's and Atkinson's sexuality to the whole of the Dublin theosophical-literary establishment (U, 9:1181). Mulligan's puns on "French letters," his parody of Yeats, and his spoof play for the Abbey together read the various cultural enterprises of the Revival as substitutions for and evasions of sexual creativity.

"Scylla and Charybdis" is thus structured to reveal pervasive cultural associations between a Revivalism intimately connected to theosophy and a secretive sexual deviancy. Stephen's Shakespeare theory allegorically interrogates theosophy to unfold provocative connections with a cautious, concealed, and claustrophobic sexuality; Mulligan's verbal play names this sexuality as masturbation, thus alluding to a highly contentious scandal which theosophy was trying to conceal. If, as I have argued, the Leadbeater affair is a submerged intertext in "Scylla," then the chapter parallels the repressive hypothesis which dogged contemporary occultism. The Leadbeater controversy was so eloquent to theosophy's critics because it articulated in a particularly obvious manner the sexuality suspected to lurk beneath theosophy's high-minded facade, exposing fault lines in that facade already evident in speculations over spiritualism's salacious seance manifestations, Blavatsky's marital status and illegitimate children, or even Annie Besant's former enthusiasm for contraception. This repressive dynamic is similarly encoded within "Scylla," where Mulligan's remarks explicate Stephen's Shakespeare monologue: his naming of masturbation as the dark side of theosophical chastity offers to retrieve what the movement's sexual politics buried. Masturbation in "Scylla" is thus deeply politically resonant, as Joyce uses Mulligan to signpost the implications of Stephen's monologue in that "history of cultural conflict" which Len Platt defines as central to "Scylla." If "Scylla" is an episode dominated, as Platt suggests, by snide attacks and bitter asides, by submerged hostilities and battles for cultural possession and prestige, then Stephen's assault upon theosophy's sexual pretensions is a particularly effective instance of this maliciously playful sniping.

4. The Sewer of Their Clique: Theosophy, Joyce, and Censorship

A further dimension to Joyce's sustained attack on theosophical sexuality can be glimpsed in the publication history of "Scylla." Writing to Harriet Shaw Weaver in October 1918, Joyce regretted "my book Ulysses appears to be giving you a great deal of trouble," adding "I am writing now the Hamlet episode" (i.e., "Scylla and Charybdis"). 51 Joyce linked Weaver's eventually thwarted attempts to find a British printer willing to risk the obscenity laws and print episodes of Ulysses in the Egoist with the composition of "Scylla," a connection which must have become increasingly evident as the months progressed. Joyce was composing the first draft of "Scylla" from late 1918 through to February 1919, and was simultaneously preoccupied with Ulysses's travails with the censors in the United States, where the novel was appearing in Margaret Anderson's and Jane Heap's the Little Review (JJ, 441-2). The Little Review was first suppressed in January, 1919, when John Sumner, chairman of the New York Society for the Suppression of Vice, forced the U.S. Post Office to confiscate the "Lestrygonians" number. It was confiscated under a law initiated by Sumner's predecessor, the notorious vice crusader Antony Comstock, which prohibited material deemed obscene from being distributed through the postal system. 52 Further confrontation with these moral vigilantes was to come: "Scylla" itself was confiscated in May 1919, "Nausicaa" in August, and in February 1921 social purity triumphed over Ulysses when The Little Review was convicted of obscenity. 53

Margaret Anderson had struggled with Sumner for some years, unwillingly attempting to appease the N.Y.S.S.V. in the May, 1919, issue of her journal, where she prefaced "Scylla" with a complaint over the January confiscation: "To avoid a similar interference this month, I have ruined Mr Joyce's story by cutting certain passages in which he mentions natural facts known to everyone." 54 However, while Anderson in America was excising "natural facts," Joyce was adding them: successive drafts of "Scylla" disclose a process of revision with a distinct flavor of Joycean bloody-mindedness. The revision process began in late 1918, when Joyce added specific theosophical details to The Little Review proofs; "allfather, the heavenly man. Hiesos Kristos, magician of the beautiful," "the life esoteric is not for ordinary person. O.P. must work off bad karma first" and "the name ineffable." 55 However, after "Scylla's" suppression in May 1919, Joyce's revising pencil is directed not so much towards theosophy, but more to the chapter's disputed sexual content. In November 1920, alterations for Sylvia Beach's planned edition exacerbate the allusions: "argive Helen" becomes "the wooden mare of Troy in whom a score of heroes slept," the passage describing various "incests and bestialities" which Anderson reluctantly removed is elaborated with "lesbic sisters, loves that dare not speak their name" (JJA 18: 182, 198). 56 Joyce adds also "between conjugal love and its chaste delights and scortatory love and its foul pleasures," and, more provokingly still, transforms Ann Hathaway into a faded, jaded tract-reading Puritan, a dangerous equivalent to contemporary purity censors: "She read or had read to her his chapbooks, preferring them to The Merry Wives and thought over Hooks and Eyes For Believer's Breeches and The Most Spiritual Snuffbox to Make the Most Devout Souls Sneeze" (JJA 18: 197). In late 1921, making final revisions to the Darentiere and Beach galley proofs in the aftermath of The Little Review's suppression that year, "Scylla" becomes more confrontational still. "Prologue to the swelling act" is added to Stephen's initial portrait of Ann Hathaway, "jailbirds with keyholes" to the "incest and bestialities" catalogue Anderson felt forced to censor (JJA 23: 248, 332). Furthermore, the final couplet of Buck Mulligan's Yeats parody, "being afraid to marry upon earth / They masturbated for all they were worth," only appears on this final revision: until then, Joyce avoided naming so explicitly the solitary vice for which theosophy had become renown (JJA 23: 340). Joyce's modifications emphasise, rather than tone down, the connections between theosophy and a disordered sexuality my chapter has outlined. His modifications can only be interpreted as deliberate defiance of the vice-crusaders which made Ulysses a target.

Two early poems, "The Holy Office" and "Gas From a Burner" offer a key to the role of theosophy in Joyce's strategy of provocation. Both responded to Joyce's early experiences of sexually-motivated censorship. "The Holy Office" was distributed after Harry Norman refused to take any more Dubliners stories for The Irish Homestead on the grounds that "his readers had complained," and after Joyce's essay "The Day of the Rabblement," attacking the Irish Literary Theatre for producing moral but mediocre Irish plays had been rejected by St. Stephen's, the University College magazine to which Joyce also unsuccessfully submitted the poem itself. "Gas from a Burner" commented on the collapse of negotiations with George Roberts to publish Dubliners: Roberts complained "the book's implication were anti-Irish and therefore out of keeping with an Irish publisher," whilse his printer George Falconer reneged on a promise to sell Joyce the sheets, burning them instead. 57 Joyce made his assault upon social purity censors more explicit still in his initial title, "Mr Falconer Addresses the Vigilance Committee." 58 The broadsides foreshadow Joyce's critical method in "Scylla" because both associate the threat of censorship with theosophy. "The Holy Office" singled out Dublin Hermetists Yeats, Roberts, Russell and Eglinton for particular censure; "all these men of whom I speak / Make me the sewer of their clique. / That they may dream their dreamy dreams / I carry off their filthy streams" (PSW, 98). Joyce's insistence that his "office of Katharsis" ventilated the kinds of somatic desires theosophists, with their "timid arses," feared to acknowledge was repeated in "Gas from a Burner." Ventriloquizing George Roberts, Joyce complained "I printed mystical books in dozens: / I printed the table book of Cousins / Though (asking your pardon) as for the verse / T'would give you a heartburn on your arse" (PSW, 104). Having assumed a corollary between Roberts's timid refusal to print Dubliners and his role as a staunch supporter of theosophical effusions, Joyce again insists that mysticism and bad verse masked a furtive, secretive, and dishonest sexuality: "In the porch of my printing institute / The poor and deserving prostitute / Plays every night at catch-as-catch-can / With her tight-breeched British artilleryman" (PSW, 105). Joyce's striking image of Maunsell and Company at night metonymically discloses the underbelly of its daytime reputation for moral probity. Sexual transgression hovers ratively on the porch of the establishment, under cloak of darkness: so Joyce suggests it lurks on the margins and in the shadows of Irish theosophy, a disavowed yet nonetheless present force.

These broadsides offer an intriguing perspective on "Scylla" because they emphasize how closely the young Joyce's early experiences of censorship were bound up with and attributed to Irish theosophical prudery. In "Scylla," written fifteen years after his flight from Dublin, Joyce addresses the contemporary censorship threat at one remove, returning to the Dublin of his youth, where theosophy was the strand of social purity ideology most influential in Irish literary circles. Theosophy stands for social purity censorship in "Scylla": its sexual timidity exemplifies a wider climate of artistic censorship with which Joyce's writings so often conflicted. Joyce's assault upon theosophy is thus a metonymic assault upon the censorious gaze. His revisions show how, during the months Ulysses was under this gaze, Joyce elaborated and amplified the provocative connections between theosophy and the obscene which have been outlined. His revisions make bold one of "Scylla's" themes; how sexual tensions disrupt the moralizing facade. Sexuality seethes through "Scylla," most obviously in Stephen's innuendo-laden Shakespeare theory which playfully subverts theosophy's doctrinal enthusiasms for brotherhood and ghosthood as states of inviolable purity. Stephen reappraises "universal brotherhood" as a stifling homosocial bond, ghosthood as a condition of sexual obsession, and "Scylla" slyly substantiates the theosophical resonances of his argument as Joyce encodes allusions to contemporary suspicions and scandals which theosophy was unable to contain. By anchoring theosophy to the kinds of scandals it unsuccessfully attempted to ignore, from spiritualism's associations with sexual hysteria to, more specifically, the long-running Leadbeater controversy, Joyce places moralizing discourses of sexual proscription upon an eloquent continuum. The prostitute on the porch of Maunsell and Company in "Gas from a Burner" is marginal, extraneous to the institution, in its shadow: in "Scylla," Joyce goes further, representing sexuality not as a force prudishly excluded from theosophy and clamouring at the door to be let in, but rather as intrinsic to it, submerged within a discourse of chastity which, as the Leadbeater case so devastatingly suggested, incites while it prohibits. Joyce's depiction of theosophy in "Scylla and Charybdis" argues that rather than repressing and controlling sexuality, moralizing propagandists covertly participate in and amplify the desires they deny. "Scylla" thus contributes to a sustained Joycean theme: the underground correlation between vice and vigilance.
