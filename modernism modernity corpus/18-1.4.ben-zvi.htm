Beckett and Disgust:The Body as “Laughing Matter”

Linda Ben-Zvi

Writers speak a stench.1 Franz Kafka (Diary Entry)

The farther he goes the more good it does me. I don’t want philosophies, tracts, dogmas, creeds, and ways out, truths, answers, nothing from the bargain basement. He is the most courageous, remorseless writer going and the more he grinds my nose in the shit the more I am grateful to him. He’s not fucking me about, he’s not leading me up any garden path, he’s not slipping me a wink, he’s not flogging me a remedy or a path or a revelation or a basinful of breadcrumbs, he’s not selling me anything I don’t want to buy—he doesn’t give a bollock whether I buy or not—he hasn’t got his hand over his heart. Well, I’ll buy his goods, hook, line and sinker, because he leaves no stone unturned and no maggot lonely. He brings forth a body of beauty. His work is beautiful.2

Harold Pinter

Written forty-five years ago to honor Beckett on his sixtieth birthday, Pinter’s assessment—like that of a number of critics at the time and since, but with more idiosyncratic verve—points to Beckett’s refusal to offer up any “goods” not based on the candid embrace of the specific, the material, which has been the central concern of recent studies of modernist writing. The “weasel under the cocktail cabinet” was Pinter’s early image of his own creative terrain and cultural/consumerist position; to describe Beckett’s he offers an even more disturbing and repellent image: the maggot under the stone. He is not talking about a body of classical beauty, whose exterior perfection creates aesthetic pleasure by hiding any hint of its animal nature, but rather a fleshly body shown eating, excreting, copulating, decaying, declining, and dying: those physiological stations of the cross described in all their vivid specificity and materiality in Beckett’s works. Nowhere is Beckett more the modernist writer of the quotidian than in his somatic descriptions. His people may be “falling to bits,”3 or “not half alive nor anything approaching it,” as Maddy Rooney describes herself in All That Fall; just lips, tongue, and teeth “whole body like gone,” at least not visible, as Mouth reveals in Not I.4 Or even dead, presumably, like the interned, hiccupping man in Play, yet still clinging to that tenacious trace of material existence.5 If there is a “truth” in Beckett’s writing it is the truth of the body as Lucky intones in his frantic monologue in Waiting for Godot: that “in spite of the strides of physical culture the practice of sports,” and “the labors of men,” “for reasons unknown,” “but time will tell,” the human animal “wastes and pines,” and “fades away,” “but not so fast.”6 A part remains—decrepit, decaying, disgusting; and it is this part that Beckett refuses to beautify, disguise, deny, or turn into metaphors or symbols that hide it or refine it out of existence. It is his refusal to do so that Pinter praises: the maggot under the stone bringing forth “a body of beauty.” How Beckett turns disgust into a form of beauty, the material into “laughing matter,” will be the focus of the following discussion. The idea of using disgust and disgust-eliciting objects to create beauty seems to be a denial of the Kantian claims that: (1) “nothing is so much set against the beautiful as disgust;”7 (2) that disgust is “the one kind of ugliness which cannot be represented in accordance with nature without destroying all aesthetical satisfaction;”8 and (3) that disgust as a “dark” and “strong sensation” “so categorically indicates something ‘real’ that it strains the distinction between ‘real’ and ‘Imaginary’—and therewith the condition for aesthetic illusion.”9 In recent years, however, modernist studies have attempted to rethink what constitutes beauty and taste (UF, 332–54).10 They have focused on what Sianne Ngai calls “ugly feelings,” including disgust, in order to formulate through them a new, relevant aesthetic that allows the “sentiments of disenchantment” (UF, 5) that color so much of modern life and lived experience to express themselves freely and powerfully. Ngai’s work is part of a growing number of studies on emotions, which—at least in the Anglo-American tradition, as philosopher Robert Solomon argues—were “typically dismissed as ‘mere subjectivity’ or, worse, as nothing but physiology plus dumb sensation,” but have recently become respectable, even “mainstream” areas of research, part of the recognition of the importance of lived experience.11

Unheard Laughter

Of the trend Solomon describes, the work of Martha Nussbaum is a case in point. A multi-hatted scholar—classicist, ethicist, philosopher of law—whose ethical stance derives from Aristotle, she has been one of the most prolific and visible proponents 683 of emotion studies, arguing that emotions have cognitive content and, therefore, are determinants of, and guides to, “how we might choose to live.”12 Based on her assumption that emotions are taught through stories, she has undertaken what she calls her “project”: using novels to supplement philosophy, since their stories can provide “positive” lived experience that may effect and alter emotions. Her ideal writer is Henry James whose works, she claims, offer a “projected morality” and an active “sense of life” (LK, 289). Given her project, goal, and author, it is not surprising that Nussbaum has a hard time with Samuel Beckett, whose novels seem the antithesis of James’s, proffering no apparent positivist ethic or “projected morality.” Yet she takes on Beckett and his novels at great length and with great philosophical gusto in her essay on “Narrative Emotions: Beckett’s Genealogy of Love.” The reason for her engagement soon becomes apparent. Beckett is to her what Milton’s bogey was to Virginia Woolf: an author who must be either vanquished or neutralized if her own work, her project, is to proceed. As Nussbaum indicates, Beckett’s “voices,” specifically those in the trilogy—from which she draws her epigraphs and central quotations—have long been “audible to me in the background of this work, speaking their subversive claims,” and need to be heard and “judge[d],” to determine how they might influence the project or even “end it” (LK, 288).

However, rather than actively engage Molloy, her novel of choice, critically listening to the nuances of its language, the forms and structures of its narratives and voices, the ways its rhythms, repetitions, imagery, and musicality create its world—the elements of literary criticism, which Robert Eaglestone faults her for omitting in her readings of James as well—Nussbaum launches, instead, into a long, vigorous attack against the disturbing emotions she finds embedded in the novel, wrestling Molloy and Beckett to the ground, the writer conflated with his characters throughout her analysis.13 Unfurling her interpretation, already set forth in the essay’s very beginning, she states that the complex emotions of desire, fear, guilt, shame, and disgust all stem from religious narratives related to “the mother’s body” (LK, 287), and that the “very despair,” both of the characters and Beckett, “gives evidence of their deep religiosity” and their inability “to go far enough outside the Christian picture to see how to pose the problem of self-expression in a way that is not shaped by that picture” (LK, 310). Nussbaum makes no reference to Kristeva’s analysis of abjection related to the mother’s body or the subversive possibilities of embracing it and thus rebelling against the constraints of the symbolic order. Any hint of what she calls a “radical undoing” (LK, 293), such as her interpretation of Moran’s final return to his garden, acceptance of nature, and attempts to distance himself from “religious meanings”—which Nussbaum sees as a potential “happy ending,” echoing Candide—is finally impossible because it would contravene Beckett’s “deeply religious sensibility at work” (LK, 309), a sensibility that denies human agency in the face of divine plan and that makes equally impossible any alteration of “the story of original sin, of the fear of God’s judgment, or the vain longing for salvation” (LK, 298). Nussbaum can only keep Beckett’s novels from swamping her humanist project by labeling them an individual aberration and comparing them to those of Virginia Woolf and James, for whom religion does not play “a role of

684 prominent importance.” Her consolation is: “Not all persuasive voices speak Moran’s language” (LK, 308). She is correct; disgust saturates Molloy. The entire compendium of sources of disgust are there: excrement; vomit; bodily fluids and emissions; soft, runny, slimy substances; bad smells; decaying bodies; illnesses; deformities; grotesque copulations; corpses; and death. And she is also correct when she argues:

There is a peculiar movement in Beckett’s talk of emotions . . . from a perception of human limits to a loathing of the limited, from grief to disgust and hatred, from the tragedy and comedy of the frail body to rage at the body, seen as covered in excrement. It is as if Beckett believes that the finite and frail can only inspire our disgust and loathing. (LK, 309)

But to assume as she does that what the reader finally experiences is only “the disgust at the disgust with human disgust” (LK, 305) is to miss completely how this disgust is registered through Beckett’s mastery of literary form, particularly through his use of humor. One of the best examples of it is found in an epigraph Nussbaum chooses for her essay, the embedded story near the end of The Unnamable that offers a lesson about emotions and love:

They love each other, marry, in order to love each other better, more conveniently, he goes to the wars, he dies at the wars, she weeps, with emotion, at having loved him, at having lost him, yep, marries again in order to love again, more conveniently again they love each other, you love as many times as necessary, as necessary in order to be happy, he comes back, the other comes back, from the wars, he didn’t die at the wars after all, she goes to the station, to meet him, he dies in the train, of emotion, at the thought of seeing her again, having her again, she weeps, weeps again, with emotion again, at having lost him again, yep, goes back to the house, he’s dead, the other is dead, the mother-in-law takes him down, he hanged himself, with emotion, at the thought of losing her, she weeps, weeps louder, at having loved him, at having lost him, there’s a story for you, that was to teach me the nature of emotion, that’s called emotion, what emotion can do, given favourable conditions, what love can do, well well, so that’s emotion, that’s love.14

Nussbaum’s only response to this passage is to argue once more that it derives from general disgust and “the thought of mother’s body” (LK, 287). She makes no mention of its humorous narrative details, use of alliterative repetitions (“the wars” and “weeping,” “love” and “lost”), numerous marriages that turn and turn about, trains coming and going, husbands dying and returning, the grotesque image of “a mother-in-law taking him down,” and particularly the narrative interjections such as “conveniently,” “well well,” and the twice-repeated “yep,” which point to the plodding, human desire and need to go on, even “worstward ho,” expressed more explicitly and frantically eight pages later in the famous conclusion to the The Unnamable.

I have gone on at length describing Nussbaum’s critique because the essay is an excellent example of the challenge that Beckett’s writing poses to those who seek stories displaying positive emotions and clearly delineated, realistic models of ethical behavior to follow. It also throws into sharp relief Pinter’s opposing argument that it is precisely Beckett’s remorseless and courageous revelation of the maggot under the stone—that 685 disgust-eliciting image of life gone to rot—that can bode forth a new notion of beauty. Pinter’s view reinforces Ngai’s contention that avant-garde writing in the modern period, by using “ugly feelings” like disgust, purposely expands and transforms the category of “aesthetic emotions” beyond “the potentially ennobling or morally beatific states” (UF, 6). While it offers “no satisfactions of virtue, however oblique, nor any therapeutic or purifying release,” it is important, she claims, because it succeeds in launching a “critical assault on art’s identification with beauty” as traditionally constituted (UF, 334). By stripping off the patina of perfection from the human body depicted in art, it reveals at last what has always been there but has lain hidden from view: the “folds, wrinkles, warts, ‘excessive softness,’ . . . discharge of bodily fluids (nasal, mucous, pus, blood) and old age . . . the criminal index of aesthetics, as ‘disgusting’” (D, 7).

Outing the body as Beckett does, uncovering its materiality, produces an effect similar to the unexpected punchline of a joke, as Simon Critchley notes: “A disjunction or incongruity is created between the way things are and the way they are represented,” producing a sense of “the world with its causal chains broken, its social practices turned inside out, and common sense rationality left in tatters.”15 Such incongruities may offer possibilities of a new sensibility, just as the revelation of the body may offer a different narrative of human experience and a different notion of what constitutes beauty. In terms of humor, Critchley continues, “the subject looks at itself like an abject object and instead of weeping bitter tears, it laughs at itself,” the laughter offering “a profoundly cognitive relation to oneself and the world” (OH, 102). He has a particular type of laugh in mind, the one described by Arsene in Watt: “the mirthless laugh,” “the dianoetic laugh.” “It is the laugh of laughs, the risus purus, the laugh laughing at the laugh, the beholding, the saluting of the highest joke, in a word the laugh that laughs—silence please—at that which is unhappy.”16 This is the laugh in Beckett that Nussbaum does not hear, its frequency pitched beyond the humanist decibel range. It is the laugh, derived from the “fundament”—joke intended—that echoes through Beckett’s writing: all he could manage “in such a place, and in such a world.”17

This laugh of cognition in response to the human condition of abjection is also found in practical experiments with the emotion of disgust. Paul Rozin, the clinical psychologist who has made the most extensive studies of the emotion, notes that participants in his experiments often react to disgust objects with laughter, displaying what he calls “benign masochism. . . . The civilized mind seems to take pleasure in the fact that it can rise above its animal instincts.”18 Winfried Menninghaus also mentions laughter in relation to this emotion:

The “vital sensation” of disgust might well be considered a property no less characteristic of humanity than the capacity to laugh—a property, in fact, that represents the negative complement of laughter. The sudden discharge of tension achieves in laughter, as in vomiting, an overcoming of disgust, a contact with the “abject” that does not lead to lasting contamination or defilement . . . Disgust, which undergoes a countercathexis (or sublimation), and laughter are complementary ways of admitting an alterity that otherwise would fall prey to repression; they enable us to deal with a scandal that otherwise would overpower our system of perception and consciousness. (D, 10–11)

686 Such “overcoming” Menninghaus claims, finds its expression not only in theory but also in the praxis of art, in which disgust achieves “a place and a specific ‘reality,’” offering the possibilities of a type of pleasure through the reclaiming of “what has been rejected and abandoned” (D, 11). He does not cite Beckett as the creator of such an art; Kafka, to whom he devotes one quarter of his book, is his model, a writer who, he claims, displays the disgusting and simultaneously hides it, through a willed “childlike innocence” and “purity,” that succeeds in creating a deceptive nonpresence predicated on a stench that is ultimately unsmellable (D, 227). Beckett might well have served as another example. However, the mirthless, di-anoetic laugh of his characters does not disguise disgust as Menninghaus claims that Kafka does. Neither does it cause that “sudden evaporation of expectation to nothing” that Kant described in his definition of laughter (quoted in OH, 5); nor does it bring, as Critchley claims, “elevation and liberation, the lucidity of consolation” (OH, 111), which is a way of creating a new occasion for laughter, a tendency Beckett abhorred and which distorts the purpose of his laughter. Unlike Nietzsche’s Übermensch Zarathustra, a superior laugher who laughs disgust away, becoming “the man without disgust . . . the overcomer of disgust” (quoted in D, 170; ellipsis in original) and thereby achieving, in Nussbaum’s words, “a joyful affirmative life for the spirit and the body together” (LK, 307), Beckett’s people—as well as his readers and audiences—remain painfully aware of the human condition: elevation is hardly their angle of vision. I agree with Laura Salisbury, who writes in a finely nuanced essay that Beckett’s humor does not mask his characters’ “rage and desperate desire for mastery” in his early works nor resolve the incongruity between “the hopeful sublimations of the mind” and the “desublimated,” irreducible body—an incongruity that is the basis of his comedy.19 Beckett’s laugh-producing incongruity is more of the trickster variety, verbal and structural sleights-of-hand which present bodily disgust and then play at jokingly dismissing, discarding, or denying it, but never successfully. His technique is hinted at by the speaker in The Unnamable, in lines immediately preceding the story of emotions that Nussbaum cites: “It’s evening already . . . I call that evening, I wish you could see it, I believe it this evening, it’s announced and I believe it, you announce, then you renounce, so it is, that helps you on, that helps the end to come.”20 Announcing and renouncing have applicability to Beckett’s process of mediating disgust through laughter. It allows him to have it both ways: now you see it; now you still see it but are told to forget it, eject it, laugh it away. However, just like evidence a lawyer may present to a jury, knowing full well that the presiding judge will disallow it and tell them to “forget what you’ve heard,” once disgusting images are described (and especially when described with great detail and gusto, as Beckett does)—be it the arsehole whose functionality Molloy extols, his descriptions of “lovemaking” with Lousse or Macmann’s with Moll—they remain so palpable that they cannot be dispelled into nothing by a laugh or the punchline of a joke. This is the nature of disgust objects; they evoke a power, even a fascination, depending on the sensory impact they generate for those who come into contact with them. Their evocative power is what provides the potential of “ugly feelings,” as Ngai suggests, to undermine traditional notions of beauty, and

traditional social, political, and cultural hierarchies as well. They can also, as in the case 687 of writers, undermine traditional forms of writing. Beckett is a case in point.

None of the recent books on disgust, all of which appeared in English between 1997 and 2004, in the wake of interest in the study of emotions in general, mentions Beckett.21 However, his work provides interesting case studies of how disgust with the body may be courageously and remorselessly depicted and, through laughter, endured. What follows is a very brief overview of a few general theories related to disgust which may suggest a theoretical framework for future Beckett studies on the subject.

Discussing Disgust

Darwin, in his 1872 book The Expression of Emotions in Man and Animal, included disgust as one of the thirty-two basic emotions. He describes it as a food aversion, as the French etymology of the word suggests, that functions to protect humans against what is “actually perceived or vividly imagined” to be harmful objects, this repulsion triggered by the senses of taste, smell, or touch, taste being the most significant.22 The incorporation of such an object elicits vomiting or gagging, as the body struggles to rid itself of the unassimilable and revolting material. In addition to his definition, Darwin includes two photographs of someone with facial contortions around the mouth and forehead illustrating disgust and its performative aspect. Beckett in 1932 had purchased a copy of On the Origin of the Species for sixpence, and reported to his friend Thomas McGreevy that he had “never read such badly written catlap”; so he most likely skipped Darwin’s later study, which formed the basis for the understanding of disgust for more than a hundred years.23

Though disgust “got off to a good start with Darwin,” Rozin claims, the study of the emotion in subsequent years lagged far behind that related to other aversive emotions such as fear, anger, or contempt, and even further behind the emotions of sadness and happiness. That its focus was originally assumed to be specifically food-related and thus limited and hard to assess scientifically may be part of the reason for the disinterest of scientists. Or it “may simply be,” as Rozin puts it, “that disgust is disgusting,” a position scientists and theorists of the emotion invariably state at the beginning of their studies, as if to excuse themselves for daring to immerse themselves in this questionable and repellent area of investigation.24

After Darwin’s brief discussion of disgust, fifty-seven years elapsed before the first study of the emotion: Der Ekel (On Disgust), written in German by the Hungarian-born phenomenologist and philosopher of ethical theory Aurel Kolnai and published in Husserl’s 1929 Yearbook of Philosophy and Phenomenological Research. It would take another seventy-five years before it was translated into English in 2004. Perhaps because the etymology of the word in German does not tie it so directly to food revulsion but rather to general repugnance, Kolnai did not limit his study to Darwin’s narrow definition. Building on Husserl’s theories of phenomenology and Brentano’s concepts of intentionality, he starts with the assumption that emotions have a cognitive basis,

688 that they “reach out toward their objects,” that intentionality is thereby determined by sensory reactions to these objects in the world, and that the sensations provoked thus shape the mental experience and affective responses to disgust objects.25 Kolnai’s study breaks new ground in two ways. First, his emphasis is less on the implications of disgust for the person experiencing it than on the centrality and delineation of the object eliciting it, apprehended through the senses, and consciously causing what Kolnai calls the “rejecting emotion.”26 Sensory observations are paramount for disgust, even more than for other emotions. However, breaking with Darwin, he omits the sense of taste as central to the experience of disgust, and replaces it with tactile contact with the object. Of his nine types of physically disgusting objects, only one relates directly to food; the others include putrefaction, excrement, bodily secretions, disease, and bodily deformations—all reminders of the vulnerability of the human body and its process of decline toward death. Moving toward Freud (about whom he says very little in his study), Kolnai argues that the sense of smell is the most important sensation giving rise to disgust. However, he does not describe the experience of disgust as a repressed action formation, as Freud does, whose function is to inhibit the acting out of unconscious desires; rather, he argues, disgust is a conscious, cognitive emotion activated by sensory stimulation and contact. Second, and more important, Kolnai expands the definition of disgust to include moral disgust, revulsion caused by acts done by oneself and others which are “not ‘ethical’ in a strict and narrow sense,” he explains, “but rather: mental or spiritual, albeit more or less with reference to ethical matters, in contrast to physical actions” (OD, 62). The first type of such moral disgust is what he calls “tiresome monotony,” which he connects to satiety, similar to the feeling experienced by excessive food intake and which, like an aversive food reaction, can produce nausea. He also compares this feeling of monotony to what one experiences when hearing the same joke over and over: that sense of, as he puts it: “enough of this joke! I can take it no longer”; or the experience “when one has been served daily with a specific kind of biscuit, or when one has rested in bed for too long” (OD, 63). His example of the latter is the eponymous antihero Oblomov in Goncharov’s novel, whose inaction becomes a form of sickness (OD, 64).27 All these examples, Kolnai argues, point to “a one-sidedly exaggerated kind of life . . . endlessly oscillating within an enclosed space” (OD, 63). He follows this type of moral disgust with that which arises from the opposite condition: “excessive vitality or vitality whose unfurling is misplaced” (OD, 65). In this broad category he lists acts of physical proximity; brutality; aggressiveness; forms of repellent, embarrassing, or overwhelming sexuality; specific behaviors such as mendacity, infidelity, and betrayal; and those elicitors of disgust related to what he calls moral softness (italics in the original), which consists of “inconstancy, flabbiness [and] weakness of character” as well as by excessive sentimentality, such as that found in Russian literature, which depicts “the soft gushing type of life which resists all solid formations, all discrimination” (OD, 71), or that of soldiers who question orders from superior officers (the latter example expunged from the 2004 English translation).28

Kolnai’s list supports the observation Walter Benjamin made that “an exact un-689 derstanding of a person would prove itself in the divination of his cardinal object of disgust.”29 For Kolnai, it would appear to be those elements in modern life connected with what he calls “frenetic rapidity and furious zest for life” (OD, 75), or life “always pregnant with death” (OD, 74) that are disgust-producing and dangerous, in human physiology as well as in societal organization, since they may be “taken over by inferior, grosser, vital forces, such as dictatorship” (OD, 75). At the same time he argues against the overcoming of disgust simply in the name of “a narrow-minded prejudice,” or driven by a desire to do away with the “multi-layered richness of tensions of the world” (OD, 88–89). Conservative, on the one hand, Kolnai is aware that to limit emotions, even those of disgust, is to impose the type of censorship and control attempted only by “pseudo-optimistic and immoralistic stupidity which begins to whine in protest at any more powerful accentuation of the negative” (OD, 89). His recognition of the power and the danger of disgust in relation to moral action echoes in certain ways Ngai’s contention that “ugly feelings” might be the means by which those outside political and social hierarchies might gain an entry, not through dictatorship but through the inevitable ferment and vitality of life forces.30

A second book I want to briefly discuss is The Anatomy of Disgust by law professor William Ian Miller which came out in 1997, before Kolnai’s English translation was available; that may be the reason he makes no mention of it, although the two studies have elements in common. Like Kolnai, Miller argues that disgust is a central emotion that contributes to the structure of various social, moral, and political orderings. But rather than offering warnings about the invasive and contaminating nature of disgust-evoking objects and their potential to subvert individual and societal institutions and morals—as Kolnai tends to do—Miller is more concerned with presenting phenomena related to disgust. He does so by first describing the physical meeting of the perceiver with the object of disgust perceived—“what it feels like to be too close to it, to have to smell it, see it, or touch it”— and then stepping back to reveal or, as he puts it, “map out” the pervasiveness of the emotion and “its first cousin contempt” in the construction of social and political practices and their consequences.31 Miller’s exuberant language reinforces his claim that feelings and emotions are linked to ways of talking about them, the linguistic as much a component of disgust as social and cultural phenomena. To prove his contention, he names the organic world from which disgust arises “life soup” (AD, 18), in which the having lived and the living, vegetative and animal, unite to make up the organic world of “generative rot”: “slimy, slippery, wiggling teeming animal life generating spontaneously from putrefying vegetation” (AD, 40–41). Should these images not be powerful enough, he also invokes Shakespeare, citing Hamlet’s depiction of the world as an “unweeded garden” and Lear’s awareness of the pervasive smell of mortality.

Miller’s central argument is that what causes disgust is the truth that humans struggle to avoid: their animality displaced onto animals that act as signs of this shared condition. However, “We needn’t have recourse to the animals for that reminder;” he writes: “all we need is a mirror” (AD, 50). “It is not that animal bodies decay, excrete, suppurate,

690 and die that makes these processes sources of disgust to us: it is that ours do” (AD, 49). Miller devotes chapters to specific sensory organs, to a history of disgust in myth and religion, and ends with an example of the ways disgust can be used as a potent analytical tool in literary exegesis. His example is George Orwell; the title of his chapter is “George Orwell’s Sense of Smell”; and his thesis is that “the very matter-of-fact Orwell is the twentieth century’s real poet of disgust, not the self-indulgent writers of sex and sexuality, sadism and masochism, pornography and criminality, not the likes of Genet or Bataille” (AD, 238). Disgust, for Orwell, he surmises, “was what stood in the way of socialism’s success” (AD, 239). Like Menninghaus’s chapter on Kafka, or Kristeva’s on Céline in Powers of Horror: An Essay on Abjection (1982), Miller’s on Orwell and Ngai’s section on Clarice Lispector illustrate the ways in which disgust can be a useful way of approaching modernist writers like Beckett. Those familiar with Beckett’s writing can immediately draw parallels between what Kolnai and Miller theorize and what Beckett practices, particularly in the trilogy, where disgust elements figure most directly. Molloy’s possible experience of love is set in a rubbish dump, where he is “bent double over a heap of muck, in the hope of finding something to disgust me for ever with eating.”32 Moran proudly indicates that his parenting involves “inclining” the mind of his young son “towards that most fruitful of dispositions, horror of the body and its functions” (M, 122). And Mahood is one mutation of an oozing, distended, number of voices, without shape or dimensions, that resemble in their lack of structure the very putrefaction and generative rot that elicits disgust, marked in Beckett by the constant slippage of pronouns culminating in the title The Unnamable, the word itself, implying something unspeakably vile or disgusting. In addition, Kolnai’s descriptions of the disgust emanating from ennui, the old jokes no longer funny, the old biscuits no longer appealing, and the sense of “life endlessly oscillating within an enclosed space,” could well be a parse of Endgame. Also Miller’s claim that disgust is linked to verbal expressions of the emotion, reinforced by Menninghaus’s assertion that it is through art that disgust gains its most powerful evocation, is demonstrated in Beckett’s potent language of the body. To prove his point Miller often discusses words that carry disgust charges, for example the disgust interjection “pah,” particularly Lear’s double “pahs” invoked by the thought of female genitalia and conception (AD, 163–66). These are echoed by Molloy’s description of his encounter with his mother:

Once I touched with my lips, vaguely, hastily, that little grey wizened pear. Pah. Did that please her? I don’t know. Her babble stopped for a second, then began again. Perhaps she said to herself, Pah. I smelt a terrible smell. It must have come from the bowels. Odour of antiquity. Oh I’m not criticizing her. I don’t diffuse the perfumes of Araby myself. (M, 16)

The second “Pah,” placed between mother and son, points to a perfect Beckett ambiguity: is it the mother disgusted by her son’s smell, or he by hers? The answer is only partially clarified in what follows.

These books on disgust as well as Disgust and Its Disorders, the first collection 691 of essays on the subject written by clinical psychologists including Rozin, are useful sources to frame discussions of disgust materials found in Beckett’s work. A number of studies by Beckett scholars already follow certain lines of investigation pursued in disgust research, although they do not mention the word or the theorists writing about it. The most striking example is Salisbury’s essay in which her argument about words that contaminate the body and cause something akin to a gagging or vomiting reflex “which brings up and reconsumes the same old words, the same old jokes” follows directly the reaction to the feared contamination by disgust objects,33 and relates to Kolnai’s theory that monotony brought on by unending repetitions is disgust-provoking, as well as Miller’s claims that the ways we talk about disgust “help constitute the very thing spoken of” (AD, 164). Another study that bears resemblance to studies of disgust is Ulrike Maude’s Beckett, Technology, and the Body, in which she focuses on the “inescapable materiality” of bodies in Beckett and the “repugnance,” and “extreme revulsion” felt when encountering their “abject or monstrous nature” which “unsettles us not only because it threatens to be our own, but because . . . it is always already our own.”34

The Work of the Abscess

In recently published archival materials it is also possible to trace the ways in which Beckett discusses embodied experience in details that give rise to the emotion of disgust, generated by the body in decay. Judging from the first volume of Beckett’s letters from 1929 to 1940, written when he was between 23 and 34 years old, the abject body so central to his writing was “always already his own”; “there from the beginning, the deteriorating body, only grown worse with age.”35 Like James Knowlson’s Beckett biography before it, the letters document Beckett’s history of illnesses, including poor eyesight, weak lungs, panic attacks, racing heart, various types of cysts, and skin eruptions. To find examples of corporeal suffering and bodily disgust, Beckett didn’t have to look far.36

However, what the letters provide that the biography cannot is context: where in a particular letter, for instance, Beckett’s complaint or description of illnesses is located and its relative weight in relation to other matters he touches on in the same correspondence. The letters reveal that rather than focusing on his maladies, Beckett, at least up to 1940, tends to mention them near the end of a correspondence, often as quick asides, given less space and weight than discussions of his reading, writing, and desire to be published. What is surprising, however, and revealing—even shocking for those not familiar with Beckett’s writing—is the frequent references he makes in his correspondence to disgust-evoking bodily functions and emissions including vomit, feces, turds, sanies, pus, farts, and semen, all used descriptively, with flourish and feeling, to describe his writing and the reactions of others to it. It is apparent that at the very beginning of his career, Beckett was already creating not only “a syntax of weakness,”37 as he later described his writing, but also a syntax of disgust drawn from

692 experiential knowledge of his body’s weaknesses and diseases. He was turning sensory disgust matter into linguistic forms that enlivened his letters, as they would give vitality and what Pinter called “beauty” to his body of work. For example, he rechristens the publishers Chatto and Windus “Shatupon & Windup” (L, 1, 211). Elsewhere, the act of publishing is likened to “trying to sell . . . a load of manure or a ton of bricks” (L, 1, 19), a letter to McGreevy compared to “dégueulade” (vomit) (L, 1, 223), the completion of two new poems described as “a double-yoked orgasm in months of aspermatic nights & days” (L, 1, 87), other poems dismissed as “three turds from my central lavatory” (L, 1, 42), and an imagined publication “the Beckett Bowel Books, Jesus in farto. Issued in imperishable tissue . . . 1000 wipes of clean fun. Also in Braille for anal pruritics” (L, 1, 383). Beckett uses such images of bodily functions for self-deprecating humor—“It’s an ill cyst blows nobody any good.” He also employs them to explain the serious and profound struggles he is going through in order to create works that have the materiality and the immediacy of bodily functions. “I find it more and more difficult to write and I think I write worse and worse in consequence,” he confides to McGreevy. “But I have still hopes of its all coming in a gush like a bloody flux” (L, 1, 159). The word “flux,” connoting abnormally copious flow of blood or excrement from the bowels or other organs, indicates Beckett’s desire to capture the same embodied experience in his writing found in disgust-provoking emissions, the internal body displayed and bodied forth in prose. In a more somber key, he describes his desire to bring forth in writing “the work of the abscess” (L, 1, 134), that pus that does not try to pass itself off as anything more than material matter, like those “sanies”—fetid pus mixed with serum or blood—that he chose as the titles of two poems. “Why is the spirit so pus-proof and the wind so avaricious of its grit,” he asks McGreevy, admitting “I’m in mourning for the integrity of a pendu’s [hangman’s] emission of semen,” found in “Homer & Dante & Racine & and sometimes Rimbaud” (L, 1, 134–35). In addition to the letters, a similar preoccupation by Beckett with bodily functions and decay and a similar frustration about how to bring them forth in his writing are apparent in another important archival source from this same period: the three “Human Wishes” notebooks.38 These notebooks contain Beckett’s research done in preparation for what he called his “Johnson Fantasy”: his projected four-act play on the unrequited love Beckett believed Dr. Samuel Johnson felt for Mrs. Hester Thrale, wife of his patron. Notebook 1 records details of Beckett’s voluminous reading on the lives of Johnson and Thrale, but by notebook 2 the focus shifts; rather than Johnson in love, it is Johnson’s decaying body, specifically his illnesses—many of which Beckett was suffering from at the time—that preoccupy Beckett, who even copied Johnson’s autopsy, translating its medical terms from the Latin. By notebook 3, Beckett widens the scope, collecting material on those who shared Johnson’s home, all but one elderly and of failing health: “the lame, the blind, the sick, and the sorrowful,” as Mrs. Thrale described them.39 Beckett’s struggles over several years to bring forth his Johnson play out of this extensive research is recorded in letters to several of his correspondents; so too his belief in the project, “Which will enable me eventually to see how it coincides with

the Pricks, Bones and Murphy, fundamentally and fundamentally with all I shall ever 693 write or want to write” (L, 1, 569). On May 21, 1941—two days before his resistance cell was broken and he had to escape from Paris with his partner and later wife, Suzanne Deschevaux-Dumesnil, to Vichy France—Beckett finally reported to George Reavey that he had completed a fragment of the first act of Human Wishes. It never went further. What is apparent, however, in the short scene that remains, and what makes it important in the Beckett canon in general (as he predicted), and in the study of disgust and laughter in particular, is the stench of corporeality in decay it emits and the mirthless laughter it provokes, both already discernible in this first Beckett play and both to become more prominent in his future drama and fiction.

Corpses, those most potent reminders of what Kolnai calls “our own affinity to death” (OD, 77), are absent presences in the parlor setting. There is that of Henry Thrale, Johnson’s patron, who died on April 4, the date Beckett chose for the action (listed correctly by Cohn in Just Play but misidentified as the fourteenth in the Disjecta version); Oliver Goldsmith, dead seven years earlier on the same date; and Johnson himself, whose death Beckett knew was soon approaching. Like Waiting for Godot, which it anticipates, those waiting engage in mundane activities to fill the time—Mrs. Williams “meditating,” Mrs. Desmoulins “knitting,” Miss Carmichael “reading,” and Johnson’s beloved cat Hodge “sleeping—if possible.”40 Beckett, as in Godot, leavens the potentially dark scene by injecting mirthless humor: for example, he has the young Ms. Carmichael, brought there “to enliven the house,” read from Jeremy Taylor’s Rules and Exercises of Holy Dying. He also has the older women employ what they call their “colloquial powers” to avoid the subject at hand: death and the dead or capital “D-EA-D,” as the blind Mrs. Williams shouts. In another example of dark humor he has them vociferously debate “who is merry”: the consensus, “No one, not even the cat.”

Johnson never arrives; the only entrance is made by an inebriated co-habitator Dr. Levett, who silently crosses the stage, emits a giant hiccup that almost knocks him off his feet, and exits, performing the first Beckettian pantomime, one that elicits the first Beckettian metatheatrical exchange: Mrs. Williams’s “Words fail us,” is followed by Mrs. Desmoulins’ “Now this is where a writer for the stage would have us speak no doubt.” Speak they do, not about Levett, but about whatever humor is still to be squeezed out of the subject of death, through an iteration of the names Beckett had been collecting in his research, perhaps his inside joke about the moribund work.

Why Beckett was unable to complete the play is a much debated question; implausible thesis, excessive “intellection” (Beckett’s word), his move to Paris, the war—all have been suggested. Another reason may have been his inability to sustain the contrary pulls between disgust with decrepitude and death and the dark laughter he employs to address the subjects. As Critchley reminds us, “the succession of tension by relief in humour is an essentially bodily affair” (OH, 7). Having expanded it through what amounted to an entr’acte pantomime by the hiccupping Levett in the first ten minutes of the play, Beckett, in this first attempt at drama, may not have known where to go next. Once “the rules and exercises of holy dying” had been trumped by “the rules and exercises of holy living,” practiced by his characters, Beckett had his answer to

694 the question he posed in a letter to McGreevy: “Why two books, Holy Living & Holy Dying, when one would have done the trick. Surely the classical example of literary tautology” (L, 1, 172).41 Whether Beckett intended to continue work on Human Wishes in Roussillon, where he stayed during the war years, is not certain; but life there must have seemed so far removed from the world of Johnson, or even Dublin where he did his research, that he may well have found the play no longer relevant. Watt was more in keeping with the tenor of the times. What is clear is that after the war; after his volunteer work in St-Lô with the Irish Red Cross, helping to rebuild the destroyed French city; and after his eventual return to his life in Paris, where he heard firsthand accounts of the horrors of occupation and the decimation of the Jews in the Holocaust, including several who were close friends, Beckett turned to a different kind of writing, directly based on embodied experience rather than on research.42 “I began to write things I felt,” he told Charles Juliet.43 Feelings and emotions, particularly negative ones like disgust, became the driving forces in the first-person narratives of the trilogy, which use language of, what Critchley calls, “uncanny ordinariness” (VLAN, 184). That “recumbent scurrility of polite society” that Mrs. Williams demanded gives way to the recumbent scurrility of the body. Beckett once told Juliet, “nothing is sayable,” referring to the possibilities of meaning, explanation, or even the expression of indignation.44 But what is left is what is “showable”: what the body is—its inner workings and outer emissions; what the body does—eat, urinate, defecate, vomit, bleed, and procreate; and what the body knows—its own corporeal destiny and ultimate finitude that it cannot escape or transcend, the “how it is” to be human, embodied, and aware of all that means. But how to show what can’t be shown, particularly that “ungraspable” nature of death and human finitude? One way is a variation on what I called earlier “now you see it; now you still see it,” that is, to create what Critchley calls “narratives of that which narration cannot capture” (VLAN, 188), or, I’d add, in theatre to present dramas of that which drama cannot present. Beckett does this by displacing the impossibility of the telling onto the imaginative plane, through stories, chronicles, or narratives that stand apart from the “real” but are in fact the only “real” possible. “I’m too frightened this evening to listen to myself rot, waiting for the great red lapses of the heart, the tearings at the caecal walls, and for the slow killings to finish in my skull, the assaults on unshakable pillars, the fornications with corpses. So I’ll tell myself a story,” the first-person speaker in “The Calmative” reports.45 The frame is created by the voice; the other is the figure in the story, the two conjoined in that humorous but significant game of pronoun slippage which Beckett engages in, causing, as Ruby Cohn says about the story, a “transmutation of experience into fiction.”46 A variation of this technique is to present bodies visibly “falling to bits,” that would emit bad odors, were we close enough to smell them; and that would feel flabby, soft, and repellent, were we close enough to touch them. And then have them speak of the weather, their food, their honeymoon, happy days, past loves, lost loves, and no love, details of everyday life often embedded in stories, narratives, or chronicles that they “make up” as we watch them fade before us and disappear.

This show of telling, that claims not to be telling, is related to that humor predicated 695 on incongruity, which Critchley describes, “with its unexpected verbal inversions, contortions and explosions, a refusal of everyday speech that lights up the everyday” (VLAN, 187). The greatest incongruity lies in the fact that disgust with the human body becomes the material for laughter, the abject body becoming “the object and subject of humour . . . estranged, alien, weakening, failing” (OH, 51). When characters laugh, they laugh “wild amid severest woe,” as Winnie reminds us in Happy Days.47 They know, as Nell—another of Beckett’s wise women—tells us in Endgame: “Nothing is funnier than unhappiness.”48 The greatness of Beckett is that he shows what we don’t want to see and usually turn from in disgust—our own finitude—and finds ways for us to face this truth by prodding us, through the very evocation of disgust, to laugh.

It is this dark, unhappy laughter responding to mortality that Nussbaum misses. I want to conclude by going back to Nussbaum, not her analysis and dismissal of Beckett discussed earlier, but to her 2004 book Hiding from Humanity: Disgust, Shame, and the Law. This time she makes no references to Beckett or literature; her intent is to argue against using societal disgust concerning a crime as a measure for determining the severity of the punishment, a position Miller champions, although not directly in his book on disgust. Her claim is that disgust may “play a useful function in our current daily lives,” since “perhaps we cannot easily live with too much vivid awareness of the fact that we are made of sticky and oozy substances that will all too soon decay.”49 But the emotion, she finds, is ultimately dangerous. Her first argument, a recapitulation of her charge against Beckett, is that disgust’s “thought-content is typically unreasonable, embodying magical ideas of contamination, and impossible aspirations to purity, immortality, and nonanimality, that are just not in line with human life as we know it” (HFH, 14). Her second and more central argument is that disgust has historically been used by individuals and societies to deny their human nature through transferring offending sources of disgust onto others—for example Jews and homosexuals—comparing these others to odious animals including cockroaches, vermin, and rats, and arguing that they give off repugnant smells, and have the potential to contaminate individual bodies and the body politic. Such displacement, she argues, establishes social and political hierarchies created by the normative community to denigrate and stigmatize others, denying their shared humanity. Nussbaum’s aim in unmasking disgust, therefore, is to create “a society that acknowledges its own humanity, and neither hides us from it nor it from us; a society of citizens who admit that they are needy and vulnerable, and who discard the grandiose demands of omnipotence and completeness that have been at the heart of so much human misery, both public and private” (HFH, 17).

Given her aim and her analysis, I would argue that Beckett should be her ideal writer; he fits her requirements perfectly. It is hard to think of a writer who more insistently shows what it is to be human, “neither hides us from it nor it from us,” and who consistently over his long career has illustrated the human neediness, vulnerability, and lack of omnipotence, which she calls for. And nowhere is his humanity more apparent than in his humor, which, as Critchley writes, “recalls us to the modesty and limitedness of the human condition, not for tragic-heroic affirmation but comic acknowledgement”

696 (OH, 102). Unlike Nussbaum’s writer of choice this time, Walt Whitman, who sings the body electric, Beckett realizes that this poor frail, dying body is not singing matter, but laughing matter, and through this laughter the human condition is made bearable.
