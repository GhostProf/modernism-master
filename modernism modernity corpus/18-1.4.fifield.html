Samuel Beckett: Out of the Archive,

An Introduction

Peter Fifield

A generation younger than the other great modernists, Samuel Beckett (1906–1989) is currently playing catch-up. His afterlife has barely begun, even while the publication of W. B. Yeats’s Collected Letters continues, the final volume of Virginia Woolf’s essays has just been published, and James Joyce has just escaped from European copyright, promising a flurry of new, commercially driven editions.1 By contrast, the first volume of Beckett’s Letters was published only in 2009, and the second, reviewed here by John Pilling, in 2011. His German diaries, written in 1936–37, are to be published in 2015, and a previously unpublished short story is imminent from Faber. Beckett was a copious compiler of reading notes, and many fragments of unknown material are flowing rapidly “out of the archive.” This special issue itself contains a translation of an essay by Beckett not available for sixty years, and fragments of another formerly accessible only to scholars and francophone readers.

Beckett is an archivist’s author. Storing his proofs, drafts, diaries, and notebooks for up to sixty years, the author has left textual remains that document his development as a writer in great detail. These are held in numerous libraries—public, academic, and private—but are also steadily becoming available to a wider audience via publications in print and online.2 Their use in academic research is burgeoning, and images of manuscripts are now included even in the standard readers’ editions of his works. Equally, he thinks in archival forms. Krapp’s Last Tape depicts someone who records, stores, and orders information for regular recall; Malone Dies is born, the reader infers, from the notebook left by its dead protagonist; and Rough for Radio

With this in mind, this special issue assays the value of the archive: it ponders the merits of archival remains, including offcuts, first drafts, notes, proofs, correspondence, and assesses the worth of the research that this material allows. Beckett reliably deprecates the value of his writing. The letters to Thomas McGreevy play ceaselessly on the execrable and excremental worth of his literary efforts, and a letter of August 1931 to Charles Prentice implies that a well-crafted work would be nothing more than a well-turned turd, “twice round the pan & pointed at both ends.”3 Similarly, the later lament that “every word is like an unnecessary stain on silence and nothingness,” suggests something spilt or leaked, as if writing were an effluent.4 But the ferocious negation that resounds in Beckett’s oeuvre so troubles the basis of value that the valueless becomes valuable, failure becomes success, and each might, at any moment and recursively, turn into its antithesis. Failing may be bettered or worsened—if they were ever different options—and there may be no more economical way to spend words than by wasting them. The same ambiguity that sees failure as a form of success characterizes the value of the archive itself. For the archive is prefigured in one of Beckett’s most iconic—and thus valued—props: an ashcan. An archive is a dumping ground, where unpublished documents and unwanted scraps reside while final drafts and unabandoned works fill bookshelves, newspapers, examinations, and readers. The archive’s content is that which the author casts off as inessential, whether sold on or donated. But the process of archiving—of taking these resources in—is primarily one of a reversal very much like those found in Beckett’s work. The archive sees by-products and exuviae as its special treasure. Responding to Beckett’s statement to Jacoba van Velde that “There are two moments worthwhile in writing; the one where you start and the other where you throw it in the waste-paper basket” the archivist must add a third: when it is fished back out.5 For the archive has the eye of all those disdained and now defunct trades: the night soil man, the rag-and-bone man, or even the soap maker, who would collect ash for lye. What is no longer of use to the maker is of particular use to them. If archival resources are increasingly the fertilizer—more waste made good—with which modernist scholarship is grown, as is clearly the case in Beckett studies, they are frequently boosted by authorial intervention. Cast-offs are often re-authorized by having been donated, and the archive’s own authority bestowed by the author who directs his or her false starts to a good home. For the archive is a space of such investment that nothing there can be valueless. To be there is to be made special. This transforming function belongs both to the author and the archive, the manufacturer and purchaser, as Beckett knew and played with. If we consider some of the more unusual manuscripts held by archives of his work, it seems he quite understood what it means to collect artifacts and manuscripts. Scribbles on whiskey labels and disposable tablecloths are particularly special: archival showpieces where Benjamin’s aura can still get a good foothold. In an affirmation of values akin to that in the Nobel committee’s often criticized citation for Beckett, which celebrates an author who transforms wretchedness and suffering into comfort and redemption, the low is again made high by the author’s touch.

A sign of authorial power, this transmutation is also the archive’s weakness or limit. 675 For the archive cannot hold anything worthless. Its positivity infects every item it touches, so that even the author’s outright rejections are made valuable by inclusion. The publication of Eleutheria in 1995, which followed a heated argument between Barney Rosset and Beckett’s literary executor Jérôme Lindon, shows the dangers of leaving traces of rejected works that can be treasured and thus transformed.6 We will soon see publication of the short story “Echo’s Bones,” written at the request of Charles Prentice at Chatto and Windus. When he saw the fruits of his suggestion that Beckett add another five or ten thousand words to the manuscript of More Pricks than Kicks, he thought better of it and asked to publish it in its original form. In 1933 Prentice wrote to Beckett fearing that, quote, “‘Echo’s Bones’ would, I am sure, lose the book a great many readers.”7 Almost eighty years later, this unwanted padding is to be a centerpiece of the new editions of Beckett’s work.

There is a personal aspect to this, which is itself an effect of the archive’s peculiar pressure. Authorial intention has regained much of its lost prestige, together with related scholarly methods in abeyance during literary theory’s sovereignty. The doodles, annotations, emendations, letters, diaries, and notes are enlivening not only Beckett’s texts, but Beckett himself. Together with biographies and memoirs, these documents mean that Beckett is more alive now than he has been since he was actually alive. This impression is further cultivated by the drip-feed of unpublished texts into the public domain, which has seen him become newly prolific. While scholars have known about these texts for a long time, the moment of academic discovery in a reading room is now being restaged on a much larger scale. To the casual observer Beckett is writing again, and he does his work in the archive.

Of course such claims are all rhetorical flourish in the sense that the recent publications contain old works whose preparation and authorization—by the estate rather than the author—has finally come to fruition. Yet Beckett increasingly resembles one of his own moribund characters: unable to say anything of untainted value, unable to say anything wholly without value, unable simply to stop writing or, more properly, á la The Unnamable, unable to prevent the language saying him. If there has been a shift from theory-heavy readings to detailed historical, archival, and biographical interpretations, the activity’s very vigor and detail has meant that Beckett, more than ever, is a function of his discourse.

The academic’s wish to produce criticism matched to an authorial taste appears more compelling than it was when Beckett was alive. An incident related by Siegfried Unseld of Suhrkamp Verlag, Beckett’s German publisher, indicates both a certain interpretative stance and Beckett’s response to it. When entertaining his authors at a dinner organized in Beckett’s honor, he recalls how

Adorno immediately developed his idea about the etymology and philosophy and the meaning of the names in Beckett. And Adorno insisted that “Hamm” (in Endgame) derives from “Hamlet.” He had a whole theory based on this. Beckett said “Sorry, Professor, but I never thought of Hamlet when I invented this name.” But Adorno insisted. And Beckett became a little angry . . . In the evening Adorno started his speech and, of course,

pointed out the derivation of “Hamm” from “Hamlet” (adding that “Clov” was a crippled “clown”). Beckett listened very patiently. But then he whispered into my ear—he said this in German but I will translate it into English—“This is the progress of science that professors can proceed with their errors!” (DTF, 479)

As interesting as Adorno’s stubbornness is, perhaps more intriguing is Beckett’s anger at a perceived “error.” In a similar context, Knowlson records that when “a reviewer had claimed that [Catastrophe] was ‘ambiguous.’ ‘There’s no ambiguity there at all,’ he said angrily” (DTF, 680). It is no surprise to hear that an author prefers his own reading of a work; but that a counter-reading, with either its misplaced conviction or an overkeen ear for indeterminacy, actually provokes anger, marks a particular critical climate.

A fear of riling the absent author might have a role in the changing scholarly culture, but more convincing are the examples drawn from the oeuvre. Beckett’s early essay “Dante . . . Bruno.Vico..Joyce” sees him hesitate before the conceptual cocktail to be made of Joyce’s “Work in Progress.” He asks “Must we wring the neck of a certain system in order to stuff it into a pigeon-hole, or modify the dimensions of that pigeon-hole for the satisfaction of the analogy-mongers? Literary criticism is not book-keeping.”8 His preference for facts rather than grand synthesis is made clear in an entry in his German Diaries made in 1937. He writes, “what I want is the straws, flotsam, etc., names, dates, births and deaths, because that is all I can know . . . Rationalism is the last form of animism. Whereas the pure incoherence of times and men and places is at least amusing” (DTF, 244). These facts and figures mark the frontier of Beckett’s epistemological skepticism, so that if the artist’s task is to exploit “impotence, ignorance,” the academic’s is to exercise a positively Joycean mastery in detailing this weakness.9 Beckett’s sympathy “with a fine Dostoievskian contempt for the vulgarity of a plausible concatenation” has undoubtedly discouraged those who would tie a text’s every loose end. Consequently, critics are increasingly resisting what Beckett, quoting Finnegans Wake, called “the temptation to treat every concept like ‘a bass dropt neck fust in till a bung crate,’ and make a really tidy job of it.”10 In particular, the proliferation of various critical and facsimile editions, annotations, a concordance-type companion, and a chronology, speak of a growing reluctance to leap to a narrative exposition.

But this hesitation is not the property of a particular method so much as of a reader attuned to Beckett’s attempt to find a “form that accommodates the mess” (BCH, 219). Adorno may have been embarrassingly headstrong, as Unseld suggests, but he was acute in identifying the tension between the details of Endgame and their cumulative significance. He roots the action in detritus: “forlorn particulars that mock the conceptual, a layer composed of minimal utensils, refrigerators, lameness, blindness, and the distasteful bodily functions. Everything waits to be carted off to the dump.”11 Here the starkly material waste of bodies and tools—rather than Beckett’s preferred facts and figures—resists the elevation of the mundane into abstract explanation. Adorno’s “forlorn particulars” are close to Murphy’s “demented particulars,” indicating a shared resistance to conceptual summation.12 Adorno, in attending to Beckett’s refuse, may not be faithful to the author’s own interpretation; but he does show undeniable fidelity

to the texts themselves. After all, as the archive itself teaches, no waste is worthless in 677 the Beckettian universe.

The questionable standing of conceptual schemata in Beckett criticism dovetails with the current pluralism within the broader field of modernist studies. Theory’s decline is itself presaged in Beckett’s skepticism towards contemporary philosophy’s power as literary criticism. Dismissing the relevance of existentialism to his work, he made clear that his writing is intrinsically, rather than incidentally, literary: “I wouldn’t have had any reason to write my novels if I could have expressed their subject in philosophic terms.” Beckett not only refuses Sartre or Camus, but philosophy’s explanatory function, as “There’s no key or problem” (BCH, 217) to be found or solved in his writing. But this same doubt resists the grip of any single methodological successor. Indeed, despite the rapid expansion of “genetic criticism” in the preparation and study of Beckett’s unpublished writings, the majority of new work combines detailed manuscript study with philosophical, psychological, contextual, biographical, historical, and performance-based approaches. The variety of critical forms mirrors the newly apparent diversity of Beckett’s writing. In the face of diaries, letters, notebooks, and drafts, the insufficiency of a single interpretative stance is more obvious than ever.

The act of critical recovery—making something of the cast-off—is actively encouraged by Beckett, and is shared by his strongest readers. Hence, Adorno’s essay sees the play pulled taut between the meaningful and meaningless, form and mess, as the play’s thoroughgoing negativity is partially recuperated as an apt response to the holocaust. We return to the ashcan when we recall that Shivaun O’Casey, who wanted to stage the prose text From an Abandoned Work, was advised by Beckett to portray an archivist’s act of salvage:

Moonlight, ashcan a little left of centre. Enter man left, limping, with stick, shadowing in paint general lighting along. Advances to can, raises lid, pushes about inside with crook of stick, inspects and rejects (puts back in can) an unidentifiable refuse, fishes out finally tattered ms. or copy of FAAW, reads aloud standing “Up bright and early that day, I was young then, feeling awful, and out—” and a little further in silence, lowers text, stands motionless, finally closes ashcan, sits down on it, hooks stick round neck, and reads text through from beginning, i.e. including what has been read standing. Finishes, sits a moment motionless, gets up, replaces text in ashcan and limps off right. Breathes with maximum authenticity, only effect to be sought in slight hesitation now and then in places where most effective, due to strangeness of text and imperfect light and state of ms.13

What little drama there is here derives from the interplay of the recited story and its blunt substance. The condition of the manuscript, as well as the conditions of its recitation, are to provide the distinctive rhythm of its delivery, which is to be hesitant rather than fluent; itself imperfect. The appropriate character for the Beckettian scenario is one who is prepared to rifle through the bins to find the right item, but also ready to throw back the grubby manuscript when the job is done. This both resembles and surpasses the task of the archivist. For if, as suggested above, Beckett’s textual waste finds a good home in the archive, such a home is one that won’t treat waste with the respect it deserves, but with a whole lot more.

678 In storing the old, the archive deepens and renews the familiar works of the modern canon. Indeed, in recovering and redeploying fragments with the aim of reinvigorating its culture, archival scholarship partakes of the archetypal modernist gesture: keeping the modern new. The essays in this issue embody the most important research in the study of Samuel Beckett. Ironically, in doing so they contest the depiction of “life” so often observed in his work: a continuous, unvarying, unremitting, and unenlightening activity. “The sun shone, having no alternative, on the nothing new,” begins Murphy. But in the world of Beckett scholarship, something new is indeed transpiring, leading to a more nuanced and inventive understanding of a major modernist author.14
