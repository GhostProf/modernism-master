“Scarcely Disfigured”: Beckett’s Surrealist Translations

Pascale Sardin and Karine Germoni

Samuel Beckett, a Passeur of Surrealism

Samuel Beckett was in his mid-twenties when he translated a great many texts (prose, poems, essays, articles) by the French Surrealists. His translations were made in a formative period when the “young Irish poet,” as he was called by Parisian expatriates, was the author of only a few poems, critical essays, and reviews, and when, working in the wake of James Joyce, he was straining to find a voice of his own for his first novel, Dream of Fair to Middling Women. Between 1929 and 1932, Beckett translated professionally from French for several journals and anthologies. He rendered a great many Surrealist texts into English for Transition, the literary review edited by Eugene Jolas that was critical in linking British, American, and Continental avant-gardes.1 He also translated works for Nancy Cunard’s Negro Anthology (1934), including texts by André Breton, Benjamin Péret, René Crevel, and the Belgian poet and film-maker Ernest Moerman.2 Most important, however, were his translations for the “Surrealist Number” of This Quarter, a special issue co-edited by Edward Titus and André Breton in September, 1932 (vol. 5, no. 1).3 It contains the greatest concentration of Surrealist texts translated by Beckett, especially literary texts; more than fifty out of the volume’s two hundred pages are assigned to him via the lapidary phrase: “rendered into English by Samuel Beckett” (TQ, 76, 98, 128, 165).4

Owing to the fact that dyed-in-the-wool surrealists have consistently refused to explain themselves in any but their own publications, which circulate chiefly in France and some of which have become exceedingly rare, and that, with some few inadvertent or expedient exceptions, they have declined to contribute to non-surrealist publications in France or elsewhere, their work has been practically inaccessible to English and American readers. After much persuasion, and on condition that an issue of This Quarter were exclusively devoted to surrealist work, M. The issue is rather eclectic. It contains a number of theoretical articles, poems signed Paul Éluard, Tristan Tzara, and Benjamin Péret, and pieces of “prose expérimentale” by René Char, René Crevel, and Marcel Duchamp. It further comprises drawings and engravings by Max Ernst, Man Ray, and Yves Tanguy. It contains excerpts from André Breton’s two Surrealist manifestoes, alongside the first English rendering of the screenplay for Un Chien andalou (1929), by Luis Buñuel and Salvador Dali (TQ, 149–157). It also has a long section entitled “Surrealism and Madness,” entirely “rendered into English by Samuel Beckett” (128); it consists of extracts from Nadja, the poetic novella first published by Breton in 1928, and a section of “Possessions” excerpted from The Immaculate Conception, a work co-authored by Breton and Éluard in 1930.5 Beckett also translated “L’Union libre” and “Le Grand secours meurtrier” (72–75), two poems taken from Breton’s Le Revolver à cheveux blancs (Revolver with White Hair, 1932); an extract from Breton’s Les Champs magnétiques (Magnetic Fields, 1920), entitled “Factory” (75), as well as a passage from his Poisson soluble (Soluble Fish, 1924); and another from Le Clavecin de Diderot (Diderot’s Harpsichord, 1932) by René Crevel (158–165). Finally, Beckett also transposed into English a dozen poems by Paul Éluard, written in free verse or in prose (86–98), among which “L’Amoureuse” (“Lady Love,” TQ, 86), “À perte de vue dans le sens de mon corps” (“Out of Sight in the Direction of my Body,” TQ, 86–87), and “À peine défigurée” (“Scarcely disfigured,” TQ, 87).6

Beckett’s translations were well received by Edward Titus, the editor of the review, who singled them out in his introduction: “We shall not speak of the difficulties experienced in putting the material placed at our disposal into English, but we cannot refrain from singling out Mr. Samuel Beckett’s work for special acknowledgement” (TQ, 6). Such satisfaction seemed to have been shared by Beckett, who confides to Tom McGreevy in October of 1932—just a month after the issue was published—that he has recently written to Nancy Cunard, saying, “it was always a pleasure to translate Eluard and Breton,” albeit in the context of hoping to receive further commissions for translations.7 A few months later, in a letter dated 12 December, 1932, he welcomed the idea of translating pieces by Breton and Eluard which Nancy Cunard was to send him: “I think I’ll have real pleasure transposing them.”8

Nevertheless it is more likely Samuel Becket did not feel satisfied with most of these

translations, as is evident from his response when he was asked in the mid 1970s to

prepare a new and augmented collection of his Collected Poems (1961).9 Apart from his own poems from the 1930s and 1940s, he chose to include only seven poems by 741 Paul Eluard, eight “Maximes” by Sébastien Chamfort, and his translations of Guillaume Apollinaire’s “Zone” (done in 1950) and Arthur Rimbaud’s “Bateau ivre” (commissioned by Edward Titus and done in 1932). An editorial note explained: “Other translations, many unsigned, made during the thirties with which Mr. Beckett is unsatisfied, exist in old magazines, but he is not willing to see them reissued in book form.”10 Significantly, Beckett did not include translations of André Breton’s “L’Union libre” or any other pieces by the leader of the Surrealists. From the many works contained in the 1932 “Surrealist Number,” only the seven by Eluard were kept: “L’Amoureuse,” “À perte de vue dans le sens de mon corps,” “À peine défigurée,” “Seconde nature,” “La vue,” “L’univers-solitude,” and “Confections.”

It is notorious that Beckett recurrently felt dissatisfied with his works in general and his translations in particular. Nevertheless, the omission of Breton’s texts may also signal Beckett’s lack of interest in, or even aversion to, some productions of French Surrealism. One work, for example, he dismissed as “miserable rubbish” in a letter to Tom McGreevy.11

A Long-lasting, Paradoxical Influence

A good many arguments militate against Beckett’s connection with Surrealism, whether concerning literary form or content. In an interview with Lawrence Harvey, Beckett denied his work had anything to do with “Surrealist formlessness.”12 James Knowlson believes that Surrealist influence on Beckett was strongly overshadowed by that of James Joyce in the late 1920s and early 1930s: “He could not feel close to the Surrealists, largely because . . . they were distinctly cool, if not actively hostile, to Joyce . . . But Beckett shared in the thrilling atmosphere of experiment and innovation that surrounded Surrealism.”13 Still, it is quite unlikely that he shared the Surrealists’ optimism. André Breton’s faith in “l’amour fou” and the power of words and imagination to change the world is very far from Beckett’s sarcastic vision of romantic relationships in Dream of Fair to Middling Women, written during the same years. Such pessimism is also found in poems like “Alba,” written at the time and modeled on the Provençal dirge or complaint that Beckett knew well.14

Both Breton, in his first Surrealist Manifesto, and Beckett, in his Dream of Fair to Middling Women, condemned the traditional novel. In his preface to the “Surrealist Issue,” Breton urged that the “realist attitude . . . seems to me antagonistic to all intellectual and moral loftiness” and damned the “super-abundance of novels” (TQ, 20), while Beckett mocked the “chloroformed world” of Balzac.15 Yet what results from their common critique of realism is quite different in nature. While Breton writes meditative novels based on real-life experiences, clinical descriptions of events, and dreamlike evocations of places, as in Nadja, Beckett opts for the more parodic, ironic, and rhapsodic mood of Dream of Fair to Middling Women. Indeed, a form of mockery aimed at the Surrealists can be detected in the very title. Even if, in the words of John

742 Pilling, the “tongue in cheek title” of Dream “is adapted primarily from Tennyson’s poem ‘A Dream of Fair Women’” and can be traced back to several other English-language sources, it also resonates with two main Surrealist preoccupations—dreams and women.16 With this derogatory title Beckett negates the Surrealist belief in the “omnipotence of dreaming” evoked by Breton in his introduction to the “Surrealist Number” (TQ, 17), a topic upon which he later enlarges: “I believe in the future transmutation of those two seemingly contradictory states, dream and reality, into a sort of absolute reality, of surreality, so to speak” (TQ, 22).17 What’s more, the “fair to middling women” of Beckett’s Dream are very far from the idealized vision of women as expressed in Breton’s “L’Union libre” or Eluard’s “La Dame de Carreau,” the latter a prose poem which Beckett translated as “The Queen of Diamonds” (TQ, 89–90), one in which the Surrealist obsession with women and dreams is closely linked. Years later, in Eleutheria (1947), Beckett will openly mock the Surrealists’ erratic fancy when he has Violette Krap decorate her drawing room with barbed wire “after having spent a whole afternoon under the spell of the Surrealist Exhibition.”18 On the other hand, it seems more difficult to decide whether the short story entitled “Dante and the Lobster,” with its evocative title redolent of the unexpected conjunctions valued by Surrealists, is to be interpreted as a positive or negative nod in their direction. For in his 1934 article on “Recent Irish Poetry,” Beckett very explicitly singles out the poetry of Denis Devlin and Brian Coffey because it shows the influence of “Corbière, Rimbaud, Laforgue, the surréalistes and Mr. Eliot, perhaps also . . . Mr. Pound,” before mentioning as well the “grace of Eluard.”19 More importantly, it is possible to establish points of contact between Surrealist and Beckettian aesthetics, in much the way as Breton voices “a desire to establish between [his English and American readers] and [himself] the greatest possible number of—if only fugitive—points of contact” in his introduction to the “Surrealist Number” (TQ, 10). Traces of Surrealist images and texts certainly surface in the Beckett corpus, as has been well documented.20 The setting of Happy Days, for example, is reminiscent of the last sequence in Un Chien andalou, which is described thusly in the “Surrealist Number” of This Quarter: “Now we see a limitless desert. In the centre and sunk in the sand up to their breast-bones, the cyclist and the girl, are seen to have become blind and ragged and are being roasted by the sun and eaten by a swarm of insects” (TQ, 157). Verbal echoes of Surrealism are also present in the Beckettian canon: in the first verse of “La Vue” by Paul Éluard, Beckett found the word viduité (TQ, 92), which he was to thematize years later in Krapp’s Last Tape (1956). Surrealism may also have had a more long-lasting influence on Beckett, who shared with Breton and his followers a rejection of rationality and a fascination for the unconscious and the intricacies of the psyche throughout his oeuvre.21 Consider the definition of surrealist practice that Breton issued in his first manifesto, “pensée parlée” (or “spoken thought” as it is translated in This Quarter), specified as “a monologue poured out as rapidly as possible, over which the subject’s critical sense claims no share” (TQ, 15). 22 Surely it reminds us of the form assumed by the monologues in The Unnamable, Not I, or That Time. Beckett “Transposing” Breton and Éluard

When compared with his contemporaneous writing, Beckett’s translations for This Quarter shed light on the paradoxical and complex relationship he had with French Surrealism. The tension between literal translation and interpretation is very strong and illuminates a translator’s position vis-à-vis the work he is translating. Henri Meschonnic draws a contrast between “translation,” which foregrounds the “text to be translated,” and “adaptation,” which foregrounds “the ideas of the translator about language and literature,” ideas that invade the translated text, whether “voluntarily or not.”24 Beckett, as we will see, makes “adaptations” containing alterations that are numerous and rather systematic.

The first noticeable “transposition,” to use Beckett’s term, concerns matters of rhythm and punctuation. The seven poems by Eluard that Beckett included in his Collected Poems in English and French are written in free verse; they contain a few punctuation marks, especially commas and periods respectively at the end of lines and stanzas. Sometimes Beckett keeps the original punctuation, but more often than not he simplifies it drastically by deleting many commas and periods, almost as if abiding by the principle uttered by Breton when introducing the “Surrealist Number”: “Punctuation of course necessarily hinders the stream of absolute continuity which preoccupies us” (TQ, 19). In a way, Beckett’s rendering discloses a form of faithfulness to the spirit rather than to the letter of the text. Such is the case in “Scarcely Disfigured,” in which the closing period is omitted, as it is also in “Second Nature.” In “Scene” and in “Confections,” all the periods that close stanzas disappear.25 This stylistic trait can only remind us of Beckett’s own poems. Indeed, except for “Enueg I” and “Dortmunder,” no poem collected in Echo’s Bones and Other Precipitates, Beckett’s first book of poems published in 1935, contains any punctuation.26 Beckett adapts Eluard’s verse to his liking, liberating it from the constraints of French versification, much as Apollinaire had already done in 1913 in “Zone.”

This typographical infidelity is striking in “Lady Love” (TQ, 86), the translation of “L’Amoureuse,” as Beckett deletes all the original punctuation marks of this twelve-line poem:

Speak when I have nothing to say

The deletion of punctuation complements the way Beckett reinforces the doubling effects and parallelisms already found in the original text. In stanza one, he repeats “hair,” where Eluard uses the anaphoric possessive pronoun (“les miens”). At line seven, there is a semantic inversion as Beckett adopts “She will never close her eyes” to represent “Elle a toujours les yeux ouverts.” This modulation enables Beckett to end the verse on “eyes,” echoing the word’s first occurrence at the end of line three. Likewise, the translation of “lumière” (l. 9) as “day” enables the poet to create a rhyme with “say” (l. 12). Adding the rhythmic “and” at the beginning of lines nine and eleven underscores an effort to make Eluard’s poetry more dynamic. Again this is a trait found in Beckett’s own poems, as in the fourth stanza of “Enueg I” (written in 1931):

Then for miles only wind and the weals creeping alongside on the water and the world opening up to the south across a travesty of champaign to the mountains and the stillborn evening turning a filthy green manuring the night fungus and the wind annulled wrecked in the wind.27

These repetitions in Beckett’s translations of Eluard’s poems modify our perception of them in several ways. Emphasis is laid on the apparent simplicity of Eluard’s poetry, sometimes dubbed “easy” (“facile”), a term adopted by Eluard himself and plainly opposed to the elaborate poetry of Paul Valéry.28 Beckett, though adapting Eluard to his own taste, is to a certain extent even more Eluardian than the French poet himself. This may explain why one critic of Beckett’s surrealist translations feels that Beckett “captures the strange understatement of Eluardian Surrealism.”29 Beckett translates Eluard as a poet and musician by reinforcing the rhythm and subtly changing the piece’s musicality. It is not surprising that Beckett uses the verb “transpose” to evoke his rendering of Eluard and Breton (“I’ll have real pleasure transposing them”). Indeed one of the senses of to transpose is a musical term referring to the reproduction of a 745 piece in a different key, by raising or lowering a pitch from that originally designated.

Another striking alteration has to do with register. Surprisingly enough (since this new trend seems to go against the simplifications we have just analyzed), Beckett also makes both Eluard’s and Breton’s poetry more formal, imparting an archaic flavor to the pieces, and sometimes a more affected style. The systematic recourse to the archaic forms thee, thou, thine in Beckett’s translations is striking; it appears in his renderings of Breton’s “Soluble Fish” (TQ, 76), Eluard’s “Out of Sight . . .” (TQ, 86–87), “Scarcely Disfigured” (TQ, 87), “Do Thou Sleep” (TQ, 90–91), “Universe-Solitude” (TQ, 95–96), and “Confections” (TQ, 98), as well as the “Attempted Simulation of General Paralysis” (TQ, 123–125) from the Immaculate Conception.

This archaizing impulse may have been inspired by Ezra Pound.30 Pound translated Provençal texts of the twelfth and thirteenth centuries using an archaizing approach, and it is likely Beckett had read some of Pound’s translations when he was studying troubadour poetry, from which he borrowed several poetic forms: the enueg (a song of grief or “ennui”), the alba (a morning song), and the serena (a night song). He may also have encountered others via his reading of Dante, for the Provençal poet Arnaut Daniel, one of Pound’s favorites, is quoted in the Divine Comedy (Purgatorio, XXVI). Pound and Beckett also shared an interest in the alba form.31 In his translations, as Lawrence Venuti notes, “Pound increased the play on the signifier, cultivating inverted or convoluted syntax, polysemy, archaism, nonstandard dialects, elaborate stanzaic forms and sound effects–textual features that frustrate immediate intelligibility, empathic response, interpretative mastery.”32 One feature that contributes an archaizing note to Pound’s translations of Provençal poems is his use of the –st or -th inflections of the second and third person singular forms of verbs, typical of early modern English; other features include the contraction “o’ver” and the interjection “lo,” features also found in Pound’s Cantos as well. This archaizing trend, far from contradicting the modernist injunction to “make it new,” actually contributes to it by inventively departing from ordinary language.

The Surrealists, not unlike the modernists, were very much interested in primitive art, and in its figurative corollary, archaism. André Breton’s “postulate of the necessity of change,” which he reasserts in This Quarter (TQ, 11), stems in part from a primitivist view, for the Surrealists “looked upon themselves as continuing a trend of anti-rational exteriorizing of the subconscious that went back to the alchemists and included Rabelais, the Marquis de Sade, Gérard de Nerval, Lewis Carroll, Baudelaire, and Rimbaud.”33 Georges Bataille, for instance, took an interest in medieval “fatrasies,” nonsense poems from the thirteenth century, several of which he published in modernized French in the sixth issue of La révolution surréaliste (1 March, 1926). Nevertheless, Beckett’s recourse to archaic forms might still seem a questionable choice when translating contemporary Surrealist poems otherwise nearly devoid of archaic turns of phrase and demanding an aesthetic response based on immediacy and spontaneity.34

Setting aside the archaism added by Beckett with “thou” at the beginning of lines three to five, “Scarcely Disfigured” differs strikingly from “A peine défigurée” on a syntactic level. When Beckett condenses three lines (ten, eleven, and twelve) into two (“Mightiness of the love that lovable/Starts up as a bodiless beast”), the syntax becomes more complex and the poem takes on a slightly convoluted tone: because of the repetition of the word “lovable” (at the end of lines nine and ten); because of the use of the poly-semantic verb “start up” to translate “surgit”; and because the gerund “smiling” can be related to “the poorest lips” or to “thee.” All this blurs the passage’s meaning. What’s more, the intrusion at the end of the poem of a Beckettian allusion adds a further deviation. In “Head of hope defeated” for “Tête désappointée,” the phrasing recalls a line in Dante’s Divine Comedy, a work of well known significance to Beckett, as it was translated by Henry Longfellow: “Thou art the living fountain-head of hope.” (In the Divine Comedy, Saint Bernard is praying to the virgin Mary).35 Consequently, Beckett’s version is more hermetic and thereby less immediately understandable than Eluard’s, which clearly goes against the poetics of immediacy inscribed in the very title of the collection from which the poem is taken, La Vie immediate (Immediate Life). Beckett has indeed “disfigured” Eluard’s prosody and poetic voice by these small changes; they make the poem more contorted and fragmented, as if he were trying to imitate the “convulsive beauty” articulated by Breton in Nadja (1928) and L’Amour fou (1937), a phrase that epitomizes an irrational, uncontrollable, and unstable aesthetic experience.

Here, the imprecatory tone contrasts with the absurdity of some images or phrases, such as “les renégats . . . à cheveux de poule” (“renegades . . . with goosy locks”) or “des inscriptions comme ci comme ça” (“with so-so inscriptions”). And if the unusual imagery originates in someone’s unbridled imagination, as is supposed to be the case in automatic writing, the syntax of the piece hardly comes out as being anarchic. In his version, Beckett uses the archaic form “thou,” “thy,” and “thine” in all instances where “tu,” “ta,” or “ton” appear, and he also inserts several archaic interjections such as “lo!” and “yea,” while adding the interjection “oh” and one exclamation mark where there is none in the French. He has recourse to the formal modal “shall” (in place of the French simple future) and even to the subjunctive mood (in “And if the shuttlecocks be as big as houses”). He renders the closing image of the “mains gelées” by the metaphoric phrase “arctic hands”—which is reminiscent of Rimbaud’s “arctic flowers” in “Barbare” (Les Illuminations). He transforms a number of nouns into compound ones, such as “pan-significations.” All these deviations from the source text impart a more affected tone to the target text. Consider the following passage:

And lo! it is thou the bandit, the bandit, ah! assassin, thou water bandit thou sheddest thy knives in mine eyes, thou art then quite pitiless, radiant water, lustral water that I cherish!

It is as if Beckett were recreating a carnivalesque version of Elizabethan tragedy. Note also the five occurrences of the word “hand” at the very end of the piece; there are only three occurrences of “mains” in the French. The result adds still more emphasis to the final lyrical climax. The emphatic effect produced by all these deviations gives a good sense of Beckett’s mocking attitude towards Breton’s so-called automatic writing.41 The bombastic conceitedness of the translated text throws into relief the lack of spontaneity and the fabricated character of its source.

Likewise, a more precious convoluted syntax, added to the archaic, poetic register chosen by Beckett in “Attempted Simulation of General Paralysis Essayed” (his translation of “Essai de simulation de la paralysie générale,” taken from The Immaculate Conception), heightens the mannerism of the piece, especially the recourse to the old-fashioned “thou” form, which once again “contradicts the spontaneous flow of words.”42 Let us consider the last paragraph of this text alongside its translation by Beckett and a more recent, less stilted translation by Jon Graham:

To a certain extent this “simulation,” in which the woman stands for the universe, reads like a euphoric “hymn to love,” in which the desire for union is both physical and spiritual.44 It reflects the exalted status assigned to heterosexual love being devoutly worshipped by Breton and Eluard.45

Interestingly, Beckett mistranslates the alternating pronouns in “une multitude de bras qui veulent m’enlacer moi à genoux entre tes jambes et tes bras pour t’enlacer.” Beckett renders: “a multitude of arms that want to clasp me kneeling between thy legs and thine arms to clasp me.” Compare with Graham: “a multitude of arms that want to clasp themselves around me kneeling between your legs and your arms to clasp you.” The image of a sprawling octopus-woman clasping the man on her lap in a maternal, voracious embrace is tampered with by this subtle mistake in Beckett’s version. The French text contains an idea of reciprocity in its fusion of man and woman, an idea clearly explicated in Graham’s translation. In Beckett’s version, the lack of any such idea may well point to his very different vision of women as conveyed in Dream of Fair to Middling Women, in which it is Smerry who rapes Belacqua, while Belacqua can only think of ways to escape. Indeed, a fusion of man and woman is impossible in Beckett’s world, while in the Surrealists’ view women are idealized and seen as mediators towards the surreal.46

Yet this piece, a sort of desacralized Song of Songs, is also imbued with much humor and sarcasm, to which Beckett responds more favourably.47 The vision of the woman running on all “thirteen full legs” and all “empty legs” compounds that of the “multitude” of “swaying” arms, and is very entertaining. Playfulness is also discernible in the oddness of the noun “locomotives,” which turns the man’s body into a hybrid machine-body. The overall image is in keeping with the Surrealists’ vision of the woman as a fusion of opposites, but, at the same time, the woman is both magnified and ridiculed.

750 Even though Beckett drops the modal shall here, contrary to what takes place in the text previously analyzed, he again has recourse to the archaic forms thou, thine, thee, thinkest, and art. Again, he heightens the text’s carnivalesque dimensions, imbuing it with a mock air of religious solemnity, magnifying the satirical and humorous tone rather than the lyricism and eroticism it also contains. Similarly, in his version of “Attempted Simulation of Mental Debility,” Beckett proves very responsive to the irony of the two writers who paint bourgeois values in a satirical light on the pretext of simulating the delirium of a madman. Society and conformity to social norms are critiqued here. By introducing additional rhetorical effects, Beckett heightens the text’s pompousness and verbosity. Indeed, in the following passage the repetition of the noun merit and the reversal of canonical word order underline the stupidity of the commonplace on offer:

Il faut en effet que l’Ordre de la Chevalerie n’ait pas de bon sens pour reconnaître du mérite là où il n’y en a pas.(IC in OC, 1, 851)

The Grand Master of the Order of Chivalry must indeed be deficient in common sense to discern merit where merit there is none. (TQ, 121; our emphasis)

In another sentence already saturated with clichés, Beckett adds still one more to emphasize the speaker’s anti-intellectualism:

Quand on est capable comme mon frère aîné, qui a concouru plusieurs fois dans les journaux, de décrocher la timbale contre des bacheliers ès lettres ès sciences, on peut dire qu’on a de qui tenir. (IC in OC, 1, 851)

When, as was the case with my brother, who entered for newspaper competitions on more than one occasion, one is capable of carrying off the palm in the teeth of bachelors in arts and Science, then it is a case of a chip off the old block and no error. (TQ, 121)

All in all, if some of Beckett’s translations appear to be literal renditions (especially in Eluard’s case), slight differences in punctuation, rhythm, syntax, or diction prove Beckett adapted or transposed, as he said rather than translated literally. His translations are independent, very personal pieces. In that, they are in keeping with the prevalent modernist vision of translation as the creation of an autonomous work, a vision epitomized by Ezra Pound’s own way of translating.48 Indeed, as we saw, Beckett often “beckettized” the Surrealist texts he put into his native tongue, archaizing the register, dispensing with punctuation, adding erudite references—in other words rendering the pieces more pedantic and modern at the same time. We further saw that these deviations from the source texts revealed Beckett’s mocking attitude toward some Surrealist pieces and methods of composition such as automatic writing. Indeed Surrealism is the target of Beckett’s irony; Beckett is possessed by the “demon of irony,” like Belacqua in Dream of Fair to Middling Women (DFM, 86).

Nevertheless, despite this critical attitude, and despite the autonomy of Beckett’s 751 surrealist pieces, it might be more accurate to say that this translator actually only “scarcely disfigured” them. Indeed, in proceeding with such deviations from the French, Beckett proved he had not only very well grasped the violence of the social critique underpinning the Surrealist project, but also that he felt in tune with its ethical stance. As shown in his translations, Beckett shared the Surrealists’ antireligious and antibourgeois feelings which he applied, discreetly but efficiently, to his translator’s art. In that, he was quite in tune with the pieces’ debunking and revolutionary spirit, while still disagreeing with the Surrealists’ vision of women and their instrumentalization as mediators to the Surreal.49
