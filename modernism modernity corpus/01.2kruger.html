Placing "New Africans" in the "Old" South Africa: Drama, Modernity, and Racial Identities in Johannesburg, circa 1935

Loren Kruger

On Sunday, 3 June 1934, African artists and intellectuals, American Board Missionaries, liberal whites associated with the Joint European/African Council, and an educated, predominantly African audience gathered at the Bantu Men's Social Centre (BMSC) in downtown Johannesburg for an Emancipation Centenary Celebration, commemorating the abolition of slavery in the British Empire in 1834. 1 The Celebration included speeches by American missionaries and leading Africans, such as R. V. Selope Thema (editor of Bantu World) and A. B. Xuma (later President of the African National Congress [ANC]); performances of Handel's Messiah; hymns composed by Europeans, Africans, and Americans; and "Negro spirituals." It culminated in a "dramatic display," the subject of which was not the emancipation of slaves in the Empire, but rather the lives of slaves in the United States. Incidental music was written by Reuben Caluza, known for composing and collecting Zulu folk-songs, and the text by his cousin Rolfes Dhlomo. The evening's performances were organized and directed by Herbert Dhlomo, Rolfes's brother and a dramatist in his own right.

Drawing on the variety format of Edwardian pageants and the music hall, the performance also borrowed freely from the speeches of Lincoln and Frederick Douglass and the portrayal of the "life of the lowly" in Uncle Tom's Cabin. The "dramatic display" itself, including a slave auction, labor in the fields, and the coming of freedom performed by adults and children in chorus, highlighted the pathos of the lot of both slaves and ex-slaves: "the suffering of the American Negroes on the slave market, in the cotton fields and at home, until the joyful news of the liberation"; 2 and the "feelings of joy and relief, which the right ending of the struggle for freedom had brought." 3 Although these reports in Bantu World and Umteteli wa Bantu, the major African papers, give us no details about the music accompanying these scenes, it is likely, given Caluza's international reputation as a composer of nationalist hymns and Zulu folksongs (recorded by His Master's Voice in London in 1930), that it drew on his "sorrow songs" about the dispossession of South African blacks. 4 Likewise, the dramatic interpretations of key speeches such as the Gettysburg Address, delivered by Griffiths Motsieloa, leader of the minstrel troupe the Darktown Strutters, and the record company Gallophone's answer to His Master's Voice, probably resonated with the audience differently from the praise heaped on British and American emancipators in the African press.

Politically charged as this event certainly was, it had nonetheless no single political meaning. Permitted by the neocolonial state and applauded by white liberal paternalists, it was enthusiastically received not only by the members of the BMSC but also by the more diverse--and more disaffected--audience at the Eastern Native Township performance. This occasion thus eludes the familiar dichotomy between white colonial hegemony and the oppressed black masses (or between high English culture and clearly identifiable working-class practices), a dichotomy that has long pervaded South African criticism and underlies key formulations of current postcolonial theory. 5 In this schema, members of the New African elite, the small intermediary class of clergymen, teachers, and other professionals who organized events such as the Emancipation Celebration, are distinguished above all by their attachment to European civilization and thus by their alienation from the masses of black people, whether this alienation is read as the irredeemable result of their colonial assimilation and distance from popular African cultural expression 6 or as a measure of the obstacles to their attempts at representing either African concerns in general or the interests of an emerging working class in particular. 7 This retrospective judgment carries some weight in present-day debates about South African cultural politics but it does not fully acknowledge the fluidity in 1934 of these class and ethnic affiliations, or the volatility of New African identification with "European civilization." To be sure, the editor of Bantu World joined the American Board Mission in urging his readers to beware of the unChristian tactics of the Bolsheviks and to "honor the great emancipators" such as Livingstone or Lincoln, whose examples show the way to "rescue [the world] from the thraldom of nationalist and racial passions." 8 He did so, however, with the knowledge that other New Africans were disputing European claims to represent Christian civilization on the grounds that the South African state's treatment of most of the population, a century after emancipation, betrayed Christian ideals and universal rights.

The imperial decorum of the missionary school syllabus, which supplemented the great tradition of Shakespeare and the post-Romantic poets with the likes of Kipling and John Buchan, was in its turn challenged by American and African American popular culture ranging from abolitionist melodrama and political rhetoric to a popular mixture of jubilee singing, ragtime, jazz, and minstrelsy, all of which reached a wide African audience in venues that ranged from the BMSC to the slumyard and dance halls frequented by city workers. 9 New Africans saw in the metropolitan, modernizing impulse of the New Negro an alternative, on the one hand, to neocolonial retribalization and the essentially anti-urban complexion of English culture in the mission schools and, on the other, to the allegedly degraded forms of jazz and minstrelsy, which the African elite saw as corrupting the abaphakathi (the people in between--neither assimilated to "European" modernity nor any longer at home with indigenous custom). 10 Even in the 1930s, after the New Negro star had faded, the distance travelled by African Americans from slavery to visible (if uneven) participation in American life--as well as the distance still untravelled--provided a crucial point of reference for criticizing the curtailment of civil rights for black South Africans.

Despite their identification with the African American elite, the "talented tenth," in W. E. B. Dubois's phrase, New Africans were compelled to acknowledge the appeal of African American popular culture, including jazz, if only in response to African American assertions of pan-African affiliation. Thus, musician and critic Mark Radebe uses a Garveyite term, "Africans in America," to pay homage to African American inflections of popular American music and to "the enormous influence of Bantu rhythms on the world's music." 11 New African negotiation of these African American currents thus complicates any received dichotomy between British imperialism and African resistance, between imported art and indigenous practice or, indeed, between modernity and its apparently premodern, "savage" other.

New African exploration of the territory marked out by these oppositions was both contradictory and productive. It involved often passionately inconsistent appropriation of English cultural artifacts, American popular entertainments, and indigenous traditions, in which the transformation (but not the destruction) of "Africanness" through colonization formed the basis for a thoroughly modern hybridity. In the performance of hybridity--in drama, pageants, and national commemorations--any distinction between a degraded missionary literature "produced under imperial licence" 12 and the irony, parody, or "sly civility" of postcolonial (post)modern writing is thoroughly blurred. 13 Postcolonial criticism, as exemplified by the influential anthology, The Empire Writes Back, acknowledges this blur when it uses "the postcolonial to cover all culture affected by the imperial process from the moment of colonization to the present-day" (EWB, 2). 14 It also admits that the difference between "licensed missionary writing" and "postcolonial appropriation" is not simply a matter of the mastery or appropriation of literary form but rather the effect of the colonial institution of literature, in which a narrow range of normative models is promoted and in which publication, patronage, and reception fall under the control of the colonial authority (EWB, 6). Acknowledging that constraints on cultural production under colonialism or a neocolonial regime such as South Africa's include the inculcation of European literary norms as though they were universal does not, however, lead the authors of The Empire Writes Back to challenge the norm of the "literary" as such, whose central value as an exemplary form of (post)colonial culture is not self-evident in societies in which universal literacy was not and still is not the rule.

The presupposition of literacy, indeed of a highly developed mastery of English or other European languages, as the necessary condition for postcolonial culture betrays the metropolitan and literary orientation of postcolonial criticism. Even if it draws from writers and theorists who are not metropolitan in origin, it grants pride of place to the knowing self-reflexive texts of the contemporary cosmopolitan (such as Salman Rushdie or J. M. Coetzee) and treats the work (textual only in part) of less mobile, less recent (post)colonial subjects as colonial imitation or immature approximations of postcolonial hybridity. By using literary norms to test (and discredit) cultural production from places and periods quite different from the contemporary metropolis, postcolonial criticism tends to treat the complexity of hybrid performance practices as insufficiently sophisticated writing and thus to subordinate local histories, cultures, and politics to the dictates of a present-day cosmopolitan hegemony.

This postcolonial avantgardism has a contradictory relation to the history of imperialism (and anti-imperialism) on which it rests. On the one hand, it acknowledges "the continuity of preoccupations throughout the historical process of European aggression" (EWB, 2). On the other, it asserts, rather prematurely, that "the postcolonial world is one in which the destructive cultural encounter [of colonization] is changing into an acceptance of difference on equal terms" (EWB, 36). Far from "dealing with the problem of transmuting time into space," theories that associate postcoloniality with a mode of reading (EWB, 163) or even identifiable discursive features (EWB, 193), rather than a condition defined by the cultural, political, and economic negotiation of the shifting--but by no means receding--conditions of metropolitan power, exacerbate the problem. By treating the uneven developments and parallel but decidedly unequal histories within a particular political terrain as a spatial relation between center and margin or between different but equal discourses, postcoloniality threatens to recolonize the tangled partial and unsynchronized histories of neocolonial culture and society 15 and, in so doing, to disavow its own roots in this uneven development.

Stepping back from the brink of the beyond, the "trope of the 'post'" that Homi Bhabha has identified as the sign of our times, 16 or what I would call the abstract finality of the postcolonial, we ought to begin by looking at how the New Africans came to terms with the institutions within which they emerged and against which they struggled to represent themselves as legitimate South African subjects. New Africans were indeed marked by an "imperial licence" in the form of the missions and their schools, state surveillance of African newspapers, and leisure organizations sponsored by European philanthropists, as well as the more overt racial discrimination of the self-governing Union (since 1910), but they were not completely controlled by it. At a moment when political rights for even elite Africans were curtailed, culture, whether seen as arts or entertainment, became a crucial site for the contestation of the legitimacy of African participation in public. As the Emancipation Celebration and other "pageants" commemorating important dates in a New African calendar suggest, theatre in the broadest sense (dramatic representations for an audience) was a primary site of contestation. Marginalized in a general South African public sphere defined in racially exclusive terms, New African theatre articulated what might be called a virtual public sphere, a discursive and social field within which alternative representations of South Africa could be entertained without direct exposure to the wrath of the state. 17

As a virtual public sphere, theatre operates in the subjunctive rather than indicative mood. It skirts the dangerous field of immediate political action in order to gain a testing ground for competing representations of the nation. At stake here is the attempt to seize the idea of modernity--citizenship, emancipation, individual and collective agency--from its prevailing colonial representation; the attempt, in other words, to "deploy, deform, and defuse" neocolonial institutions and formations so as to make "many modernities" out of colonial modernization. 18

In order to understand the terms of this virtual public sphere, we should specify the character of neocolonial domination in South Africa in the 1930s. These were years in which the South African state dramatically increased restrictions on the economic, political, and cultural mobility of Africans through legislation such as the Urban Areas Act and its amendments in the 1930s: the Slums Act , which allowed for the clearance of urban slumyards while restricting African access to property or residence in urban areas, and the Native Representative Act , which was to eliminate the last Africans from the Common Voters Roll. It was also a period of political and cultural fluidity among urban and urbanizing Africans in which different modes of resistance to and negotiation of this dispensation were played out. Conflicts between the modernizing drive of the state and its increasingly segregationist ideology, between chiefs keen to maintain traditional ways and the "civilizing" ambitions of the missionaries, and between African nationalist and communist interpretations of the risks and benefits of modernization for the black majority made for fractious and unstable allegiances among different dominant and subaltern groups. The partially transracial class solidarity among industrial workers in the 1920s was giving way, under pressure from demands for the reservation of skilled work for "poor [Afrikaans] whites," to class divisions on racial lines, while the African National Congress, the Communist Party of South Africa, and other groups that were to galvanize the population in the 1940s and 1950s were not yet mass organizations with effective political force.

Beset by internal disputes, African Nationalists struggled in vain to present a united front against the state. The conservative ascendancy in the ANC, led by Pixley ka Seme and John Dube (president of the Zulu-dominated Natal branch), tended to see in the state's program of "separate development" an opportunity for what might be called retraditionalization. 19 Retraditionalization implies not so much a return to premodern rural life as a reappropriation of clan authority and custom as the means to a tangible if limited autonomy, consolidating the power of the chiefs eroded by the emergence of the New Africans on the one hand, and the mass migration of landless blacks to the cities on the other. 20 It was thus not so much a rejection of modernity as an attempt to appropriate certain aspects of modernization, especially vernacular literacy and the technological reproduction or recording of traditional practices and artifacts, so as to reinvent habitual tribal affiliation as a "natural" respect for traditional authority.

This retraditionalist tendency collided with the habits and convictions of New Africans in the cities, especially Johannesburg, who by and large held to the belief that the benefits of modernization would ultimately outweigh the disruption of communal life and tribal loyalties caused by colonization. The editor Selope Thema, for instance, defended African appropriation of the benefits of European civilization, such as literacy and individual rights, while acknowledging the value of traditional communal organization. 21 Revaluating the authority of the chiefs, in particular the legendary power of the Zulu king Shaka over a now scattered Zulu nation, Herbert Dhlomo argued that those invoking Shaka defended the glories of a Zulu past rather than civil rights in the present and were advocating a "new kind of tribalism...[that] encourages non-progressive institutions among the people and [makes] the government the supreme chief and dictator." 22 And, in his only published play, The Girl who Killed to Save , the exhortations of the Xhosa prophetess Nongqause, who in 1856 urged the Xhosa to kill their cattle in anticipation of divine wrath being visited on the colonizers, and the consequent starvation of the clan are ultimately rered and the deaths redeemed as the price paid for civilization. 23

Yet an editorial published almost a year before the Emancipation Centenary Celebration remained sceptical of European intentions to honor the promise of universal rights: "it is true that Natives can no longer be bought and sold...but they are still in bondage as a people and there is a determined stand by Europeans against the grant to them [sic] of that full liberty to which all men are entitled.... Natives are not slaves, but they are not freemen." 24 Emphasizing the entrenchment of racial prejudice despite formal freedoms, the editorial draws on the insights of W. E. B. Dubois's The Souls of Black Folk to combat the notion of separate development under segregation accepted by Seme, Dube, and others influenced by the example of Booker T. Washington. 25 Profoundly suspicious of European definitions of the authentically African that were promulgated by apologists for development along the "Native's own lines," this and other editorials urged readers to bring together African and European traditions by participating in cultural institutions such as the BMSC, the Gamma Sigma debating societies, or the Eisteddfodau, festivals of choral singing and dramatic monologues loosely based on Welsh (and British imperial) models.

At its most instrumental, culture became a form of social control. Mining industrialists argued that monitoring their employees after hours meant a "good business investment" in the "moral and physical health of the workforce." 26 Missionaries hoped to curb social and economic unrest and the "swaggering, sweeping claims of Communism" by "moralizing the leisure time of the natives"; 27 Ray Phillips, author of the assimilationist The Bantu are Coming , for instance, appealed to the Chamber of Mines to fund activities ranging from the elite BMSC to Chaplin evenings for mine workers. More subtle, perhaps, were the aspirations of the African retraditionalists such as Dube, Caluza, and others who saw in the state's interest in preserving "native ways" an occasion for reestablishing a coherent ethnic inheritance from rural practices fractured by colonization. In the space between the instrumentalization of culture in the production of modernized subjects, whether among the mine and other industrial workers or in the black intermediary class, and the evocation of culture as a retraditionalized refuge from the ravages of modernization, the ambiguous notion of the disinterestedness of culture offered an escape, at once compelling and imaginary, from daily humiliations. This relative autonomy could and did give cultural production an affirmative cast, in Marcuse's sense of reinforcing the status quo by providing an imaginary refuge from it, 28 but it also offered at least the framework for a virtual public sphere, as I have defined it, as a site for the subjunctive enactment of an African agency.

The variety of theatre by and for Africans in 1930s Johannesburg suggests the contemporary persistence as well as the limitations of oppositions between elite and popular, imported and indigenous, literary and oral culture. She Stoops to Conquer, the Bantu Dramatic Society's inaugural production , was perhaps closest to the English genteel culture of the mission schools, but the "tribal sketches" of the Lucky Stars and the All African Eisteddfodau, which combined European and African theatrical and musical forms, were also indebted to missionary education. They in their turn inspired the commercially successful African "revues" and pageants by the popular Darktown Strutters and their successors, De Pitch Black Follies, whose leader Griffiths Motsieloa also participated in the Eisteddfodau. On the other hand, the Bantu People's Players' 1936 version of The Hairy Ape (which was to have been the Bantu Dramatic Society's first production) combined the agitprop skills of Belgian socialist Andre van Gyseghem (then in South Africa as Director of Pageants for the Empire Exhi-bition) with the local resonance of Fanagalo, the pidgin used by miners and their bosses, to produce a more emphatic representation of social and economic injustice. The Bantu Dramatic and Operatic Society's production of Moshoeshoe, Herbert Dhlomo's dramatization of the charismatic nineteenth-century Sotho leader, attempted to bring together the "tribal sketch," the history play, and the Eisteddfod recital in a dramatic response to the ascendancy of segregationist ideology by the end of the decade.

The hybrid character of these experiments resides not only in their use of form but also in the tensions between form and occasion of performance, especially in the representation of African tradition in the modern European medium of naturalist drama. The reconstruction of a legitimate (rather than verifiably authentic) folk culture in this period relies heavily, as Veit Erlmann has demonstrated in his account of South African music, on the invention of tradition and a "usable past" 29 "out of the disparate remnants of a shattered rural order" (AS, 150). In the creation of an African National Theatre, as in that of an African music, finding a usable past was not so much an act of pure discovery as a process of collection, recollection, and invention, of grafting together African and European material in ways that would resonate with performers and audiences. The strict correspondence between actor and embodied character, and the naturalist convention of situating the actor's expression of interiority in a set designed to represent a particular exterior or interior environment, both characteristic of the early twentieth-century metropolitan mainstream, had little in common with the more fluid and more overtly rhetorical relationship between performer, character and location in the praise-songs, folktales, and community dancing characteristic of southern Bantu performance. 30

The question of language, the hegemony of literary culture and genteel norms, and the weakness (relative to music) of an African American mediating term made the development of an African National Theatre more complicated than its musical counterpart. The central site for this "invention of tradition" was paradoxically also the crucible for elite modernization: the mission schools, such as Lovedale, Amanzimtoti--later Adams--College, or the Ohlange Institute, John Dube's counterpart to Tuskegee. Institutions such as these had long encouraged school performances of dramatic sketches, sometimes on English subjects, and often as "reconstructions of tribal life" (AS, 64). 31 These sketches, in combination with exposure to metropolitan literature and music, provided the inspiration for the Lucky Stars, founded in 1929 by the Mthethwa brothers, who were educated, like the Dhlomos, at Amanzimtoti College. Their sketches portrayed such subjects as Ukuqomisa (courting) or Umthakathi (the sorcerer) and often concluded with a rousing dance finale. Although formed in large part by an elite place and occasion (mission schools, the BMSC, and the like), the Lucky Stars were enthusiastically received by black workers and those on the fringes of the formal economy, especially in less industrialized Natal, where audiences found these "idealized images of a rural past" a welcome escape from their double alienation, in racial and class terms, from "civilization." 32

Yet claims that the Lucky Stars offered an early but unmistakable instance of critical or satirical black theatre 33 and should therefore be seen as forerunners of the anti-apartheid protest theatre of the 1970s, 34 should be treated with caution. The origins of the sketches in the folkloric recuperation of the missions did not prevent them from being appropriated by a diverse popular audience, but the ethnic emphasis, even exclusiveness, of the sketches was problematic; it reflected as much the Zulu pride of the performers, most of whom came from the Zulu-identified Amanzimtoti College, as the desire to challenge the social and economic discrimination against all Africans. Since they were performed in the Zulu language and in historically reconstructed Zulu costume (rather than the mixed traditional and modified attire worn in rural Zululand at the time), the alleged satirical intent of the sketches would have been only partially recognizable to speakers of other Bantu languages who saw the shows, whose responses may have been affected as much by the music (itself a hybridization of urban and rural forms) and the tangible presence of African actors as by the sketches' authentic details. The account of the Jewish South African impresario, Bertha Slosberg, who promoted the group from 1933 through its crowning appearance at the Empire Exhibition in 1936, emphasizes the spectacular as well as the folkloric dimension the thrilling dancing as well as the need "to salvage from European influence, the remaining power, the native simplicity, the splendid savage grandeur of a dying pagan land." 35 Banishing "Natives" after 8 P.M. and thus excluding them from "entertainments for Europeans," 36 the Exhibition marginalized the urban African who might disturb the ethnographic entertainment for white audiences. This arrangement fostered primitivist nostalgia among whites anxious to preserve "images of native domestic life," but did not prevent the performance from "influenc[ing] Johannesburg Location productions." 37

If the Lucky Stars were praised by white spectators of the Empire Exhibition for their displays of rural life, their successors attempted to capitalize on the appeal of the sketches for urban black audiences, while deploying the form to somewhat different ends. Although De Pitch Black Follies were, like the Lucky Stars, dependent on the benevolence of white city officials and white capital (including the patronage of Gallophone), they did not limit their performances to the "coon songs" (izikhunzi) implied by their minstrel label and popular with working-class audiences, or to the Zulu folksongs and sketches that formed the repertoire of many of their mission-educated members such as Motsieloa (AS, 164, 171), and which appealed more to the sensibilities of the retraditionalists. A description in Bantu World of De Pitch Black Follies performing "Africa--a revel pageant" in 1940 suggests an emphasis on evolutionary modernization, "depicting the march of progress among the Bantu, from the primitive stage right up to that of the 'Upper Tenth,' the academic class, with a didactic climax--the necessity of 'Unity in Diversity' in Bantudom." 38 Performed in the state-administered "community hall" in Western Native Township, the sketch would have had an audience that included industrial and service workers as well as intellectuals, but probably few abaphakathi.

This description of "Africa" echoes the aspirations of the Emancipation Centenary rather than the primitivist nostalgia of Slosberg's "dying pagan land" , insofar as it reiterates the evolutionist views permeating the earlier event. The format of the "revel pageant," however, recalls the Empire Exhibition's presentation of the Lucky Stars. Like that performance, this one included spectacular displays of "African traditional dance" as well as comic skits from the minstrel repertoire. This motley combination of uplift and self-mocking exaggeration, familiar from the vaudeville revues throughout the decade, was regarded with some concern by New Africans, who feared, as one commentator wrote, that excessive exposure to jazz and other distractions would "deafen our appreciation for art," including the "greater beauty and entertainment" of drama. 39 Herbert Dhlomo, regarded by himself and other New Africans (and some whites) as a spokesman for an "African tradition grafted onto the European," was critical not only of the commodification of African tradition as "exotic crudities" 40 but also of its embalming as "static museum-like plays." 41 Like the New Negro who challenged white society's "sentimental interest" in black traditions and the "double standard" of the "philanthropic attitude" among white American liberals, 42 the New African was suspicious of those who expressed concern for "native" traditions while remaining either indifferent or hostile to black aspirations to transform society as a whole.

Even prominent critics of retraditionalist rhetoric such as Herbert Dhlomo were nonetheless obliged to recognize its usefulness in addressing an audience caught up in the contradictions of colonial modernity. In the period between his first play, The Girl Who Killed to Save (written in 1934), and the 1939 production of Moshoeshoe, Dhlomo moved closer to an emphasis on "tribal drama" and traditional forms. An early version of an essay entitled "The Importance of African Drama" indicates the prospects and the pitfalls Dhlomo saw in the idea of an African National Theatre:

Modern drama is not a mere emotional entertainment. It is a source of ideas, a cultural and educational factor, an agency for propaganda and, above all, it is literature. What part will the new African play in modern drama? On its physical side, he can contribute strong, fast rhythm, speedy action, expressive vigorous gesture and movement, powerful dramatic speech--no small contribution when modern plays drag so tediously.... We want African playwrights who will dramatize ...African History. We want dramatic representations of African Serfdom. Oppression, Exploitation, and Metamorphosis.... The African dramatist...can expose evil and corruption and not suffer libel as newspapermen do; he can guide and preach to his people as preachers cannot do. To do this he must be an artist before a propagandist; a philosopher before a reformer, a psychologist before a patriot; be true to his African "self" and not be prey to exotic crudities. 43

Dhlomo defends the priority of literary drama, yet he insists that the strong performative dimension of African oral traditions should inform any African National Theatre. He calls for the dramatic representation of social injustice, but also argues that the modern African dramatist should be artist before propagandist. This inconsistency certainly expresses the tension between the precepts of Dhlomo's missionary education (which defended the timelessness of art against the contingencies of politics) and the pressures of the historical moment that push him and his peers towards commitment, but it also speaks for the concept of art, in this case theatre, as a form of negative critique or virtual public sphere.

The appeal to art before propaganda therefore should not be read as a capitulation to imposed norms of gentility. Rather, it attempts to use the notion of artistic autonomy to negotiate cultural space for the contestation of oppression in an environment in which more direct action (from political mobilization to adversarial journalism) was increasingly curtailed. Framing the revitalization of tradition in the generalizing terms of African nationalism, Dhlomo challenges both white and black separatists when he argues, in a 1939 essay entitled "Why Study Tribal Dramatic Forms?" that this revitalization should transform not only African forms but also modern drama as a whole by "infus[ing] new blood into the weary limbs of the older dramatic forms of Europe" ("WST," 35). The hint of deference is more than offset by the critical reference to the "weary limbs...of Europe" or, in the earlier essay, to the "tedium" of modern (by implication, English) plays. This disdain for the "acting plays" of the commercial theatre in favor of "literary drama" ("WST," 39) that might also propagandize for social change echoes the aspirations of the advocates of the "new social drama" (G. B. Shaw, John Galsworthy, and their contemporaries) in Britain, but, like them, stops well short of a radical abolition of aesthetics in the manner of Brecht's Lehrstücke or the proletarian agitprop in both the United States and Britain during this period. 44

The example of metropolitan social drama, its attempt to be both literary and engaged, independent of the pressure to make a profit yet able to draw an audience, exercises a significant but not overwhelming influence on Dhlomo's reflections on drama and the African. Whereas Dhlomo's suggested themes may confirm the influence of Shaw or Galsworthy, his ideal institution of theatre differs from that of the metropolitan "new drama," whose producers relied on a modified version of the commercial theatre. Against this commodification of drama, Dhlomo envisages performance legitimated as an occasion in the social life of a community. In "The Nature and Variety of Tribal Drama" , he points to the close relationship between the occasion of performance and the commemoration of significant events, rites of passage, seasonal festivals, and the like and argues that traditional African drama shares with the classical Greek its integration into the social fabric. 45 This evocation of a theatre of ritual power, of "sympathetic magic," does not entail a nostalgic revival of tribal community, but rather the reverse: "The African artist cannot delve into the Past unless he has first grasped the Present. African art can grow and thrive not only by...excavat-ing archaical art forms, but by grappling with present-day realities" ("WST," 40). Instead of "static museum-like drama based on primitive tradition and culture" ("WST," 39), Dhlomo envisaged an African National Theatre that might appropriate those African performance forms that lent themselves to a critical engagement between past and present.

While highlighting the public character of these forms, however, he insists that they be evaluated as "ends in themselves," in aesthetic as well as functional terms ("NVT," 27). He appropriates the courtly tradition of izibongo (praises--usually of the king or noble), rather than the more demotic convention of izinganekwane (folktales), and suggests a desire to legitimate a tradition of high culture as universal as Greek tragedy in the European pantheon; but his appeal to tradition rests on the distinctly modern invention of a term, izibongelo, to bolster his claim that this panegyric form contains within it traces of dramatic structure and that its function, like that of drama, is to "tell the story of the nation" ("NVT," 23, 35). 46

These essays thus record the impact of retraditionalist rhetoric. They appeal to a "sacred inheritance" ("WST," 37) as the "ground on which a great original African drama can be built" ("NVT," 35), even as they speak to New African scepticism of the "museum-like" construction of ethnicity as a display in ethnography's exhibition of discrimination. They also register the strain in New African faith in modernization through Europeanization. The ineffectual response of most white liberals to the disenfranchisement of Africans and enforcement of the pass laws, the state's indifference to the protests of the All African Convention (AAC) held in December 1935, and the spectacle of European savagery in Spain and Ethiopia, undermined the authority of "white civilization" as such. As D. D. T. Jabavu, convener of the AAC, had it:

After hearing a great deal for twenty years about the rights of small nations, self-determination, Christian ideals, the inviolability of treaties,...the glory of European civilization, and so forth, the brief history of the last eight months [since the summoning of the Convention in December 1935] has scratched this European veneer and revealed the white savage hidden beneath. 47

As Tim Couzens and Martin Orkin have pointed out, Dhlomo's dramatic writing in this period responds more directly than before to the contradictory impulses of colonial modernity, if not quite with the ferocity of Jabavu, then in the more cautious spirit of Selope Thema: "tribal life is not a peculiarity of the African race; it is a stage through which the great nations of today have passed ...we have a right to decide our own destiny and to make our distinctive contribution to the world's civilizations." 48 The play most often cited in this context is Cetshwayo (1936-37). Portraying the betrayal of the last autonomous Zulu king in the 1870s by Theophilus Shepstone, Governor of Natal, and his ally John Dunn, "English adventurer" gone native, Cetshwayo is Dhlomo's most explicit indictment of segregation and disenfranchisement conducted in the name of civilization and Empire. 49 For all the argument for African forms and conventions, however, the dramaturgy of Cetshwayo is thoroughly Aristotelian or, more precisely, Schillerian, in that it focuses on a national hero whose inability to see through the machinations of his enemies brings about his tragic defeat; African motifs, especially izibongo of the king, tend to be subordinated to the forward drive of the plot. Drawing on the Schillerian history play in its English derivation as well as the conventions of Victorian melodrama, especially in its treatment of the "white Zulu" Dunn, Cetshwayo makes for stirring reading today. We can, however, only speculate on the contemporary effect of this play, since it was neither published nor publicly performed.

Moshoeshoe, written in the same period, was performed by the Bantu Dramatic and Operatic Society at the BMSC in May 1939, in honor of Moshoeshoe Day. 50 Less adversarial than Cetshwayo, Moshoeshoe portrayed the wisdom of the legendary Sotho statesman, revered both for his ability to resolve conflicts between his subjects and refugees from the Difecane (the mass migration caused by Zulu expansionism in the 1830s), and as a model African leader whose perspicacity was sadly missed a century later. By placing at the center of his play a pitso, or people's assembly, Dhlomo called to mind the current disputes of the ANC and AAC. The play's emphasis on Moshoeshoe's criticism of ethnically divisive custom and the temptations of dictatorship highlighted the relative success of the Basutoland Protectorate's resistance against attempts at incorporation and retribalization by the South African government.

The significance of Moshoeshoe does not lie solely in an allegorical representation of a modern African state in embryo, however. Equally important is the play's attempt to negotiate a new association of form, content, and occasion of performance. Form, in particular language, appears to have been the most problematic. Although the play was praised in the African press for portraying an exemplary African leader, its evocation of Sotho custom and its entwinement of "legend and reality, action and philosophy," 51 the attempt to render Sotho praises, proverbs, and dialogue in English received a mixed response. Whereas the Bantu World reviewer praised the play as the first indigenous drama combining the efforts of Zulu (Dhlomo and the composer Caluza) and Sotho (the choreographer, A. P. Khutlang, and songwriter, Salome Masoleng) and justified the flowery verse and choppy syntax by claiming that the play's "chief charm lies in the dialogue which is a literal translation from the language in which it was originally written," 52 his Umteteli counterpart maintained that "there was too much of the refined" in the dialogue and accused the actors of not being "typically Masotho." Songs in Sotho rather than English, he argues, "would have contributed much to making the play a strong living drama of a significant episode in our national life." 53 This dispute reflects the strain in the language of the play between a desire for idiomatic authenticity and an aspiration to poetic language that would transcend local specificity and "find the changeless in the changing, the fundamental in the ephemeral." 54 It also suggests that the question of authenticity was itself subject to dispute and could not be resolved in terms of form or stable literary value, but rather in terms of the significance of the occasion of performance.

The occasion of the performance was likewise subject to strain. Although the play was performed at the BMSC and not, for instance, in the setting dominated by the mountain Thaba Bosiu, where Moshoeshoe held his pitso, or its namesake in Pimville, near Johannesburg, where political meetings were held, 55 its loosely linked scenes--the pitso at the center, framed by suitors' quarrels and encounters between the Basotho, a wandering bard, and refugees from the Difecane, and ending with a harvest festival--suggest a recurrent commemoration rather than a unique story. The music for the performance, like that for the Emancipation Centenary, would have at least alluded to melodies already familiar from Caluza's songs of national sorrow such as "iLand Act" and "Elamakosi," which lament African dispossession and appeal to the chiefs for guidance. Dhlomo would have liked to legitimate his play in the eyes of an African public by drawing on the immediate authority of the Convention as well as the comparison with numerous Moshoeshoe Day rallies, but this legitimation was far from secure. While reviewers picked up the contemporary resonance of the pitso and readers and audience alike would have made the connection between the pitso and the rallies, the harvest festival that concludes the play does not quite bring in the yield. The festival envelops but cannot incorporate the missionaries who praise Moshoeshoe's genius only to cast doubt on the enactment of his legacy: "Moshoeshoe...a man consumed by the smouldering and devastating fire of...great expectations unfulfilled, of plans and ambitions whose very attainment would give birth to plans and ambitions never to be attained." 56 This ambiguous praise strikes a cautionary note against the soaring izibongo at the end; the allusion to the citadel of Thaba Bosiu and to "Moshoeshoe the man-mountain, the mountain-mind" has the status of tradition but not the force of historical inevitability. 57

In dramatizing the discrepancy between the celebration of the African exemplar and the implied deflection of his legacy while not providing a resolution, Moshoeshoe represents the historical impasse faced by the New Africans. In 1934, the Emancipation Centenary Celebration was still able to sustain the legitimacy of liberal paternalism, bolstered by the conviction held by the promoters, participants, and many in the audience of the necessary conjunction of emancipation, modernity, and European civilization. In 1939, however, this quintessen-tially New African conviction was evaporating in the white heat of European savagery at home and abroad. In the decades to follow, a revitalized African nationalism was to break this impasse and galvanize resistance to the segregationist state. Moshoeshoe, with its juxtaposition of incompatible elements and its lack of resolution--in short, its inauthenticity--does not offer a model for the protest literature that was to accompany (but not necessarily reflect) this resistance and that was to characterize much black South African writing from the 1940s to the present. The flaws and fissures in the text of the play, however, and the occasion of its performance speak more truly than any tragic catharsis or resounding fictional triumph to the pressures of the historical moment, the aspirations of New Africans, and the fundamental incoherence of colonial modernity.

The aporia at the heart of colonial modernity consists most obviously in the incommensurability of the promise, on the one hand, of universal rights and emancipation--in short, modernity--and, on the other, the practice of liberal paternalism and limited civilization under neocolonial tutelage. What is at issue, however, is not simply the details of a particular South African history, but rather the character of modernity in general or, more concretely, modernity historically and presently generalized from the metropole to the margins. New African interpretation of what Clifford Geertz has called the "dispersions and discontinuities of modern life" is in part "local knowledge," but it is also vernacular redeployment of metropolitan knowledge and, as such, has broad implications for the interpretation of current postcolonial "dispersions and discontinuities." 58

New African hybridization of the dramatic and critical repertories disseminated by missionary schools and other neocolonial institutions may be historically distinct from current postcolonial hybridity but is nonetheless structurally comparable to the appropriation of metropolitan discourses in other colonial and postcolonial environments, including the current one of The Empire Writes Back. The authority of this text and others like it depends on a paradoxical inheritance: on the one hand, postcolonial critics draw on metropolitan modes of interpretation historically disseminated and deformed in local institutions--from mission schools to universities--which are at best only partially accessible to colonized subjects like the New Africans or their descendants. On the other hand, these critics owe the legitimacy of the field to precisely those local knowledges and particular histories that have so far eluded appropriation. If postcolonial theory is to take seriously its own assertion that "the inauthentic, the marginal," rather than practices legitimated by either traditional or metropolitan authority, "is the real" of postcolonial writing (EWB, 41), then it must begin by acknowledging this ambiguous relation to its objects of study and, with it, the debt of postcolonial (post)modernism to the contradictory legacy of colonial modernity.
