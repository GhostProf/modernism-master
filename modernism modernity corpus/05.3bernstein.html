Making Modernist Masterpieces *

Michael André Bernstein

Errata

1

In 1936, Gertrude Stein was invited to England to lecture at Oxford and Cambridge. She prepared two talks for the occasion, one of which was called "What Are Master-Pieces and Why Are There So Few of Them." 1 Like all of her theoretical pronouncements, this text is a bravura combination of speculative audacity, adroit playfulness, and flagrant self-promotion. It is, in other words, a miniature attempt to produce her own version of precisely that class of object whose extreme rarity the lecture's title asserts. However few the number of genuine masterpieces, Stein is not only certain that they exist and are worth striving to create, but, more interestingly, she is equally sure that her audience shares the same convictions for reasons wholly independent of any particular works she might invoke. Indeed, her lecture scarcely bothers to mention, let alone analyze, any specific examples at all--apart, of course, from her own writings, along with passing glances at Hamlet and Robinson Crusoe. To Stein, the features of individual masterpieces are almost incidental compared to the overwhelming significance of the category as such.

Paradoxically, though, in spite of Stein's near hypostatization of the masterpiece, she deliberately avoids drawing on the rhetoric of the sublime when defining it, relying instead on the unemphatic, homespun verb "to make." No word occurs more frequently in Stein's writings, or resonates with more obvious authorial self-identification. Even in the charged context of defining of the masterpiece, she depends wholly on the notions of deliberate intention and repeated labor suggested by the workmanlike concept of making. For all their self-evident and timeless authority, masterpieces are not the result of some transcendent revelation or supra-rational inspiration; masterpieces are made, rarely and against extraordinary odds, but, like other, more ordinary works of human days and hands, they must nonetheless be made. Through this insistence, Stein enables us to hear, behind its post-Enlightenment, idealist sublimation, something of the term's more prosaic history.

Unlike most of our critical vocabulary that emerged from a subtly nuanced Graeco-Latin philosophical and aesthetic discourse, the word "masterpiece" is entirely medieval in origin and first appeared in the context of regulatory legislation governing artisanal activity. 2 The exercise of a profession during the Middle Ages necessitated admission to a guild or corporation, confirmed by the candidate's recognition as a master, usually through the production of a chef d'oeuvre or Meisterstück that had to conform to strictly defined criteria and was subject to an official examination by the senior members of a guild. Such a requirement is mentioned for the first time in Etienne Boileau's Livre de métier, which is datable between 1261 and 1271 and contains the statutes governing the corporations in the city of Paris. As the record amply demonstrates, "the most striking feature of the Medieval institution of Masterpiece-making was its wholly artisanal purpose. Masterpieces were executed not just by goldsmiths and tapestry weavers, where a certain 'artistic' quality might be assumed, but also apothecaries, carpenters, rope-makers, barbers, locksmiths, tailors, and cooks" (M, 5). 3 My favorite example of such officially mandated masterpiece making, one that a dedicated connoisseur of the dinner table like Gertrude Stein surely would have applauded, is the following list of dishes required of a candidate for master chef in 1576 in Calais:

a turkey in rum and vanilla sauce, three partridges in a pâté, a milk-fed kid roasted on a spit, a boar with lard, a tart with jellied filling decorated with fleurs-de-lys of different colors, a basket made with candied sticks interlaced and variously colored, a lion carved in jellied cream, an imitation bunch of grapes made of jelly and an almond tart [M, 5-6].

Stein, of course, is not the only modernist who would have found such a list imaginatively compelling. To a remarkable extent, the modernists were fascinated by catalogues and lists of every description, and when they incorporated lengthy inventories into their own texts, it was not just because doing so was the easiest way to signal an affinity between their work and classical epics. Such catalogues also functioned as affectionately parodic microcosms of the project of producing encyclopedic masterpieces. A list may be the most primitive of all transcriptions, but it also has the potential to be the most peremptory and willful affirmation of a text's power to incorporate anything at all into its own unfolding. 4 The scarcely veiled proprietary relationship of the list maker to the objects itemized makes the creation of lists like an ever-expanding appropriation of the world's physical and imaginative commodities. Insofar as we are all both story tellers and story dwellers, we rely on syntax and plot to impose some order on the heterogeneity of experience. Lists, however, often flourish by flamboyantly demonstrating their arbitrariness, and they can suggest entirely new patterns of meaning without any discernible story line or grammar. Similar to a connoisseur's collection of cherished objects or texts, a catalogue need not be based on any prior consonance among its components, nor need it be organized according to fixed hierarchic principles established before the items on the list were invented or found; it is sufficient that at one time each element pleased the cataloguer. Precisely because the rules governing the placement and sequence of a list's elements seem so strict, although never explicitly stated, lists compel their reader to work at divining the intention of the listmaker. Indeed, seen in this light, Stephen Dedalus's famous account of the quasidivine nature of the artist resembles nothing so much as a description of a particularly adroit maker of lists: "the personality . . . finally refines itself out of existence, impersonalizes itself, so to speak . . . The artist, like the God of creation, remains within or behind or beyond or above his handiwork, invisible, refined out of existence, indifferent, paring his fingernails." 5

Although the most minimal of structuring devices, the list's apparent conceptual poverty is compensated for by its capacity to marshal the potentially infinite richness of montage, where, as Richard Sieburth has pointed out, any term "may enter into sudden adjacency with another" and generate new meanings out of the juxtaposition. 6 Whether the catalogues are sovereignly comic like James Joyce's, or deeply melancholic like Walter Benjamin's, it is striking how thoroughly the logic of list making crystallizes the modernist masterpiece's ultimate ambition. In all such lists, the randomness and contingency of history and the memorializing impulse of the library intersect, bringing everything, from the culture's canonical topoi to arcane, almost forgotten citations, and from contemporary, public controversies to the writer's most personal, private concerns, into a meaningful, new narrative order.

Early in his Cantos, almost as a manifesto of the poem to come, Ezra Pound offers a terse catalogue of what he thinks the modernist masterpiece must incorporate and make new:

And they want to know what we talked about?
"de litteris et de armis, praestantibusque ingeniis,"
Both of ancient times and our own; books, arms,
And of men of unusual genius,
Both of ancient times and our own, in short the usual subjects
Of conversation between intelligent men. 7

Beneath their lapidary assurance, there is an extraordinary amount of aggression in these lines, a preemptive dismissal of all those skeptical of such princely modes of cultural transmission. The record of heroism and luminous intelligence kept vivid by a small band of "intelligent men"--and in Pound, the gender of the speakers is axiomatic--will always remain a source of suspicion and fear to the "they" who must ask from outside what "we talked about." The aggression itself, however, is cunningly balanced by a flattering seduction of the reader. "Learn to cherish the same values and cultural highpoints as we do," so the implicit promise runs, "and you too will become worthy of being included in the conversation." The threat of exclusion from, and the prospect of admission to the charmed circle work together to try to captivate the reader and make him into the poem's studious acolyte.

The Cantos' defiantly hierarchic vision, although enormously influential, is far from the only or even the most widely imitated model of what a twentieth-century masterpiece might encompass. Against Pound's story of a small, permanently endangered, permanently renewed community quickened into thought and action by the inspiration of the finest moments of its past, it is tempting to invoke such counterexamples as Joyce's Ulysses and Finnegans Wake. The domestic heroism of Joyce's ad-canvasser and sleeping publican are undoubtedly admirable, but we are unlikely to confuse them with Sigismundo Malatesta's ferocity, and their conversation, if endearing and alert, scarcely seems comparable to the quattrocenteschi civilities Pound celebrates. Most of Joyce's characters, that is, would never be able to identify what Pound called his poem's foundational "list of beautiful objects," and they surely do not possess the kind of virtù required to be included in The Cantos' catalogue of world-transforming artists, thinkers, and builders of cities. 8

Such a ready-to-hand antithesis is largely spurious, however. As most readers know only too well by now, the speech of even Joyce's least educated characters is as resonant with literary echoes as anything in The Cantos, and almost every paragraph of Joyce's novels contains multiple references to "books, arms, / And men of unusual genius, / Both of ancient times and our own." The more basic distinction, it seems to me, is that all of the major res in The Cantos are alert to their historical and literary antecedents; their self-consciousness consists precisely in first acquiring and then striving to make new the instigation of their cultural heritage. Although its readers require enormous assistance in tracking down the poem's many references, these are usually fully present to the characters who either articulate or incarnate the indispensable cultural wisdom. We have trouble understanding the sense of numerous passages, but no one within The Cantos faces a similar plight. In Ulysses, on the contrary, none of the characters, whether highly cultured or semiliterate, is fully aware of the extensive provenance of his words and actions. Such knowledge belongs to the consciousness shaping the work as a whole, and, to a significantly lesser extent, to the novel's painstakingly instructed readers. Even for those who enjoy rendering such judgments, it cannot be an easy matter to determine whether Joyce's network of disguised allusions, shared between author and reader over the heads of the fictional characters, is more complicitous with a hierarchic and totalizing system of culture than are, if the oxymoron be permitted, The Cantos' openly arcane references.

More pivotal than these distinctions between Pound's and Joyce's references is how their texts require students, a trait common to a whole constellation of modernist masterpieces, but quite different from the scrupulously attentive readers demanded by even their most intricate predecessors. An explosion of citations and a deliberate effort to thwart an unworked-for comprehension on the part of their audience characterize many of the most significant modernist works and are direct consequences of their makers' drive to create a new kind of encyclopedic masterpiece. Both formally and biographically, this urge predates the realization of any specific works and constitutes the horizon of expectation for each fresh attempt. The pressure to produce such a masterpiece has a complex history of its own, and I now want to focus on the relationship between that history, the nature of the need it answered, and its consequences for ensuing generations of artists.

2

Perhaps the most succinct way to begin mapping the complex trajectory whose end result is a theory of modernist masterpieces is to go back to two nineteenth-century writers whose programmatic declarations at first glance would seem to share little with one another, and still less with Pound, Joyce, or Stein.

In February, 1844, Honoré de Balzac wrote a characteristically emphatic combination of love-letter and self-description to his lover and future wife, Éveline Hanska:

This, in sum, is the game I am playing. Four men will have had an enormous life: Napoleon, Cuvier, O'Connell, and I intend to be the fourth. The first one lived the life of Europe; he engrafted himself to it through his armies! The second wedded the whole globe! The third made himself the incarnation of a people! And I--I shall have carried an entire society in my head! 9

What I want to emphasize here is the carefully chosen range of Balzac's self-comparisons. First, there is Napoleon Bonaparte, the parvenu whose conquests showed the virtually limitless potential of genius to make a single individual's will the active principle of European history. 10 Second, there is Baron Cuvier (1769-1832), the celebrated naturalist whose work in comparative anatomy and systems of zoological classification led to novel ways of ordering, and thus understanding, life itself. 11 Third, there is Daniel O'Connell, the champion of the disenfranchised, whose political gifts and moral passion made him "the incarnation of an entire people." Finally, there is Balzac himself, the prodigious novelist whose ambition encompassed nothing less than the creation of a parallel, self-contained social and political totality. This imaginative project's challenge was, at its most grandiose, "to compete with civil society" ("faire concurrence à l'Etat-Civil"). 12 The terms of Balzac's comparisons are no more accidental than is the all-embracing, epic title, The Human Comedy, that he finally chose for his sequence of novels. I want to suggest that by placing himself last in the list, he was also indicating both that his own work would unite, in a fundamentally new way, the distinctive energies of all three preceding res, and that The Human Comedy would eventually be seen as the summa of the century's most decisive accomplishments and aims.

Against this colossally ambitious self-definition, I would like to balance Stéphane Mallarmé's equally provocative announcements in "Crise De Vers":

Languages are imperfect precisely because there are several of them; the one supreme language is lacking . . . the very diversity of idioms on earth prevents one from uttering the words which otherwise would be, by a single articulation, the fullness of truth itself. This prohibition exists expressly . . . to eliminate any grounds for fantasizing oneself God. 13

It is a critical commonplace that no two writers could be further apart in method, craftsmanship, or intended audience than Balzac and Mallarmé. Yet when one reads their various programmatic declarations, what stands out is the unprecedented insistence with which they both claimed for their art the power to create a separate world that is as comprehensive as the realm of quotidian reality. Or, as Mallarmé put it with characteristic absolutism, "everything in the world exists to culminate in one book" ("tout, au monde, existe pour aboutir à un livre"). 14 Not in "a book," as the words are invariably translated, but rather in one unique, all-encompassing text that would absorb not just all prosaic objects and events, but also all prior artistic creation. I have argued elsewhere that, unlike for Pound or Joyce, for Mallarmé the poetic text is neither the discoverer nor even the celebrant of previously extant values. Instead, it is their sole originator, at once the source and locus of meaning. The words of such a poem must be the noumenal meaning absent from the concrete realm of human activity, and the relationship between text and values is not one of embodiment but of absolute identity. 15 In rethinking Mallarmé's project from the perspective of Joyce's or Pound's texts, however, what now strikes me as crucial is how the sheer multiplicity of poems and languages is an implicit rebuke to Mallarmé's aspirations. In principle, there can only be one ultimate text, "Le Livre," and hence only one language in which the entire, anarchic raw material of separate human discourses would be incorporated, transformed, and made whole again, an ultimate poem purer even than Homer's work, of whose epics Mallarmé was to complain that they represented a "fall" (déchéance) from the magical plenitude of Orpheus's verses. If the decline into separate texts is a permanent reminder of how far we are from divinity, it also issues what amounts to a cosmic challenge to undo the Fall by inventing a new tongue in which the lost totality of separate discourses could be synthesized anew. It also contains the rather startlingly antinomian implication that such inventiveness would be quite literally a God-like act.

For all their intransigent absolutism, Mallarmé's formulations undoubtedly owe a great deal to the discourse by and about Wagner that marked French aesthetic theorizing for over four decades, beginning in the spring of 1861 with Baudelaire's epochal essay on the composer. 16 Mallarmé, much like Joyce a generation later, was both spurred on and left profoundly apprehensive by Wagner's attempt to create a Gesamtkunstwerk or "total work of art" in which music, verse, and spectacle were united into a single aesthetic creation. Mallarmé saw in such forms the most potent, and hence dangerous, competitors for the poet's prerogative to be the alchemist of the new totality, and any full treatment of this topic would necessitate an extended evaluation of both Beethoven's and Wagner's roles in a theory of the modernist masterpiece. 17 Even if we restrict ourselves to pondering Mallarmé's statement from "Crise de vers"--"Languages are imperfect precisely because there are several of them; the one supreme language is lacking"--it is impossible not to be reminded of the linguistic inventiveness of Finnegans Wake or The Cantos' mixture of different languages, hieroglyphs, musical notations, and Chinese ideograms. In "Canto 86," Pound tells us "It can't be all in one language," but if this modestly suggests that different cultures each have something of value to contribute to humankind's self-understanding, then it also implies, with more truculence, that the new masterpiece itself ought not to be written in a single language (C, 563). In order to be a masterpiece at all, it had better strive to become the one, central book containing the essential wisdom, the talismanic "piths and gists," of all cultures.

I am far from introducing the absurd claim that earlier artists had not been concerned, programmatically and self-consciously, with the creation of enduring works. From Horace's certainty that his lines would outlast the ruler and city they ostensibly celebrate to the innumerable Renaissance variations on the topos of the poet's bestowing immortality on an all-too transitory lover, it would be fatuous to recite the list of earlier texts that carry clear evidence of their makers' hopes for a measure of permanent cultural centrality. There is, however, something essentially different about the terms in which modernist writers conceived of the project of masterpiece making. In defense of the criticism aimed at the apparently haphazard ordering of The Cantos, Pound retorted that, unlike Dante, he had no "Aquinas map," no culturally shared, philosophical-theological system by which to plot the progression of his tale of the tribe. 18 He kept up a steady stream of prose essays, letters, and public pronouncements designed both to justify his procedure and to help his readers make sense of the work in progress. For similar reasons, although in a characteristically more hidden but also more systematic way, Joyce helped create a critical framework for Ulysses by providing carefully chosen admirers with the various schema for the novel from which we all, no matter what the ambiguities and questions, continue to benefit. The very need, however, for such efforts underlines the severity of the dilemma both Pound and Joyce confronted. 19 Their task was not really commensurate with the efforts of even their most ambitious predecessors, in part because the category of the whole had become essentially arbitrary. One could accept, in other words, the abstract principle of a Gesamtkunstwerk, but since there was no longer cultural agreement on what constituted a coherent totality in the first place, each artist was compelled to work out his own, particular, and thus necessarily idiosyncratic selection. The logic of a representative masterpiece makes any acknowledgment of its arbitrariness inadmissible, however, so the text must not only select its materia poetica from a range of possibilities and with an absence of generic guidelines hitherto unthinkable, but it must also do so in such a way as to make that selection appear simultaneously inevitable and complete. In the hope of making the stakes--and the strains--of such a project more explicit, I want to conclude by offering a series of imperatives, illicitly modeled on Wallace Stevens's categories in his Notes toward a Supreme Fiction, the most lucid example in American writing of a modernist masterpiece composed as an interrogation of the very possibility of its existence. My argument, to express the matter as concisely as possible, is that it was essential for the modernist masterpiece to claim to fulfill these imperatives--that they are, in fact, its necessary categories--but that for powerful logical and historical reasons they remain, in actuality, impossible to satisfy. What emerges out of the tension between the fulfillment and the frustration of these demands is precisely what we have come to recognize as the characteristic triumphs and particular problems of the modernist masterpiece.

It Must be Universal

In resonance, implication, and significance, the modernist masterpiece must make clear that no matter how gritty with the texture of local details its narrative, and no matter how autobiographically determined the selection of its specific settings and themes, its true reach is global and transhistorical. Thus, for example, Joyce has to insist that "Ulysses/Bloom is the only complete all-round character presented by any writer," 20 and Pound needs to assure us that "with one day's reading a man may have the key [presumably to a just society and a wiser life] in his hands" (C, 427). In this regard, one can regard Sigmund Freud's project as the creation of a special sort of modernist masterpiece. Freud's theorizing increasingly laid claim to an all-inclusive understanding of human nature and civilization, not just of his patients' individual psyches. Freud's Standard Edition is thus not only his encyclopedic masterpiece, but it is also a remarkably Balzacian one. Throughout the Freudian corpus, a few specific res, the "Case Histories", function both as exemplary instances and pedagogic models for a theory of humanity as a whole so that Dora, the Wolf Man, Little Hans, and the Rat Man have a very similar function in Freud's Standard Edition to the one occupied by Rastignac, Vautrin, César Birotteau, Louis Lambert, and the other central characters in the The Human Comedy.

One way to enact this balancing act between the local and the universal is to establish an apostolic succession in which the new work is seen as the rightful successor of the masterpieces that preceded it. The early years of this century showed an unprecedented use of classical myth for just this purpose. The modernists' widespread reliance on mythological materials is ultimately less important for its specific thematic or even structuring function than as a means of asserting two indispensable, general claims, namely that the new masterpiece is the legitimate, present-day incarnation of the culture's foundational texts and topoi, and that its alertness to the contingencies of the present is congruent with its status as an incipient classic. Baudelaire defined the secret ambition of modern art in exactly these terms, "it longs to be worthy of itself becoming antiquity." 21 Linked to this strategy, but logically independent of it, is the decision to make the text's universality palpable by creating a new kind of language for it that will embody in its very articulation the universal claims of the narrative. Ultimately, not just the work's characters and events, but even its language will be drawn, in the wonderful formula of Finnegans Wake, from "all marryvoising moodmoulded cyclewheeling history." 22

However, no matter how widely an author casts the net of his thematic concerns, nor how polyglot his textual inclusions, the ambition to show and tell the epoch's fundamental truths is a desperately hopeless gamble. The felt obligation of providing his readers with the "key" to unlocking these truths places an intolerable pressure on the masterpiece maker. The increasing shrillness of texts like the Cantos, or the accelerating fragmentation into incompatible plot outlines of Robert Musil's The Man without Qualities, is due in part to the writer's recognition of his task's impossibility. In the case of Pound or Louis-Ferdinand Céline, one solution to the crisis of an unattainable universality is a retreat into a paranoid style and vision. When the author's truth finds no echo in the larger community, the sole accessible model of totalization is the resentment-riddled sense of being an unrecognized sage whose indispensable but unheeded wisdom is blocked by the age's stupidity and malevolence. In brief: the encyclopedic dream of "showing and telling all" requires an omniscience that can decline quickly into the eccentricity of the crank or the political complicity of the demagogue.

In the End There Can Be But One

Perhaps the most peculiar and historically unprecedented corollary of my first category is that the modernist masterpiece must desire the annihilation of any possible rival. Céline was only the most brutally honest modernist masterpiece maker when he replied to an interviewer's question by declaring, "Why do I write? I'll tell you: to make the others unreadable." 23 Moreover, this need has very little to do with merely wishing for the success of one's own texts, and still less with anything resembling so thin a concept as "the anxiety of influence." As the example of Joyce makes clear, the requirement for sole supremacy is not even genre specific, but rather follows from the imperative of universality. In this agon, there are no parallel universes, and so there can only be one ultimate winner. Ulysses, for instance, was intended not merely to render every earlier novel obsolete, but to obliterate music as well by absorbing all of its affective power. This is clearly what Joyce meant when he told Georges Borach, "I've finished the Sirens chapter during the last few days. A big job. I wrote this chapter with the technical resources of music . . . Since exploring the resources and artifices of music, and employing them in this chapter, I haven't cared for music anymore. I, the great friend of music, can no longer listen to it" (JJ, 459). The unrealizability of Joyce's claims is, I suspect, why he reacted with such anger when those claims were resisted, as though the whole status of his text were thrown into doubt if other works could exert a comparable power. After reading "Sirens" to Ottocaro Weiss, for example, Joyce and Weiss went together to a performance of Die Walküre. Joyce listened to Weiss praise Wagner during the intermission and then broke out with, "Don't you find the musical effects of my Sirens better than Wagner's?" When Weiss replied, "No," Joyce at once turned on his heel and did not show up for the rest of the opera (JJ, 460).

It Must Be Impersonal

Everything in the text must be presented so as to remove any sense that an individual author's temperament, interests, and background are responsible for the inclusion of just these details, for the selection of just these puns, ideograms, or mythic parallels. The belated critical catch phrase, "death of the author" is best understood not as a global pronouncement about writing or subjectivity as such, but as a specific technical device intended to safeguard the authority of the work from any trace of a "mere individuality" that must always, in this context, carry the mark of an unacceptable arbitrariness. 24 Hence it is instructive, as well as amusing, to watch Pound and Joyce after the early days of their alliance reciprocally accuse each other of eccentricity and obsessiveness. 25 More fascinating still is the breakdown of the impersonal finish and the reinscription, often in the accents of a threnody, of the author's individual, partial, even confessional voice once the project's contradictions have overwhelmed its willed and indispensable self-deception.

If the imperative of impersonality might still be seen as a self-conscious attempt to recreate the "objective" tone of traditional epic narratives, there is another, strictly modernist conception of the relationship between the artistic ration of a consciousness and any actually lived, biographical identity. When Claude Lantier, a painter in Zola's novel, L'Oeuvre (wonderfully translated, for my purposes here, as The Masterpiece) cries out, "It will kill me and it will kill my wife, my child and everything around me, but it will be a masterpiece, by God," 26 or when Yeats declares, "The intellect of man is forced to choose / perfection of the life, or of the work," 27 they are both insisting on an opposition whose absolutism would have seemed absurd only a century before. Leo Bersani has coined the phrase "the culture of redemption" to describe the idea that art exists to give value to, and thus to salvage, an otherwise meaningless existence. Only art, in the form of a timeless masterpiece, can restore and make whole all the suffering, triviality, and wastefulness of life. 28 From Mallarmé's longing to absorb the world into a single Book, to Stephen Dedalus's explicitly theological formulation: "time's ruins build eternity's mansions . . . in woman's womb word is made flesh but in the spirit of the maker all flesh that passes becomes the word that shall not pass away," the bedrock certainty about the redemptive power of great art remains constant. 29 Although the disruptions, incompleteness, and fragmentation of so many modernist masterpieces function as a kind of immanent critique of the project, they do not ironize it through humor, but rather, through the darker topoi of failure and the fear of failure. The modernist masterpiece, that is, can ironize everything except its own desire to be one. Masterpieces may be pluralistic texts, but faith in masterpieces, it turns out, is the strictest of monotheisms. 30

It Must Be Difficult

"I've put in so many enigmas and puzzles that it will keep the professors busy for centuries arguing over what I meant, and that's the only way of insuring one's immortality." 31 Literary critics are continuing to pile up hecatombs of explanatory volumes that demonstrate the rightness of Joyce's words. But his comment must once have rung far odder than we can easily recognize today. The changing role of the university and the very success of the modernist masterpiece have tended to "naturalize" Joyce's longing to be argued over by academics and obscured just how far-fetched Joyce's hope was when he first uttered it in 1921. At that time, contemporary novels--whether under accusations of incoherence and obscenity or not--were not part of any university curriculum and not likely to become such either. At Oxford, for example, it was not until 1970 that any works published after 1900 could appear on the English Honors Syllabus. Nor were matters all that different in the United States, where William Lyon Phelps, a distinguished Yale English professor, found that his 1910 study On Modern Novelists elicited "astonishment" from reviewers "that a book of essays on contemporary writers should come from a university professor." 32 When Joyce "put in his enigmas and puzzles," he, like Pound and Eliot, was not so much anticipating, but to a significant degree actively creating the situation his phrase seems only to describe. All of these texts were composed from the outset with the intention of being studied, not just read, and their way of advancing such seemingly preposterous expectations required what one might call "a strategy of presumptive immortality" (M, 122). 33 Modernist masterpiece making entails making an entirely new scholarly and critical context in which the works will be received, and this requirement is a constituent part of the whole project, directly affecting the composition of the piece itself.

In a paradoxical return of the repressed, the values of bourgeois probity and industriousness against which artists had proclaimed themselves in rebellion throughout the nineteenth century were transferred largely intact to the aesthetic sphere. Instead of the sprezzatura valued by earlier periods, modernist masterpieces were expected to exhibit the scars of an intense and agonizing labor. The only realm in which Marx's labor theory of value seems entirely convincing is in the domain of the modernist masterpiece, where the artist's labor earns the right to exact a reciprocal effort from the reader. Ultimately, the reader's willingness to undertake that labor depends on his already having accepted the principle that a masterpiece is entitled to make such extraordinary demands. Whether any particular work merits the designation of a masterpiece is open to debate, but the existence of the category itself is not. Joyce's motif of the artist-as-God works precisely because in the unprecedented breadth, depth, and extent of his representation of his characters' existence, he can make them totally legible, and, correspondingly, leave his readers subservient to the fullness of his knowledge. 34 What makes this situation particularly strange, however, is that although the necessary fiction of texts like The Cantos and Finnegans Wake is that we are being taught to read a new kind of universal idiom, what we are actually acquiring is a discrete and nontransferable fluency in a series of heterogeneous, radically incommensurable works. Indeed, learning how to make sense of one modernist masterpiece is of remarkably little help in being able to understand a different one. It is as though the effort to acquire even a preliminary interpretative competence with any particular work encourages a self-protective, mutually confirming consumer loyalty to a product in which both artist and audience have already heavily invested. The Absolute Artist now requires something like an Abject Reader whose devotion to the modernist masterpiece is both self-abnegating and all-consuming. 35 Or, as Joyce said, not all that playfully, to Max Eastman, "The demand that I make of my reader is that he should devote his whole life to reading my works" (JJ, 703).

It Must Be Ideally Real

The oxymoron here is deliberate. The modernist masterpiece simultaneously seeks to escape and to incorporate both the historical-social world from which it springs and the literary tradition whose summa it intends to be. It must, that is, stand in an explicitly dialectical relationship to all previous texts, resolving their antitheses and partialities into the final synthesis of its absolute form. The initial thrust of the much contested and increasingly threadbare catch phrase "postmodernism" arises from the systematic subversion of all of the imperatives I have just listed. Such a critique dismisses the idea that art can redeem lost time or damaged lives, and counters assertions of the masterpiece's universality with an insistence that every work explicitly acknowledge its own historicity and partiality. At its most radical, postmodernism throws into doubt the category of the genius, which from Immanuel Kant to Marcel Duchamp, and especially in Marcel Proust, Pound, Stein, and Joyce, grounded and justified the artist's exempla whose universality was guaranteed precisely and tautologically because they were discovered by a genius. 36

If Hegel's Phenomenology of Mind crystallized the ideology of the universal in the formula, "Truth is the whole," (das Wahre ist das Ganze) the skepticism of the countermovement is admirably captured in Adorno's negative reformulation, "The whole is truth" (Das ganze ist das Unwahre). 37 Often our contemporary rereadings of the modernist masterpieces are grounded in critical perspectives that see only the emancipatory, joyful implications of Adorno's aphorism without recognizing that he is also defining the tragic loss of a utopian dimension once embodied in the categories of aesthetic wholeness and intelligibility. We proceed, that is, as though the gaps and fissures in the modernist masterpieces were simply invitations to a celebratory playfulness rather than scars of an impossible struggle to hold dissolution at bay. Near the end of his poem, when Pound cries out, "And I am not a demigod, / I cannot make it cohere," it is the possibility of a comprehensive modernist masterpiece whose collapse he is confessing (C, 796). We are right to be grateful for that collapse, to prefer the fragments and radiant moments that survive amidst the "errors and wrecks" of Pound's failed attempt at a new, totalitarian synthesis (C, 796). Pound's admission of defeat, however, like Musil's inability to finish his great novel, can move us only if we take seriously the stakes for which both writers were contending.

In the end, perhaps the most riveting feature of the modernist masterpiece is how its imaginative energy is distributed so uncannily between the antithetical movements of integration and dispersal, between a rage for order and a search for what can survive the death of the dream to impose that order. The modernist masterpiece shuttles continuously between a totalizing project it intermittently recognizes as inaccessible, if not outright paranoid, and a contradictory attentiveness to the particular moment and the purely local, contingent realization. If the first impulse is encyclopedic and epic, then the second can be thought of as essayistic, flourishing precisely by refusing to subordinate its curiosity to any larger systems. Essayism's complete absorption in the immediate and local resists subordination to the lure of the whole, and the strain of shuttling between the epic and the essayistic fractures the modernist masterpiece as it is producing it. So, to complete my catalogue, the last imperative ought to encompass the impossibility of accomplishing all the earlier ones, and might read something like: "It Must Resist and Yield To Its Own Essayism Almost Successfully." The realization that, in Musil's wonderful phrase, "even God probably preferred to speak of His world in the subjunctive of possibility . . . for God creates the world and thinks while doing so that it could just as well be done differently," is one that the modernist masterpiece, whose creator's aspirations are more absolutist than those of Musil's imagined Deity, cannot afford to admit until its own splintering compels it to do so, thereby abandoning its claims to an unconditional universalism. 38
