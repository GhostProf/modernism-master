Barbara Bray: In Her Own Words

Marek Ke˛dzierski

I fell in love with Beckett’s work even before I met him personally and had the “real,” once-and-for-all “coup de foudre,” in the late 1950s, through my work as Drama Script Editor, Radio, in the BBC in London . . .

Beckett was already famous in England for Waiting for Godot & Endgame on the stage (1953 & 1957) & his first radio play All That Fall, an instant classic on the Third or cultural Programme (1957). As in the case of Harold Pinter, he too still unfamiliar to the general public except for the radio plays we had commissioned from him, the Department was trying to convey the widest & deepest nature of Beckett’s “voice” by broadcasting readings from his earlier & then little-known prose works . . .

We soon became close friends and colleagues, and until I moved to Paris some years later he used to write to me every day if we didn’t meet. I knew and greatly valued his works already, but it was encountering him in person that was the great epiphany of my life. The 18 years difference in our ages didn’t come into it. I was so much in awe of his genius, goodness, and beauty that it never crossed my mind that we might become emotionally attached . . .

At our first business meeting in Paris, I experienced my first real “coup de foudre,” intensified by further professional meetings later in London & Paris, & though nothing was said about it, it was clear to both of us that something of importance in our lives had happened. I was too awestruck to imagine developments, & was thunderstruck when he phoned me out of the blue while I was directing a radio play in a studio in Broadcasting House in London & invited me out to dinner. It was obvious at once that we were natural soul- and mind-mates, & about then that he started writing to me, & I replying, almost every day. It was not until a few years later that we got around to discovering that we were also natural body-mates as well.

(From Samuel Beckett: A Memoir, by Barbara Bray)

888 I met Barbara Bray [see fig. 7] in 1996 but it was only after Walter Asmus and myself, as the organizers of the Beckett in Berlin 2000 festival, invited her to read “Dante and the Lobster” at Berlin’s Akademie der Künste, that we became friends. During our meetings, not unlike Samuel Beckett, she satirized the efforts of academics to help tout le monde comprehend his work, preferring via activa, convinced that the best way to disseminate his message was to let his words and images speak for themselves. She was visibly very fond of and attached to “her” theatre, the Dear Conjunction Company, a Paris-based troupe which she founded in 1991 with actors Trish Kessler and Les Clack. Playing mostly to English-speaking audiences of expatriates and visitors, by then they had produced Beckett’s shorts and Krapp’s Last Tape as well as the works of mostly British authors, including Harold Pinter who was the company’s official patron. I invited them to produce Krapp’s Last Tape and Pinter’s Ashes to Ashes at a festival in Cracow in 2002 and we were thinking of joint projects in the future. The stroke Barbara suffered one evening in late 2003, shortly after a Dear Conjunction performance, radically changed her life. Partly paralyzed, she had to come to terms with the fact that the rest of her life was to be spent in a wheelchair. After staying in hospital for several months she was placed in a nursing home near Gare de l’Est. Barbara vehemently opposed the prospect of staying there indefinitely and eventually her strong will prevailed and she was able to return to her studio in Rue Seguier after it had been adapted to the needs of a handicapped person. During my visits to the nursing home as early as 2004 I started taping our conversations about her past, something I noticed she could cope with incomparably better than with her present. Her long-term memory was intact: she could remember the smallest details concerning people met in a specific restaurant near the broadcasting studio in London back in 1959. She seemed as eloquent as before her stroke, and continued to tackle the most complex issues with great lucidity, formulating complete sentences, and frequently showing a great deal of humor. But her wit was now displayed only in reference to the past. As soon as we had finished our session, returning to the present was very hard; she seemed all the more worried and anxious. That was the price she paid for her short sojourn in the past. Another price, even higher, was the grief she felt in realizing that it was all gone and done with. In Proust, Beckett writes about “the poisonous ingenuity of Time in the science of affliction.”1 On the whole, however, during the period 2004–2009 when we met to talk, most often about Beckett, I think that speaking of the past eased Barbara’s affliction. If not a cure, it became an effective anesthetic which worked remarkably well for at least a few hours a day. She would read Samuel Beckett’s letters to prepare herself to write about him. Out of some seven hundred letters that I scanned and saved on her computer, so that she could enlarge the image, we read together well over two hundred, frequently with my recording device turned on. Sometimes she would stop and make some remarks in the way of digression; when I listened to it later I noticed that what she had said spontaneously could be written down practically without editing. Her intellectual qualities were remarkable.

Born in London in 1924, Barbara Bray was educated at state schools in London and 889 graduated from Girton College, Cambridge in 1945. She married a former Royal Air Force pilot and gifted poet and went with him to Egypt, where she worked for three years as a lecturer in English language and literature at the University of Alexandria and at a college in Cairo. Barbara returned to England in the early 1950s, and joined the BBC after separating from her husband in 1954. He died in 1958.

As a script editor, writer, and producer in the Radio Drama Department, London, from 1953 to 1960, she was responsible for all play projects and, at an important turning point in the evolution of European and world theatre, working closely especially with new writers. She helped to introduce the general public to the writings of Samuel Beckett, Harold Pinter, and Marguerite Duras, with all of whom she continued to work closely in radio, cinema, theatre, television, and literature. She collaborated with Harold Pinter and Joseph Losey on The Proust Scenario, a film adaptation of À la recherche du temps perdu.

She moved to Paris in 1961, principally to live close to Beckett, and while continuing to work for the BBC as a free-lancer for a few years, she took to translating literary and critical texts and in a relatively short time became an accomplished translator of literature in French. Her translated texts range from works by Crebillon fils, Sartre, Robbe-Grillet, Turgenev, Flaubert, and Amin Maalouf, to Anhouil’s Antigone and Veber’s Diner de cons; from the correspondence between Flaubert and George Sand to the very specialized monograph Lacan by Elisabeth Roudinescu. She won many international prizes for translation, including, on four separate occasions, the prestigious George Scott-Moncrieff Prize for translation from French. She developed a long, difficult, and by her own choice exclusively professional friendship with Marguerite Duras, whose esteem in the English-speaking world owes much to Bray’s brilliant translations of the French writer´s seminal works.

Confined to her bed in the nursing home, preferring discretely to suffer alone rather than ask for help, she did not like to speak about the financial worries that threatened her plan of returning to Rue Seguier. With the support of her friends James Knowlson, Anthony Gill, and Harold Pinter, she was awarded a Civil List Pension from Her Majesty the Queen, and another from the Royal Literary Fund “for her services to literature.” These allowed her to have a live-in caregiver in her small studio. Moving to her own place brought her the comfort of being able to better concentrate on “the project” of her final years, the most important in her life, as she repeatedly said, and one that was to remain far from finished: a personal memoir of Samuel Beckett, commissioned as early as the nineties by Harvill Press.

Her memoir was meant above all as testimony, as she writes in the prefatory fragment: “It was not until some years after his death in 1989, after re-reading some of the biographical and other studies, however admirable, of Beckett and his work, that I realized I was the only person who could bear witness to certain precious and unknown aspects of his being.” Its title, Let Mortals Rejoice . . . , was a reference to a tombstone inscription composed by Jonathan Swift in honor of a friend: “Let Mortals rejoice that there has existed such and so great an ornament of the human race.”

890 “I deliberately refrained from any Boswellian note-taking or anything of that kind, considering it completely incompatible with the nature of our relationship,” states Bray. Rather than a biography, her book was intended to be a personal reminiscence “inspired” by two quite different biographical texts. One was Norman Malcolm’s “nobly simple and restrained” Memoir on Ludwig Wittgenstein, “the philosopher to whose work I introduced Beckett and whom in some ways he greatly resembled.” The other was Genji Monogatari, a Japanese classic novel, written in the 11th century by the courtesan Murasaki Shikibu, known in the West as the Tale of Genji, the Shining Prince, which Barbara had read in Arthur Waley’s translation. To explain her intention she quotes Murasaki’s words from the 1935 George Allen & Unwin edition:

[Such writing] happens because the storyteller’s own experience of men and things has moved him to an emotion so passionate that he can no longer keep it shut up in his own heart . . . something so important that he cannot bear to let it pass into oblivion. There must never come a time, he feels, when men do not know about it.2

The goal Bray set herself in her memoir was, to quote its prefatory fragment:

That there should never come a time when what I had the unique honor & privilege of knowing about certain aspects of S.B. should be forgotten. These being what they were, there was an inevitable risk of producing something that might be derided as hagiography, which of course I’d do my best to avoid. As I would the more common faults of self-appointed “keepers of the flame”: those who try to “appropriate” their subjects, or write out of self or financial interest or to reveal, wrongly, intimate details about the private lives of the famous—“publishing scoundrels,” as Henry James called them in The Aspern Papers.

I last saw Barbara Bray on December 22, 2009, exactly twenty years after Beckett’s death. On Christmas Eve she flew to Edinburgh where she died two months later. Her Memoir remained in notes and fragments, the materials recorded in sound, on paper, and in my memory, which may serve as the second best version of her tribute to the man of her life. They may also be testimony to intellectual capacity and personal modesty of this remarkable woman.

Radio: Beginnings

BarBara Bray: It was all part of a general movement to try to get radio to be a really civilized and international forum for the arts. And the same thing was going on with music and so on. He wrote All That Fall without any consultation with the BBC; it was just that they asked him to do it and we produced this. With the next one [Embers] there was a certain amount of to-ing and fro-ing between us; particularly between him and me because Donald [McWhinnie] was at television then. I can remember some funny correspondence which I’ve still got somewhere. After All That Fall and its huge success in England and our hopes that we would be able to continue in this exploratory vein, we, that’s Donald and I (Donald being the great instigator of this), did a series of readings from the prose and it was usually I who selected the passages. Pat Magee 891 read From an Abandoned Work and Donald produced it. And we did excerpts from Murphy, and from Molloy, and from Malone Dies, and from The Unnamable, and gradually educated the public’s ear to hearing prose of that kind, if there is any other prose of that kind. And so by the time Embers came, their ears were more adjusted to the kind of logic or non-logic, or the kind of use of words which was peculiarly Sam’s. So it wasn’t just isolated incidents. We deliberately did that and there are some absolutely wonderful records of Jack MacGowran and Pat Magee reading these things. We used to send Sam the tapes, some of which he heard and some of which he didn’t. We sent him the tape of Pat Magee reading From an Abandoned Work, with which he had enormous trouble because actors always wanted to use their full diapason when they were acting. What Sam wanted was a kind of almost lifeless croak, ha! [laughs] So if you imagine how eager actors were to define themselves by an almost lifeless croak; you used to have to frankly sit on their heads and stuff their mouths up! But, they got the hang of it in the end and on hearing the tape of Pat reading this—and I dare say we’d all had a few drinks together in London and Paris and so on; we weren’t all unknown to each other—on the strength of hearing Pat’s attempt to produce this particular voice which Sam wanted and which Donald managed to coerce out of him—or curse out of him—Sam wrote . . .

acoustically. To me it was a tour de force, with Magee using certain registers in his voice which were almost, I would say, mimetic. Some kind of [makes a dry throaty sound] there was a kind of sound that was incredible.

BB: But it was very impressive him being at once sort of human and inhuman, wasn’t

it? I mean he didn’t sound like a robot. MK: The physicality, you know . . . BB: Yes. MK: . . . the very physiological . . . côté of his voice . . . BB: Yes. Anyway, all those things, though they sound rather accidental and sort of

capricious, they all actually built up to one thing. Because whenever Sam changed his medium, whether it was from theatre to radio or from radio to television, he always went straight to the essence of the new medium and what there was to be done with it.

MK: And in the process he has learned the technicalities of . . .

BB: Oh yes, he and I had a long correspondence about tape machines and so on and so forth. But it’s really the limits, which are also of course the challenges, of each medium.

892 Our Rapport

BB: We got into the habit of exchanging letters about Embers as he was writing it. He would send me bits of it and I would write back and say what I liked and what I didn’t like and that sort of thing. And when I came here [i.e. France] and it was possible to meet more often and speak on the phone more often, and write more often. He always showed me what he’d written when he met me, you see, and we always went through it together and talked about it. Sometimes he would send it ahead, the night before he came, and I would have already made notes and suggestions. Sometimes we would go through it together and sometimes I would have written to him depending on what our various movements were.

Our rapport as far as work was concerned was complete. I don’t say we never disagreed about the emphasis of a tone or anything like that. [But] we understood each other perfectly. That doesn’t mean that I added more than what I consider a sort of editor’s contribution, and of course sometimes they were helpful and sometimes they were not helpful. But it wasn’t in any sense all this stuff about Brecht, you know, all his girlfriends writing all his plays; there was nothing of that about it in the very slightest.

As soon as I knew his work I was in sympathy with it and knew what it meant and knew what he was saying, or thought I knew, most of it. But our minds were very close and we could always make each other laugh, you see, because he tended to be then rather melancholy and I always tend to be, as you know, rather facetious. I could always make him laugh; we were always laughing, all the time. Because of course his philosophy, his worldview, is ironic and satirical and my sense of humor is sort of facetious, there was certain kind of interface between what he saw as sort of tragicomic and what I saw as sort of farcical-comic. And I have always been very sensitive to the ambiguities of words and to the ambiguities in the collocation of words.

Play. Ussy

MK: Not everyone reading or watching Play on stage realizes that the play is so steeped in his private life. More than any other work. It seems to be so universal.

BB: I should say that very early on I said to Sam there were certain strict reservations on my part about our relationship . . . I knew him very well and one of the most striking and unchanging things about his character was his loyalty, his gratitude, and his absolute horror of hurting people.

MK: Did he intend from the beginning to write something for which a clear incentive was turmoil in his private life?

BB: I said to him: “I really think you should write something which is a sort of a tribute to Suzanne.” When he tried with this, with Play, it didn’t come out like that because it turned out to be farcical. And I think another attempt was Happy Days, but that also got itself twisted. And of course lots of people think that I am Winnie in Happy Days; if it works at all that way it’s rather ironical.

MK: You wrote the first review of Play. I’m quoting from The Observer:

The story itself is, superficially, the most banal tale imaginable: a triangle—man, wife 893 and mistress—which comes to grief. And not only is the basic situation itself abysmally conventional and trite, but Beckett does everything possible to render it ludicrous in its details. All three characters are ordinary, mediocre, lamentable: in short, painfully familiar. The man, scooting breathlessly back and forth between the two women, is perhaps the worst of the bunch: all need and weakness and feeble, if amiable, duplicity. This intentionally commonplace and in current terms despicable material Beckett transmutes into an astonishing, hilarious and moving experience. The most ingenious and comic irony is extracted from this triple counterpoint. Of all Beckett’s works so far, this is the one that most openly approaches the everyday experience of any audience, yet at the same time makes the fewest concessions. The language is of classic simplicity—a single touch gives classic resonance to the ridiculous: “He went on and on. I could hear a mower.” Beckett’s inventiveness, formal mastery and poetic power are now so rich and intense that these three suffering heads conjure up not only three whole lives, but also awaken the reverberations that transform them from the trivial to the universal. Here are people in all their funny, disgraceful, pitiable fragility, and all the touchingness, in spite of everything, of their efforts to love one another, and endure.3

Eventually the subject inspired also yourself to render the situation into a literary form. I mean here your radio drama about Jonathan Swift entitled How I Remember Names.

BB: Working on Play as it was being written and having been to the first performance, the world premiere in Ulm [in 1963] and having been on it from the very beginning and having on my own account been working very much on Swift and his life and work, it struck me as really a clinching proof of the universality of Sam’s play that it fitted to the events of Swift’s life and the situation. His dramatic presentation of it with the people being forced to give evidence of their feeling was a very enlightening way of dealing with Swift and of underlining the universality of Play. As with Sam, Swift was in a position of divided loyalties. This impossible situation between three people, the human situation, was exactly relevant to Swift, somebody who was as committed to Stella and Vanessa as Beckett was to Suzanne and myself.

MK: Although Play plays somewhere in the world beyond, there are clear references to Ussy, like those about burning leaves or Signy-Signets and Sept-Sorts (in the French text), the names of nearby villages. Beckett spent his quiet time there. He told me once Ussy was “necessary.”

BB: Yes, I agree; to get away from all the people who were trying to get him in Paris, to have the time to work in quiet without interruptions. Because he was besieged by people who wanted to see him and he was very kind, didn’t like to disappoint people, so you know, the time would be quite frittered away in Paris. And also, he needed peace and quiet, because being so finely tuned, it was torture to him to have to sit being talked at and asked when he would rather have been quiet and walking or something; he was polite and very kind and available to people but after a certain time it would be difficult, it would make him nervous.

MK: And to work around the house, physically I mean, with his hands . . .

BB: Of course, he loved the garden, he always gardened in the afternoon and cut down trees in the woods in the back of the house and mowed the grass and planted

894 plants and saw after the trees and so on. There was a lot of manual labor. He had a big shed with all his tools and he arranged it, it was always clean. He took walks in the afternoon as well. MK: And the bonfires, as in the snapshots you took in the 70s, that was his element. BB: Yes, quite. MK: He also had a minimum of social life there as well. BB: Of course the Haydens lived quite nearby for a long time and he used to go to dinner with them in the evenings. MK: Ussy was also a very orderly place, I mean his work quarters in the little house. BB: Very ascetic, very monastic. Sam was very methodical about everything. Very simple, no superfluities, whatever. Until he finally decided to get a TV set to watch tennis and rugby. MK: Watching tennis and rugby was a way to relax. BB: He was an athlete himself, he played rugby and tennis and all the rest, and he was interested in it. I remember watching snooker with him. In London, I remember watching Muhammed Ali with him. Because he was a boxer too. He was very interested in sport. He was interested in everything, really. You couldn’t spend your whole life writing, you couldn’t keep up burning candles so fiercely all the time. And of course he did a lot of walking. As a lot of writers have said, their best ideas come when they walk. He was a great walker. MK: What kind of programs did he watch on TV? BB: Just the games; I never saw him watch other programs. I don’t think he was interested in the news. I think he was listening to it on the radio. He was always well informed. MK: Was there a phone in Ussy? BB: Yes, but he didn’t give the number to everybody. But he always used to phone me. MK: Did you like being in Ussy? BB: Yes, it was wonderful.

Poetry and Genre

BB: When he translated things and I would make suggestions because we would sometimes spend days and days and days just trying to find one word and of course, in some areas in English my vocabulary, my experience in English, was wider than his. But then of course in the last result, in last resort, the choice of the word, or the phrase, or the turn of phrase, would depend on how it fitted the text, and how it would play if it was in a play. And of course it was on his judgment of what he was trying to do.

There was a book that was written at about that time which unfortunately I never read, but I read about, and [with which] I agree mainly because of what I saw with Sam. It was called The Poet’s Voice and the theory was that the poet’s poetry or prose, whatever it was, was as he heard the language himself: as his own voice would hear it, and as his own hearing would hear it. I’m quite sure that was true with Sam and that it wasn’t just words on a page; it was a voice and it wasn’t perhaps just his voice—you 895 know he’s always talking about the voices—but it would be a voice speaking in the voice that he heard himself speaking, and in the voices he heard others speaking. I’m sure that’s very important for a serious writer.

He wrote in a language of his own, it was neither Irish nor English, and I think that by the time he left Ireland, his Irish world, which was his world, was already formed. I mean, his imagination, the world of his imagination, was Ireland, whatever he wrote and whatever language he wrote in. And I don’t mean that there was no interaction and interference—in the wider sense—of course there was, but like Camier and Mercier [sic] and so on, and lots of other things, there’s an interchange between certain things which are English and certain things which are French. But it doesn’t clash, it’s all, molded together in his imagination.

It was very revealing that, when we used to do reading on radio, I mean a few public readings we used to do, it was the more uneducated sort of people who found Beckett depressing. Those more available to the analytical meaning didn’t find it depressing. The point about poetry is that it transports what might be miserable and mean and low and resentful into something noble and glorious. It was like Milton: I mean when Milton describes the devil, Satan and Lucifer, he can’t help describing them in a way that is awe-inspiring and full of great characteristics . . .

I think the evolution of the forms he used was entirely his own, but whenever he changed the form from say radio to television he changed his technique completely. When he changed from stage to radio, he changed completely. And in From an Abandoned Work he has these absolutely fabulous sentences like, “Ducks have done this.” I mean, he can use ordinary words and write a ridiculous sentence and it is just very funny. “Ducks have done this,” in the middle of this narrative!

Comic Element

BB: He is using words in such a way that they close what they say in some celestial light, some aura, which makes them seem significant and valuable and makes you see how everything we say and think we have to say and think in terms which, which are quite funny. Whatever we have to say, it almost always has a funny side to it. I’m not setting this up as a literary theory but I think some people are born with a kind of eye which sees everything sort of quizzically. I mean it doesn’t mean they can’t see tragedy or cruelty or anything like that but there’s something in your brain that makes you see [that] there’s a sort of comic or ridiculous or facetious or incongruous thing about it, and then it makes almost everything seem quite a different genre from what it is if you take it literally. I mean people like Dickens and Zola describe terrible social conditions but they don’t make you laugh. You don’t want to laugh about a description of Nana or any of the others; it’s just reporting.

Actually everything was to him a sort of joke, I don’t mean that he didn’t take things seriously. It’s the same with me: potentially, because of all the ambiguities and differ

896 ent ways you can stress things and the way some people think some details are far too important, some of no consequences at all: everything really is, potentially, social farce because you can add or change a very small thing, a comma or emphasis, and it can make things seem to be appealing to the audience’s pity or piety . . . You can just shift it and it becomes something completely different.

Politics

BB: He didn’t like talking about politics. As you know I’m a bolshie, and I’ve always made myself laugh because I’ve always thought of his description of the politician in one of the nouvelles, you remember, when he has a satirical description of somebody else’s soapbox ranting and raving about this and that, and I always could see his eyes slightly glazing over when I got on my high horse about some political thing. He wasn’t interested. And I got him to sign some petitions for freedom of speech and so on.

He wasn’t really interested in things at that level but of course as you know he let all the royalties of [Antoni] Libera, he gave them to Libera and he sent lots of money to lots of people and gave all the Nobel money away and of course he was in favor of humane things rather than inhumane things. But you know what politics is like; he knew what politics was like, through being in Germany before the war, and being in France for a long time and having an eyeful of that, and of course in Ireland as well.

Not I, Mysticism

BB: I think this Not I thing is a motif throughout his work, from the beginning. And it’s the sort of thing that is close to mysticism. I don’t know whether you know much about the history of mysticism all over the world, and all the asceticism of the practice of mysticism, whereby you divest yourself of all interests and all activities until you’ve got yourself down to your essence? The religious mystics all thought that when you got yourself down to this small center which doesn’t take in any of the worldly things but takes in the cosmos, the religious mystics think that the farther away you get from yourself the nearer you get to God. But I don’t buy that. I’ve studied the mystics a lot and I think their sense of exaltation is not because they’ve got near to something else, it’s because they’ve got away from the world. The farther away you get from the world, and worldly preoccupations and ambitions and passions—I don’t mean you can’t have them—but that state of being, that particular state of being, is not because you’ve got nearer something else, it’s because you’ve got farther away from all the petty things.

MK: That’s why they were exalted?

BB: Yes, I think so. I think it’s like a sort of purification. Well, that’s what it was supposed to be. Nearly all mysticism has, you know, the cloud of unknowing and the ladder of being all the rest of it. You’re supposed to mount, stage by stage, and cast aside all preoccupations and all petty considerations until finally you reach the state of, I suppose, grace, where you’re more or less pure. You’ve escaped from the world and its snares and all the rest of it, and for a few moments you’re free. But how are 897 you going to put that into words? That was the problem. I don’t mean he [Beckett] thought about that, about it literally, but trying to use words to get to the state where there are no words, that’s a bit of a problem.

I think the word was love. But that’s a rather wild guess. Not love in the sense of amorousness but love as something which permeates the universe. Or is a sort of ingredient, the ingredient of life, and of everything that’s worthwhile. But not, as I say, love, sexual love. I mean that came into it of course; that was one of the reasons he was so wonderful. But to express the inexpressible: because the thing about mysticism, it doesn’t have any expression, it’s beyond words. It’s not dealing with what words were invented to cope with.
