Music, Modernity, and the Foreign in the New Germany *

Philip V. Bohlman

Figures

Straßenmusik

Music fills the streets of the New Germany. 1 Straßenmusik, or "street music," is everywhere to be heard. On a given day in virtually any German city with a population greater than one hundred thousand, one can encounter an array of performance sites separated from each other by, perhaps, one hundred feet, a distance sufficient to prevent the performances of one group from interfering with those of another. On this same day, the musicians performing would surely represent various mixtures of Andean pan-pipe music, a Slavic folk-music troupe, a youthful chamber trio from the local music academy, Roma or Sinti musicians assuming various guises, an American with a guitar and minimal stock of songs from the 1950s and 1960s folk-music revival, and a desperate-looking German in a wheelchair, playing a cassette recorder as a musical instrument. After one has spent some time in German cities, it may well seem that music is a permanent fixture; the Andean pan-pipe ensembles are everywhere; so too is Irish folk music in one form or another. Street musicians, nevertheless, are mobile, and many travel from city to city so that they might find new audiences, ready for new sounds and new entertainment.

Passersby respond to this street music in different ways. Some listen intently, particularly to the most foreign and exotic music, or to the youthful prodigies from the music academy. If the musical performance is not particularly accomplished or the musicians present a rather ragged-looking front, pedestrians march quickly by, looking with an intense nervousness in another direction, but usually still dropping some coins in the hat or guitar case on the sidewalk. For the most part, street musicians earn well from their performances. They are sensitive to the flow of pedestrian traffic and rarely situate themselves in such a way that they intrude upon the coming and going along the street. To intrude physically with pedestrian traffic would also bring them into conflict both with the merchants, whose shops are the eventual goal of the pedestrians, and with the police, whose responsibility it is to maintain orderliness. It is hardly surprising, therefore, that street music has come to provide one of the ways with which German cities represent and disseminate their own public images. Street music makes the city a pleasant place to visit and to shop, to enjoy and to be entertained.

Other musical traditions frequently inflect this soundscape, and the exact mixture of tra-ditions usually indicates shifting public attitudes toward the ways in which the city represents the past and present. In the New Germany, these shifting public attitudes are evident in the growing presence of foreignness, not just the Slavic troupes from Eastern Europe, which have increased markedly in number during the past four years, but in the repertories drawn from Jewish and Turkish traditions. These repertories transform the streets because they are musics to be listened to, not just heard. 2 They direct the flow of pedestrian traffic and the pace with which foreignness can be released from the narratives of the past and integrated into the struggle to build a multicultural present.

Street music is no single musical style or repertory, but rather a sound mix from the past and present, which also cuts across national, ethnic, and racial borders. Street music is also not simply an international phenomenon. If the mix of repertories suggests a sort of world music, a global potpourri played and played out for a society capable of connecting itself to cultures throughout the world, closer observation and listening fix instead on the German-ness of the conditions that make this modern street music possible. Political themes in many German songs are local; others refer directly to the problems inherent in Germany's growing political presence in a Europe struggling with union on one hand and resurgent nationalism on the other. The local is evident also in the songs of those who sing in dialect or in foreign-language repertories from across the borders. In these ways, street music marks and remarks upon many of the conditions that have made the New Germany possible and that establish the historical connections between the New Germany and its past.

The modernity that Straßenmusik represents is unavoidable. The modern German city is not a complex of simply streets, but of streets converted to Fußgängerzonen, or "pedestrian zones." It is as if the twentieth-century German equivalent of Walter Benjamin's Parisian arcades is the pedestrian zone. 3 The twentieth century, in fact, has necessitated the German pedestrian zone, which replaced many of the streets of the inner city destroyed during World War II; it is as if the pedestrian zone is a monument to the past, at once given meaning by and establishing routine with the rhythm of pedestrians going about their business. 4 In the German pedestrian zone, the aural replaces the visual, mapping the urban German land-scape with sound. 5 The pedestrian must negotiate this landscape not as the flâneur, choosing the route according to whim or fancy, but rather as the good Bürger, following those paths that map out the route, that routinize the everyday.

If the soundscape possesses the qualities of everyday experience, its individual com-ponents, nevertheless, are highly differentiated in the New Germany. The musicians whose aural domains continually reconfigure the street and the historical stage it creates for the everyday represent vastly different musical traditions. The street is not the only stage on which these new forms of foreignness have emerged to contest the modern German cultural landscape. A Yiddish folk-song revival and the mass production of Turkish music have increasingly benefited from an expanded market and media interest in the past decade. The bins in German record stores overflow with Yiddish CDs and cassettes, most of them made by young Germans, but many of them productions of "historical recordings," remastered artifacts and rare recordings recuperated as documents of pre-Holocaust Europe. 6 For many Germans this music memorializes "their" Jewish past. This past, however, never existed, at least in Germany, where Yiddish was never a modern vernacular and Jewish klezmer music was never more than an exotic import from Eastern Europe. 7 These attempts do serve as modern versions of previous music histories, for example those that yielded earlier Yiddish folk-song revivals (e.g., at the turn of the century and during the 1920s). 8 The revival of Yiddish folk song sustains itself as another form of memorializing the past.

The growing presence of Turkish music in Germany depends on a different transform-ation of the cultural landscape, an implosion from within that has necessarily spilled over to the public sphere. Turkish music initially entered German intellectual history together with orientalist passions, the poetry of Goethe, notably West-östlicher Divan, 9 as well as music historical writings, the best-known of which was Raphael Georg Kiesewetter's Die Musik der Araber, which despite its name was based largely on Turkish sources. 10 The music of new Turkish musicians, however, is not the same as that imagined by the orientalist, but rather a modern popular music, which chronicles the experiences of Gastarbeiter (guest workers), who keep Germany's industrial machinery running but remain suspended between the cultures of two worlds. The Turkish music that fills Turkish-German television and appears in new published and recorded anthologies is by no means a revival; like its earlier incarnation, it depends on a modernity born of the expansion of industry in a country whose own population is unable to provide a sufficient work-force to keep the assembly lines moving. Denied German citizenship, Turkish guest workers have become an irreplaceable cog in Germany modernity.

Modernity and foreignness are therefore inseparable from the street music of the New Germany. The conditions that undergird street music are the same as those that conflate modernity and foreignness, while rendering their relationship uneasy: their cohabitation in the music of the city is an unsettling reminder of the past. Modernity, as I wish to examine it in this essay, arises because of the particular ways in which past and present are connected. Through musical performance, a composition from the past or a song that signifies an event of the past enters the present. In addition to connecting past and present, music also exhibits the capacity to embody difference, hence the foreignness that penetrates German modern-ity. I should also wish to argue in this essay that German modernity, like German street music, is not simply an international phenomenon or another version of globalization. German modernity depends, so I shall argue, on the specific ways in which the present can index the past; music--especially street music--renders these ways specific.

Street music creates venues in which the past is contested and challenges traditionalized notions of music's ability to convey identity--particularly the intractable presence of Germanness, the will to rediscover the nationalism that has reproduced tragedy so effectively throughout the past two centuries. It is precisely in street music, moreover, that the constructed distinctions between folk, popular, and élite music come undone, for it is a music that singles out no one audience, but rather invites all to listen. This, too, is a condition of modernity in the New Germany. Modernity has historically undergirded the arts in Germany. "New music," that is, "musical modernism," receives considerable financial support from public coffers, and the production of modern music in centers such as Darmstadt and at festivals such as Donaueschingen thrives in unparalleled fashion. During the past decade new musical practices have proliferated in Germany, reestablishing connections to more troubling moments in the German past and undergirding modernity in its generation of German history. The places in which one finds these new musical practices assume many forms and create diverse publics. In fact, these new musical practices would seem to mirror many different Germanies.

The insistent presence of modernity in public musical practices not only makes the proliferation of Germanies possible, but also reifies them through forms of performance that connect past and present. Music played out before the public may narrate that past as if it were the present. It therefore is of only secondary importance whether the history narrated by music recreates a moment of Reformation history, the wedding dances played by a Jewish klezmer ensemble, or the songs of Peruvian migrant workers. Such musics are performed on many different stages in Germany and attract diverse audiences. These audiences may well be an élite, concert-going public; they may well be shoppers negotiating the pedestrian zone. I should like to argue here, in fact, that it is characteristic of German modernity that music challenges many different publics to confront the presence of foreignness in Germany in the past and present.

The Straßenmusik of the New Germany embodies what initially seems like the ineluctable contradictions of modernity, the juxtaposition of tradition and contemporaneity, of folk music and the technologies of modern music. Music itself--as a performative art--makes these contradictions possible, for it so effectively uses the present to index the past. It is impossible to shake the burden of the past from music, and yet each performance reflects the conditions that made it possible. The Straßenmusik of the New Germany is therefore no more separable from the revivals of invented traditions or the steady production of German automobiles than from the busy traffic of pedestrians, whose affluence and engagement with music are necessary for the financial support of musical performance of all kinds, in the concert hall or on the street. If indeed sharp political differences separate the German from the street musician, the performance of music unites them. 11 German street music, moreover, has historically been at base political, and hence transforms the pedestrian zone into a site for the contestation of contemporary problems. That resulting sense of expectation, nevertheless, is not at base political but rather apolitical, ameliorated by the long history of Straßenmusik in Central Europe. 12

The contradictions of modernity embodied by street music are at once immediately apparent and immediately displaced from the moment of its production. The resurgence of Jewish, Turkish, and other musics representing foreignness during the past decade conveys the immediacy of a persistent problem confronting Germany throughout the twentieth century: namely the presence of non-Germans on its soil. At various historical moments, these non-Germans have borne the designations of different "races" (Rassen) or the somewhat more neutral designations of different "peoples" (Völker). Their differences, nevertheless, demanded that they occupy special places in German society and history. These places, when properly maintained, can mask and hide the conflicts of race, yet history has dictated a repetition of the same acts of displacement, necessitating repeated forms of deracination, that is, of excluding foreignness from notions of Germanness. Contemporary Straßenmusik is one of the most recent, most public, and most modern of such forms.

The entangled interrelations of music, modernity, and foreignness not only symbolize but have become a simulacrum for the presence of the modern pedestrian zone in Germany. In this essay I wish to interrogate this entanglement, adopting a rhetorical strategy that reflects the contradictory ways in which one negotiates the pedestrian zone itself. We shall move, therefore, from site to site, from performance to performance, not so much to prove that we can unravel the fabric of modernity or that we have solutions for the troubling responses to racism and foreignness in German history. Instead, the musical performances we shall encounter here will represent the conditions of modernity that make this entanglement possible. We shall confront modernity, then, as we would in the New Germany, as an every-day experience, whose components, nonetheless, are anything but everyday.

Die Moderne: The Aesthetic of Foreignness at the Turn of the Century

Our lecture halls were the streets, the coffee houses, and also on occasion the Reichstag.

--Heinrich Hart, Literarische Erinnerungen, 1907 13

German artists and intellectuals consciously began to cultivate the concept of modernity (die Moderne) at the end of the nineteenth century. Imbuing the concept with revolutionary potential, these artists and intellectuals, such as Heinrich Hart, one of the ideologues of the so-called Berliner Moderne, moved modernity to the streets of the city. Formed first from a sense of rejecting the past, the new aesthetic of modernity encountered foreignness on the streets, a foreignness formed out of the migration from rural areas to the city, and out of immigration to the city, largely of Eastern European ethnic groups. The city, in turn, became a context for modernity, but this context mixed in various forms of foreign culture represented by new residents and new communities. Modernity, therefore, assumed no single form in Berlin, Munich, or Hamburg, but instead reflected the flux and complex bricolage that an expanding urban population fostered. 14 Berlin guidebooks even took pains to publicize the city's modernity as multicultural, publishing statistics that listed the different national origins of its new residents, proclaiming at the end of one such set of statistics that "the main railway terminals were situated in such ways that the foreigner [der Fremde] will find himself immediately caught up in the swirl of the gigantic city." 15 Modernity thus assumed local forms, which encouraged Jewish and Eastern European intellectuals to regard Berlin as the site for establishing new forms of artistic production, even those with political ends. 16

The new modernity also signaled a liberation of the many places in which the arts were produced and displayed or performed, were made public. On one hand, these places were physical, the lecture halls of the universities that Heinrich Hart and his colleagues avoided, or the institutions that canonized the artistic production of late romanticism. On the other hand, these places were temporal, for it was a liberation from history--the oppressive spaces carved out by Hegel and Heinrich von Treitschke, occupied by "historical peoples" (Geschichtsvölker)--that provided the essential literary impulse for aesthetic modernity. The processes of liberation, however differently they were construed, nevertheless ran head-on into the contradictions of modernity. Munich poets, such as Ludwig Thoma, adapted modernity to dialect and to the forms of folk literature (e.g., "D'Marie," the eponymous poetic narrative of a waitress), and criticized the foreignness that increasingly characterized Munich. 17 In contrast, non-Germans played an important role in staking out new aesthetic turf, for example, Vassily Kandinsky, through his seminal position in the Blaue Reiter movement, and Arnold Schoenberg, through his break with tonality in music and his position as aesthetician for the so-called New Viennese School. Both Kandinsky and Schoenberg, moreover, tempered their modernism with folk and popular traditions, Kandinsky with folk Catholicism and Schoenberg with cabaret.

The contradictions in turn-of-the-century modernity were by no means isolated phenom-ena in German intellectual history. German science, too, concerned itself, at times obses-sively, with the definition of progress, one of several conditions giving way to modernity, through its dialectical relation to Rasse and Volk. 18 By the beginning of the twentieth century, these terms were essential to the understanding of difference. Rasse might mean "species," used for example to describe different animal species. When describing humans, Rasse referred at one level to biological differences, but at another to the distinctive ways in which peoples created culture. Such terminology was totally scientistic in its capacity to parse the world into meaningful differences.

Progress and modernity in music, too, relied on this scientism. Central European music-ologists conflated racial difference and teleological history, "heredity and development" as Richard Wallaschek referred to them, demonstrating unequivocally that the greatness of modern German music was an inevitable component of the culmination of world history. 19 Central Europeans epitomized what German folk song scholars such as Werner Danckert called Geschichtsvölker, or "people with history," whereas other Europeans represented the Randvölker, the "people at the edges," that is, "marginal people." 20 By the turn of the century, however, the borders between edges and history were collapsing, confronting peoples with or without history with the conditions of musical modernity.

The measurement of racial and ethnic differences in music continued during the course of the twentieth century. 21 The exact balance between biological and cultural explanations of these musical differences shifted and continues to shift, sometimes politically correct, at other times not. Quantification relies on observable phenomena in the music itself--harmony, as the supreme achievement of a people with history--but also on the proximity of musical to language structure. Seemingly neutral classificatory judgments connect music to place, privileging those repertories and genres that are bodenständig, "rooted in the soil." The pedestrian zone, in contrast, offers little chance of rooting musical traditions in soil; its musicians are clearly without history, perpetually in need of seeking out new places in which to perform modern musics, whose timelessness confuses its relation to history.

Excursus 1--Das Judentum in der Musik ( Jewishness in Music)

[Of all the street songs and hits in Goldfaden's operettas his] "The Orphan" from the Witch particularly stands out for me. When I was a child, this song could be heard issuing from every window and gate. These songs, performed by the beggars who went around to every courtyard in Warsaw and Lódz and sang into the windows, tear your heart apart, and not just if you are a child.

--Chemjo Winawer, 1936 22

In 1933, the new National Socialist government of Germany conceived of a cultural ghetto, in which all Jews in the major cities of Germany would be forced to live: the Jüdischer Kulturbund. 23 The Kulturbund presented Jews with a new dilemma, for its aim was to prevent them from interacting with Germans in the cultural life of urban Germany; Jewish artistic life was to be isolated. That these new laws arose for racial reasons is obvious, but they also resulted from a German response to modernism in the arts, with which Jews were explicitly connected (or for which they were blamed). Jewish students would be blocked from attending German universities, and Jewish orchestral musicians would no longer be able to play with non-Jewish colleagues in symphony or opera orchestras. There was an alternative, however, namely the possibility of building up an entirely Jewish cultural life; indeed, this was the only alternative. In the early years of the Kulturbund, the alternative, even though greeted with skepticism and bitterness, unleashed a flurry of cultural activity and organization. Jewish orchestras and Jewish theater flourished; Jewish publishers (e.g., Schocken in Berlin) expanded their lists; evidence of a modern Jewish aesthetic in the arts began to take shape.

The landscape of the Kulturbund, soon synonymous with the cityscape of Jewish Berlin, quickly acquired the contradictions of modernity. The means of artistic production depended on the city, literally on the possibilities of negotiating the streets of Berlin in order to reach a concert or theatrical performance. The alternative of creating Jewishness in music or in the other arts unsettled the urbane culture of the German-Jewish community. At one level, it was an alternative that Jewish intellectuals and artists had rejected, for example when polemically voiced by the anti-Semitic Richard Wagner. At another level, there was little consensus what a Jewish music was, what its fundamental melodic and formal structure were, and where its modern institutional venues were. The efforts to create a Jewish culture in the German city were ironically successful, with sold-out concert seasons and ambitious artistic experiments of all kinds documenting the transformed Jewish neighborhoods in the German city. By Kristallnacht in November 1938, the boundaries of these neighborhoods again collapsed, ultimately during the Holocaust destroying those who failed to escape. The destruction of this German-Jewish cultural space was paradoxical, for in fact a history of Jewish aesthetic modernity had formed and had subverted the possibility of a German modernity without Jewishness in music. 24

In 1992, the Kulturbund again made an appearance in Berlin, historicized and memorialized by exhibits at the Akademie der Künste in Berlin. 25 A half-century later, the experiments that transformed the public spaces of Jewish Berlin had been reduced to the semipublic spaces of the exhibit, fixed in history because of their overt foreignness. Even within contemporary German historicism, it is difficult to know what to do with the artistic activities of the Kulturbund, for the theaters and concert halls in which they took place--often, but not exclusively synagogues--no longer exist, nor can they be revivified by new performances. New histories--modern histories--have accrued in the streets that once connected the performances of the Kulturbund orchestra to the synagogues and libraries of the 1930s. Defined at that time by those who passed along them, these streets have different meanings today. It is only through different cultural routes that musicians in the 1990s can represent the modern Jewish music of the 1930s.

German folk song demonstrates a seemingly obsessive concern with place. Images of place yield entire genres, notably the Heimatlied, the "song of the homeland." Folk song differ-entiates regions through their specific histories and dialects. The song of the alpine meadow is no less characteristic of the folk-song canon than the urban broadside ballad, sung from printed leaflets on the street and purchased by passersby for their own entertainment at home. The musical connection between language and place is central to that canon, so it is hardly surprising that folk song does far more than describe the places and regions of the German cultural landscape. Many songs, in fact, symbolically bind regions on the cultural map, establishing the conditions for their maintenance and even for their defense. The well-known "Wacht am Rhein" ("Watch on the Rhine") and its many variants provide one of the clearest calls to action, should the enemies of Germany challenge its western border, which the Rhine forms, at least for a few hundred miles in southwestern Germany. The Rhine, as a signifier of Germany, is the subject of countless other folk songs, as well as composed musical works, ranging from the Heine-Silcher "Die Lorelei" to Wagner's Das Rheingold. Its musical monumentality derives in no small measure from the danger that its loss would mean for Germany.

The Rhine River, in effect, maps diverse conditions of Germanness. Folk songs and operas alike fetishize the river's history, representing it through performance as a means of mem-orializing the past. In "Wacht am Rhein," it is the confrontation with France over Alsace-Lorraine that enters popular history. The physical monuments that line its shores--inscribed in the experiences of thousands of tourists each year, usually with the mnemonic assistance of loudspeakers blaring "Die Lorelei" or "Wacht am Rhein"--undergird what the river repre-sents in the German historical imagination. Again, the performative quality of music represents, and even monumentalizes, the past in remarkably flexible ways. The historically controversial "Deutschlandlied," still the national anthem of Germany, utilizes a melody, itself perhaps a Croatian folk melody, that was adapted by an Austrian composer (Franz Joseph Haydn) in the service of the Hungarian court (Esterházy). Its monumentality, never-theless, is obvious, for countless historical experiences have become interwoven into the song through its performances in string quartets, Protestant hymns, Olympic games, concentra-tion camps, and films about World War II.

Music and monuments often function together in the reproduction of the past as present in Germany. 27 Busts of great composers line concert halls, and musical auditoriums of all kinds occupy a central position of particular social and political importance. The late nine-teenth-century obsession with massive form in German romantic music coincides precisely with the mass production of physical monuments in Germany. 28 This parallel between monuments and music results from indirect associations, for the two refer to the past in quite different ways. Oskar Negt and Alexander Kluge argue that the history-making potential of monuments resides on their surfaces, whereas in fact at a deeper level in the monument history has only an impoverished representation. 29 Song, in contrast, possesses an internal capacity to reproduce history through performance, whereas its surface qualities (e.g., the melody of the "Deutschlandlied," which covers and allows very different texts) can only incompletely represent history because the meaning of the surface depends entirely on the experience of the listener. Music and monuments therefore complement each other.

One of the modern sites in the New Germany where music and monuments function most effectively in this respect is the street. On its surface, the pedestrian zone is itself a monument, often memorializing the past that was destroyed by bombing in World War II. Other monuments punctuate the surface of the pedestrian zone, however, memorializing city founders or reporting the former residence of a cultural or political figure once occupying a building on this site. The Holocaust monument, too, has become an omnipresent historical trope in the German city, 30 ironically conflicting with other monuments to the fallen soldiers of past wars, also ubiquitous in German cities. Street music, in quite different ways, erects temporary monuments. Passersby pause to listen, but also to gaze upon a performance fixed against the backdrop of the street. The street musician provides a fluid monumentality, moving from day to day to different spots along his or her routes (figs. 4 and 5); those who fail to move also fail to maximize their potential earnings and to curry the favors of shop owners who alternatively benefit from and tire of musicians near the doors of their shops. 31 Street music may acquire a radical potential to reconfigure the public sphere, to alter its meanings as different groups occupy and aim to use it as a space in which to demonstrate their different social and political agendas. The modernity of the music itself opens the public sphere to precisely such reconfiguration.

Excursus 2--Arbeiter and Gastarbeiter: Sites of Resistance

Song has provided German workers with one of the most powerful forms of resistance. At once organizing laborers into action and voicing their discontent with the machines of modernity, the workers' song (Arbeiterlied) has become a genre unto itself in modern Germany. 32 As such, the workers' song embodies and conveys German history, particularly the history of resistance against authority, which since the 1930s has often been so con-structed as paralleling and undergirding "democratic" and "revolutionary" history. Implicit in these historical streams, channeled by the working class, is an empowerment of the worker to take charge of history. Not surprisingly, then, the major folk-music anthology of the German Democratic Republic, Wolfgang Steinitz's Deutsche Volkslieder demokratischen Charakters aus sechs Jahrhunderten, cast the history of the German people as one of struggle chronicled by song. 33

The historicizing potential of folk song was by no means a trope found only in the former East Germany. At the local level, workers' choruses emerged in the early nineteenth century as the standard bearers for a sweeping folk-song movement, and by the twentieth century these local organizations often shaped their repertories to chronicle the struggle between modernity and the individual at the local level. Labor movements had the power to organize the local at a global level, and to this end they employed songbooks as means of fostering an aesthetic and political lingua franca. The songs of these labor movements migrated from songbook to songbook and from chorus to chorus, yielding an aesthetic solidarity. Songs such as the "Moorlied" quickly came to unite the local and the international. Composed in 1933 by Rudi Goguel and set to texts by a miner named Esser 34 and Wolfgang Langhoff in Papenburg, one of the first concentration camps, primarily for members of the Communist Party, by 1935 the "Moorlied" had become an international symbol of revolution. As concentration camps multiplied, both in number and in their capacity to imprison diverse groups of individuals posing an imagined threat to the German nation, so too did the "Moorlied" return to the local level, where it circulated in the oral traditions of virtually every camp. After the Holocaust, the "Moorlied" became a specifically national symbol for the struggle against fascism, for the most part losing, however, its local and international currencies.

The political nature of the workers' song serves it well as part of a musical repertory for the street. Individual songs may be topical--that is, local and specific to the political moment--but they may also rely on the public's knowledge of standard repertories. 36 Analyses of the repertories of individual street singers, at least those who fashion themselves as Liedermacher (singer-songwriters), reveal these repertories to signify diverse forms of resistance. 37 On one hand, the street seems to be an unlikely site for songs from workers' movements or political protest, which, after all, reflect little of the spirit that shoppers and tourists invest in their walks. On the other hand, the street has been the primary site of resistance--aesthetic and political--throughout much of modern German history. 38

The struggle of workers against modernity has not disappeared from the New Germany, if indeed a new worker, the Gastarbeiter, is carrying it forth. The nature of that struggle has changed, or rather the foreignness that symbolizes it has changed. The music of neither singer-songwriter nor street musician has abandoned this struggle. Wolf Biermann, the most famous German contemporary singer-songwriter, utilizes Turkish musicians and musical styles in some songs, while elsewhere adapting Yiddish songs to his personal style. 39 The expectations of listeners accustomed to his aesthetic of resistance, therefore, conflate Arbeiter and Gastarbeiter in Biermann's songs, as well as in the rock music accompanying the public demonstrations protesting the violence against Turkish guest workers and their families. Resistance--historical and modern--enters the songs of the street, making them sites for the contestation that inevitably takes place on the street.

No single leitmotif pervades German folk song as extensively and as malleably as Heimat, in the strictest sense, "home," but in the folk imagination and usage, "homeland." Heimatlieder, "songs of the homeland," evoke conflicting senses of the homeland to which one is bound and the homeland for which one longs. Regional and nationalistic songs remain at base Heimatlieder, and they usually fill volumes designated as such. German emigrant folk-song repertories generally privilege Heimatlieder, which refer not to a New World, but to the Old World, the homeland left behind. For German-speaking communities of Eastern Europe, forced to return to Germany after the defeat in World War II, Heimatlieder historicize the homeland left behind--villages in Romania, Poland, or Russia--not the homeland, Germany, to which they are bound by German bloodline. 41 The song of the homeland represents a place perennially German, at once recuperating that place and rendering it an historical presentness.

It is not by coincidence that Heimatlieder are folk songs, for the pair Volk and Heimat provides one of the most cloying tropes in popular German historiography. 42 The folk are inseparable from their homeland; one defines the other. Other conceptual pairs often take up and then modify the trope of folk and homeland, perhaps the best-known of which is Blut und Boden, "blood and land," one of the slogans justifying German expansion during World War II. These conceptual pairs inevitably anchor race to place; that is, they equate Germany with the land occupied by Germans. The Banat region of Romania or Silesia in Poland becomes German because Germans lived there, transforming place through their presence. Heimatlieder make history portable, reinventing the connection between race and place with each performance, regardless of its venue. Songs of the homeland are, moreover, geographically specific, for they catalogue local events and places, the local landscape and local customs. This historical mapping is deeply encoded in the song itself; a melody, for example, may index the homeland, even if texts sometime change. 43

Songs of the homeland are by no means limited to dialect regions, emigrant Germans, or resettled Eastern European Germans. "Du getto main," a Yiddish folk song from the Vilna ghetto, possesses all the traits necessary for a Heimatlied, not only because of its direct use of typical themes and vocabulary, but also because it localizes the history and events of a specific Heimat. That it parodies the story of ghetto residents who must steal food is possible only because German- and Yiddish-speaking Jews during the early twentieth century had transformed the ghetto into a central symbol for the place occupied by Jewish communities confronting modernity. 44 The ghetto song was at first not a folk song, for it did not derive from oral tradition in the shtetl, but rather from the migration to the city, where Jews became laborers in a rapidly industrializing society. The ghetto was a modern space, defined by the streets of the city and the life on those streets. 45 The ghetto song was therefore about accommodating the dilemma of foreignness fostered by modern society. The homeland of the ghetto song represented this dilemma, situating race again in the repertories of German folk and popular music and in German modernity.

The German city has less willingly yielded itself to the formation of other homelands since World War II. Resettlement problems have taken precedence over the remapping of urban spaces to accommodate a multicultural society. Historical memory seems, instead, to be afflicted by amnesia, forgetting about Jewish quarters in the German cities, such as the Scheunenviertel in Berlin, or about the various ghetto-like juxtapositions of race and place during the Third Reich, whether these were putatively cultural, as in the Jüdischer Kulturbund, or political, as in the concentration camp. The Kreuzberg district in Berlin, nevertheless, contains one of the largest Turkish communities in the world, and refugees seeking asylum occupy barracks abandoned since 1989 by the American, French, English, Canadian, and Soviet occupation forces. The new Randvölker, "marginal people," inhabit new margins, namely those of the city, reconfiguring also the urban modernity built previously by the Geschichtsvölker, by those "people with history." The irony persists that Volk and Heimat remain coupled, but that songs now contest the homeland available or not available for foreigners in the New Germany.

Excursus 3--The Circus as Musical Heimat for Foreignness

Elaborate circus posters bedeck the walls, bus stops, and billboards of all German cities throughout the summer. Circuses take place during other parts of the year, but it is in the summer that they are most mobile and can move quickly from city to city, from village festival to urban extravaganza. Whether or not the circus possesses specifically German qualities is moot, though many would claim this to be the case; 46 circuses consciously represent themselves as international, even as stages on which the world's diversity is put on display. The German circus--or the circus in Germany--does, in fact, put rather particular German passions on display. Not least among these is the modern version of the "Wild West Show," much beloved at the end of the nineteenth century, when Wild Bill Cody brought his to Germany, staging it even before the gaze of Kaiser Wilhelm II. The Wild West, particu-larly with its "cowboys and Indians," became a greater fascination in the German popular imagination through the stories of Karl May, whose novels--in department-store editions or multitudinous movies--are well known to all German readers. The modern German circus has transformed the literary theme of the Wild West to a grand form of spectatorship and located it in the center ring.

The German circus poster on the street announces that a circus is in town, using the head of an Indian chief stylized according to the popular imagination. The same poster announces that other forms of foreignness will be placed on display, contributing to a seductive international flavor. Visiting the German circus, one is not disappointed: the sights and sounds of the world and of multiculturalism, in its most fantastic guise, are redolent in the atmosphere of the big top. 47

Under the big top itself, the Wild West Show unfolds in narrative fashion, as a story that Karl May himself might have written. A cowboy emerges from the side entrance as the protagonist, usually quickly establishing a liaison with a woman, whose lack of equivalent equestrian skill marks her as a pioneer settler. The scene is disrupted by invading Indians, heavily clad in deerskin or some ersatz thereof, and playing on musical instruments to intensify the pace of the narrative. Drums virtually take over the story, as a flurry of abductions, fights, and eventual rescues bring the story to its predictable conclusion. It is at the intermission that one learns that the musicians of the Wild West were not Native Americans, but North Africans. Wandering through the animal displays and sideshows, one comes to several groups of Moroccans--circus Gastarbeiter--who sell leather goods (not deerskin, however) and small drums and end-blown flutes. Circus Indian music is, in fact, a specialty of North Africans who spend the summer in Europe to earn enough money to support themselves for much of the rest of the year in Morocco, where frequently they are also professional musicians. 48 The instruments sold outside the big top are Moroccan, many of them smaller versions of the same instruments accompanying the previous story of racial encounter in the Wild West. Buying a small hand drum allows one not only to purchase a musical artifact from Morocco, but also from the Wild West and the worlds of Wild Bill Cody and Karl May. These instruments reconfigure time in the narrative of the circus by performing new histories of distant and modern foreigners (cover illustration, Musikautomat).

Circuses have often afforded Germans a vehicle for recalibrating history through spec-tacle empowered by music. In the Jüdischer Kulturbund of the 1930s, the Jewish children of the Berlin cultural ghetto transformed themselves into the "Zirkus-Knorke," singing of this experience with the "Zirkus-Knorke-Lied" . This circus was not only a form of escape and entertainment, but also a site of resistance, for the children of the Jewish com-munity took charge of their own foreignness, satirizing and parodying it (in Berliner dialect, a Knorke is something like a buffoon), and placing it on a stage that they commanded.

Similarly, the workers who created and performed the "Moorlied" used the ironic moment of a self-made circus to resist the authorities in the concentration camp Papenburg, acting out the narrative of resistance in the "Zirkus Konzentrazani." The musical performance, on its surface representing the circus of self-presentation, allowed the internees to stage an act of resistance under the very gaze of those whom they were resisting.

The modern German circus is a complex site of foreignness, in which the racism of the past is confused with the racism of the present. The Native American, representing the fantasy of the Wild West as das Land der unbegrenzten Möglichkeiten, the "land of possibilities without borders," becomes one with the North African, who crosses the borders of modern Germany representing the musically exotic beyond those borders. The circus, then, is a venue in which races mix but do so unnoticed, or at least in ways that the spectators expect and desire. Each summer, this "multicultural" moment plays itself out again and again throughout Germany, erasing the boundaries of history and redrawing them according to the exotic drumbeat of foreignness.

Von deutscher Art und Kunst: The Aesthetics of Tradition in Modernity

German song embodies many different kinds of boundaries: geographical, historical, and linguistic. 49 German song contains texts in the German language, in fact, in the German language, High German, whose own history parallels that of the centuries-long political consolidation that preceded the first German unification under Bismarck in 1871. The poetic High German is not just a marker of classical art song, say the Lieder of Schubert and Schumann, using texts by Goethe or Schiller, but rather distinguishes the most important narrative genres of folk song. German ballads, therefore, are in High German, even (i.e., especially) when transmitted orally in preliterate societies, thereby reinforcing the connection to an inscribed German history. High German folk-ballad texts cut across other boundaries, both dialect areas inside Germany and ethnic regions outside. This is the case, for example, in Alsace-Lorraine, where the language of ballads is distinct both from the regional dialect/language, Alsatian, and from French, in effect historically empowering the German ballad to obviate the Rhine boundary between France and Germany. In other boundary regions, such as German-speaking Südtirol in northern Italy and traditionally multicultural Burgenland in eastern Austria, German song more clearly establishes the conditions between German and non-German cultures, between the einheimisch (at home) culture and the foreign. Even modern songbooks from these areas contain few if any folk songs in languages other than German, though these regions and most of their residents are usually multilingual.

The manipulation of boundaries within the German song results from a dialectical rela-tion between song and history. German song is a product of the Enlightenment, particularly Johann Gottfried Herder's coining of the term Volkslied, 50 and the classicization of a German literary language in the late eighteenth century, which in turn became the source for romantic song composition in the nineteenth century. In the spirit of the age, German song was constructed from racial categories, which in turn proscribed which people could sing which songs. In establishing his criteria for Germanness in the arts, Herder also privileged the foreign, illiterate, wild peoples who, "distanced from science," had no need to "commit their songs to paper." 51 It was with literate traditions that history began, and, rather than the exoticized timelessness of oral tradition, song began to occupy specific boundaries and genres, which in turn acquired musical forms, such as the strophic art song, that reproduced themselves. 52 The musical aspects of German song, too, developed within a specific history of tonality, that is, the harmonic implications dependent on relations determined by intervals of the third. At one level, German folk song demonstrated a basic melodic gestalt of melodies anchored to the harmonic movement of tonality; at another, German art song came to realize an avant-garde, a musical venue for historical experiments with tonality, as in the songs of Hugo Wolf. 53

By the end of the nineteenth century, the German song was canonized, fixed by the boundaries of a music history specific to that century. By the turn of the century, however, challenges to these boundaries arose, challenges launched by those going beyond and breaking with tonality, and embracing what they themselves called modernity. In the realm of art song, these challenges came from Gustav Mahler, and soon thereafter from Arnold Schoenberg. Neither Mahler nor Schoenberg, of course, was German; both were Jews whose families had been resident in the Slavic areas of the Austro-Hungarian Empire. Their careers involved endless crossing of boundaries, and their musical languages, particularly Schoenberg's, eventually undermined the harmonic hegemony of German song, with the institution of the twelve-tone system, in which all intervals were potentially equal.

The history of Germanness in music, as an unbroken passage from oral to literate, from simple to complex, foundered with the collapsing of these musical boundaries. Replacing this history was not another teleological history, but rather the experiments of modernity. 54 Paradoxically, but not surprisingly, Germanness did not disappear because of these experi-ments. Its obstinate historical presence served at times as the object of experimentation, as in the orchestral songs of Gustav Mahler, Des Knaben Wunderhorn, which utilized the canonic collection of German folk poetry of the same name. 55 Popular songs, on the other hand, often achieved widespread currency because they reproduced the idiom and familiarity of folk song. A modern German song emerged that was not so much the product of tonality or nineteenth-century history, but instead involved a process of interrogating the canonic German song. Its historical dynamic, nevertheless, continued to rely on its capacity to index the past, even to recuperate the past and formulate the present from it. Bound to history, the modern German song reproduced that history with each attempt to express modernity.

Excursus 4--Cabaret and the Interrogation of Germanness

Like other sites of musical practice that one encounters in the New Germany, cabaret enjoys a revival. Television devotes programs to cabaret, with Sunday night programming in the early 1990s touring new and innovative cabarets. 56 Cabaret performances are very much a part of modern nightlife, which has meant a further revival of musical practices that once throve in past cabarets, altering contexts while reviving period-specific musical styles. The Viennese popular stage provides one example of this historicism when the musician Roland Neuwirth performs "neue Schrammelmusik," adapting the elements of the musical language of the Schrammel-Quartett, the most important popular ensemble in late nineteenth-century Vienna, but embedding in them a biting, contemporary satire. 57 Like the circus and the venues of aesthetic resistance that have appeared on the excursions in this essay, cabaret depends on satire and parody, which in turn depend on modern practices of spectatorship and narrative in popular culture. At their deepest level, all these practices result when for-eignness and difference are displayed on the stage in ways that would be impossible elsewhere.

Cabaret uses satire to define a moment of deracination and to serve as a counterforce to the Germanness that enveloped musical practices during the nineteenth century. The history of cabaret begins, of course, during a period coeval with the decay of obsessive nineteenth-century nationalism. It is hardly surprising that cabaret first began outside the sphere of German cultural hegemony, in Paris, Barcelona, and Scandinavia, before crossing the boundaries and blossoming in the hands of turn-of-the-century modernists such as Frank Wedekind in Munich. 58 Little is to be gained by arguing that there were specifically German traditions of cabaret; each performance and each café in which cabaret took place conflated the international and the local. Each performer used satire to critique time and place in highly individualized ways. The essential satire of cabaret could function in no other way.

Similarly, foreignness finds its way to the stages of cabaret in very different ways. Traditions of Jewish cabaret at the turn of the century, for example, depended both on the increased population of Jews in the urban centers of Central Europe and on the growing opportunities for Jewish musicians and entertainers to perform in other kinds of cabarets or in popular theater. Because of the first of these reasons, Jewish cabaret focused its satire largely on Jews from outside Germany, particularly on Eastern European Jews inhabiting neighborhoods such as Berlin's Scheunenviertel. 59 Because of the second reason, Jewish composers turned to the cabaret to find ways of interrogating the German musical canon. Schoenberg not only performed in cabarets, but composed cabaret songs. Significantly, these songs are not in the twelve-tone vocabulary that broke with the history of tonality, but rather heighten and stylize a popular musical vocabulary. 60

The satire of cabaret is possible only when criticism is levelled equally at Germanness and foreignness, hence heightening their interrelation. Some Jewish cabaret songs had texts, therefore, that were anti-Semitic. That different spectators responded in contrasting ways to these songs was inevitable, which may in part explain the survival of cabaret into the Third Reich. The cabarets of the Jüdischer Kulturbund attracted enormous attention, generating also new forms of satire and social critique. 61 Of all the sites of musical practice and aesthetic resistance allowed in concentration camps, none survived longer than the cabaret. 62 As satire increases on the stage of the cabaret, it deflects the gaze of the spectator, as if in a hall of mirrors; criticism reflects endlessly upon itself, blinding all to the object of racism because all subjects equally have moments of becoming that object. Because of this satire, the boundaries between difference and racism are alternately drawn and erased, becoming confused and confusing. In modern Germany, these boundaries have increasingly characterized the satire of popular and political song, and for this reason characteristics of the cabaret have spilled over to the street, song being the vehicle that crosses the boundaries of genre and venue. On the street, satire often provides a context for songs drawn from foreignness, as well as those foreigners who perform them. In this process, the relentlessly reflecting and reflexive social criticism that characterized turn-of-the-century cabaret and characterizes cabaret as Germany approaches the turn of the next century further contributes to the contradictions of modernity performed again and again on the street.

History confronts the New Germany with an enormous burden of political dilemmas and contradictions. Both liberal and conservative, Left and Right, must deal with complex pasts that problematize the decisions of the present. Essays attempting to sort out these ways of dealing with the past have already come to constitute a kind of metahistory, distancing the processes of decision-making from the reality of contemporary contradictions. 65 Foreignness assumes so many forms in German modernity that it is seemingly impossible to show soli-darity with one instance without demonstrating unsettling positions toward the other. This dilemma particularly characterized the German Left's attempts to support the Palestinians while at the same time claiming not to be anti-Semitic. Stated differently, only in Germany can one not separate Israel from the Jews, if, as modern German history insists, the final formation of the state of Israel was also an inevitable consequence of the Holocaust. 66 Race and racism fail to disappear from the present because they are so embedded in the past.

The modern slogan for the changing cultural landscape of the New Germany is Multikulturalismus. This thoroughly non-German coinage, adapted of course from English, supplants German formulations such as Minderheiten (minorities) or Vielvölker (many peoples), both of which place the new foreigners in a subordinate position, or would officially remind Germans of that position. The attacks of skinheads and other groups from the German Right on Turkish homes and foreigners in general illustrate the fact that the egalitarianism signaled by terms such as "multiculturalism" simply does not exist; foreigners remain essen-tially powerless against Volk and Heimat. Yet multiculturalism is fetishized, sprawled across the surface of billboards advertising Benetton sweaters or showing blacks and whites in amorous embrace because both smoke Peter Stuyvesant cigarettes. 67

The musics of the new multiculturalism also pervade the modern cultural landscape of Germany. Turkish music in Germany possesses its own long history and an historiographic tradition dating from the nineteenth century. 68 As the settings and functions for Turkish music in Germany changed, so too did the instruments and the constitution of ensembles. Rural folk musics entered the (German) city, and the growing presence of female musicians regendered the predominantly male musical traditions of Turkey. In the past several decades Turkish-German musicians have successfully utilized the mass media, creating published anthologies and releasing recordings, such as the one from which "Çok Uzaklardan Geliyoruz" above was taken, for Turkish consumers in Germany. Turkish television stations in Germany broadcast enormous amounts of popular music, some of it from Turkish television, some of it recorded in Germany, but all of it packaged for consumption in Germany. The Turkish-German music culture, however, is not integrated into modern Germany. It is there to experience, if one wishes to enter the clubs of Berlin's Kreuzberg or to pause when searching for interesting television programming. Popular German singers too, as we have already seen in the case of Wolf Biermann, borrow Turkishness for their repertories. Turkish music itself, however, is performed along the boundaries of modern society, waiting for the moment to be integrated into a truly multicultural Germany just as persistently as Turkish workers are waiting for the moment when they will be granted citizenship. 69

It is in the margins and along the boundaries of society, moreover, that the musics of foreigners have always thrived. It is only since World War II that these boundaries have gradually collapsed. Symbolizing the musical foreignness located in the boundaries through-out Germany's modern history have been Europe's most ubiquitous and professionalized musicians, Romas and Sintis, to whom I shall refer as Gypsies. 70 Gypsy musicians have been distinguished by the ability to perform the musics of groups with whom they come in contact, which is to say, those who engage them as professional musicians. Throughout the nineteenth century and during much of the twentieth century, accounts of Gypsy music observed the ways in which it is really Hungarian 71 or accompanied the festivals and customs of other groups. The history of Gypsy music exemplifies an extreme musical contestation of race: a music with no identity of its own, but that achieves identity only through its capacity to express the non-Gypsy. This capacity is the result of mobility and adaptability, which in turn mark the presence of Gypsies throughout the streets of Europe. Depictions of Gypsy musicians, therefore, usually place them in public places: in the marketplace of the village, in the bands of outdoor restaurants, in the ensembles that play for weddings. 72 In modern Germany, Gypsy musicians have adapted the domain of the street to their musical Heimat, paradoxically not residing in or near this domain, but rather again on the outskirts of the city or at the edge of villages. In this function, they exhibit an extraordinary modernity, for, despised by the public, they nevertheless become the ultimate mirror of multiculturalism. Their history has been one of assimilation and adaptation at the deepest cultural level of music making. In essence, they symbolize the fallacy of insisting that Germany cannot be a multicultural nation.

--"Die schöne Jüdin" ("The Beautiful Jewish Woman") 73

The story of "Die schöne Jüdin" describes the love relationship between a young Jewish woman and a gentile, a relationship made impossible because the woman may not go out onto the street, even to meet her lover. The story begins with entreaties from the woman to her mother to allow her to "go for a little walk" (verse 2). The mother repeatedly refuses, rejoining that "you may not go alone" (verse 3). After repeated attempts to find an appro-priate family and Jewish companion to accompany the young woman's entry into the gentile world (the version here raises the possibility of the little brother and sister), the young woman waits until her mother is asleep and violates the rules of the community and family by going alone, in some versions jumping from her window onto the street below. "Die schöne Jüdin" ends either with the conversion of the woman as necessary preparation for marriage or with suicide by drowning (the case in this version), whereby the woman does not cross the boundary into a gentile world, nor does she reverse her exit from the Jewish community. The theme of homeland, in the end, proves irreconcilable. The ballad ends as a Heimatlied with no homeland: "'Leb wohl du liebe Heimat, wir sehn uns nimmermehr!'" ("'Be well, my dear homeland, never again will we meet!'").

As a German ballad, "Die schöne Jüdin" has one of the most distinctive histories of crossing the cultural boundaries of modern Europe. The ballad was sung in both High German and in Yiddish, with versions collected not only in Germany and German-speaking areas elsewhere, but also in Jewish communities of Eastern Europe. 74 "Die schöne Jüdin" is therefore both a German and a Yiddish ballad; its history, in fact, is at once German and Jewish. It has, nevertheless, a troubled history, which symbolizes the difficulties of recon-ciling its two ethnic domains, which in turn mark racial domains within the song itself. This history has defied the boundaries between German Central Europe and Jewish Eastern Europe, entering the song canons of both. Variants in oral tradition respond to the Jewishness of the narrative and its symbols in divergent ways, ranging from anti-Semitic variants to those that carefully record the norms of Jewish law. 75 In none of the German or Jewish versions of the songs do the boundaries between communities yield to the lovers; entry into the German or gentile world--being seen on the street by non-Jews--makes the decision to leave the Jewish community irreversible. Ultimately, the street outside the Jewish woman's window, found in most variants, becomes a world open to Jews only when they are no longer Jews.

Like the other songs experienced on the excursions in this essay, "Die schöne Jüdin" embodies disturbing contradictions of modernity. It is, after all, a song explicitly about the moment of passing from the traditional to the modern, but that moment offers no possibility of compromise. Past and present confront each other as if the former may yield to the latter when the Jewish woman sets foot on the street. In the song, however, it is not possible simply to pass from the past to the present. Embracing the present means denying the past--either conversion or suicide.

"Die schöne Jüdin" refers to indistinct pasts and presents, and its sense of modernity depends not on specific indices of real individuals. For the most part, the music one encounters on the streets of modern Germany relies on such indistinct references to time and place. Even the surface specificity of "Die Wacht an der Isar" refers back to "Wacht am Rhein," which in turn acts as a simulacrum for historical German concerns with the ways in which boundaries circumscribe contested homelands.

This is not to say, however, that the songs of the street or of the other moments that this essay has recounted lack history. Quite the contrary, they are profoundly historical, precisely because they are inseparable from the moments of their performance. The historical conditions on which the specific performances are premised are the result of contradictions: cabaret and its biting satire; the uncertainty about the constitution of Germanness and its relation to homeland; the self-made circuses in the Jüdischer Kulturbund and concentration camps; the songs of Turkish Gastarbeiter and Gypsy musicians without homes. Together, these contradictions form the long history of a nation that insists it, unlike the United States, has no history of assimilating foreigners.

On the streets of the New Germany, music exists only through performance. Passersby experience musical performance largely as fragments. Most passersby merely slow their pace in order to hear a few words in a song; others listen intently to the performance of an entire song; there are even a few hangers-on who listen to entire repertories and return repeatedly to the same street musicians. In fact, street musicians earn quite well in the New Germany, as they did in the past. It is perhaps a sign of the persistent financial well-being of Germany that it can afford to support the performance of its historical narratives on its streets.

Through performance, music creates the illusion of timelessness, one of the tropes of a modern German historical imagination. Timelessness allows one to collapse long periods into symbols, virtually footnotes in a larger history. Hence, the Third Reich, the Holocaust, world wars, and the socialist GDR become local aberrations, not "our history" but "theirs." This timelessness distresses when the contradictions of modernity begin to trouble again, as the unwillingness to accept foreignness has increasingly troubled the racial consciousness of the New Germany. Again, the past has invaded the present, and modernity repeats itself. The street music of the New Germany insists on a public awareness of this repetition, for once passersby turn away, they are again confronted with another performance, another repetition. The contradictions of modernity reside in and turn on these repeated musical performances, with musical representations of history continuously undermining the possibility that the present might erase the memory of the past.
