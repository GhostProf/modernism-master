The Modern(ist) Mackintosh

Celia Marshik

“No, Charles, I will not wait while you put on a Burberry.” Dorothy Sayers, Unnatural Death (1927)

The mackintosh in modernism sends a warning. Consider just two of the many examples. In the “Hades” episode of James Joyce’s Ulysses, the reporter Hynes takes down the names of those who attend the funeral of Paddy Dignam. Hynes asks Leopold Bloom,

—do you know the fellow in the, fellow was over there in the. . .

—Macintosh. Yes, I saw him, Mr Bloom said. Where is he now?

—M’Intosh, Hynes said, scribbling, I don’t know who he is. Is

that his name?1

The mysterious figure, henceforth known as M’Intosh, appears throughout Ulysses, known only by the coat that confers a spurious identity. The character’s personal history is, however, a tragic one: he eats dry bread, a clear sign of poverty; he “loves a lady who is dead,” a mark of mourning and loneliness; and he is identified by medical students as suffering from “trumpery insanity,”2 a sign of ill-health.

A more violent example appears in the best-selling novel Not So Quiet. . . Stepdaughters of War (1930) by Evadne Price (published under the pseudonym Helen Zenna Smith). Price’s text complements Erich Maria Remarque’s All Quiet on the Western Front through a narrative about English women who work for the war effort. Her Burberry will never be any use again.”3 In novels targeted at both high- and middle-brow readerships, the mackintosh, under its various incarnations, is more than just unflattering. It causes loss of individual identity; it cloaks the unscrupulous, deadly and cruel. It is even, apparently, a garment with a bull’s-eye on it, protecting against the elements but not the violence of technological warfare. The irony of such associations is increased by the garment’s very ordinariness; it was commonplace on the streets of major cities and in the wardrobes of people of all conditions and histories. At the same time, British literature of the 1910s, 1920s, and 1930s associates the mackintosh with a relatively uniform set of characteristics and suggests the garment was historically encoded with a range of qualities that writers drew upon in order to create characters and advance arguments. While the mackintosh was commercially and popularly aligned with positive traits, including technological innovation, protection, value and perseverance, writers of the period would emphasize the garment’s less flattering connotations, including violence, anonymity, and the paralysis of individuals dwarfed by economic and social structures they could not transcend. The case of the mackintosh suggests that modernist writers used clothing to advance subtle analyses of both specific psyches and group behavior—to communicate, often quietly, a profound disquiet about the way bodies were imprinted by forces beyond their control. Scholars of modernist literature have examined representations of the mackintosh within individual novels and have explained what the garment signifies about particular characters, the limits of interpretation, and the social and ethical dimensions of each work.4 In contrast, this essay looks at the modernist mac by reading across a range of texts to excavate the cultural work the mackintosh performed. During first four decades of the twentieth-century, more and more people came to appreciate the power of clothing (whether fashionable or practical) to shape self-presentation and thus to assert a particular social significance and role. But clothing could also work against such efforts: as an animate object, clothing gathers meanings and connotations that individual wearers cannot always control or manipulate. The modernist mac is one such case. In this piece, I combine material history with cultural and literary analysis to suggest why the mackintosh became imbued with negative connotations and how knowledge of such connotations can advance our understanding of individual texts. If we look at the mac through the lenses of history, children’s literature, fashion columns, and advertising as well as modernist novels, it becomes clear that local meaning reflects the cultural history of a garment that was, in the eyes of both high modernist and middlebrow writers, either too innocent or too guilty for an adult to wear. The modernist mac became a mute witness to histories of industrial production and class divisions, to assumptions about which types of people could act upon individual volition, and to a historical cataclysm that, until WWII, was unprecedented. Given the presence of the mac in such locations, it is little wonder that it became a symptom of—and, eventually, a kind of shorthand for—negative qualities and narratives.

Before turning to a brief history of the mac, I want to clarify my use of the term 45 throughout this essay. I subsume a number of terms under “mackintosh,” including raincoat, trench-coat, weather all, and mackintosh proper, in part because Macintosh was the first commercial maker of the garment, and the word thus became the British catch-all for raincoats. According to the OED, the mac was “originally: a full-length coat or cloak made of waterproof rubberized material. Subsequently: a rainproof coat made of this or some other material.” Manufacturers and retailers did not always differentiate between different kinds of coats: the Harrods General Catalog of 1928–9, for example, did not distinguish between coats made of different textiles, indexing and identifying all raincoats with the term “mackintoshes.” The OED demonstrates the term’s universalizing function with an example that follows the definition above: “Noblesse Oblige 30 Burberry and raincoat are of the same genre, macintosh or mac being normal.” In Noblesse Oblige, a footnote to the definition reads, “this use of Burberry no doubt arose because, even before 1914 [. . .] this was a good and expensive kind of macintosh.”5 This definition explains the use of differing terms as an attempt by manufacturers and consumers to assert and maintain class hierarchies, but the “mac” was considered the “normal” term for a waterproof garment.6 As I will argue, however, there was nothing “normal” about this coat’s history or representative status in literature.

Making the Mackintosh

The commercial history of the mackintosh indicates some reasons why negative connotations would be mapped on to the garment. Charles Macintosh (1766–1843) invented a means of waterproofing fabric and patented it on June 17, 1823. Macintosh’s process involved sandwiching rubber, softened by naphtha, between layers of cotton cloth.7 Although the coats succeeded in keeping out water, they were essentially unattractive and uncomfortable. Nineteenth-century macs were a drab green and long, shapeless, and hard to maneuver. The original material couldn’t be tailored closely to the body because the rubberized fabric didn’t breathe, and a tight-fitting mac was a prescription for perspiration.8 The coats themselves also smelled unpleasant. As early as 1839, The Gentleman’s Magazine remarked that “a mackintosh is now become a troublesome thing in town from the difficulty of their being admitted into an omnibus on account of the offensive stench which they emit.”9

Mackintoshes were unique in that they were an early artifact of mass production. During a period when most street clothing and outerwear was either home-or custom-made, the production requirements for macs, which had seams that required chemical proofing, meant that “the mackintosh industry was one of the first clothing trades to be substantially carried out in factories.”10 Workers in the trade made individual parts of the garment, which was then assembled elsewhere on the premises, rendering the mackintosh a pre-Fordist exemplar of mass production. Because they were not made to measure for individual customers and were manufactured far away from where they were bought and worn, the mackintosh foreshadowed the eventual popularity of “ready to wear” clothing. The coats were thus an emblem of modernity, but they

46 were also marked by a lack of exclusivity as mass production rendered mackintoshes inexpensive. In 1893, the India Rubber Journal asserted that “Now the servant girl out of her slender means can procure a really stylish and serviceable waterproof for a mere trifle.”11 Manufacturers and servant girls were no doubt pleased that the coat had become so affordable, but such comments led to a growing sense that mackintoshes were cheap, and thus second-rate, articles. The first—and over the long term, best—customer for the mackintosh was the British army. When other manufacturers entered the market with different means of waterproofing cloth, they too found the army to be their best market. Ordinary soldiers wore mass produced mackintoshes, but officers wore “Aquascutums” during the Crimean War12 and, later, Burberry waterproofs. These companies offered tailor-made mackintoshes, but the cut and color of the garments they produced followed military specifications, making the coats of the officer class visually uniform. Even as the quality of one’s mac expressed social and economic status, the coat became a de-individuating garment worn by members of an institution that functioned because of group behavior, experience, and history. From its earliest days, the garment was visually aligned with the masses, whether these were soldiers, officers, or the “servant girls” of towns and cities. Advertisements for the mackintosh tended to promote the garment to two constituencies: sportsmen (and, to a lesser extent, women) and servants. In other words, the upper echelons of British society would wear their macs while participating in extraordinary leisure activities while the working-class wore the same garments during their ordinary, workaday routines. During the nineteenth century, Macintosh brand waterproofs touted the product as a necessary implement for the sporting life, particularly for dangerous sports. In an 1890 advertisement in The Illustrated Sporting and Dramatic News, a man and woman wear the coats while yachting in rough seas.13 Although this advertisement was no doubt intended to display the protective qualities of the coat—the couple is endangered but not wet—it paradoxically aligns the garment with exciting pastimes that verge on the perilous. If the yachting class wore mackintoshes when in danger of being washed overboard, they purchased these garments for their servants when the latter were occupied in the routine business of driving carriages, opening doors, and carrying packages. Early Harrods catalogues depict mackintoshes as suitable for coachmen and footmen. While thus attired, they could escort their employers around town with the sheltering aid of umbrellas. These same catalogues make plain why no woman of fashion would have worn a mackintosh when paying calls or going to evening entertainments: in 1912, the coats were still voluminous, chin to foot affairs (Figure 1). Before the war, the coats were thus associated with the military, sports, and the working class. They were practical, utilitarian garments that were mainly aligned with de-individuating activities such as army or household service. They were appropriate for select leisure activities, but they positively interfered with making a distinctive, stylish appearance.

Sartorial Shorthand: Early Macs

In literature of this period, writers represented the coat using a constellation of associations including sport, de-individuation, and protection. These qualities emerge most clearly in books written for children, a constituency that, like servants and soldiers, does not choose what it wears, and in representations of working-class characters, who are read by observers above them on the social scale. Both constituencies are clothed in macs for pragmatic reasons, but when adults wear the coats, their garments come to represent—and even to invite—a peculiar form of non-progression. These early representations of mackintoshes suggest that the coat eventually became a trope for threats to the body and individual identity in a culture where personal choice was severely circumscribed by age, class, and gender.

Beatrix Potter’s The Tale of Mr. Jeremy Fisher, a 1906 sequel to her famous tale of Peter Rabbit, depicts the mackintosh as sound garb for children and, in fact, as nothing short of life preserving. In Potter’s illustrated tale, the title character, a frog, dons a mackintosh before going out to fish in the rain. The mac would have been regarded as appropriate sporting wear, so this choice seems unremarkable, a specific instance of Potter’s general tendency to dress her animal protagonists in human clothes.14 As the story progresses, predator becomes prey, and a large trout swallows Fisher. Lest

48 Potter’s young readers fear, however, the narrator quickly reassures them: “the trout was so displeased with the taste of the macintosh, that in less than half a minute it spat him out again.” Fisher then pulls himself out of the water, “his macintosh all in tatters.”15 Potter pairs this description with an illustration that depicts the mackintosh with vertical tears, perhaps caused by the trout’s sharp teeth (Figure 2). Although Potter avoids overt moralizing, the lesson of the story seems clear: when venturing out, children should wear a mackintosh. It will protect them from rain and, apparently, the larger dangers of the natural and social world. The valorization of the utilitarian mackintosh for youth was reflected more broadly in illustrated newspapers of the early twentieth century. Older children wearing macs were depicted as having the plucky resolution to “carry on” in the face of Britain’s notoriously wet weather. The Sunday Pictorial of November 21, 1926, included a photograph captioned, “Modern Gumboot Girls Don’t Care: They Despise Rain’s Dreary Drip.” The accompanying text reads, “Rain doesn’t frighten the modern girl—she just puts on sensible raincoat and gumboots and ‘doesn’t care if it snows.’ Here is a typical group at the woman’s hockey match.”16 Although the individuals pictured could be young women, the newspaper twice calls them “girls,” a term that enhances the general emphasis on youth, sport, and a cheerful attitude. Such news items, together with Potter’s Jeremy Fisher, suggest that the mac serves an eminently practical purpose when worn by children or young adults. Fisher experiences a violent attack while wearing it, but the coat also shields him from harm. More prosaically, the “gumboot girls” are protected against rain and snow. This protective association, however, alters when the mac migrates from a garment for children to a coat for adults, who apparently enjoy freedom of choice when it comes to attire. A practical coat becomes an object that de-individuates, a coat that only adults enslaved by common sense, and unable to engage in self fashioning, would wear. Rebecca West’s 1918 novella The Return of the Soldier uses the mackintosh as shorthand for one character’s poverty, which motivates a sartorial and social innocence. In West’s book, a British officer, Chris Baldry, forgets his adult life as the result of shell shock. His cousin Jenny, the novella’s narrator, enables Chris’s daily visits with Margaret Grey, a woman living in suburban poverty whom Chris loved in his youth fifteen years earlier. Grey’s appearance is marked by her continual wear of a “yellowish raincoat,” which leads Jenny to reflect, “it would have been such agony to the finger tips to touch any part of her apparel.”17 Grey seems relatively untroubled by her appearance—if she recognizes her dowdiness, she is used to it—but the upper-class women in the book project characteristics and emotions such as “unpardonable” and “sick” onto her mackintosh.18 Grey’s raincoat deftly captures her poverty and personal limitations, and through Jenny’s descriptions and diction, West neatly illustrates the snobbery of her well-meaning narrator. According to Jenny, Margaret Grey “was repulsively furred with neglect and poverty, as even a good glove that has dropped down behind a bed in a hotel [. . .] is repulsive when the chambermaid retrieves it.” Later, Jenny describes Grey as “not so much a person as an implication of dreary poverty,”19 a characteriza

tion that reduces the woman—and her coat—to a perambulating sign of a class that, before the war, remained segregated from the luxurious world of Baldry Court.20 Like the glove that Jenny uses to figure Margaret’s physical decay, the yellow mackintosh becomes “unpardonable” in part because the object is out of place. As a result of the disruption caused by World War I, such ambulatory objects permeate the world of the upper-class, where servants might wear such garments but not visitors to the house.

While West clearly aligns the mackintosh with the dreary practicality poverty necessitates, she also uses the garment to illustrate that upper-class women view those below them on the social scale as child-like. The mackintosh prevents Jenny from perceiving Margaret as fully adult or, indeed, as feminine. During her most positive assessment of Grey’s appearance, the narrator comes to see her shabbiness as akin to “the untidiness of a child who had been so eager to get to the party that it had not let the nurse fasten its frock.”21 Even though Grey displays superior understanding toward the end of West’s novella, her appearance remains less than fully mature—less than fully individuated—because of her mac. At the conclusion of the text, when Chris Baldry recovers his memory, Margaret is reduced to a “figure mothering something in her arms.”22 She will have to return to her life of respectable poverty, a “figure” instead of a person.23

West’s text thus uses the mackintosh as shorthand for comparative poverty, which leads upper-class characters to view Margaret Grey as aged but not adult. Surprisingly, when an author places the garment upon a privileged figure, the coat’s infantilizing properties similarly appear. In The Last September, Elizabeth Bowen’s 1929 novel about the waning days of the Irish Ascendency, the mackintosh at first seems simple and utilitarian. Lois Farquar, the young British ward of Sir Richard and Lady Naylor, wears the coat when driving and walking in the rain.24 Because Lois is on the brink of coming of age, such childish apparel seems appropriate. The fact that she wears a mackintosh in the Irish countryside, and while driving and walking, also makes her seem to be properly attired given the coat’s history as a garment worn for sport.

As the novel continues, however, the mackintosh signals a kind of arrested development in its protagonists and the society that they represent. As Jed Esty has noted, Bowen’s novel represents Lois’s “peculiar nonprogress to adulthood” and depicts the character as “frozen in her youth, stuck between cultures, hopelessly virginal.”25 Glamorous and mature characters, such as Marda Norton, get drenched in rainstorms and are chastised for going out without a mackintosh.26 Marda, who moves into adult society and married sexuality when she leaves the Naylors, is the kind of character who doesn’t wear a mackintosh; Lois, on the contrary, wears a coat that protects her from the rain but also communicates her refusal to become, or act as, an adult.

In contrast to the childish macs that Bowen’s “overgrown juveniles” wear, soldiers and Irish insurgents in her novel wear trench-coats. This distinction, which is unique to Bowen, serves to highlight the contrast between the moribund Anglo-Irish Ascendency and British soldiers and Irish nationalists. Unlike Lois and the others in her set, individuals wearing the trench-coat are purposeful: early in the novel, she hears “branches slipping against a trench-coat. The trench-coat rustled across the path ahead to the swing of a steady walker. She stood by the holly immovable [. . .] and there passed within reach of her hand [. . .] some resolute profile powerful as a thought.”27 The figure is unidentified, abstracted to the point that the coat alone seems to take action (“the trench-coat rustled”). Lois, and thus readers, only knows that the character wears the coat and seems bent on movement and perhaps violence. “It must be because of Ireland he was in such a hurry,” Lois speculates; the man in the trench-coat thus emerges as a silent sign of the politicized violence dividing the country.28

The novel does not specify which side of the struggle this ghostly figure takes. Although Lois suspects that this trench-coat cloaks a rebel, Bowen’s novel pointedly depicts armed men on both sides of the conflict in the coats. Later, Bowen similarly animates the object: “a trench-coat flickered between the trees, approaching.”29 On this occasion, the man wearing the coat is quickly identified as Gerald Lesworth, a British soldier, but the coat moves and acts before the character does. Indeed, Lois initially stares in the direction of the coat, unable to differentiate between insurgents and British soldiers from a distance. A great deal rests on making this distinction given the violent attacks that take place at the novel’s periphery, but through the trench-coat and other tropes, the novel underlines similarities between the two constituencies. Both the soldiers and the insurgents are militarized, armed, and capable of violent acts. And both groups dispossess the Anglo-Irish: the insurgents bury guns on estate property 51 and burn the great houses, but the army is an “army of occupation” and not entirely welcome. As Sir Richard opines, “‘this country [. . .] is altogether too full of soldiers.’”30 Or, to follow the diction Bowen uses elsewhere, it is altogether too full of trench-coats.

Bowen’s novel thus demonstrates the range of negative connotations that the mackintosh signals in the 1920s. In The Last September, the mackintosh figures a juvenile innocence in characters that should possess other garments and qualities. While West’s Margaret Grey might not be able to afford better outerwear, Lois Farquar could make different sartorial choices. But arrested development isn’t the only quality linked with the mackintosh in The Last September, which also associates the coats with anonymity and militarism. If they do not always rob individuals of their particular identity, they demonstrate that identity can be troubled by garments, which blur distinctions between opposing factions in an armed conflict. Although Gerald Lesworth briefly rises above his coat to assert his individual self, we as readers know that he is on the losing side of the struggle the book represents. When Bowen’s novel was published in 1929, readers would have known that the anonymous insurgents secured their aims as the Irish Free State had been established in 1922. Perhaps it was better to be the anonymous, resolute figure in the coat.

The Mackintosh at War

Bowen’s use of the coats in her novel reflects her historical position writing in the decade after the end of the Great War. The association between mackintoshes and war was cemented when they were tailored and marketed for use at the front during WWI. One of the necessary items in any officer’s “kit” was the trench-coat, a long belted mackintosh cut differently for each branch of service.31 Ordinary soldiers and female volunteers also wore such coats, although the cut and quality of individual examples depended on the wearer’s financial wherewithal.

The pervasive presence of trench-coats during the war is well known, but at our historical remove, it is difficult to imagine the visual impact of the sheer proliferation of such garments. People saw men and women in mackintoshes everywhere: in the streets, at rail stations, and in the newspapers. In addition, soldiers and volunteers were routinely represented in the coats in photographs and advertisements for a variety of products.

Indeed, the trench-mackintosh reached iconic status as the image of the coat proliferated in advertising for products both associated with, and far removed from, the war. A promotion for war bonds in the Daily Telegraph on January 10, 1918, included a line drawing of a soldier in a trench-coat “standing to” that pulled patriotic heart strings and encouraged readers to give to the cause. But similar images also encouraged viewers to buy prosaic consumer products. Phosferine, a patent medicine for “nerves” and general conditioning, used pictures of a soldier in a mackintosh in a July 5, 1917, advertisement in the Daily Telegraph (Figure 3). Although the text accompanying the photograph claims that the product helped Private W. G. Amatt recover from shell shock, the advertisement depicts the very environment—and garment—associated with incurring such maladies. Readers of advertisements would thus have seen the coat associated with protection, common sense, and grit, but the garment was also aligned with shell shock, wounds, and the violence that created them.32

While newspaper illustrations and advertisements for an array of products helped to align the mac with the war, the firms that manufactured these coats were most responsible for keeping macs in the public eye. These companies targeted their advertising to soldiers and civilians, and in so doing followed a nineteenth-century advertising tradition. As Brent Shannon has demonstrated, in Victorian advertising, “the most masculine—and therefore most popular—male figures were athletes and soldiers, and their presence was used to make even ostensibly gender-neutral products masculine and appealing to male buyers.”33 Soldiers thus helped to promote consumer goods by gendering them male, but the use of soldiers in advertisements that ran during the war had an additional effect. With coats marketed to and by officers and soldiers, consumer products generated a sartorial blending of home and front. Such blending no doubt made wearers feel that they were visually aligned with the war; as Jennifer Craik has argued, uniforms are essential when nations and cultures want to emphasize group identity.34 While trench-coats were not officially a uniform, they were stylistically 53 similar and ubiquitous, thus producing a cohesive national style.

The marketing of these coats helped to produce this style by framing the same garments and makers as appropriate for soldiers and civilians. In 1916, for example, the front page of the Daily Mail touted goods sold by Derry & Toms, including “Gentlemen’s Raincoats,” which are described as “suitable for military or civilian wear.”35 By suggesting that the same garment could be “suitable” for both groups, such advertisements demonstrate the visual continuity between the trench and city streets that consumer goods created. When Aquascutum described itself as “Military and Civil tailors” on a Daly’s Theatre program published in September 1916, readers would not have been surprised or concerned by the proximity of military and civilian models and modes (Figure 4). The militarized nation had a visual coherence created by clothing, which aligned individual bodies with one another and against the foe.

Such conformity no doubt appeared unproblematic, the innocent desire of those back in Britain to model themselves on their “boys” and to look like they too were participating in the war effort. As Craik has argued, however, uniforms constitute one of the “central body techniques in the actualization of persona and habitus.” Indeed, Craik sees this technique as so powerful that she questions, “do people wear uniforms or do uniforms wear people?” In signaling attributes such as “unequal power relations,”36 the trench mac’s sartorial code inscribed civilians with qualities otherwise ascribed to officers and soldiers. Readers at home would have been familiar with these qualities because they would have seen advertisements directed at military men. In retrospect, major figures of high modernism such as Virginia Woolf and James Joyce and popular authors such as Helen Zenna Smith (Evadne Price) and Dorothy Sayers would point out the troubling meanings that accreted onto the mackintosh in such promotions, which subtly signaled the threats to the body and identity the coats came to signify.

Each brand of military mac had a range of strategies for promoting itself, but four general trends emerged as the war went on. Firms celebrated the quality and class associations of the garments, cast the coats as suitable for all fronts and in all weathers, supplied testimonials that praised manufacturers for keeping men comfortable in the trenches, and suggested that the coats protected against all threats to the body of the wearer. In practice, these approaches often overlapped. I trace these advertising campaigns in some detail below, not only to explain how trench-macs were sold, but to explain the narrative backlash against these garments and manufacturers after the war. Ironically, wartime ads contained the seeds of the very qualities that would be seen as unflattering by writers in peacetime. If homogeneous appearance and protection from harm promised comfort and ease in a hostile environment and historical moment, at peace, these same qualities emerged as unappealing and as beacons of false promise.37

Because multiple manufacturers marketed mackintoshes and trench-coats, makers vied to position their product as the best in quality. Burberry and Aquascutum in particular sought to create the sense that their products were exceptional, worn by men of a certain caliber and class. In reading these promotional materials, one gets a glimpse of a world of privilege that some officers managed to enjoy even at the front.

An Aquascutum ad that ran in Punch on April 18, 1917, quotes from a letter penned by an officer in German East Africa: “We are constantly having Tropical Rains, which wet one through in about two minutes. You will be pleased to hear that in spite of these awful storms, my ‘Aquascutum’ keeps me quite dry—when I am fortunate enough to have the native carriers near enough to get it.” This advertisement not only promotes the coat as keeping its wearer dry; through the officer’s testimony, the garment is aligned with a class that enjoys “native carriers,” whose burdens include the Aquascutum coat. Unlike the mackintoshes of Margaret Grey and Jeremy Fisher, this coat is worn by those who have someone to “do” for them. Burberry similarly positioned its products as maintaining “the highest Service tradition in point of smartness and distinction” in 55 the Daily Telegraph (October 7, 1918). It seems counterintuitive that looking stylish mattered to men four years into the war, but the advertisement clearly positions the coat as a means toward the end of an elevated, upper-class appearance. Burberry and Aquascutum were and are invested in promoting themselves as distinctive, luxe brands, and wearing one of their coats communicated economic privilege as well as attention to sartorial detail under trying conditions.

Even today, one can see that these ads would have appealed to non-military personnel as well as to soldiers because they present the garments as pertaining to a class apart from more utilitarian mackintoshes. A decade after the war, the popular author Evadne Price would use this association in Not So Quiet, when Blimey, a working class woman, buys a Burberry with the wages she earns working at the front. For Blimey, a Burberry is a sign of style and a mark of her class aspirations. No other coat would demonstrate that she has improved herself and achieved “distinction.”

And yet, this distinction is limited in scope. Even as Blimey selects a coat in a light shade to signal her individuality, she purchases the same brand worn by others. This choice demonstrates the effective nature of Burberry’s advertising, but the death Price/ Smith metes out to her character casts the “distinction” offered by the coat as dangerously circumscribed. When this fictional “noncombatant” meets the same type of death experienced by soldiers in the trenches, the coat effaces the differences between soldier and cook. As she mutters, “‘Now see wot’s ‘appened to me new Burberry, all covered in blood,’”38 her wounding and death remind readers that women, too, became casualties of the Great War.

Not So Quiet thus deploys Burberry’s cultural status to remind readers that the uniform identity attained by wearing such coats was hazardous. Although Blimey’s final words remind readers of her individual history and working-class status, the bestselling novel’s fictionalized erasure of distinctions reflects a broader truth about the way the coats were marketed. In contrast to the emphasis on distinction, other advertising campaigns conducted by the same firms suggest that the trench-macs erased individual identities, in part because numerous men (and women) in so many different places wore them. Advertisements thus aligned the mackintosh with the military and with the loss of individuality required by military life.39 The marketing campaigns for trench-macs asserted that the same garment could be used anywhere, thus effacing distinctions between the different fronts, campaigns, and individuals fighting around the globe.

In these advertising campaigns, illustrations worked synergistically with text to represent the coat as universally needed and worn. In a July 1, 1915, Burberry advertisement in the London Times, the firm’s coats were said to “ensure advantages of the greatest value to Officers in France or Turkey” (Figure 5).40 This claim depicts the Burberry weatherproof as suitable for officers stationed on either front in any weather. The accompanying sketch shows a universalized soldier, whose face displays no remarkable features. Although he sports a small moustache, he is otherwise “any officer.” This depiction allows readers to picture themselves in a Burberry, but it also points to a disquieting truth of military service: once at the front, a man fills a place

Each manufacturer had a limited number of illustrations they used to promote their coats in a given year; while the text of ads would change, the images would not, thus further effacing individual identities.

Apparent exceptions to such standardized bodies and identities were depicted in “praise from the trenches” ads that quoted from unsolicited letters. In most cases, the letter writer was not indentified although the ads promised that an original document to support the claims was available on request.41 While such letters asserted individual experiences and personalities, the illustrations accompanying them served to undercut the specificity of the writer’s words. For example, a Burberry advertisement in the August 23, 1915, issue of the London Times featured a letter from a J. K. Dunlop. Dunlop 57 described his Burberry as “my best friend for some months in front of Ypres” because it both stood “the rain and wind perfectly” and “seemed impervious to the filthy mud of the trenches.”42 Although Dunlop’s testimony is replete with particulars of his time in the forces, there is no photograph to complement his words. Instead, the sketch that illustrates Dunlop’s letter depicts an officer gazing stalwartly into the distance as he stands in a trench pelted with rain. The officer’s face has nothing distinct about it—he may or may not look like Dunlop—and the coat therefore seems appropriate for all officers. In other words, the Burberry promotion works to standardize individual experience, which helps to cast its product as a garment that any officer on the western front could wear, a “best friend” to one and all in the trench.43

Other ads that included officers’ testimonials similarly generalized them through illustrations and often by omitting the writer’s name. Aquascutum advertisements collected as many as four testimonials without identifying any of the writers; in such cases, the sheer multiplicity of testimonials mitigates their uniqueness. Here too, the image works against the specific experiences articulated in the quoted materials; when one sees that the Daily Telegraph and Morning Post ran Aquascutum ads that used the same image with different testimony, the value of individual identity and experience seems diminished.44 And yet, such declarations by officers continued to be used. Although readers in London were unlikely to experience a blizzard, a 1919 Aquascutum promotion in The Illustrated Sporting and Dramatic News informed them that the firm’s coat “kept out the rain during the blizzard at SUVLA BAY.”45 The unidentified officer’s experience at Gallipoli and in the Great War thus continued to be invoked after the conflict was over, a circumstance that suggests that advertising—and advertising for the mackintosh in particular—helped to keep the war in the minds of postwar readers.

Praise from the trenches ads thus used officers to promote garments to one another as well as to civilians. In most cases this advertising strategy effaced differences between men, fixing identity through the clad body and advancing a collective, soldierly style. During wartime, this balance between a satisfied, particular consumer and a broadly-targeted advertising strategy makes sense. As Jean Baudrillard argues, the uniform reflects “a structured, hierarchical society and seals like an emblem the ideological cohesion of a nation.”46 What is surprising, then, is that the same advertising strategies advanced the mackintosh-emblem after the war. In postwar Britain, when ideological cohesion was less consistent and widespread, dissident writers would seize on the garment as a sign of cohesion gone wrong.

During the war, the mackintosh could already be seen to signal the costs of militarism. The collective struggle in which the coats displayed their merits was obviously a dangerous one. Because service at the front was uncomfortable and life-threatening, the manufacturers of trench-mackintoshes touted the protective qualities of their coats. And yet, advertisements that claim the coats insured bodily health seem both deceptive and naïve in light of the clear and consistent evidence that no garment could protect men and women from the dangers of service.

Burberry’s advertisements must have been seductive, allowing consumers to fantasize that the right purchase might somehow help them to beat the odds. The firm promoted their trench-coats with language that cast the garments as a kind of armor. In a July 16, 1915, ad in the Times, for example, the company promoted its “Military Weatherproof” as “ALL-PROTECTIVE, no matter what the weather—rainy or cold, warm or temperate.”47 Although the immediate context for this claim is the weather, the advertisement suggests that the coat could protect against other dangers. Readers are told that the Burberry mac “ensures healthful security and comfort under each and every condition,” a statement that, in retrospect, seems naïve about the conditions of trench warfare. As the war went on, Burberry dropped the “all-protective” claim, but other campaigns contained similar rhetoric. On July 2, 1917, the Daily Telegraph published an advertisement that asserted, “From every front—from soldiers fighting in the desperate battles of France and Flanders, beneath the sweltering sun of Palestine and Mesopotamia, amongst the wind swept Balkan mountains, and in the miasmic depths of African jungle—comes the same consistent story of the perfect protection afforded by the Burberry.”48 This ad appears to acknowledge the difficult, and even deadly, conditions experienced by men at the front, but it promises “protection” in a context that covers not only weather but also “desperate battles.” Purchasing and wearing a Burberry enabled consumers to participate in a concrete form of wishful thinking. Buyers must have known that the garments could only protect against wet, dirt, and wind, but the desire for “perfect protection” against all threats was no doubt appealing.

Such advertisements made claims that would, over the course of time, come to seem incredibly disingenuous. And yet, other companies similarly promoted garments in terms that are shocking to contemporary eyes. The “Harrods Weekly Price List” for July 23–28, 1917, offered “Special Values in Weathercoats” named after battles. The “Somme,” which was “cut after Cavalry pattern,” and the “Meuse,” a “Trenchcoat, adapted for ladies, cut on service lines” were both offered at sale prices (Figure 6).49 When one recalls that the 1916 Somme Offensive resulted in over 1.5 million casualties, and that the Meuse was the French Department in which the 1916 Battle of Verdun (or “Meuse Mill”) took more than 250,000 lives, it seems incredible that a company would name coats after two of the bloodiest battles of the war. At the time, however, such names must have functioned like the “All protective” claims; until people achieved historical distance from the war—a process in motion in the 1920s, when many modernists wrote about mackintoshes—such names and claims must have signified a determination to “carry on” despite the grim losses at the front. Advertisers banked on the emotional appeal of protection and of participating—if only through consumption—in the war effort. As Camila Loew writes in her analysis of war posters, WWI was the first conflict in which women “had a right to [. . .] claim their experience as a part of war.”50 Harrods’s mackintoshes went even further and allowed women to wear the war. While we cannot know how the public felt about such ads, most readers must have found them acceptable. Harrods would not otherwise have continued to promote the “Somme” and “Meuse” model coats.

If the Harrods catalog and Burberry ads suggest that consumers participated in a garment-mediated form of denial during the war, newspapers provided readers with ample evidence that mackintoshes could not protect vulnerable bodies. On the same page of the London Times issue that carried the 1915 “ALL-PROTECTIVE” Burberry ad, readers saw several smaller classified notices for second-hand clothing dealers. During the war, such dealers traded and advertised used uniforms, including, no doubt, trench-mackintoshes made by Burberry and Aquascutum. Used uniforms could have different provenances, but some of these garments undoubtedly came from bereaved families, who often received a loved-one’s “kit” after he died at the front. Vera Brittain’s Testament of Youth describes reviewing her fiancé’s belongings after they were returned upon his death.51 Many families kept their dead soldiers’ garments as mementoes, but given wartime shortages and economic necessity, garb in good condition was also traded

60 in for cash or distributed amongst family friends. Brittain “never knew”52 what happened to her fiancé’s clothes, but the classifieds suggested one possible destination for them. Classified ads that ran throughout the war make the link between used coats and dead men even more direct. With the exception of the Times, these ads typically ran on the front page of daily papers, not toward the end where they are located today. As a result, anyone picking up the paper would see these small announcements first. In many editions, advertisements for second-hand stores that dealt in used uniforms and trench-coats were located, without evident irony, right next to the “Killed in Action” columns or appeals for charities that served wounded soldiers. The Daily Telegraph, for example, of January 5, 1917, ran a call for used clothes by “Salmon and Co” in the column next to “Killed in Action.”53 This unhappy juxtaposition was not unusual: on July 5, 1917, the Telegraph similarly placed “Killed in Action” announcements right next to offers to purchase “Officers Uniform and All Effects.” (Figure 7). 54 One might almost suspect the Telegraph of pacifism, but the paper was firmly conservative in outlook. Ads by Salmon and Co. and other second-hand dealers continued to promote their trade in officers’ garments for years after the war ended, reminding readers of the business long after the armistice.55 Ads for trench-mackintoshes were thus surrounded by reminders that such coats could not, in fact, defend against “all” of the discomforts and threats of the trenches. The garments could keep rain, wind and mud at bay, but they could not shield against bullets, bombs, poison gas, and other weapons. In the years after the war, battlefield tourism and memorials helped to cement the association between violence and mackintoshes sold for use at the front. As Jane Marcus writes, “blood-soaked [ . . . ] coats are exhibited in the museum of World War I at Le Linge in Alsace.” Such “pathetic and moving personal belongings”56 remind viewers of the bodies that fought, were wounded, and sometimes died in their military macs. The death of Evadne Price’s character “Blimey,” whose final words express horror at soaking her new Burberry with blood, serves as a fictional mirror for the men and women who wore a uniform that could not protect them. For Price and others who wrote in the decade after the war, it is little wonder that the mac remained linked with a loss of individual identity and violence.

Postwar Reflections and Transformations

The world of James Joyce’s Ulysses seems a long way from the battlefields of France, the Burberry and Aquascutum advertisements, and the Harrods catalog. The novel, set as it is in Dublin in 1904, appears culturally and historically removed from the conflict; Joyce’s landmark aesthetic experiment seems equally removed from the war fiction of the 1920s and 1930s. As any student of modernism knows, however, Ulysses was written during the war and published less than four years after it ended. Joyce’s man in the mackintosh (M’Intosh), who remains unidentified throughout the book, has seemed to take no part in the text’s historical moment, but his sartorial choice begins to make sense when viewed through the history of his garment.

Because the character is so oddly attired—he sports a raincoat on a sunny day during a drought57—and because he is never identified by his proper name, M’Intosh has attracted the notice of many scholars. Although the arguments about this character are as heterogeneous as Ulysses itself, they tend to fall toward two poles: those that claim that the character can be identified,58 and those who use M’Intosh as a lens into modes of understanding Ulysses.59 Like this second cohort of critics, I do not think that Joyce’s riddle can be solved. We can, however, explain why this garment is particularly suitable for his character. Because the name of the garment can revert to a proper name—in Michael Sidnell’s words, it is “an eponymous noun which is (unintentionally) made to revert to a proper one”60—the selection of the coat might appear self-explanatory. The mackintosh also reflects the character’s presumptive poverty: he eats dry bread, and his inexpensive garb manifests his limited means.61 More important, however, is a hitherto ignored reason that the garment “works”: it is associated with loss of individual identity and with violence. If the violence visited upon Joyce’s character is symbolic rather than literal, it is none the less striking and (within the world of Ulysses) permanent.

In “Circe,” the violence associated with the character becomes most apparent. Leopold Bloom’s wild fantasies of power and punishment include a group of sightseers who die after stating, “Morituri te salutant.” M’Intosh then appears through a trapdoor—a feature often used in medieval and renaissance plays as a sign for a grave or hell—and accuses Bloom of being “the notorious fireraiser.” When Bloom responds, “Shoot him! Dog of a christian! So much for M’Intosh!” he disappears with martial signs and rhetoric: “A cannonshot. The man in the macintosh disappears. Bloom with his sceptre strikes down poppies.”62 While the cannonshot does not apparently hit M’Intosh—he “disappears” but does not expire—his appearance is juxtaposed with those who die saluting their leaders. In the aftermath of WWI, it makes sense that a man wearing a mac would

62 be removed by “cannonshot” and his absence marked by “poppies,” which were, of course, associated with the war dead because of the flower’s prevalence in France. Although Bloom thinks about the man in the mac later in Ulysses, M’Intosh disappears in this scene, which surely reminded readers in 1922 of the war and its attendant losses.63 As Robert Spoo has argued in his analysis of “Nestor,” “Joyce’s text, though ostensibly out of battle, is a neutral zone crossed and recrossed by rumors and phantoms” of the war.64 The man in the mackintosh is one such phantom, his garment and name silent reminders of the militarism and death visited upon millions of combatants. In the 1920s, when Joyce (1922), Bowen (1929), and Price/Smith (1930) wrote and published their novels, the mackintosh was making something of a comeback in serials that focused on fashion. The garment was still marketed as utilitarian and practical to working-class customers: advertisements for this class of macs generally emphasize the garment’s cheapness and value, as in promotions for John Blanford’s inexpensive models in navy and fawn (Figure 8).65 Although Mattamac, another firm offering inexpensive mackintoshes, claimed that its coats were “identical in appearance with the usual five-guinea Weatherproof,”66 the sales pitch for this class of coats focused on low prices. If the working class had to make do with dull, cheap mackintoshes, then fashion columns demonstrate that those with money could look quite smart in their macs. These new coats, however, had to be carefully distinguished from the old mackintoshes. A striking number of columns begin discussions of the mac by acknowledging readers’ preconceived notions of the garment. Eve, a British women’s magazine, opened a discussion of “the modern conception of the old-time mackintosh” by observing, “Let us hope that history will not repeat itself, for it would be nothing short of a catastrophe to have to return to the drab, ill-fitting, evil-smelling garb of yore.”67 The rhetoric of history and catastrophe locates the mackintosh in time, marking modern macs as distinct from previous iterations of the category. And yet, this differentiating gesture was repeated on other dates and in other publications, indicating that the “old-time mackintosh” was a vivid memory for many consumers. In 1929, the Sunday Graphic’s “Vogues and Vanities” column, which focused on upper-class society news as well as fashion, described the garment’s transformation after first acknowledging its history: “those of us who can remember the days when a waterproof was the last word in dowdiness were forced to ‘sit up and take notice’ last week. On Wednesday it rained relentlessly at intervals on the Goodwood racing crowd. But [. . .] society merely slipped on its newest macintosh of rubberized velvet or silk in a gay plaid design, or a satin coat made rainproof by some secret process.”68 In this column, the mackintosh is redeemed from “the last word in dowdiness” through new fabrics, colors and technologies. By distinguishing the coat from earlier incarnations, the mackintosh of the 1920s was cast as a fashionable object. In the 1920s and 1930s, then, the mackintosh was becoming popular for a select group of customers. At the level of consumer culture, the women’s mackintosh had been—with some labor—transformed. At the same time, styles based on military coats continued to be popular for men and women. Even as the outer garment and fashion industries framed some mackintoshes as stylish means of self expression, there were reminders of the coat’s association with technological warfare.

Fig. 8. John Blanford’s mackintoshes, marketed here to readers of the Daily Graphic (September 9, 1928), were promoted as inexpensive. Blanford offered payment plans to consumers who couldn’t afford to pay 19’ 11 at one time.

In ads for mackintoshes based upon Great War military styles, allusions to the conflict could be indirect. The February 3, 1932, issue of British Vogue, for example, contained a promotion for the “‘Mosco’ Mackintosh,” which was “cut on the lines of a cavalry overcoat.”69 The reference to the cavalry in this advertisement signified perhaps no more than the design of the coat. In ads for Aquascutum weatherproofs, however, the war provided more than a style: throughout the 1920s and 1930s, the firm used their garments’ war-time performance to promote the brand. An advertisement in the Early August 1923 issue of Vogue proclaimed, “in the Field of Sport women come to regard the Aquascutum Field Coat in the manners officers on Active service esteemed the Aquascutum Trench-coat—as a trustworthy bodyguard in worst of weather.”70 The parallel between sports—and specifically hunting, which would require a Field Coat—

64 and war reminded readers of what men wore at the front and advocated analogous garments for women’s civilian use. A 1930s ad headed “The Famous ‘Aquascutum’ Storm Coats” similarly promoted military styles to men, particularly model G.9, which was “originally designed for the rigorous conditions created by trench warfare” (Figure 9). Over a decade after the war, such language continued to remind the public of the garments worn at the front. In these advertisements, the Aquascutum coat becomes a kind of sartorial memorial: if women and men purchased the coat because similar garments had proved themselves during the war, daily acts of individual consumption were undertaken through a constellation of memories of vulnerable bodies and trench warfare. Because consumers could buy everything from stylish velvet mackintoshes to storm coats in the decades after the war, writers of the 1920s and 1930s had a range of associations to choose from when clothing their characters in mackintoshes. And yet, very few authors chose to depict the coat—and the characters who wore them—as stylish, sporty, comfortable, or modern. More often, mac-clad figures look like Mrs. Dalloway’s Doris Kilman, who tutors the Dalloways’ daughter Elizabeth and “year in year out” wears “a green mackintosh coat.”71 As Clarissa speaks to Elizabeth, she senses Kilman’s presence:

outside the door was Miss Kilman, as Clarissa knew; Miss Kilman in her mackintosh, listening to whatever they said.

Yes, Miss Kilman stood on the landing, and wore a mackintosh; but had her reasons.

First, it was cheap; second, she was over forty; and did not, after all, dress to please. She

was poor, moreover; degradingly poor.72

After Elizabeth and Miss Kilman leave to go shopping, Clarissa muses about “love and religion,” which she envisions as “clumsy, hot, domineering, hypocritical, eavesdropping, jealous, infinitely cruel and unscrupulous, dressed in a mackintosh coat.”73 The range of qualities that Clarissa projects onto Kilman—and specifically onto her coat—points to the latter’s repulsive physicality, to Clarissa’s upper-class distaste for the unfashionably dressed, and to Kilman’s position as Clarissa’s rival for Elizabeth’s love and attention. If Joyce’s M’Intosh seems disembodied under his coat, Kilman’s abject body is all too present, and her mackintosh underlines rather than disguises that abjection.

There are obvious reasons for Kilman to wear the coat even if the weather makes it seem unnecessary. Kilman’s poverty is encapsulated by the mackintosh. She “could not afford to buy pretty clothes,” so Kilman cannot make a striking, distinctive appearance. Instead of a silk or velvet mac, she is likely wearing something like the Blanford or Mattamac coat, in which case price is the garment’s sole virtue. Like Rebecca West’s Margaret Grey before her, then, Kilman wears a coat that marks her as out of place in upper-class homes. When wealthy women criticize these characters’ coats—Clarissa describes Kilman’s appearance as inflicting “positive torture”—such comments reveal as much about upper-class assumptions and values as they do about the women who wear the mackintosh.74 Horrified reactions to the garment neatly emphasize class divisions that neither group can bridge; if the war purportedly unified the nation, the mackintosh signals deep rifts in that unity.

The qualities that Clarissa mentally clothes in the mackintosh indicate that Miss Kilman carries more than the garment’s persistent class connotations on her back. The coat makes Kilman appear clumsy and awkward in its expression of practicality. While a child might be expected to wear a mackintosh, an adult—and particularly a woman—should have other values, whether they are “dress[ing] to please” or personal expression. Although her pragmatic and authentic appearance no doubt contribute to the tutor’s appeal to Elizabeth, Kilman herself “minded looking as she did,”75 and her

66 mackintosh is not so much an attempt to transcend fashion as an admission of sartorial defeat. The power of the negative qualities Clarissa imagines “in a mackintosh coat” signals, furthermore, that the garment serves as an emblem for the “cruel and unscrupulous” war that Woolf, and the other writers I mention in this essay, regarded as barbaric and wasteful. It is a reminder of the women and men, including Miss Kilman’s brother,76 who died in their mackintoshes. As the sole character who wears such a coat—even the shell-shocked Septimus Warren Smith wears a “shabby overcoat” instead of a mac77—Kilman becomes a perambulating reminder of the war most people wanted to forget. Her coat articulates a history of technological innovation and mass production that kept generations of children and adults dry but could not keep men and women whole, psychologically or physically. No other garment could so succinctly capture and reflect the range of qualities that make Doris Kilman unattractive to Clarissa. Kilman’s child-like, unattractive appearance; anger and suppressed violence; and class status are all captured by the mackintosh, which wears Doris as much as Doris wears it. When Kilman stumbles into Westminster Abbey, which contains the “tomb of the Unknown Warrior,”78 Mrs. Dalloway points toward the many reminders of wartime trauma that continue to haunt postwar London. Her coat suggests that the work of mourning continues, and is distributed, across time, space, and garments.79

Long-Lasting Legacies

Although the mackintosh would have other qualities accreted to it over time, it remained characterized by many of the traits I have discussed through the middle of the twentieth century. In Graham Greene’s The Third Man (1949), for example, the British military police officer Major Calloway appreciates the anonymity of his costume, which allows him to be “just a man in a mackintosh.”80 Greene inherits and deploys the tradition that the coat conferred anonymity and was associated with intrigue, subversion, and violence. In postwar Europe, the mac enables police surveillance by erasing individual identity, making each man who wears it nothing more than a “man in a mac.”

In this example and others, the modernist mackintosh points to the power of garments to animate meanings over which individual characters have only partial control. Representations of the mac highlight, and often intimate concern about, the individual’s inability to determine the meaning of his or her appearance: Joyce’s man in the mac becomes M’Intosh; Price/Smith’s female cook wearing a Burberry falls victim to the same bombs dropped on male soldiers; Bowen’s Anglo-Irish juvenile in her mac is arrested in a permanent state of adolescence. While the fashion press and advice columns of the same period insisted that one could control the meaning of one’s aspect—that men and women could look distinctive, stylish, and modern— modernist literature depicts characters as out of control by virtue of their garments, which become habitable histories whether a wearer wants them to or not.

As Alison Clarke and Daniel Miller have argued in their work on fashion and 67 anxiety, dress transcends “the idiosyncrasies of individual agency, instead operating as manifestations of socialization and power relations.”81 The mackintosh manifests such relations particularly powerfully, articulating a history in which bodies were standardized and de-individuated. The coats were worn by some bodies that could transcend their social roles: when Punch ran a poem called “Happiness” in its June 18, 1924, issue, it depicted a little boy named John who had a “Great Big / Waterproof / Macintosh.”82 John would clearly grow up, and macs would stop being the very stuff of pleasure and fun. Most bodies who wore the mac were, however, less able to move out of the places in which socialization and power relations fixed them. Servants, the poor, soldiers and officers had little opportunity to assert their individual agency. Their macs articulate, as Jennifer Craik argues about clothes more generally, “the relationship between a particular body and its lived milieu.”83 In this case, the mac speaks of relationships of inequality, straightened circumstances, and extreme physical vulnerability.

The only option characters seem to have is whether to wear such overdetermined objects in the first place. This is the choice Dorothy Sayers offers to her detective protagonist Lord Peter Wimsey in the 1927 mystery Unnatural Death. Like her highbrow counterparts, Sayers was clearly willing to mobilize the negative connotations imbued in the mackintosh: in both Unnatural Death and Whose Body? (1923), murderers wear the coat to evade capture and to dispose of corpses, respectively. Given such examples, and given the history I have discussed, it is no wonder that Wimsey, a shell-shocked veteran of the Great War, refuses to wait for his friend Charles Parker to don raingear in the middle of an investigation, asserting, “No, Charles, I will not wait while you put on a Burberry.”84 Wimsey’s dedication to solving crime, and his desire to discover where the next clues will lead, renders him too impulsive and passionate to care about something so plebian as a little wetting. He is, moreover, all too aware of what can happen when wearing a Burberry. Readers of modernism might well take this awareness as our own. When we see mackintoshes—garments that history, advertising, and consumer culture turned into tropes—it’s time to pay attention to who puts them on and off.

In this surprisingly cheerful story, the coats obscure the difference between men on opposite sides of international conflicts, a distinction normally crucial to safety during wartime.
