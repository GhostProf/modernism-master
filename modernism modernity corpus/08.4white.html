Johannes Baader's Plasto-Dio-Dada-Drama: the Mysticism of the Mass Media

Michael White

[res]

The First International Dada Fair held in Berlin in 1920 holds a most significant place within the history of the 20th-century avant-garde ( 1). It marks the point where modernist practices of fragmentation, disruption and shock were aimed directly at the German public by utilizing the strategies of contemporary journalism and advertising. Political protest was combined with attack on the exalted status of art, the death of which was famously proclaimed by one placard at the exhibition. A German soldier's uniform with a pig's head was hung from the ceiling, provoking legal action and ensuring good tabloid coverage. The walls of the exhibition rooms were covered with posters and banners which competed for attention with the more traditional paintings of Otto Dix and George Grosz, although their subject matter--crippled war veterans and urban disorder--was suitably confrontational. Photomontage, a new medium recently seized upon by the dadaists, was prominently placed, recycling imagery from the illustrated press for satirical or absurd ends. And in the midst of all this could be found a tower of found objects, newspapers, and textual fragments assembled by Johannes Baader and known as the Plasto-Dio-Dada-Drama ( 2).

Some objects from the Dada Fair have subsequently become museum pieces and are now iconic images of the period, an example being Hannah Höch's large photomontage Cut with the Kitchen Knife. In fact, art historians have slaved long and hard to identify as many of the sources for this montage as possible and offered sustained interpretations of it on the basis of their findings. 1 Baader's sculpture has a very different status, given that it did not survive the exhibition and is only known through photographs. Even so it is widely considered to be a crucial object in the history of modernism: "definitely the first assemblage-environment in the history of art," for Helen Adkins, "one of the first large assemblage-environments," according to Eberhard Roters, "an assemblage of, until then, unparalleled proportions," in Stephen Foster's words. 2 It has also been described as "the most audacious of the diverse array of assemblages at the Dada Fair" and, according to Hanne Bergius, it deserves a new category of its own, démontage, because of its unique, deconstructive nature. 3 Even though it has not been preserved, it is surprising that no similar attempt has yet been made to produce a close reading of Baader's object. It is my intention here to redress this situation so that the Plasto-Dio-Dada-Drama might be more accurately situated within our current understanding of Dada and modernism in general.

The first point of redress is to give the assemblage its full title, not often quoted alongside reproductions: Das große Plasto-Dio-Dada-Drama: Deutschlands Grösse und Untergang oder Die phantastische Lebensgeschichte des Oberdada(The Great Plasto-Dio-Dada-Drama: Germany's Greatness and Decline or The Fantastic Life of the Superdada). It did have, therefore, a very specific subject matter. It was intended to be read both as a description of recent German history and as autobiography (Baader was known amongst his colleagues as der Oberdada). In the first case the assemblage was a record of the fall of the German empire, and in the second Baader's rise to fame. We shall see how in both instances a departure was made from predominant models of, firstly, history as rational progress, and secondly autobiography as self-revelation. To the two movements which Baader aimed to represent, decline and ascent, he gave the form of a sculptural and architectural hybrid part tower, pyramid and spiral. The ultimate function of the object was to be "Dada monumental architecture," and I will conclude by considering how Baader's conception of death and remembrance was also red in the object.

1.

At first glance the Plasto-Dio-Dada-Drama appears to be totally chaotic, an arbitrary collection of objects and texts stacked up in no particular order. However, given the large amount of textual material it is comprised of, it demands to be read. For its original public in 1920 there were two tools to assist them in this task. The first was the exhibition catalogue. It described the piece as:

Dada Monumental Architecture in five stories, 3 parks, 1 tunnel, 2 lifts and 1 cylinder cap. The ground level or floor represents the fate determined before birth and has no other relevance. Description of the stories. 1st floor: The Preparation of the Superdada. 2nd floor: The Metaphysical Test. 3rd floor: The Inauguration. 4th floor: The World War. 5th floor: World Revolution. Penthouse: The cylinder twists into the sky and proclaims the resurrection of Germany by means of schoolmaster Hagendorf's lectern. Eternally. 4

This draws our attention to the numbered labels attached to the assemblage, dividing it into the sections specified. In the best known photograph we can see numbers 1, 2, and 5. A second photograph of unfortunately much inferior quality shows the assemblage from the reverse angle and reveals that the number 4 was placed next to the hanging carbide lamp ( 3). It is safe to surmise therefore that 3 completed the pattern, and that the viewer was expected to follow the numbers around in an ascending spiral. Baader imaginatively incorporated both the floor on which the spectator stands and the empty space above into the work. His reference to it as monumental architecture has connotations of both commemoration and potential scale.

The key to the work that the full catalogue entry provided was further enhanced by the appearance in the Dada Almanachwhich was published to coincide with the end of the exhibition, of a "closer description" of each story. 5 Here Baader offered various references which explain some but not all of the items in the assemblage. The first story, for example, connects his birth in 1875 to the emergence of the unified German state represented by Kaiser Wilhelm I's express train, erroneously misled by the assumption of an ever-unfolding future. Symbolically depicted, it continues traveling around the powder keg on a circular track until it finally explodes. 6 The second story is intended to represent the emergence of a "collective artistic culture . . . in the tunnel of the doomed Reich," above which appears "a museum of masterpieces through the ages" represented in the assemblage by two arched gateways and a mousetrap. 7 At the end of the tunnel stands Baader himself, seized by Platonic ideas which are "violating him, overpowering him," and he is only saved by the "spectre and outcome of the World War." 8 The third and fourth stories concern the declaration of war, interpreted by Baader in terms of Revelations, and the war itself which he, seventy years before Baudrillard had the idea, declares to have been a media event: "a newspaper war. In reality it never existed." 9 The newspapers included to represent this statement carry us on up to the fifth story, World Revolution, which emerges from the peace declared at Spa. Broadcasting into the ether via the aerial which is the final component of the work, Baader proclaims "the final redemption from suffering and death." 10 Some items that are identifiable in the assemblage but not directly mentioned by Baader include, at the bottom, Raoul Hausmann's sound poems, cuttings from Dada journals such as Franz Jung's Die Freie Strasse (The Free Street), to which Baader had been a contributor, and at the summit an issue of Die Pleite (Bankruptcy).

"Germany's Greatness and Decline: or the Fantastic Life of the Superdada," therefore, can be interpreted autobiographically, and it stages a strange Bildungsroman which takes us from Baader's childhood through to maturity. This is red in the object by the transformation from Hausmann's sound poems, deliberately primitivizing and perhaps mimicking baby talk, to the newspaper texts and their "adult" language. That this route leads ultimately to Die Pleite is an idea that will be returned to. From what we can see in the photograph, we also have representations of Baader himself, first as a small drawing to the right of the cog wheel with his name underneath ( 4), and then in the middle left standing next to the assemblage in the form of a tailor's dummy from whose hand hangs the number "2" ( 5). The appearance of a moustache on the latter is another means of representing the passage of time. That this passage has now become the rejection of reason rather than the adoption of it should not deflect from the formal model with which Baader was playing.

Simultaneously, Baader intertwined images of the Wilhelmine empire keyed to particular ideas of modernity. The express train already mentioned can be understood as a reference to the rapid expansion of the railway network under the Kaiserreich. Just behind the number "1" on the assemblage, the toy train used to represent this can be seen ( 6). An important policy of the Wilhelmine government, investment in the rail infrastructure, was brought to a grinding halt by the war, quite literally Wilhelm's express train going nowhere. Baader may also have picked up on the use of a similar image used by Jakob van Hoddis as the climactic line of his 1911 poem Weltende (End of the World) which reads, "trains tumble from bridges everywhere." 11 Another point of reference may have been the fact that Wilhelm II, seeing his role in the First World War as military rather than political, spent much time away from Berlin near the front line. His preferred means of transport on these occasions was the royal train which doubled up as the imperial residence for much of 1914--1918. 12 At the summit of the assemblage is a railway carriage, separated from the engine down below, stranded at Spa ( 7). Between first and second story is another reference to a major Wilhelmine construction project, the "museum of masterpieces through the ages." The architectural fragments and mousetrap undoubtedly refer to Baader's previous career as an architect specializing in funerary monuments, with the Mausefalle (mousetrap) punning with Mausoleum ( 4). Pinned just above to the right, however, we find a reproduction of Dirk Bouts' Christ in the House of Simon, a fifteenth-century Flemish masterpiece which had been purchased for the Kaiser Friedrich Museum in 1904 ( 8). The placement of the mousetrap/mausoleum over the tunnel cleverly refers to the construction of the Museum-Insel (Museum Island) in Berlin, an ambitious attempt to confirm the cultural as well as political significance of the new capital city. By positioning it over an empty space, Baader satirizes its lack of foundation both literally and metaphorically. This cultural center in Berlin had been begun with the building of Schinkel's Altes Museum (1823-1830) (to the cupola of which the shape of the mousetrap may also allude) and the Neues Museum (1843-1850), but the rapid expansion of the royal collection led to the addition of the Kaiser Friedrich Museum (1898-1903) and the Pergamon Museum (1909-1930), all on the same site. The last of these museums, designed specifically to house antiquities transported from Turkey, clearly expressed a desire to place Berlin ahead not only of national rivals Munich and Dresden, but also other European capitals in the arts. It was also a famous characteristic of the Museum-Insel that it was bisected by a major railway line leading in and out of Friedrichstrasse Station, and that passengers were given fleeting glimpses of the great collections as they passed through. That the museum should follow the railway in Baader's assemblage is a cunning reference to Berlin topography.

Perhaps the most significant marker of modernity in the assemblage, though, is its collection of newspapers. Baader has carefully selected for inclusion local Berlin newspapers renowned for their "new journalism." Prominent in the center of the construction is a copy of the Lokal-Anzeiger which, as Peter Fritzsche has demonstrated, was the first paper to bring "urban reportage" to Berlin. It was a paper through which to understand the growing and ever more confusing metropolis:

New arrivals depended on newspapers to find work and housing, to sell goods and services, and to identify urban types and negotiate local institutions. Increasingly elaborate local reportage provided readers with sketches of homelife in tenement buildings, public life along fashionable streets, and work discipline inside factories and department stores. Over time newcomers--readers--filled in the blank spaces of their mental maps of Berlin. Not to read the newspaper was to risk losing orientation. 13

Also prominently displayed by Baader is BZ am Mittag, a Berlin tabloid founded as recently as 1904 which was sold on the street as a "boulevard" newspaper. It takes its place alongside the Dada periodicals some of which, such as Die Pleite, were also sold directly on the streets. At the top, Baader created a signpost from newspaper cuttings: Köthener Strasse in one direction, Die Freie Strasse in the other ( 7). Köthener Strasse is one of the roads adjoining Potsdamer Platz, Berlin's busiest junction, which was a favorite location for journalists to linger and report on the state of the traffic. 14 Once again, Baader integrated local topography into the assemblage, this time connected to journalism and its role in guiding the public through the chaos of the city.

While it is interesting to consider the significance of the newspapers and Dada journals in regard to urban orientation, it should also be noted that, by 1920, Baader had himself become something of a tabloid celebrity in Berlin. One of his most infamous acts, the disruption of a service in the Berlin Cathedral in 1918, was reported in not fewer than three of the local Berlin papers. 15 In addition to the reporting of this and other public stunts, Baader often played practical jokes through the Berlin press, such as the announcement of his death as an April fool's joke in 1919, followed up a little later by the notice of his resurrection. 16 The Plasto-Dio-Dada-Drama was itself preceded by a similar prank, the announcement in two Berlin papers, BZ am Mittag and the Berliner-Börsen Courier of the loss of the manuscript of the Fantastic Life of the Superdadain the following absurd circumstances:

While the Superdada was traveling home after his ascension on the evening of the 13 May on the "W" train from Stettin station to Steglitz, in the company of two angels who escort him, he left behind in the carriage the manuscript of his Fantastic Life Story, due to be published in five installments by Ernst Rowohlt. The finder is requested to deliver the manuscript of the Superdada to Ernst Rowohlt Publishers, Potsdamerstr. 123b, Berlin W.35 in return for a superdadaistic reward. 17

To read the Plasto-Dio-Dada-Drama, then, involves understanding how Baader had begun to live his life through the press and was no longer interested in distinguishing reality from reportage. In the assemblage, he has literally constructed his subjectivity from tabloid journalism. In the context of the Dada Fair, we can compare this to the appearance of another copy of the Lokal-Anzeiger, which George Grosz collaged into his painting Germany, A Winter's Tale (visible in the background of 1). Here, a good Bürger is depicted sitting in a café, drinking a glass of beer and reading the local newspaper, oblivious to the violence and chaos surrounding him. The newspaper is represented not as the key to interpreting social conflict, but as escape from it; to read the Lokal-Anzeiger is red by Grosz as a marker of bad faith. When exhibited at the Dada Fair, the painting was accompanied by a large banner stating that "Dada is Political," which confirmed Grosz's opinion that the role of Dada should be to counter the misinformation disseminated by the press by representing all the brutality of contemporary circumstances. Grosz's attitude to the press was confirmed by his inclusion in a later painting, Pillars of Society , of a newspaper editor depicted alongside other instigators of the bloody repression of the German revolution such as the clergy and the military. Once again the banner of the Lokal-Anzeiger was prominently featured.

The difference in attitude between Grosz and Baader to the mass media is indicative of the divide between the politically committed wing of the Berlin Dada group and its rather more anarchic side. By 1920 Richard Huelsenbeck, the erstwhile leader of the Berlin Dadas, had become distinctly irritated by Baader's self publicizing antics and was almost certainly responsible for the stinging attack in the Dada Almanach, where he described him as "a comfort-loving Swabian pastor or petit bourgeois [who] attempts to exploit his mania for godliness simply in order to line his pocket." 18 He continued this line of criticism in his later memoirs where he again disparaged Baader's "psychotic exhibitionism," calling him "an itinerant preacher, the Billy Graham of his time, a mixture of Anabaptist and circus owner." 19 However, Stephen Foster finds Baader's dadaism to be more radical than that of Grosz in that he attempted to find "an extracultural position from which to speak." 20 This, Foster contrasts to Grosz's "more orthodox politicizing" which did not challenge the accepted roles of art and literature but merely used them in a satirical fashion. How Grosz responded to the inclusion of his own cover for the journal Die Pleite, exhibited at the summit of the Plasto-Dio- Dada-Drama, is unrecorded ( 4). 21

Baader's engagement with the role of the media in the construction of reality has one final arcane reference in the assemblage. Having transformed the Dada montage principle from a two- to a three-dimensional experience, the Plasto-Dio-Dada-Drama alludes in its title to the diorama, a form of spectacular entertainment invented in the early nineteenth century which had been conceived by Daguerre as a challenge to the then quite common panorama. Daguerre took the panorama's concern for illusion or fidelity to nature and added the excitement of witnessing a scene apparently unfolding in real time. Through clever backlighting of a transparent painting, the spectator could enjoy "the many momentary changes of mood which he knew so well from nature--from the hazy morning to the clear light and from the calm beauty of a sunny day to the brooding and stormy sky." 22 The "performance" of an image would last approximately fifteen minutes, and the audience was seated on a revolving platform so that they could be turned to view a second scenario. In 1834, Daguerre devised the "double-effect" diorama, where not only could changing light effects be produced but, with the use of certain filters, res could be made to appear in the scene who had at first been invisible. The craze for such entertainment spread to London and also to Germany. The Berlin diorama developed in a rather extraordinary way with the emergence of the Pleorama, a kind of moving panorama. The designers of this attraction, Carl Wilhelm Gropius and Gottfried Langhans, the latter the architect of the Brandenburg Gate, seated the audience in a ship and took them on a small "voyage." As Helmut Gernsheim records, "the illusion of movement was achieved by the back-cloth slowly traversing the stage, giving the impression of a moving viewpoint on the part of the spectator." 23

While these forms of moving narrative unfolding in time seem extremely pertinent to the Plasto-Dio-Dada-Drama, it is difficult to judge what knowledge Baader may have had of them, as the diorama did not survive much past 1850. The term has remained in common use, especially in English, to describe any form of illusionary model against a backdrop. German dictionaries are far less promiscuous with their definition of the term, limiting it to either the illusionistic show devised by Daguerre or to the building which might have housed such an event. The Berlin diorama was in fact situated in a grand building designed by Schinkel at the intersection of Georgenstrasse and Universitätstrasse, midway between Friedrichstrasse and the Museum-Inseldirectly along the route through which the railway was driven.It is tempting to interpret this as yet another reference by Baader to the actual topography of the city.

Furthermore, as Jonathan Crary has noted, the transition from the panorama to the diorama is a key moment in the creation of the modern spectator. 24 No sooner had the panorama broken with the traditional perspectival box and created a new ambulatory audience, than the diorama removed this freedom of movement, placing the seated viewers in front of a screen on which movement was depicted. Walter Benjamin's description of such spectacles in his notes for Paris Capital of the Nineteenth-Century conflates the two forms, a product it seems of his ambivalence regarding the active or passive roles adopted by the cinema audience. 25 The dominant mode of distraction, which he identifies as the effect of the modernization of vision, had its parallel for him in Dada montage techniques. As early as 1920, Hausmann had claimed that he was producing a kind of cinema, and the Plasto-Dio-Dada-Drama does have the feel of a strangely inverted panorama. Instead of the spectator placed at the center of the world, turning to view the scene all around him, he now circles around an object which has attempted to collect a whole world in itself.

2.

With the idea of the diorama as a scenographic unfolding narrative in mind, we can now turn to the second main narrative which Baader invited the audience to read into the Plasto-Dio-Dada-Drama, namely his autobiography. What I wish to pursue further now are the ways in which this autobiography would have been understood within the longstanding German tradition of the Bildungsroman, a fictionalized account of the development of a subject in connection to a cultural education. The Fantastic Life of the Superdada did indeed mention a key text in this tradition, Goethe's Italian Journey, a diary recording the famous author's grand tour of Italy and assimilation of classical culture. Goethe appears in the text at the start of the third story where he "arrives in Weimar, lays his Italian Journey on teacher Hagendorf's lectern and tells the Swabian pastor [Baader himself]: 'One cannot understand a word of literature without this lectern.'" 26

The first part of Italian Journey was originally published as an appendix to Goethe's autobiography Dichtung und Wahrheit (Poetry and Truth) and was then reprinted several times independently. It functions as an interesting model for Baader in that it combined travelogue with an account of personal development and education. We have already noted how the cultural pretensions of the Wilhelmine empire and its museum building programme are mocked in the Plasto-Dio-Dada-Drama, how "the museum of masterpieces through the ages" is something that Baader depicts himself as passing through, and how Baader uses a reproduction of a Flemish masterpiece from the Kaiser Friedrich Museum. Goethe's Wilhelm Meisters Lehrjahre (Wilhelm Meister's Apprenticeship), which founded the tradition of the Bildungsroman, dealt with the artistic training of a young man through his appreciation of the art of the past. In fact, Goethe began to write this novel while on his Italian trip, and its development is recorded in his diary entries, leading to an intriguing conflation of his personality with that of Wilhelm Meister and both their simultaneous cultural educations. This was taken up by successive imitators, such as Ludwig Tieck's Franz Sternbalds Wanderungen(The Wanderings of Franz Sternbald), which again combined travelogue with the study of Italian and Flemish masters.

An anecdote told by Raoul Hausmann reveals Baader's interest in another very particular version of the Bildungsroman, Gottfried Keller's Der Grüne Heinrich(Green Henry). Walking along the street together one day in summer 1918, Baader suddenly informed him that it was Keller's one hundredth birthday. Hausmann continues: "I made up my mind at once. 'Have you got anything by Keller on you?' 'Yes, I've got Green Henry.' 'Good, let's celebrate.'" Standing in the middle of the street, the two proceeded to take turns to read from the book "at random and read scraps of sentences with no beginning and no end, changing our voices . . . leafing backward and forward, spontaneously." 27 Performing a typical dada subversion of sense, the two transformed the text into a form of sound poetry. The logic of the life described in the novel as an emotional and intellectual development was metamorphosed into a haphazard chain of accidents. But the text they were using was not only a classic Bildungsroman, it was also known to be again a thinly veiled autobiography on Keller's part. Running to four volumes it included all kinds of material drawn from the author's own life, not all of which was flattering. The novel details the ambitions of an artist whose quest for fame destroys his family, a story that strangely parallelled Baader's own neglect of his wife and children. Just as with his attitude to the press, Baader was ever reluctant to draw the line between fiction and reality. In the context of the negative Bildungsroman that the Plasto-Dio-Dada-Drama was, it is important to note that Baader had experienced traumatic losses of selfhood, none more painful than his persistent identification with the re of Christ. Although he is perhaps the only dadaist to have been actually certified insane, I do not wish to undertake a retrospective diagnosis of him through his work or normalize his personality by matching it to a recognized psychiatric condition. His testing of the boundaries of personality is far more interesting as a cultural phenomenon and should be considered alongside Dada's broader examination of the modern subject. In the assemblage, Baader used a typical Dada motif in the image of the mannequin or dummy which stands in for himself and holds the re "2" in its right hand. Mannequins frequently appear in Dada imagery as metaphors for the rejection of the expressionist concern for the uniqueness of the individual; they register the dehumanizing effects of industrial society, or they signify aspirations to what Michael Hays has called a post-humanist subjectivity. 28 In the Plasto-Dio-Dada-Drama, the mannequin features at the end of the second story, described in the text as "The Metaphysical Test," where Baader finds himself "violated by Platonic ideas." He is rescued only by the appearance of "Herr von Münchausen's beanstalk," via which he climbs to the next story. In Baader's biography, this is the moment he published his first book, the Fourteen Letters of Christ, written in the form of a series of letters to eminent people signed by himself as Christ. A copy of the book is described as included somewhere in the lower section of the Plasto-Dio-Dada-Drama, although it is not visible in the photographs. Significantly, he reused an image of the very same mannequin for a 1920 photomontage, The Author of the Book "The Fourteen Letters of Christ" in his Home. The outcome of "The Metaphysical Test" is the rejection of a metaphysical separation of the real from the apparent along with the notion of a stable subjectivity. The result for the Plasto-Dio-Dada-Drama is that it cannot be seen as a self portrait so much as Baader's self- construction. There is no real Johannes Baader beyond the assemblage.

One final important piece of autobiographical writing should be mentioned here which again contested the construction of the subject formulated by the Bildungsroman, namely Nietzsche's Ecce Homo. Both Baader and his closest associate in the Dada group, Raoul Hausmann, were enthusiastic readers of Nietzsche, and the latter is known to have owned a copy of Ecce Homo. 29 The text proposes itself as a reflection back on the philosopher's life and career giving short accounts of all his books prefaced by sections titled "Why I am so Wise," "Why I am so Clever," and "Why I Write such Excellent Books." It is immediately obvious that Baader's Fantastic Lifestory of the Superdada follows certain traits of Ecce Homo. In the early sections of Ecce Homo, Nietzsche relates his family history and childhood to the political life of Germany, such as sharing his birthday and name with King Friedrich Wilhelm IV of Prussia. In the opening sections of the text, Nietzsche also makes clear that his life story is as much one of decline as progress. He casts himself as a decadent, a Doppelgänger with at least two faces:

To express it in the form of a riddle, as my father I have already died, as my mother I still live and grow old. This twofold origin, as it were from the highest and the lowest rung of the ladder of life, at once décadent and beginning . . . I have a subtler sense for signs of ascent and decline than any man has ever had, I am the teacher par excellence in this matter--I know both, I am both. 30

The Plasto-Dio-Dada-Drama integrates both ascent and decline in its ostensible subject matter: the fall of the German empire, and the rise of the Superdada. It also makes use of the spiral form curving around the tower or pyramid structure to signify these two movements. At the summit stands the banner of Die Pleite, but we are asked to imagine the cylinder revolving endlessly into the ether. Baader found a form to accommodate the dual movements present in Nietzsche's philosophy; the linear passage from man to superman, and the cycle of eternal recurrence. The first term takes an evolutionary model, the second a circular one. The spiral, as movement without progress, fits perfectly with the antirational nature of this dynamic.

In Thus Spake Zarathustra, Nietzsche introduced eternal recurrence as a deconstruction of idealist interpretations of history as teleology. Central to the same book is the doctrine of the superman as destiny of mankind. How the two might be compatible has created problems in Nietzsche scholarship ever since. However, in the prologue to the book, Nietzsche has Zarathustra state quite clearly that the passage to the superman is both a "going-across" and a "down-going." 31 Similarly, eternal recurrence has no status in Nietzsche's writings as a cosmological explanation of existence. Rather, he is interested in the psychological burden of repetition. These notions are red in the Plasto-Dio-Dada-Drama by the express train "erroneously misled by an assumption of an ever-unfolding future" and, in the second story, "the wheel of events (for furthering the transformation of the round by means of new lives--albeit still tied to the wheel)." 32

Baader was not alone in using the spiral as a re for the active nihilism summoned up by Nietzsche's thinking. The architect Henry van de Velde was commissioned by Elisabeth Förster-Nietzsche to build the Nietzsche Archive in Weimar in 1902-1903, and he spent the next years devising plans for a Nietzsche temple and monument for the same location. One of the surviving drawings shows how he planned to use a section of Thus Spake Zarathustra entitled The Way of the Creator as an inscription on the monument, a text which praises the solitary artist who places himself above the law, beyond good and evil: "Are you a new strength and a new right? A first motion? A self-propelling wheel? Can you also compel stars to revolve around you." 33 The only decorative element van de Velde conceived for the monument was the inscribed motif of a spiral on a square base expressing the movement described in the text which is, once again, dynamic without being progressive. The significance of the number five for the stories of the Plasto-Dio-Dada-Drama is that the spectator is lead around the square base and returns to the side where he began. 34 The life that the object traces is set merely to repeat.

3.

A few commentators on the Plasto-Dio-Dada Drama have made the connection between it and Baader's earlier career as an architect who specialized in mausoleums and funereal architecture. 35 As noted earlier, the Plasto-Dio-Dada Drama was to be considered "Dada monumental architecture" and it contained numerous references to recent building programs in Berlin. The image of a tower or pyramid that it conjures up is very much connected to his earlier visionary projects such as the Cosmic Temple Pyramid which he planned in 1906 ( 9). This unfeasible construction of colossal size (1000m wide at the base and 1500m high) was to contain "free universities, libraries and archives, modern world museums and world collections, arenas and plazas for international games, music festivals and dramas, public parks, pilgrimage centers, garden cities, and villas and gardens and forests and fields and mountains and meadows and steams and lakes and rivers and the sea." 36 The pyramid was the ultimate collection of world culture and knowledge, a Gesamtkunstwerk of astonishing ambition. It was also, as Baader conceded, "a new Tower of Babel," an image I would suggest is preserved in the Plasto-Dio-Dada Drama.

Baader's response to modern architecture appears to have been drawn largely from the satirical writer Paul Scheerbart. From the 1890s onwards, Scheerbart had been developing in his blackly comic novels the image of a new fantastic architecture of color and glass. Combining apocalyptic with utopian themes, he dreamt of crystal palaces set on mountain tops constructed through the force of sheer genius. He appears in Baader's text in the third story: "Paul Scheerbart pulls up in a coach of crystal and lies down--as a bomb--beside the dusty crate of architecture," possibly a reference to Scheerbart's death in 1915. 37 Baader is reputed to have known him well, and it is likely that he was mimicking the megalomaniacal characters of Scheerbart's novels when he appointed himself President of the Globe. 38 Scheerbart had also come up with the vision of the Earth as a star, Der Stern Erde, which Baader used in his later cosmologies. Baader also took over Scheerbart's adoption of a monistic philosophy and rejection of the Christian explanation of death, an idea which has interesting implications for the Plasto-Dio-Dada-Drama. As mentioned before, the fifth story is not conceived by Baader as the end of the narrative. Above it he imagines the cylinder soaring into the sky from which he broadcasts "into the ether the final redemption from suffering and death." The spiral form of the assemblage is important for developing its energy to continue ever upwards, spinning by force of its own momentum. The inspiration for this idea may well have been derived from Scheerbart's 1910 novel Das Perpetuum Mobile, which recounts in first person form the attempts of its narrator to invent a machine which conserves all of its energy and keeps moving eternally. As he steadily approaches bankruptcy, the narrator fantasizes more and more on the potential of such a device to solve all the world's problems, but also to conquer death as we know it, finally introducing the image of the Earth Star as the ultimate perpetuum mobile. 39 Hanne Bergius, in her account of Baader's family history, tells us that his father was a carpenter who went bankrupt in 1891 after neglecting his work in favor of trying to build a similar device. Once again we are faced with a blurring of the lines between reality and fiction, as Scheerbart could have taken the plot of his book from Baader's own life.

Finally, it has not gone unnoticed that the form of the Plasto-Dio-Dada-Drama bears an uncanny similarity to Tatlin's Monument to the Third International, both in its tower form and the dynamic rotational aspect. There is no evidence to suggest that Baader may have seen a photograph of the model that Tatlin had recently completed, but a written description of Tatlin's project had been published in Germany shortly before the opening of the Dada Fair. Konstantin Umanskij's Neue Kunst in Russland1914-1919 has long been acknowledged as the source for the placard displayed at the fair of "Die Kunst ist tot--Es lebe die neue Maschinenkunst Tatlins" ("Art is dead--long live Tatlin's new machine art"), but the text also commented on the enormous scale of Tatlin's project and his plans for it to contain a radio station, cinemas, telegraph and space for art exhibitions. 40

The synchronicity between Baader and Tatlin may also be explained by their common adoption of the Eiffel Tower as image of modern engineering and architectural ambition. While this may be rather more literal in Tatlin's case, it is interesting to note Baader's incorportation of Henri Rousseau's Self-Portrait into the collage Club der Blauen Milchstrasse of 1920 ( 10). 41 Here, Raoul Hausmann's face has been inserted in place of Rousseau's and the famous background, which included the Eiffel Tower, has been partially covered over by Baader's leaflet protesting against the constitution of the Weimar republic and declaring himself President of the Globe. Also included in the collage is a reproduction of the Cosmic Temple Pyramid in the top left hand corner, and in the lower right a flyer explaining the significance of the Handbuch des Oberdadaismus, Baader's version of the Bible. On the right of the collage, emanating from behind Hausmann/Rousseau, and where we would have originally seen the Eiffel Tower, is a cone reaching up into the heavens and to a cosmological ying/yang symbol. In the same way that Tatlin attempted to make the Monument to the Third International calendric and astronomically aligned, both he and Baader produced readings of the Eiffel Tower that were thoroughly utopian rather than concerned for the practical engineering possibilities. This was to be architecture that could relieve mankind from the alienation of urban life by reconnecting it to the cosmos rather than increase its disempowerment in the face of technology.

Furthermore, as we trace the passage of communication in the Plasto-Dio-Dada-Drama from phonetics to journalism, we should not forget the aerial at the summit that would transform these words into radio signals that could be broadcast into the ether. The Eiffel Tower was used in its earliest years for meteorological experiments and as a giant aerial. Tatlin took up the theme to suggest that the message of international communism could be broadcast from his colossal tower across otherwise insurmountable boundaries. If Baader's monument is viewed as a pyramid and his background as a designer of mausoleums is borne in mind, then it may be that the route of the spiral is meant to suggest the transformation of matter into invisible energy. Given that we have been obliged to consider this as Baader's autobiography, the Plasto-Dio-Dada-Drama becomes a tomb which will preserve his presence after his death. However, its ephemeral nature contradicts everything that a tomb should be; instead we are left with the conclusion that Baader is commenting on his own transformation into a trail of newspaper headlines, into a set of references in the media--into what he has in fact become almost half a century after his death.

We have clearly seen that beyond its reputation as the largest, most audacious assemblage of the 1920 Dada Fair, the Plasto-Dio-Dada-Drama was also an object loaded with meaning rather than the unintelligible heap it has previously been taken to be. Moreover, it was aimed at a very particular urban audience, the tabloid reading Berliner, and used visual strategies adopted from montage that related very specifically to the presentation of narrative in new media such as film. Spatial and temporal narratives connected the assemblage to the topography of the city center and represented the manner in which the contemporary subject navigated it. Most importantly, the Plasto-Dio-Dada-Drama presented itself a perpetuum mobile not because it actually moved, but because it was projected into the future as an object through which Baader would be remembered. If the main message of the Dada Fair has been read until now as the death of art, the staging of the death of the author and his resurrection as a media construction which Baader enacted through this assemblage will ultimately be seen as far more radical.
