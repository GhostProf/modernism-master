Science and Narcissism

Christopher Herbert

Science and Modernism:
A Panel

In a 1917 apologia for psychoanalysis, Freud portrays the history of science as a series of parallel revolutions, each in its own domain inflicting chastisement upon the retrograde influence of what he terms human "narcissism." The mandate of scientific thought, according to Freud, is to abolish all the fallacies that arise from anthropocentrism, from ascribing a privileged position in the natural world to human values or a human point of view. Copernican astronomy, Darwinian biology, and now psychoanalysis all give expression to this paramount motive of science, "the destruction of . . . narcissistic illusion." 1 This definition of scientific method as (in Freud's repeated term) the "wounding" of human self-love fits closely with his analysis in texts such as Civilization and Its Discontents of the cruel mutilations of libido that civilization necessarily exacts upon mankind. Given the sadistic impulse that he identifies as the crucial imperative in science, it is no wonder that he worries in this essay that many regard his own new science with "aversion and resistances." 2

The indictment of anthropocentrism was hardly original to Freud. Around the turn of the twentieth century and in the following several decades, it was proclaimed with a frequency that seems to signal some noteworthy cultural perturbation. One writer after another identifies anthropomorphism or anthropocentrism as precisely the antithesis, the nullification, of science. "Anthropomorphism plays a considerable historic role" in the genesis of physical thinking, admits the mathematician Henri Poincaré in 1902, "but it can be the foundation of nothing of a really scientific or philosophical character." 3 In keeping with a series of late Victorian writers who identify anthropomorphism as the characteristic mode of thought of "primitive man," of the prescientific mentality, James Frazer in The Golden Bough attempts a vast evolutionary reconstruction of this deluded phase of thinking. Central to his analysis is his definition of myth as "a description of physical phenomena in imagery borrowed from human life"; science, which he calls the "golden key" to truth, by contrast, is the method that as its primary operation "strips nature of personality." 4 In his analysis of the superstitious roots of primitive anthropomorphism, Frazer means to drive a scientific stake at last into the heart of this ancient source of folly, ignorance, and cruelty.

Not every contemporary observer was sanguine about soon eliminating the lethal contaminant of human narcissism from science. Arthur James Balfour declares in 1879 that "our idea of the external world is . . . anthropomorphic" inescapably, concluding from this that science is afflicted with an irrationalism that no amount of supposed methodological rigor can ever cure. 5 The anthropologist and philosopher Lucien Lévy-Bruhl, in his book Ethics and Moral Science of 1903, frames the same problem in only marginally less pessimistic terms, focusing now on biology. Scientific research strives to cleanse knowledge of what he calls its "specifically human character" and thus to attain "objectivity," he declares. 6 Yet so deeply ingrained is the anthropocentric principle in human thought, says Lévy-Bruhl, that even the mighty achievements of modern science have failed to annihilate it. "The struggle against anthropocentrism is far from ended," he concludes; "its strongest positions are not yet touched. It has only lost, so to speak, its outworks." 7

The declaration of war on anthropocentrism may constitute the permanent creed or charter of true science, as all these writers take for granted, but the prominence of this theme in turn-of-the-century discourse indicates its distinct period-specific character, too, as one component of the wave of new thought and expression, and of anxiety, known as modernism. As the reaction against a period perceived as in thrall to complacent sentimentalities and self-aggrandizing systems masquerading as timeless verities, modernism in one of its aspects takes a mood of critical astringency and of a principled denial of self-gratification as the mark of rectitude in sophisticated intellectual inquiry. This is the aspect of modernism anatomized, for instance, by José Ortega y Gasset in his essay "The Dehumanization of Art." Serious modern artists, he declares, take it as their mission to create artworks from which narcissistic appeals to human sentiment have been systematically purged by means of "an explicit act of dehumanization" 8 --and, for that matter, take reactions of "aversion and resistances" from the general public as evidence of their success. Modernist discourse on science converges unmistakably at this point with modernist literary interpretation.

However, there emerges in the same period a countercurrent to the dominant trend of argument and ideology just described: a dissident movement that opposes the definition of scientific method as a suppressing of anthropocentric fallacies and stripping nature of personality. "The subject of science is the human universe," declares the mathematician W. K. Clifford at the early date of 1872; "that is to say, everything that is, or has been, or may be related to man." 9 The proponents of the movement announced by Clifford challenge the assumption that scientific objectivity must or can rest upon what are called, in a phrase much bandied about by late Victorian controversialists, "absolute truths." They argue that scientific rationality can never free itself from "imagery borrowed from human life," and that in striving to do so, it becomes incoherent and sacrifices its claim to intellectual legitimacy. They suggest that the credo of science as the persecutor of human narcissism originates in a will to tyrannical domination. This movement of what could be called the rehumanization of knowledge is inseparable from that elaboration of the relativistic principle that constitutes one of the defining intellectual projects of the modernist period. In all the forms that this project assumes, it holds up to critical analysis the widespread, almost compulsive determination to shield from view the human image stamped ineffaceably upon all the intellectual productions of our species.

I will not dwell on Ludwig Feuerbach here, but a full retrieval of the history of the anthropomorphic theme in modern discourse would need to assign him an important chapter. In his great polemic of 1841, The Essence of Christianity, translated into English by George Eliot in 1854, Feuerbach trains his critique upon theology, but its implications extend widely into other areas of thought as well. God properly understood, Feuerbach declares, is an idealized projection of mankind; religion, the supreme expression of human narcissism, thus originates in and symbolizes "the deification of man." 10 In the words of Feuerbach's famous motto, "the beginning, middle and end of religion is MAN" (EC, 184). For malign purposes of its own, theology misrepresents this state of affairs, falsely denying the anthropomorphic character of God and in so doing "[attaching] religion to an external object" (EC, 11). In short, Feuerbach sees theology as an agency of the same denial of anthropomorphism that Freud and others ascribe honorifically to modern science--though for Feuerbach, this denial is the very reverse of a rolling back of superstition. In dispossessing humanity of its ultimate imaginative creation, it produces as its chief effect "the disuniting of man from himself"; indeed, it "sets God before [man] as the antithesis of himself," in a relation of estrangement and hostility (EC, 33). All the fanatical obsession of Christian thinkers with the category of absolute truth, all their morbid hostility to and vindictive repression of human nature, and all the sanguinary abominations of religious history--all these things flow, according to Feuerbach, from the theological denial of the truth that the divine is constructed wholly of "imagery borrowed from human life." In The Essence of Christianity, the rejection of anthropomorphism is a purely diabolical moment.

In this essay, I will trace Feuerbach's intellectual legacy in only a single representative text, Karl Pearson's The Grammar of Science, which first appeared in 1892, fifty-one years after The Essence of Christianity. Pearson never refers to Feuerbach, but seems to have him at least unconsciously in mind as he surveys the systematic perversion of another department of knowledge, that of modern physics. The vocabulary of the polemic has shifted, in keeping with the times. What Feuerbach calls "theology," Pearson in effect names "materialism."

The Grammar of Science begins on a note far removed from Feuerbachian skepticism, professing what looks like an orthodox faith in scientific knowledge, particularly in the burgeoning field of physics. Legitimate science appeals strictly to the reason and disavows the imagination, Pearson declares, in a distinctly anti-Feuerbachian spirit. Scientific reasoning forms an ideal training for modern citizenship in a period of rampant controversy and uncertainty, since it depends on the principle of what he terms "self-elimination," the principled setting aside of "personal bias"--in other words, of that element of narcissism or anthropocentrism that Freud also tells us is the enemy of rational understanding. 11 Science, says Pearson here, is above all a stringent, ascetic discipline, dehumanized in exactly Ortega's sense, treading unswervingly what is called "the hard and stony path of classifying facts and reasoning upon them" (GS, 17). The theory of modern science as an engine for the systematic mortification of human narcissism could hardly find a fuller expression than in this opening movement of Pearson's treatise.

It is with a calculated rhetorical suddenness, then, that The Grammar of Science swerves into another analytical register--flying, in fact, squarely in the face of Pearson's opening profession of faith. The human creative imagination, a few pages previously excluded from the domain of respectable science, is now identified as, after all, the source "of all great scientific discoveries" (GS, 30). And now, in place of the austere discipline of scientific analysis plodding laboriously along "the hard and stony path" of fact, science, like art, proves to be driven by the jouissance proper to the experience of beauty: "both afford material for the gratification of the aesthetic judgment" (GS, 36). Pearson does not quite assert that the beauty of a scientific theory could be a measure of its truth, as others have since, but his argument points unmistakably in this direction.

He takes a bold step further: "The universe is largely the construction of each individual mind," he declares, and is therefore "a variable quantity," altering with each succeeding generation of scientists (GS, 15). "External objects," a phrase regularly placed within quotation marks in this book, are not self-existent entities confronting human perception; rather, in accordance with what Pearson, naming his chief principle of analysis, calls "the pure relativity of all phenomena" (GS, 394 n), they are constructions of the human faculties. By the same token, scientific "laws of nature" are manufactures of human thought, without a basis in any world of external reality. "It may seem at first sight strange to the reader," says Pearson, "that the laws of science should thus be associated with the creative imagination in man rather than with the physical world outside him" (GS, 36), but such is the case, he insists. "Law in the scientific sense is . . . essentially a product of the human mind and has no meaning apart from man. It owes its existence to the creative power of his intellect. There is more meaning in the statement that man gives laws to Nature than in its converse that Nature gives laws to man" (GS, 87).

For Pearson, in other words, once physical science falls under the sign of a principle of "pure relativity," it becomes of necessity radically and militantly anthropocentric and, in Freud's sense, narcissistic. The human being is promoted in this text to the status of nothing less than the creator of the universe and the author of its laws: in this sense, The Grammar of Science proposes its own version of "the deification of man." Yet the so-called laws of nature that human scientists, driven by the craving for esthetic pleasure, labor to articulate are in fact not so much inventions as they are simply the principles of human cognition itself. "The logic man finds in the universe is but the reflection of his own reasoning faculty" (GS, 91), says Pearson, his metaphor of reflection here bringing the Narcissus myth distinctly to mind. Science does not imagine deities in human form within nature, but it depends on an analogous mental operation, for it imagines particles, waves, and forces behaving in accordance with human laws of logic. Pearson's doctrine, we may say, paraphrasing Feuerbach, is that "the beginning, middle, and end of science is MAN."

The reign of the false doctrines of what Pearson calls, synonymously, "materialism" and "metaphysics" has come about in accordance with exactly the dispossessing, traumatically alienating event diagnosed by Feuerbach in the domain of religious imagination--and, in the meantime, by Marx in the field of capitalist production. The materialist philosopher of science, says Pearson, "unconsciously severs himself from the products of his own reason, projects them into phenomena, only to refind them again and wonder what reason put them there" (GS, 91); this same materialist philosopher, we are told, constructs a universe of objects related by natural laws "and then--as his habit is--forgetful of his own creative facility, has converted [them] into a dominant reality behind his perceptions and external to himself" (GS, 167). Pearson, like his mentor the physicist and philosopher Ernst Mach, claims that science has no business explaining the phenomena it studies, and so he does not attempt to explain the appearance of this syndrome of perverse misunderstanding, which shrouds scientific rationality in metaphysics, and in the process, not at all incidentally, inculcates an ethic of subjection to a so-called "dominant reality" (GS, 167). As far as his text explicitly states, scientist-philosophers fall into metaphysics simply in a fit of forgetfulness. Such a complex would be easy to interpret, however, as the necessary outcome of the ideology of science being promoted with such fervor by Freud, Poincaré, and many others at this period, to the effect that the progress of scientific knowledge can only come about by means of--in fact is identical to--the repression of the vice of human narcissism. Science in this official view is that intellectual enterprise dedicated to teaching man his subordinate place in the universe, where he is to eat his bread in humility. Abjuring human creativity, imagining perceptions as external objects, and revering "absolute truth" in science as in other domains of human life--these are simply the epistemological corollaries of this moral commandment of poverty and self-abnegation: so runs the implicit logic of Pearson's text.

To follow out this logic is to move with Pearson's help toward understanding the anti-anthropocentric dogma of turn-of-the-century science as a residue of a cultural dynamic deeply associated with the "Victorian" moment in Europe, and as playing to some degree a dissonant, unstable role, even a regressive one, with regard to the modernist enterprise at large. A definition of science as first and foremost an agency for "the destruction of . . . narcissistic illusion" is almost too symmetrical with Feuerbach's analysis of theological authoritarianism or with, say, John Stuart Mill's assertion that the defining feature of the Victorian milieu was its domination by what he called "the engines of moral repression." 12 Feuerbach's and Mill's analyses suggest, that is to say, the heavy ideological burden of Freud's idea of science; more to the immediate point, they help to make legible the satirical idiom of Pearson's Grammar of Science. His method in much of the book centers on rigorously deconstructive studies of the categories of classical mechanics (force, mass, matter, absolute space and time, causality, the ether, and so on), showing each in turn to suffer from hopeless logical incoherence and in fact to be scientifically vacuous. If we follow the skein of implication that gradually unwinds from these exercises in The Grammar of Science, we come to view the physical world constructed by Newtonian mechanics--a preposterous world of massive bodies, mysterious forces, implacable laws of causality--as, among other things, the characteristic self-expression of a cultural and intellectual system based on the repressive rule of a caste of lawgiving authorities. And this, Pearson makes sufficiently clear, is exactly the state of affairs that his theory of science is meant to address.

What he refers to as the "maze of metaphysics which at present obstructs the entry of physics" (GS, 221) thus signifies for Pearson not an innocent intellectual miscalculation after all, but an imprisoning structure of mystification, the function of which is to lock our critical and imaginative faculties helplessly within a system of external absolutes. Physicists' elaborately circular, illogical, self-contradicting theories of such entities as force and matter, which Pearson documents at length with reference to writings of James Maxwell, Lord Kelvin, P. G. Tait, and other eminent scientists, exemplify for him the same pernicious mode of discourse allegorized by Charles Dickens in Bleak House as the "monstrous maze" of legal mumbo jumbo (an impenetrable labyrinth of "numerous difficulties, contingencies, masterly fictions, and forms of procedure"): webs of language designed in both cases to produce a condition of stupefied acquiescence to the established order. 13 Pearson's extravagant stress on human creativity and on the doctrine of "the pure relativity of all phenomena" springs from an expressly emancipatory intention, in other words. Nor does this intention confine itself to the field of absolutist Newtonian mechanics. As Henry Adams put it in his comments on what he called Pearson's "revolutionary" book, what Pearson seeks to dismantle is nothing less than the whole order of thinking that had "created a universe the essence of which was abstract Truth; the Absolute; God!" 14 Adams understood with perfect clarity the Feuerbachian impulse that drove Pearson's critique of physics.

The modern relativistic tradition from The Essence of Christianity onward seeks to counteract the regime of dehumanized absolutes by installing a frankly anthropocentric theory of science based on human and humane values. It inserts the principles of the inescapable mutability of knowledge, of the legitimacy of "alternative constructions of reality," 15 of human freedom and (as Einstein repeatedly put it) of the almost miraculous potency of the human creative imagination, squarely at the center of the definition of science. 16 From the point of view of this mode of "humanism," devoted as it is to the licensing of an enriched, more freely speculative pursuit of knowledge, the model of scientific rationality as the instrument of the suppression of human narcissism has no place at all.

Christopher Herbert is Professor of English and Associate Dean for the Humanities at Northwestern University. He is author of Trollope and Comic Pleasure and Culture and Anomie: Ethnographic Imagination in the Nineteenth Century . The present essay forms a part of his current work in progress, a study of scientific and literary discourse at the turn of the century.
