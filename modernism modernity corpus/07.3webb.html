The Centrality of Race to the Modernist Aesthetics of Gertrude Stein's Four Saints in Three Acts

Barbara Webb *

The 1934 opera Four Saints in Three Acts, composed by Virgil Thomson with a libretto by Gertrude Stein, is a landmark in the history of American performance. Broadway had never before showcased a work in which words served the usual function of music, not telling a story or communicating meaning in a traditional fashion, but primarily sounding. The casting was also revolutionary, as the entirely black cast performed a work not written in dialect and unrelated to traditional themes of black life. This intersection in 1934 of Steinian modernism and black performance is more than an interesting episode in the history of theatre. It is a prime moment in which to examine a modernist text transferred to the stage, and the transformation of its poetics of "nonidentity" when the communicative medium is a performing body inscribed with social history. An examination of the Stein/Thomson project uncovers a tension between a high modernist strategy that rejected linearity and history and a visible physical racial identity that was, by definition, the cumulative effect of historical cultural narrative(s). On one hand, the high modernist project of Stein and others can be characterized as unrelated to the historical realism, concerned not with referentiality, but with the artistic capacity of syntax. 1 On the other hand, race is intrinsically a historical product and its injection through the casting decision into this high modernist project presented a serious challenge to a poetics that sought to bypass dominant social structures.

An important question about the 1934 production of Four Saints is whether the ostensible refusal of Stein's libretto to engage the discourse of the current social structure remained intact when embodied by a black cast in performance. I will argue that cultural narratives of race, which were reinforced by certain directorial choices, defeated her linguistic strategy for circumventing dominant structures. In fact, those narratives reinforced Stein's poetic project and complemented her artistic goals.

Landscape Theatre, Narrative and Identity

Stein's landscape conception of theater, which defies the traditional use of narrative and character, has an established place among major theatre and performance theories. The philosophical and artistic framework upon which her landscape performance pieces such as Four Saints are based can be inferred from writings such as The Geographical History of America and Lectures in America, both compiled after she saw the 1934 production of Four Saints. In Geographical History, she suggests that landscape theater is the presentation of a state of existing as opposed to a story or character sketch. The first time she uses act and scene titles in the book, suggesting a play, they precede a declaration of existence: "I am I because my little dog knows me." 2 Throughout the book she questions the role of the "little dog," or audience/observer, in delineating existence; it is clear that the Steinian theatre of Geographical History is concerned with the experience and acknowledgment of being rather than with narrative. Her theatrical goals find a parallel in the concept of spectacle, a nonnarrative flood of sights and sounds similar to a circus. Stein insinuated in Lectures in America that producing a theatrical experience of general, nonintellectual sensation was her goal: "All a child's feeling of the theater is two things. One which is in a way like a circus that is the general movement and light and air which any theater has and a great deal of glitter in the light and a great deal of height in the air." 3

The states of existence and being that are necessary for this theatrical experience are not to be confused with identity, which is a product of history and narrative structure. Because of identity's grounding in history, a "state of confusion" according to Stein, it has no place in a masterpiece, which is rooted in "the human mind": "Now identity remembers and so it has an audience and as it has an audience it is history and as it is history it has nothing to do with the human mind" (GHA, 133, 175, 139). This observation links history not only to identity but also to reception, acknowledging the element of social context in performance that presents a significant obstacle to high modernist ahistoricism in Four Saints, as I will show later. The characters in Stein's landscape theatre pieces do not have identities, as we cannot make inferences about their nature or characteristics based on dialogue, narrative progression in the piece, or references within it to a time "before" the staged action. Knowledge of the lives of the historical St. Térèse and St. Ignatius does not effectively supplement the audience's understanding of their namesakes in Four Saints because historical facts are irrelevant to the libretto and to the staging of the opera. We know from the opera's title and the characters' names that they are supposed to be saints, but this information is not helpful in predicting or interpreting their actions within the opera. The opera attempted to define "saint" not based on any a priori conceptions of identity but by constituting the label performatively in the terms of the acts the characters carried out within it.

These acts did not follow from causal relationships, nor did they build cumulatively as is characteristic of narrative structure. Instead, speech, movement, and action were isolated in unrelated tableaux that proceeded in a nondeterministic flow. The series of tableaux in which characters identified as Spanish saints were seen chatting, picnicking, marching in a processional, and painting giant Easter eggs were snapshots of existence rather than traditional scenes of action. Stein herself did not even characterize the movements of the characters as "doing": "But a really good saint does nothing and so I wanted to have Four Saints who did nothing and I wrote Four Saints in Three Acts and they did nothing and that was everything." 4 Here, she defines "doing" as that which does not happen in Four Saints: history and progressive action. The hyperbolic and campy staging of the play by Virgil Thomson, Maurice Grosser, and choreographer Frederick Ashton undermined the standard definition of action as that which inherently contributes to narrative progression. This was in agreement with Stein's musings in Picasso: "A thing without progress is more splendid than a thing which progresses." 5 The saints perform their actions for the sake of performance, actions that lead to nothing else "happening," but are complete in themselves. The actions performed early in the opera are not necessary for evaluating actions carried out later on and thus avoid the syncopative disunity that so irritated her in narrative. 6

According to Stein, narrative is unworthy of a masterpiece not only because it opposes unity but because it is "not interesting" (GHA, 142) and too common: "Everybody knows so many stories and what is the use of telling another story" (LA, 118). One perspective on her regard for stories is that it betrays her own white, bourgeois identifications by suggesting that all narratives, versions of history, and human experience have already been told: "It is perfectly extraordinary how many complicated dramas go on all the time. And everybody knows them, so why tell another one" (LA, 118). Such a position assumes that all voices and viewpoints have long been present and accounted for and thus denies any historical and social specificity in human experience. Thomson and Grosser understood and embraced Stein's position on narrative and consciously chose black performers to be the vehicles for her aesthetic. 7 The production deliberately situated black persons in a timeless realm devoid of history or stories. Such an artistic move is significant given the Jim Crow environment of 1934 and the corresponding need for its stories to become more visible.

In spite of her opposition to coherent narrative, Stein herself was interested in self-definition, though only in the context of a perpetually open-ended process. Her intention was not to ignore identity and go no further, but to free entities from the limitations of socially constructed identities. In her framework, a "masterpiece" is accomplished when language, entities, and signs are freed from historically and culturally determined referents to exist self-referentially but not definitively or statically. She believed that her landscape writing and theatre offered an alternative to narrative, a new way of telling what is not known. She sought to tell the "thing" itself, which had formerly been hidden at the margins: "But in my portraits I had tried to tell what each one is without telling stories and now in my early plays I tried to tell what happened without telling stories so that the essence of what happened would be like the essence of the portraits, what made what happened be what it was" (LA, 121).

The conveyance of nonnarrative essence defined a masterpiece for Stein; she wrote that this is what the human mind sees, a vision that is textured yet free of conventional dimensional form. Stein's favorite visual metaphor for landscape theatre was the view from her first airplane ride, which was aptly en route to see a production of Four Saints in the United States. The view from the air is complete without linearity, without progression; everything can be seen in one look. In the view from the human mind, all time is collapsed into the present, resulting in a complete unitary entity: "When you look at anything and you do not see it all in one plane, you do not see it with the human mind but anybody can know that. It is naturally that. And so it is because there is no time and no identity in the human mind" (GHA, 175). In a recent article on Four Saints, Daniel Albright further described her idea of landscape theatre as a "notion of eternity as an unrolled filmstrip, a simultaneous presentation of an image in all its possible projections into time . . . everything that has been and will be is there and merely needs to display various angles of itself." 8 Since all possible elements of the landscape are already there, any drama is a result of their various formations and arrangements rather than their creation or discovery. History and time become irrelevant.

The Implications of the Performance Medium

The main obstacle to such a performance landscape, one that distinguishes it from a written landscape, is the inherently social and historical nature of the audience-performer relationship in which race and gender are visible. The audience members in 1934 carried with them theatrical and social experiences, including the viewing of other productions, that formed their expectations about what white and black people usually did and signified in performance. In at least a very basic way, these experiences structured Four Saints around narrative, despite Stein's intentions. They provided guidelines for the audience members as to how they might interpret the happenings on the stage, as well as when they should sit and stand, when they should be quiet, when they should applaud. These dynamics of theatrical reception introduced history and identity into the Four Saints production, and the disunity resulting from using the past for interpreting the present arguably survived regardless of Stein's artistic efforts to the contrary.

Four Saints juxtaposed her textual forté of nonreferentiality with the nature of performance, which, using Joseph Roach's definition, is primarily referential: "Offer[ing] a substitute for something else that preexists it . . . stand[ing] in for an elusive entity that it is not but that it must vainly aspire to both embody and replace"; it is always a reenactment, always refers to another performance. 9 This view of performance as an intrinsically citational act has long been argued for by performance studies scholars according to the paradigm of repetition with critical difference. In this model, meaning emerges from repetition and the instance of critical difference represents the potential for change. The referential nature of a performance enables the actions and images to signify to a viewer, while the idiosyncratic nature of nonmechanical repetition necessarily reinterprets the referent, nuancing the sign within the particular conditions of its expression. This paradigm is similar to Stein's approach to defamiliarization in writing, which also relies upon repetition and the related possibilities for rearrangements and change. If Stein's approach can be compared to that of Louis Zukofsky, however, the emphasis emerges differently: "We tend to write an expressive and musical verse rather than a magniloquent one. We seek the plasticity of words and their interrelations and musical connections rather than denotations." 10 This statement downplays the citational component of repetition that structures the performance model and instead focuses on the materiality rather than referentiality of language in hopes of moving beyond the preconceived and towards the horizon of the new. For instance, a portion of Stein's libretto for Four Saints reads: "If a magpie in the sky on the sky cannot cry if the pigeon on the grass alas can alas and to pass the pigeon on the grass alas." 11 The citational quality of the word "pigeon" is secondarily significant (after all, "pigeon" is not arbitrarily situated "on the grass," the association of the two referents having obviously been considered), but Stein is primarily concerned with the music resulting from the manipulation and repetition of sounds and words. In the medium of physical performance, such a concern with pliability and rearrangement finds itself in tension with the dynamics of the visual--the citational quality of raced and gendered bodies.

The visual performance text, intentionally or not, makes intertextual references to previous cultural performances. To adapt the "blank page" metaphor of Rachel Blau DuPlessis, the directors and actors of Four Saints did not face an empty stage upon which to perform. It was a stage crowded with previous representations of blackness, of opera, and of the avant-garde. When DuPlessis notes, regarding women's writing, "All our words are ringed by Words," the capital means all the uses of language that have been used to define the identity "woman." 12 In the same way, the actors in Four Saints stepped onto a stage on which their identities as black performers had already been historically constituted: "The body onstage, through its carriage, gestures and spatial relationships to other bodies, resonates with social history." 13 The visual presence of a racialized performing body evoked a cultural narrative that constructed blackness in opposition to whiteness. Aldon Nielsen agrees that certain a priori assumptions about modernist poetry were attached to the suggestion of race: "They all shared a common vocabulary of racial images. When modernist poets thought blackness, their thoughts were confined within the frozen metaphors of the language they thought in." 14 This quotation might be altered for a performance context as "when they saw blackness" to anticipate the largely white audience's reception of Four Saints and their situation of the opera in their current social context. In the case of Four Saints, Stein's libretto was more successful at helping words forget their usual contexts than helping bodies forget their races.

Her text, on paper, was necessarily incomplete as a performance text since it waited upon the interaction of the audience with performing bodies, a situation that injected social history into the performance of her otherwise nonreferential text. Stein herself wrestled with the implications of reception and the way it constructs identity. In Geographical History she decides at one point to wish away the problem of audience: "An audience cannot be layed away. Of course it can. It can but is it. Of course it can. Any audience can be layed away of course it can" (GHA, 149). At another time, she acknowledges the audience, but dismisses its defining power: "Being I I am I has really nothing to do with the little dog knowing me, he is my audience, but an audience never does prove to you that you are you" (GHA, 105). To apply this statement to the first production of Four Saints is to hypothesize the opera's existence apart from what was perceived by the audience from the various cultural locations that comprised it. Charles Bernstein seems to agree with this hypothesis in his discussion of Stein and identity: "Any cultural production can be viewed through the lens of its socio-historical circumstance. To ignore such contexts is to deny the social truths of the work. Yet such contexts are inadequate to establish a work's identity." 15 Albright went even further in this regard in his assessment of Four Saints: "[It] differs from its predecessors [Stein's earlier plays and writings] in that it contemplates a vacuum--an empty canvas, a region of the earth from which all finite objects have been removed" ("ONA," 3). This claim cannot possibly be true for the ephemeral medium of performance that, unlike other forms of art, exists only in the social context of its initial moment. Arguably, all other forms in which it is "preserved," such as play scripts, recordings, and written descriptions, cease to be "the work" Bernstein is referring to and are better characterized as residue or traces. 16 Hence, though the identity that consolidates around performance traces may evolve and change in time, the identity of the performance work itself is fixed, fleeting, and inevitably unrecoverable in its entirety. 17 In the case of the 1934 production of Four Saints, those conditions included the history of race in the United States, especially race in performance.

Minstrelsy and Black Performance Tradition

Though black operatic casts were effectively unheard of when Four Saints opened in 1934, the representation of blacks in theatre had a long history. The range of ways for blacks to be represented or to represent themselves in performance had always been extremely narrow, so that the appearance of black performers on stage would have evoked a limited number of expectations. The triggering of these expectations presented a problem for the adaptation of Stein's writing and her artistic goals of nonreferentiality to the stage in the case of Four Saints. At the time, most representations of black performers and characters were grounded in the American minstrel tradition, which certainly had been modified and expanded by new forms and performers as time passed. The very appearance of black performers on stage still referred to tradition despite the ahistorical nature of Stein's text.

Scholars debate the precise origins of American minstrelsy, though it is agreed that white entertainers such as Thomas Dartmouth Rice developed blackface burlesque acts for theaters and circuses as early as the 1840s. 18 After the Civil War, minstrel performers were more often African American, but the shows remained nonnarrative and nondramatic, emphasizing song, dance, and the spectacle of the overcostumed, overpainted construction of race. 19 In the twentieth century, as black performers became more common on the legitimate stage, their race often functioned as a stock stage device that carried certain comic and tragic inferences handed down from minstrelsy. The inferences especially important to this discussion involve the childlike nature, lack of intellectualism, and naïveté associated with the minstrel character. 20 These connotations were still well-understood at the time of the production of Four Saints, as evidenced especially by the success of Marc Connelly's The Green Pastures, which opened on Broadway in February 1930, won the Pulitzer Prize for that year, and was subsequently a cultural sensation on a national tour. 21 Like Four Saints, Green Pastures dealt with religious themes, albeit in a more conservative and traditional fashion. The play was presented as a collection of Bible stories retold from a naïve viewpoint and in the stereotypical vernacular of southern blacks. The production represented blackness with obvious links to minstrelsy and "made it on the faith of those who had no pretense of sophistication and who, therefore, could believe in an uncluttered and simple way. Doubt seemed impossible in a black child's fantasy" (HR, 251). A genealogy of Thomson's ideas about black people on the stage and his decision to cast Four Saints with black actors leads back to Green Pastures. Hall Johnson, the African American choir director for Green Pastures was the writer and director of a play in 1933 that also dealt with a variation of black culture centered on a religious theme called Run, Little Chillun'! Like Green Pastures, Run was a musical and was best known for its inspired choir numbers. Carl Van Vechten claimed that it was during the intermission for Run that Thomson decided to use black performers for Four Saints. 22 Considering his interest in theatre, it is reasonable to assume that Thomson also saw Green Pastures (which was much more celebrated than Run), though there is no concrete evidence to this effect. But even if he was not directly influenced by the production, he easily would have been able to reconcile its portrayal of simple people of faith with his own ideas about black people: "They are not afraid of religious subject matter; they do not overly intellectualize everything." 23 The review in the New York Times concurred with this view, leaving no doubt as to the function of the black actor as a cultural symbol in Green Pastures: "It follows the chronicle of biblical history as ignorant religious negroes of the South might conceive it in the childish terms of their personal experience. . . . At first you are delighted by the naive incongruities of the spectacle." 24 The use of the word "spectacle" foreshadowed later descriptions of Four Saints, though the two shows would on the surface appear to have little in common other than the color of their casts and their seemingly diverse treatments of religious faith.

Green Pastures' use of blackness as a stage device made it possible for white audiences to become "ignorant" themselves and vicariously experience a comforting, simple faith in an increasingly faithless time colored by the Depression, scientific advances, and persistent memories of the horrors of World War I. In spite of significant cosmetic and thematic differences, Green Pastures functioned much like a minstrel show as it "created a distance between white men's normative selves (what they had to be) and their natural selves (what they feared but were fascinated by)" (HR, 257). The play was successful because white Americans wanted to reclaim a simple faith, but class expectations and modern adulthood required that they remain intellectually grounded. Lott hypothesizes that this desire for regression could be found at the base of minstrel show humor with its "ability to reduce not only black people but white spectators themselves to children" (LT, 144). Green Pastures offered such an opportunity. Through this play, a white audience was able to project its desire to reclaim faith and childhood onto the epitome of ignorance and simplicity, the southern black, presumed to be untouched by class expectations and modern logic.

The spectacular, benign, fairy-tale quality of Green Pastures masked its promotion of white racial ideology regarding black people, which assumed that their cultures did not progress as those of whites did. 25 In this regard, black people epitomized Stein's conviction that "people really do not change from one generation to another, as far back as we know history people are about the same as they were." 26 Thomson may or may not have believed in the general stasis of humanity, but he did see black people as a link back to the time of St. Térèse: 27 "I often marveled at the miracle whereby slavery (and some cross-breeding) had turned them into Christians of an earlier stamp than ours . . . very much as Saint Thérèse had been" (VT, 239). The casting decisions for Four Saints take on new significance in this light, especially regarding Beatrice Wayne, who portrayed St. Teresa I and is even remembered today as the "eternal, unchanging" black woman: "She was positively saintly, a plain strong woman who radiated inner beauty" (VT, 249, emphasis added). The social inscriptions of African American bodies, combined with Thomson's pronouncements regarding blacks, such as, "They live on the surface of their consciousness," encouraged an interpretation of the cast's performances as "natural" and a perpetuation of the idea that black people, untouched by progress and history, were somehow intrinsically suitable for religious subject matter (VT, 253). The label of "natural" was commonly applied to black performers of the day, obscuring the roles that artistic skill, technical training, and hard work played in their successes.

Four Saints continued the tradition of black performance as nonnarrative spectacle. As a musical without a plot, it did not violate the white audience member's expectation shaped by minstrelsy, which did not encompass dramatic narrative. Granted, by 1934, plays such as Eugene O'Neill's The Emperor Jones , Willis Richardson's The Chipwoman's Fortune , and Wallace Thurman's Harlem had showcased black actors in mainstream theaters in dramatic productions, but the majority of roles for black actors were still in musicals. The extremely popular black musicals of the 1920s, led by Eubie Blake and Noble Sissle's Shuffle Along had no plot to speak of. By the time Four Saints opened, musicals such as Green Pastures and Run had introduced dramatic plots of varying degrees and experienced success on Broadway, but these shows still clung to traditional, stereotypical representations of blackness. While Green Pastures capitalized on the symbol of naïve southern blacks, Run emphasized black sexuality, voodoo, and primitivism. In any case, the majority of narrative dramas were still portrayed by white casts. This lack of black bodies within dramatic narrative structure carried significant meaning where racial identity was concerned. The cultural meaning attached to the narrative form was explained by the great dramatic actor Charles Gilpin, famous for starring in The Emperor Jones, in 1919:

Once, when Lillian Morrison and I were doing a turn together in vaudeville, I elaborated a routine and made it a genuine bit of a story. But the manager said, 'See here! You can't do that! That's a sketch!' Do you see? We were colored! Therefore we must not be permitted to act. . . . Apparently colored folks were not supposed to be regular human beings, with a knowledge of life. They were just human eccentricities, that did certain old tricks, wore certain kinds of queer clothes and were funny, the way monkeys in a zoo are funny. 28

Thomson betrayed a similar attitude with regard to the artistic initiatives of his black cast members, insisting, "I don't really want them to act. I want them to be moved"; he wanted Frederick Ashton to "make choreography . . . so that all movements will be regulated to the music, measure by measure and all our complicated stage-action made into a controllable spectacle." 29 Thomson's approach to the production, though motivated mainly by aesthetic intent, sought to deprive the black actors of agency in creating the art in which they were represented. Even such "purely aesthetic" goals of harmony and unity are worthy of a social critique when their achievement requires viewing black bodies as pawns on stage, to be moved to serve the purposes of the white directors. Considering the racial and social hierarchy in 1934, black actors were the logical choice for comprising a "controllable spectacle." This approach to performers is consistent with Stein's aversion to character in her modernist landscape vision of theater, in which persons should not stand out as actors or agents but should merely "be." As one critic has summed up the characters in the opera: "Saint Therese [sic] and Saint Ignatius are stage props: talking stage props. . . . Their appearance is justified by what they contribute to the set, the landscape." 30

Nonnarrativity is not the only possible comparison between Four Saints and the minstrel tradition. One of the traditional bits in the minstrel show was the oratory segment, which depended upon linguistic travesty for humorous effect. The black body of the orator, indicated not by the body of the performer but by exaggerated make-up and costuming, was coded as less intelligent than the white subject, yet aspiring to whiteness. With the audience understanding this meaning of the sign of the black body, a formal oratory performance by the minstrel actor was considered humorous. Typically, the orating performer, in the guise of a government official, preacher, or other public speaker, would use pretentious language incorrectly, failing to differentiate between homonyms and shades of meaning as, presumably, a white speaking body would not. 31 Further, the mere use of the formal oratory convention was in itself humorous travesty because the social constructions of race stipulated that a black speaking body would have no legitimate use for such conventions.

Aside from this general travesty of language, the speakers might be performing a more sophisticated satire on a different level, imitating well-known orators or politicians or spoofing current events of the day. 32 An interesting aspect of such a performance is that presumably a black performer actually would have to understand the correct usage of the language, as well as the normative versions of current events, in order to spoof them in the person of his blackfaced character. Thus, the oratory segment contained both an appeal and a threat: its appeal was that it erased adult responsibility and social codes of behavior through a mocking of those codes, allowing a comforting, childlike regression for the audience members; its threat was that it raised the possibility of real black orators who could stand in for or replace whites, introducing the possibility of whites' loss of social control. 33 The dynamic of Four Saints was such that it emphasized the oratory segment's appeal and erased its threat. Like the minstrel orators, the black performers in Four Saints used language in a pleasurable, nonsensical, childlike way. Since Stein's libretto provided no opportunity for character differentiation, however, the saints did not assume the positions of speaking individuals, even parodically. By creating "roles" in which performers need only function as pawns to be moved around the stage, Stein and Thomson minimized the threat of social replacement posed by black people speaking in public and retained a measure of control over the pleasure the travesty scenario produced. The combined forces of her refusal of character and his approach to staging reinforced a dominant social order in which the black performer appeared as the site of pleasure for whites but removed the threat of social replacement that traditionally accompanies mimicry.

Steinian Modernism and the Use of Blackness

The glistening cellophane set and elaborate costumes of Four Saints was Stein's landscape version of spectacle, embodying what she described as a child's feeling of the theatre: "A great deal of glitter in the light and a great deal of height in the air." Her libretto was a circus for the ear, a nonnarrative flood of pleasant-sounding nonsensical language, repetition, and linguistic trickery. The parallels between minstrelsy and Four Saints bring into relief the way the black cast may have worked in Stein and Thomson's favor in the production. As Weinstein has noted about Thomson's reasons for choosing an all-black cast, "There is something of a Barnum and Bailey flavor to a play about Spanish saints taking place on a stage with scenery composed entirely of cellophane, performed by an all-Negro cast choreographed to move like Balinese dancers." 34 The presumed neutrality of such a circus (or any form of spectacle) diverted attention from Four Saint's racist cultural genealogy with its ties to minstrelsy even as it implicitly referred to this cultural form. 35 Stein's success at escaping history through nonreferential language now comes into question since the nonnarrative circus that was Four Saints appears to have invoked already historical cultural tropes. Thomson, however, crudely denied that this was the case and presumed to be able to choose which "racial characteristics" were visible on stage:

My negro singers, after all, are a purely musical desideratum, because of their rhythm, their style and especially their diction. Any further use of their racial qualities must be incidental and not of a nature to distract attention from the subject matter. . . . Hence, the idea of painting their faces white. Nobody wants to put on just a nigger show. [VT, 232]

The reference to the possibility of painting cast members' faces white bore on aesthetic problems perceived by the show's designer, the artist Florine Stettheimer, who had "been concerned that the various shades of brown and black faces in the cast would disrupt her perfect color scheme" ("OFS," 110). In a solution that hearkened back to minstrel shows in which white and black performers alike blackened their faces with burnt cork to meet a white audience's expectations of blackness, Stettheimer used a single shade of brown makeup on the skin of the Four Saints cast members. She also insisted that their bare hands be made uniformly white through the use of gloves, having judged the bare black hand inelegant. 36 The need to present a seamless, undifferentiated version of blackness in culture proceeds from a social structure in which white power defines itself in opposition to a black subaltern.

Variety in blackness was threatening to the security of the place of whiteness because it suggested the possibility for unstable, shifting, less certain markers of identity and, hence, an unstable power structure. As Elin Diamond has stated, "The concept of 'differences within' complicates as well identity narratives (of nationhood or racial purity) and their bipolar structure of (true) self versus (alien) other. . . . If an identity is always different within itself it can no longer be an identity." 37 The repetition of artificially consistent black faces in Four Saints complemented Stein's literary trope of undivided landscape and her desire for artistic unity. Such a racial costume, however, was also in tension with her aversion to identity construction since it suggests a definite social identity in which all black people are the same. This tension raises the prospect of incompatibility when race is at issue between Stein's landscape aesthetic and her refusal to establish identity through character. In this situation, landscape itself becomes a recognizable social identity.

This status of black identity as a coherent "given" in performance furthered the fulfillment of Stein's wish that the audience should not need to take time to acquaint themselves with the performance terrain. She writes in Lectures in America, "I felt that if a play was exactly like a landscape then there would be no difficulty about the emotion of the person looking on at the play being behind or ahead of the play because the landscape does not have to make acquaintance" (LA, 122). In a landscape, all the elements are already present, transparent in that they do not have to "make acquaintance," that is, be discovered or found. A fragmented, complex, unfamiliar representation of blackness rather than the undifferentiated, character-less, monochromatic one created by Stein, Thomson, and Stettheimer in Four Saints would have required a process of familiarization and discovery by the audience, resulting in the emotional syncopation that so annoyed Stein in performance and narrative in general. That the audience did not have to sort out unexpected, nontraditional performances of black identity thus suited Stein's artistic goals perfectly. It is ironic that the presentation of subtly familiar (or at least unchallenging) constructions of race complements Stein's libretto, which, like so much of her work, emphasizes the defamiliarization of language.

The uniform presentation of race in the opera also complemented her landscape objectives because it was more conducive to abstraction than complexity and individuality: "The saints in Stein's opera are like people beheld at such a remote distance that they have receded into abstraction; they constitute a landscape" ("ONA," 7). Indeed, like many modernists, Stein held the view of black culture as an unchanging background. Her best known statement on blackness is the infamous passage from The Autobiography of Alice B. Toklas: "Gertrude Stein concluded that Negroes were not suffering from persecution, they were suffering from nothingness. She always contends that the African is not primitive; he has a very ancient but a very narrow culture and there it remains. Consequently nothing does or can happen." 38

Other modernist poets concurred that blackness is a state in which "nothing does or can happen." Nielsen points out that for such writers, blackness fills the need for a poetic backdrop, or landscape: "There are a number of . . . passages in which Pound reduces the Afro-American soldiers to abstract blobs of color"; "Blacks in Williams' verse seldom complete significant actions; their value is lodged in their merest presence"; and Stevens has a "pattern of employing nonwhites as local color . . . go[ing] so far as to incorporate the debris that black people might leave behind them." 39 Though Thomson, not Stein, chose the black cast, it fit the prevalent modernist assessment of black culture perfectly.

In addition to the perception of black identity as uniform and unchanging, the stereotypical assumption that blacks were childlike and somehow more "natural" than whites fit the high modernist project of Four Saints. Though the project did not benefit from the sexual stereotypes accorded to blacks, this too was an aspect of white-constructed black identity that surfaced during the production. Stein herself was worried about the possible distraction that cultural associations with black sexuality might present, and her friend William Aspenwall Bradley recounted her comments in a letter to Thomson:

And I suppose they have good reasons for using negro singers instead of white, there are certain obvious ones, but I do not care for the idea of showing the negro bodies, it is too much what the English in what they call "modernistic" novels call futuristic and does not accord with the words and music to my mind. 40

One interpretation of this comment links it to the coding of the black body in culture as more sensual and sexually suggestive than the white body. She may have assumed that the appearance of black dancers on stage in transparent flowing skirts would lend a sexually suggestive tone to her language that she did not wish. 41 This interpretation of her comment is bolstered by evidence of excessive sexualization of black bodies by white intellectuals including Van Vechten, Thomson, and Ashton--for whom Harlem was in vogue in the 1920s and 1930s--Thomson's biographer explains that for many white gay men black bodies were symbolic of a sexual world not open to them in white society: "The possibility of illicit pleasure was one of Harlem's attractions: it was where men could find men to have sex with . . . well-dressed, deferential Harlem gentlemen acted as discreet providers" (VT, 225). There is evidence that these sexual exchanges between black Harlem and white New York did not always transcend the unequal social structure of society-at-large. A disturbing photograph from the original Four Saints production shows Ashton kneeling in a dark, three-piece suit, his head inclined down paternalistically toward three of the black male dancers who encircle him on a lower plane, naked ( 1). 42 The photograph might be interpreted as of questionable significance (since its particular circumstances are not known), were it not for the explicitly stated aesthetic goals of the whites involved, who sought to exercise absolute artistic control over an undifferentiated black "landscape."

Though it is uncertain exactly what effects stereotypes of black sexuality had on the production, Four Saints unquestionably capitalized on the simple, primitive, and natural qualities associated with black people. Similar to the function minstrelsy served for the lower and middle classes, one effect of Four Saints was escapism and relief from anxiety for upper class New Yorkers and intellectuals using the trope of blackness. The composition of the audience on the evening of the opera's opening has been well documented, as in this description by society writer Lucius Beebe, "The intelligensia in tortoiseshell glasses and the an representatives of fashion with monocles descended in an avalanche of ermine and broadcloth on the old Forty-fourth Street Theatre. 43 A review from the Chicago Daily News supports the view that Four Saints reinforced cultural stereotypes of black simplicity: "No race is so gifted in simplicity as the Negro. The Negro is childlike. Miss Stein's verse is reminiscent of the processes of every child's mind in a contented, a soliloquizing, a creative but inert state." 44 Van Vechten similarly described the effect of the black cast in a letter to Stein, "The Negroes are divine, like El Grecos, more Spanish, more Saints, more opera singers in their dignity and simplicity and extraordinary plastic line that any white singers could ever be." 45 In this regard, Four Saints continued a performance tradition that led through minstrelsy and back to Green Pastures. and positivism of American culture." 46

The mere appearance of black bodies on stage was not itself responsible for the perception of the performers as uncommonly simple and natural. Stein's libretto certainly contributed to this effect with its childlike syntax, its structural bias toward natural speech, and its similarities to the minstrel show oratory segment. In an analysis of her "How to Write," Albright points out that she "elaborates, at great length, Ernest Fenollosa's (and Pound's) belief that a language--or a prose style--should be judged according to the relevance of its grammar to the grammar of the natural world" ("ONA," 4, emphasis added). Keeping in mind that Thomson chose a black cast because he presumed that they would not overintellectualize her libretto, the cultural stereotypes attached to blackness complemented her "natural," flowing grammar in which any noun will do where a noun is required. Her repetitive, nonnarrative prose invites a childlike, playful approach to language that would have been suggested and reinforced for a white audience by the presence of black performers. Eric Lott's description of the minstrelsy oratory segment discussed above echoes common descriptions of her use of language as pre-Oedipal:

Onstage these fantasies were partly represented by a vexing and unmeaning linguistic creativity, a proliferation of huge, ungainly and onomatopoeic words that were meant to ridicule the speaker but which also called attention to the grain of voices, the wagging of tongues, the fatness of painted lips. Through them could be relived the forgotten liberties of infancy. [LT, 122]

Stein and Thomson's strivings toward the high-modernist goals of childlike spectacle, artistic unity, and natural grammar certainly benefitted from the tradition of minstrelsy and stereotypes about black people. The recent claim that Four Saints "takes place in a region elevated above all judgment of similarity and dissimilarity" sounds astounding after an analysis of how the production engaged with, benefitted from, and emphasized social constructs of racial difference ("ONA," 10).

Travesty and Camp in Four Saints

Four Saints can certainly be read as referring to the comic device of travesty central to black performance in the United States. Travesty was "the single comic mode in the minstrel tradition," and its effectiveness depended upon the obvious disparity between the blackfaced performer and his costume, dress, or appropriation of social standing (HR, 263). In an effective illustration of the concept, Huggins explains that a little girl may dress up in makeup and high heels, but "no matter how she stretches and struts . . . it is impossible for the little girl to be her mother" (HR, 263). The humor of travesty required a similar racial understanding among audience members. The use of this brand of travesty in Four Saints can be reexamined in the light of queer camp, usually theorized as a liberating form of expression, but one that in this case depended upon a foundationally racist comic mode.

On paper Stein's libretto may be whimsical, even charming, but the laughter reportedly elicited from the audience at the performances could not be predicted from reading words from the printed page such as: "He did be categorically and did he did he did he did he did he did he in interruption interruption interruptedly leave" (FS, 53). 47 Where, then, did the show's humor come from? I have no doubt that the timing and skill of the performers provided one significant component, but another may have been that the audience took its cues from the same cultural framework of travesty that was necessary for a minstrel show to be funny. Faced with her nonreferential text floating seemingly aimlessly from pleasant imagery to word games to nonsense, audience members must have sought an existing metaphor with which to frame it. 48 I would suggest that the audience viewing Four Saints, a play unlike any previously witnessed on the mainstream American stage, searched for a frame of reference in which to understand the show and found such a frame in the familiar travesty mode.

Black bodies assuming the identities of serious opera singers provided a unique opportunity to use travesty as the primary interpretive frame. Opera was perceived as a "serious," European-based pursuit. Classically trained black singers were still relatively few and were known best for singing folk forms such as spirituals accompanied by orchestras. The only well-known cast member was Edward Matthews as St. Ignatius, who was enjoying "as much of a concert career as a black American in the 1930s could hope to have" (VT, 246). Other cues that travesty was the operative frame come from comparisons of the opera's libretto and the minstrel show's oratory segment, which also depended upon travesty for its humorous effect. Stein's words were apparent nonsense to listeners, yet they were sung with perfect seriousness, as if they made some sort of "sense." The sight of black opera singers singing her libretto could have been analogous, in the recesses of cultural memory, to the stump speech of a minstrel preacher or politician.

Other possibilities for travesty were suggested by the appearance of black performers on stage named Saint Teresa and Saint Ignatius. Similarly the charm of Green Pastures depended upon the fanciful nature (from a white cultural perspective) of the suggestion that personalities from the Bible--even God--could be represented as black. Likewise, a black Saint Térèse and a black Saint Ignatius would be slightly humorous and innocently charming to a white audience because of the general cultural agreement that saints are not black. This brand of humor has more insidious roots in the culture of slavery, in which slaves were similarly and satirically given names "above their stations" such as "Cicero," "Caesar," or "Moses" for the entertainment of their masters. 49

An analysis of Four Saints as a work of travesty becomes even more meaningful and complex when it is also considered as a work of queer camp, a form of expression that may intersect with travesty but maintains a distinctly specific status. Moe Meyer defines camp as an intrinsically queer expression that is more than "some kind of unspecified cognitive identification of an ironic moment." Instead, it is a type of parody "whereby the marginalized and disenfranchised advance their own interests by entering alternative signifying codes into discourse by attaching them to existing structures of signification." 50 In Four Saints, the existing structures of signification included the operatic form, Catholicism, Christian hymns, children's games, and cultural constructions of black identity. Thomson described the opera's dealings with religious subject matter in this way, "'High camp is the only thing you can do with a religious subject,' anything else gets 'sentimental and unbelievable,' whereas high camp taps into the innocence of true faith" (VT, 229). In the light of this statement, Thomson's opera can be viewed as a camp response to Green Pastures (wholeheartedly a part of the "existing structures of signification"), which used a black cast in a more sincere, sentimental treatment of Christianity. In Four Saints, Christianity provided a structure, including saints' names, musical references, and a general aura of piousness, but the content of the libretto and the tableaux had virtually nothing to do with these initial structuring references. Instead, the opera portrayed what might be called a religion of childhood, the site of "true faith." So a camp reading of Four Saints might uncover an alternative queer signifying code in which, behind the dominant culture's signifiers of opera and religion, lies nothing more than the silliness and irreverence of Stein's libretto, a playful pastiche of familiar hymns and patriotic songs, childhood games and, of course, black saints. 51

As for the black saints, a camp reading of Four Saints contains the possibility of an interpretation in which the production's use of the cultural tropes of blackness does not directly refer to existing cultural constructions in a literal way but instead refracts and critiques them through a lens of queer parody. One analysis of the mechanics of camp suggests that a work of camp exposes the high/popular art binary as a social construction (for instance, incorporating nursery rhymes into the operatic form) and by extension also begins to deconstruct structures of gender and sexual orientation. 52 Further, such a work might imply a questioning of racial binaries as well. In the case of Four Saints, the character-less, undifferentiated saints could be read as a parody of white constructions of blacks as unchanging and simple (were it not for Thomson's comment that black performers were perfect for the production because "they do not overintellectualize everything" [VT, 226]).

Aside from the slight possibility that the opera used parody to mock historically grounded racial stereotypes, the show's treatment of blacks may alternatively be redeemed by a possible open-ended model of identity latent in Stein's libretto and the production's camp aspects. Meyer suggests that the queer origins of camp provide for a performative model of identity that would seem to benefit black performers at the time of the opera and that echoes Stein's own stance toward the concept. 53 This interpretation of camp evokes both the opera's nonnarrative, nonlinear "action" discussed above and theories of the way Stein constructs an open-ended identity through language; as Bernstein has contended, "Stein celebrates her suspension of identity, this holding off naming to see what otherwise emerges" ("SI," 487). Constructing identities through performance rather than adhering to a preexisting definition, the combination of the camp aesthetic and Stein's approach suggests possibilities within the 1934 production of Four Saints for broadening the definition of black identity in America.

Working against both of these possibilities for viewing the production as socially subversive with regard to race are the necessary conditions for the opera's social visibility and historical intelligibility. The achievement of these conditions requires invoking the dominant symbolic order to some degree. Though Four Saints looked very new and different compared to prior offerings on the American stage, its success was due in a large part to the recognition of tropes gleaned from the dominant culture. Thus, references to the culturally accepted simplicity of blacks were necessary for Four Saints to function as delightfully irreverent religious parody and queer camp. The disturbing aspects of this arrangement are the "use" of black cultural identity to twist other dominant social structures as well as the possibility that the invocation of dominant ideology in camp practice may be "misread" and actually reinforce the dominant order ("RDC," 11). As an amusing example of the potential for the latter, Beatrice Wayne (St. Teresa I) perceived Thomson's own personal camp performance as a sincere attempt to model himself on "high society"; "With his slicked-back hair and superior airs, puffing on a cigarette through a long-stemmed amber holder he was 'the most ridiculous picture' she had ever seen" (VT, 248).

As an example of the use of black social identity, Thomson's strategy for his camp opera included placing black skin where it was not usually expected, using the color of his cast members to "sully" the white world of opera (VT, 227). The skin color of the opera singers was the physical counterpart to Thomson's musical allusions to nursery rhymes and church music in his score; neither was supposed to be present in opera. Four Saints succeeded in challenging the elite, serious nature of the operatic form, but to do so needed to invoke the dominant cultural (or, white) assumption that black people do not perform opera.

Stein's approach to language and identity in the opera's libretto can also be linked to camp. As Albright has pointed out, Stein tends to create a pattern of substitutions for "normal" words and sentence structure in certain places ("ONA," 4). It is in this rearrangement of familiar words and parts of speech that she saw her art: "There is complete familiarity but there is no proportion that has hitherto been known and it is this which makes the real scene exciting" (LA, 108, emphasis added). In this line from Four Saints: "Four saints have to have to have at a time," the prepositional phrase "at a time" occurs where one would normally expect an object (FS, 18). A pattern of substitutions like this one cites the dominant "normal" sentence structure we would expect, highlighting Stein's choice as "abnormal," exposing the constructedness of the "normal" and raising questions about what counts as a paragraph, sentence, or meaningful communication in general. In the same way, the black cast in Four Saints functions as a kind of citation of the "normal" white operatic cast that fails to appear. The black operatic cast appears "abnormal" by contrast, and in the most positive end result, raises questions about what can qualify as legitimate opera at all.

Roach's definition of performance suggests a further theorization about these two instances of substitution (linguistic and racial) in Four Saints, "I believe that the process of trying out various candidates in different situations--the doomed search for originals by continuously auditioning stand-ins--is the most important of the many meanings that users intend when they say the word performance" (CD, 3). Applying this definition, Stein's language and the cast of Four Saints expose the myth of the original and the correct, suggesting that words and performers are always only provisional stand-ins. Herein lies the potential for camp performance's power. While both refer to the dominant symbolic order through purposeful noncompliance with it, they expose the arbitrary nature of its dominance and its defining power. Black performers can successfully sing an opera. Poetic language does not depend upon adherence to the usual systems of grammar and expression. Further, an opera can contain musical references to common hymns, patriotic songs, and nursery rhymes and still be an opera (or, alternatively, "opera" itself comes into question).

Thomson found Stein's linguistic use of "stand-ins" especially freeing when he was composing Four Saints: "There was no temptation toward tonal illustration, say of birdie babbling by the brook or heavy heavy hangs my heart" (VT, 90). The few instances of racialized language in the libretto, however, do not refuse such an illustrative tendency when combined with a black cast. St. Teresa I and St Teresa II exchange these lines: "And make him prominent. / Could a negro be be with a beard to see and to be. / Never have to have seen a Negro with it there and with it so" (FS, 25). Even considering only the written text and not the performative dimension, Stein arguably fails to defamiliarize the word "negro." One reading of the text proposes a question about "prominent," "negro," "beard," "to see," and "to be." This question is answered in the negative in the case of the Negro: "Never to have seen a Negro with it there," inviting a reaffirmation rather than a challenge to the never "prominent," never "seen" status of black people in society. The addition of the performative dimension of black people singing these words makes successful defamiliarization even less likely. No matter how Stein plays with the language, such racially charged words are never reducible to mere sounds. They consistently invoke the white/black racial dichotomy. Likewise, the bodies of the Four Saints cast members were never reducible to mere "neutral" bodies, nor apparently, were they intended to be. Their obvious status as stand-ins was made possible by the camp critique of opera and religion that the original production of Four Saints effected.

Conclusion

Four Saints did not explicitly rely upon traditional white constructions of black life that theatergoers were used to seeing in hit plays such as Green Pastures. The actors did not sing in a dialect that reflected a white author's interpretation of black speech nor did the physical setting on the stage attempt to signify a black cultural space. Based upon these facts alone, the 1934 production of Four Saints would seem to present an opportunity for black performers to escape an imposed cultural narrative, to start fresh in Stein's nonrepresentational utopia, free from the preconceived identities that often bound black artists in the 1930s. 54 This opportunity unfortunately did not materialize.

As further testimony of Four Saints' failure to displace, challenge, or defamiliarize racial structures that privilege whiteness, the production has survived as no more than a footnote in histories of black theatre in the U.S. Considering the buzz in the white press and on the streets of New York that surrounded the opera's opening and Stein's visit to the United States, surprisingly little reaction to the opera appeared in the black press during the show's run. A short piece in the Pullman Porter-distributed Afro-American sheds a less than favorable light on Stein, quoting her from The Autobiography of Alice B. Toklas on the "nothingness" of Negroes and dwelling on the fact that she "flunked her classes" at Johns Hopkins medical school. 55 A piece in the Harlem-based Amsterdam News laments the misuse of the talented cast for the exposition of Stein's libretto, "All of this seemed a waste of beautiful voices capable of higher attainments on a nonsensical extravaganza." 56 As testament, perhaps, to the failure of Four Saints to be relevant to the lives of African Americans, the black papers gave much more space and attention in the same month of February to a play called They Shall Not Die.

This play was a black-authored version of the events of the Scottsboro trial, a case portrayed unanimously in the black press as a racially motivated miscarriage of justice. At the same time as the premiere of Four Saints, a production of They Shall Not Die was canceled by the National Theater in Washington D.C., ostensibly because children younger than sixteen were cast members. The Amsterdam News points out that Green Pastures played the same theater with many young cast members. While Green Pastures was a nonthreatening, fantastical representation of blackness, They Shall Not Die was a narrative highlighting a real-life cause for anger among black people. A reviewer for the Amsterdam News described the emotional content of the play:

And you and every other witness of the stirring drama on Forty-fifth street, will leave the theatre with the same feeling of choking bitterness and seul-searing [sic] horror that the writer carried from the small courtroom in Decatur in December of last year. Except that you know that this thing has actually happened, you will be moved to mutter: "Such things could not be. Human beings could not do this thing. This is the twentieth century. We are civilised." 57

The papers speculated that the theater did not want to provide an outlet for such a provocative and controversial show: "A play of this type disseminating propaganda against race prejudice which resulted in the conviction and death sentence of eight youths for a criminal assault they did not commit on two white mill girls, was not wanted on the boards of the National Theater." 58 Albright maintains that the "absolute goallessness" of the saints in Four Saints seems frightening, but apparently African Americans with social and political goals were even more frightening to the theatrical establishment in 1934 ("ONA," 9).

Judging from the space and comments allotted to the two shows in the black press, the right to claim a self-created narrative, a new social identity in the theater was far more important for black people than the Stein/Thomson production with its modernist aspirations of avoiding identity and its camp comments on religion, language, and opera. While black theatre had yet to create an identity of its own choosing, Stein and Four Saints were already dismissing the concept's limitations. This dismissal, rather than erasing tropes of black identity, had the unfortunate effect of perpetuating the stereotype of black people as changeless and outside history. Bernstein remarks on Stein's absence of identity politics, "Such a writing beyond identity is utopian. But then only someone who has felt trapped needs to imagine freedom" ("SI," 487). I would counter that one cannot advocate freedom unless the advocate's historical condition is first established as "trapped." Without a revision of the prevailing social narrative, as was the purpose of They Shall Not Die, the social conditions and rights of black people would remain lost in the smile of the minstrel clown. White society had long taken the liberty of constructing the identity of black people as naïve, primitive, unchanging in their essential "Africanness," without history, incapable of progression, and used that identity to justify their inferior status in society. "Holding off naming" is only possible if one possesses the power to name in the first place.

The Four Saints in Three Acts of 1934 was a revolutionary production. In many ways it was an important triumph for both theatrical high modernism and camp expression. Most analyses of the opera are textually based, however, lauding the poetry of Stein's nonreferential language and the deceptively simple beauty of Thomson's musical composition. 59 Considerations specific to the identity of the work in performance, such as the social history of race on stage, are rarely critically addressed, though they contributed to the acceptance and understanding of the opera in the mainstream theatre. Yet the production's modernist and camp successes were inextricable from this social history, and these successes ultimately reinforced an oppressive social identity rather than defamiliarizing it. Kenneth Burke's wry assessment of the opera during its original run speculated on its meaning in a hypothetical cultural setting free from race and class struggle: "Perhaps it is one kind of light entertainment which all the world will someday care for, when a new day has imposed the privileges and problems of leisure upon all and the wealthy patrons of this bounteous performance will have been generously admitted into the ranks of the spectacle-loving masses." 60
