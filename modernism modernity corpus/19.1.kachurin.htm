Working (for) the State: Vladimir Tatlin’s Career in Early Soviet Russia and the Origins of The Monument to the Third International

Pamela Kachurin

During the first decade of Soviet power, Vladimir Tatlin (1885–1953), the Russian avant-garde artist best known for his Monument to the Third International (fig. 1), was an active member of the cultural apparatus of the Bolshevik “dictatorship of the proletariat”—the newest and sole patron for the arts in post-Revolutionary Russia.1 Between 1918 and 1925, the seven year period in which Tatlin conceived and built at least three models of his Monument, the modernist artist was employed by no less than four different Soviet art institutions: the Department of Fine Arts (Otdel Izobrazitel’nikh Iskusstv, or IZO) of the People’s Commissariat for Enlightenment (Narodnyi Kommissariat Prosveshcheniia, or Narkompros) in Moscow; the Petrograd Free Art Studios (SVOMAS)/ Petrograd Higher State Artistic and Technical Studios; as well as the Petrograd Museum of Painterly Culture (Muzei khudozhestvennoi kul’tury, MKK) and its successor, the State Institute of Artistic Culture (Gosudarstvennyi Institut Khudozhestvennoi Kul’tury, or GINKhUK). At each one of these organizations, Tatlin found himself within a network of like-minded co-workers: modernist artists who helped each other find a job, supported each other’s projects, and protected each other from governmental persecution. Thanks to such networks,2 as well his official positions in the administrative hierarchy, Tatlin was able to receive a regular salary (an unheard-of luxury in a time of shortages, economic chaos and high inflation caused by three years of civil war)3, and to secure the resources (studio space, materials, and assistants)

The fact that Tatlin successfully operated within the Soviet system is in no way meant to besmirch the artist’s moral character or to denigrate his prodigious creative output as pure expediency. On the contrary, as I hope to demonstrate, Tatlin’s aesthetic and conceptual decisions which have been discussed at length by numerous scholars can be fruitfully analyzed through the lens of early Soviet social history.5 Furthermore, it builds upon extant scholarship that seeks to revise the dearly held vision of a Russian avant garde as “political virgins,” disengaged from the realities of Bolshevik ideology.6

This article seeks to inform, rather than supplant, previous art historical studies, most 21 of which do not take into account either the social reality of the early Soviet bureaucracy in which Tatlin was enmeshed, or the cliques and patronage networks of which he was a member. It does so by providing a cogent narrative of the first part of Tatlin’s career, one that sheds as much light on his political acumen as his artistic versatility. In particular, the article revisits the chronology that has shaped the standard narrative about the origin of Tatlin’s Monument to the Third International in order to demonstrate the spuriousness of the claim made by the artist about the early, enthusiastic endorsement that this project had supposedly received from the highest levels of Soviet authority, a notion that conforms to the still widely-held, but increasingly discredited argument that a flourishing of the avant-garde took place under the protective wing of the utopian, revolutionary early Soviet state. As I will argue, such a narrative obscures the artist’s own agency and political agenda in the genesis and realization of his grandiose project. For when we examine the chronology and the sequence of events, it becomes clear that Tatlin repeatedly used his personal connections and his position within the state art apparatus to seize upon every opportunity to get funding and state support for a project that would have been unthinkable before the Revolution and remained controversial after it. More broadly, this examination of Tatlin’s self fashioning as both artist and bureaucrat provides us with a relevant case study of how modernist artists survived and occasionally thrived in an atmosphere that was increasingly hostile to formal experimentation.7 What becomes clear is that Tatlin was committed to promoting his radically modernist projects as part of the construction of Socialism, a fact that challenges the notion that Tatlin was apolitical,8 or simply scrambling to survive. Rather than adopting a “top-down” methodology, which strips artists of agency and paints them as purely reactive in responding to state-issued directives, this article describes one artist’s repeated attempts to develop a successful survival strategy for himself and his modernist art, and to adapt to the radically new circumstances of life and work in the nascent Soviet Union.

The Flying Dutchman of Russian Modernism

Vladimir Tatlin was born in 1885 in Moscow to a family that traced its lineage to Dutch merchants who had settled in Russia during reign of Peter the Great. When Tatlin was still a child, his father (a railway engineer and minor poet) moved the family to Kharkiv, Ukraine. At age 13, Tatlin, apparently eager to live up to his ancestors’ original calling, enlisted as a cadet in the Russian merchant marine. Just four years later, however, Tatlin quit the navy and decided to become an artist. In 1902 he enrolled in the Moscow School of Painting, Sculpture, and Architecture, where he studied under Valentin Serov (1865–1911) and Konstantin Korovin (1861–1932), the two foremost Russian impressionists. He finished his studies at the Penza Art School in 1910, and between 1908 and 1917 actively exhibited in, and shuttled between the two Russian capitals. In the course of his travels between Moscow and Saint Petersburg, the former naval cadet became affiliated with some of the most important modernist art groupings in Russia, including “The Union of Youth” and “Jack of Diamonds.” However, even

22 though he had abandoned plans for a career in the merchant marine in favor of one in art, Tatlin’s experience as a precocious seaman continued to resonate in his work. This is obviously the case with his 1911 painting called Sailor, one of the few works explicitly dealing with the navy and, also, one of the first instances in which Tatlin deliberately associated his work with a popular cause for personal gain.9 Tatlin’s exploration of the naval theme can also be seen in the sets and costumes that he designed between 1915 and 1918 for The Flying Dutchman, an unstaged production about the captain of a mythical ghost ship doomed to sail the oceans forever. 10 The peripatetic lifestyle of the Flying Dutchman of modern Russian art persisted well into the Soviet period.

The First Safe Harbor:The Department of Fine Arts (IZO), Moscow

Because the new Bolshevik leadership considered the education and ideological training of the country’s population as the key to both the short- and long-term survival of Soviet authority, one of their first acts was the creation of the People’s Commissariat of Enlightenment (Narkompros). This state organ was formed in November 1917 and entrusted with all matters related to the cultural sphere: literature, theater, music, fine arts, publishing, primary and secondary schooling, and professional education. Thanks to the leadership of Anatoly Lunacharsky (1875–1933), a Marxist philosopher and literary critic who served as the first Commissar of Enlightenment,11 Narkompros enjoyed a special status within the Soviet bureaucracy and was funded accordingly. So was its Department of Fine Arts (IZO), which was created in January 1918 and charged with the administration of art education, art museums and exhibitions, public art projects, and publications related to the fine arts.

The founding of Narkompros and especially IZO created many new opportunities for working artists. Lunacharsky allowed Narkompros to hire professional artists, who were considered “necessary specialists for the republic” and, hence, were offered many special benefits, including triple rations during the Civil War.12 Those employed in State art institutions were also exempt from the so called “Extraordinary Tax” that was levied in 1918.13 Yet another incentive to work for the state was Lunacharsky’s 1918 decree which guaranteed “suitable working conditions for artists” including “a studio and a separate room in which to live,”14 despite the extreme housing shortages in the cities. The first artists to offer their services to the Bolsheviks were the so-called “Board of Seven,” which included, among others, Tatlin’s friend from pre-revolutionary St. Petersburg, the Russian art scholar and “Futurist” theorist Nikolai Punin (1888–1953).15 These men were soon followed by other artists, many of whom also identified themselves as “Futurists”—the umbrella term for all artists working in a modernist visual idiom. Within a year of the Bolshevik takeover, artists associated with Russian modernism and the “avant-garde” occupied key positions within the government arts administration, and the Commissariat’s Department of Fine Arts (IZO) had become the primary patron and supporter of their activities and artistic production.

Tatlin was in the vanguard of the general artistic migration towards the newly-created Soviet art bureaucracy and in so doing, realized his vision for a unified artists’ organization, expressed as early as 1914.16 From spring 1918 to summer 1919, he 23 worked simultaneously in three positions at, and received three separate salaries from Narkompros. First, he held down a teaching position at the Moscow Free Art Studios (SVOMAS), a new art school founded in 1918 from an amalgamation of the Moscow School of Painting, Sculpture, and Architecture with the Stroganov Art School, and administered by the IZO.17 Second, he worked as a staff member in an IZO Sub-Department devoted to “Art Construction,” which was responsible for two important and ongoing public art endeavors: the decorations for the mass festivals staged by the Bolsheviks and the so-called “Plan for Monumental Propaganda,” about which more will be said below. And finally, and perhaps most crucially, he was the President of the IZO Collegium, the administrative board responsible for dispensing funds, approving all activities and expenditures, and the operation for the entire department. In his relatively brief stint at IZO, Tatlin used this key administrative position to access funds necessary to put into motion two of his own projects: the formation of a Museum of Contemporary Art and the erection of the model for his Monument. He was able to do so because as of July 1918, the Moscow Collegium had come to employ many of his long-time friends and acquaintances from the Russian art world, including such major players of Russian modernism as Pavel Kuznetsov (1878–1968), a painter and graphic artist closely associated with modernism; Ilya Mashkov (1881–1944), a painter who belonged to the “Jack of Diamonds” group of which Tatlin was a member; Robert Falk (1886–1958), one of the founders of “Jack of Diamonds”; Sergei Konenkov (1874–1971), the “Soviet Rodin”; Vassily Kandinksy (1866–1944), the painter and art theorist; and Kazimir Malevich (1879–1935), of Suprematism and Tatlin’s long-time rival.18 Although theoretically the Collegium was responsible to Lunacharsky himself, in practice the Commissar of Enlightenment delegated much of the day-to-day work to David Shterenberg (1881–1948), another modernist painter who served as the director of IZO and who appears to have turned a blind eye to the fact that his colleagues were using their position in the Collegium order to secure government funds to realize their own plans.

An examination of the two projects that Tatlin proposed during his stint at Narkompros, and how he went about funding them, offers a good example of how the modernist clique within IZO operated. The first project, a plan for a Museum of Contemporary Art, was articulated by Vladimir Tatlin and Sophia Dymshits-Tolstaia (1886–1963), both of whom worked under Malevich in the Art Construction Department. Tatlin and Dymshits-Tolstaia proposed that the Soviet state finance the organization of a novel type of museum, one that would serve as a showcase for the “best works of living art,”19 which would be displayed not only in the capital, but also in newly-established provincial branches spread out across Soviet Russia.20 Tatlin further specified that acquisitions for this museum would be approved only by the Collegium of IZO—of which he was the President—and that artists themselves would choose which works they would sell to the State Purchasing Commission, which they themselves administered. 21 In other words, artists who just a year before were in financial straits, would now be in a position to sell their own works to a generous patron: the Soviet state. The

24 Collegium immediately approved Tatlin’s suggestion, justifying this obvious conflict of interest on the basis that this plan would “enable the proletariat to understand the significance of contemporary art.”22 The Collegium then went ahead and approved the purchase of paintings from living artists, that is, from each other. Needless to say, in such circumstances, nepotism was unavoidable. The first acquisition made by the Purchasing Commission, in September 1918, was of five paintings by Malevich, for 20,000 rubles, the equivalent of his salary for 10 months. Next Tatlin sold three paintings to the Purchasing Commission for 21,000 rubles. By October 17, 1918 a total of 61 works had been purchased for 215,000 rubles, all for the non-existent Museum of Contemporary Art.23 The machinations of the President of the IZO Collegium apparently did not raise the hackles of either Shterenberg or Lunacharsky, who went on to publish an innocuous article about the proposed plan for the Museum of Contemporary Art in the Futurists’ own printed organ Iskusstvo kommuny [Art of the Commune]. The contemporary Soviet press also paid little heed to the implementation of this controversial plan, although Pravda did object to the fact that the acquisitions were made “not from artists who deserved it” but only from Futurists whose “future is still very controversial.”24 Those individuals who were more attuned to the “agony of the intelligentsia,” however, were more perceptive and, sometimes, even more blunt. In a 1919 letter, the writer Count Alexei N. Tolstoy attacked “the Futurists here [in Moscow]—Mayakovsky, Tatlin and others,” for “creating a lot of fuss in art” in order to “glorify themselves and sell their products”:

They are now buying paintings and statues for the People’s Museums and the first place is given to the feverish smearing of the Futurists. Aside from that, the Bolsheviks have assigned this Tatlin a bulk sum of 500,000 rubles to be used at his own discretion”25

By the time this philippic against the Futurists was published, however, it was already obsolete. For, as early as December 1918, the plans for a Museum of Contemporary Art, as it was originally envisioned, were abandoned for another new museum: the Museum of Painterly and Plastic Culture. The Futurists belonging to the IZO Collegium, on alert that unchecked self-promotion would be noticed, found it prudent to retreat from their original idea of creating a showcase for their own work, in favor of a museum which corresponded more closely to Narkompros’ stated goals of displaying art from all trends and periods.26 However, the new plan made no dent in the operation of the Purchasing Commission, which continued to acquire works of modern art even after the Collegium withdrew its support for the proposal for a Museum of Contemporary Art. If anything, the IZO Collegium’s quick response to the threat of exposure of Tatlin’s scheme to profit from the largesse of the biggest patron in town attests to the successful operation of the Moscow “family circle.”

Tatlin’s second major project was his proposal for the grandiose architectural endeavor that would become known as the Monument to the Third International. Like the idea for a Museum of Contemporary Art, this project was also born in IZO’s Art Construction Department. One of the tasks of the Art Construction Department in which Tatlin was employed was implementation of Lenin’s April 1918 decree “On 25 Monumental Propaganda.” 27 This ambitious plan, which was conceived as one of many tactics to educate and engage the urban population of Soviet Russia, mandated the erection of statues of revolutionary heroes or historical figures that had furthered the cause of socialism.28 The list of such “heroes of the Revolution” included international Communist leaders like Karl Marx, as well as reform-minded members of the pre-revolutionary Russian intelligentsia, like Aleksandr Herzen (1812–1870), the so-called father or Russian socialism, and Nikolai Chernyshevsky (1828–1889), a Russian revolutionary democrat who authored the utopian novel What is to Be Done? Upon Lenin’s orders, the Council of the People’s Commissars (Soviet Narodnykh Komissarov, or Sovnarkom), the highest government authority under the Bolshevik system, ordered 50 statues to be executed in a very short time. Struggling artists eagerly complied in the hopes of securing some payment (and perhaps larger future commissions) from the Soviet state; yet the resulting crudely executed gypsum busts deteriorated within a few weeks.

Tatlin did not lose any time and immediately seized this opportunity to create something more grandiose and aesthetically more interesting than a figural bust of a historical figure. According to Tatlin, the initial plan for the Monument was formulated in late 1918;29 and in early 1919, Tatlin had already drawn up the initial ideas for a non-representational Monument to the October Revolution.30 As expected, the President’s project was supported by the IZO Collegium and Tatlin was given 21,000 rubles, a relatively large sum of money to carry out his ambitious undertaking.31 The earliest description attests to the fact that Tatlin envisioned the Monument to the October Revolution as standing 400 meters high—twice the height of St. Isaac’s Cathedral in Petrograd and 100 meters taller than the Eiffel Tower. A giant spiral constructed out of iron and glass and tilted at a precarious 23 ½ degree angle32, the Monument would be composed of three large, revolving rooms, stacked one on top of the other. The room on the bottom floor was to be in the shape of a cube. It was intended for legislative assemblies and would revolve once per year—a wry commentary on the plodding nature of legislative work. The room on the second floor was in the form of a pyramid. It was to house the Executive Committees of the Soviet Government and was to revolve once per month. The leaning tower of geometrical shapes would be topped off by a cylindrical room, which was meant to house the information office for the international proletariat, where newspapers would be printed, proclamations announced, and manifestos issued. Corresponding to the news cycle, this space would revolve once per day. Tatlin’s “Tower” would also have a “telegraph office, motorcycle ramps, hundreds of telephones, movie theaters, parking garages, a radio tower, and megaphones to broadcast the latest events from around the world, as well as a projector which would project slogans onto the clouds on overcast days.”33 In accordance with the letter (if not the spirit) of Lenin’s decree, Tatlin dubbed his structure a “monument.” But, as is clear from the above description, the Monument was in fact intended as a functional building, which was meant to house Soviet governing bodies.

There is still some debate about the exact date that Tatlin’s Monument was rededicated to the Third Communist International, as well as about his reasons for doing so. The standard version of events was presented by Punin, Tatlin’s chief publicist, in a pamphlet called The Monument to the Third International: A Project by the Artist

V. E. Tatlin, which was published by IZO in 1920. According to Punin:

“In 1919 The Fine Arts Department of Narkompros commissioned the artist V.E. Tatlin to design a project for a monument to the Third International. The artist started work immediately and designed the project.’34

A year later (and over the course of his career), Tatlin himself averred that the project was commissioned by Narkompros in mid-1919 specifically in honor of the Communist International and that he had received funds from IZO to work on this commission.35 But it is Punin’s pamphlet that is actually the first piece of documentation to record the new identity of the Monument. Although Punin dated the text July 1920, the pamphlet itself was not published and distributed until December 1920. Thus we can deduce that Tatlin’s Monument to the October Revolution became the Monument to the Third International no earlier than July 1920.

The contemporary press provides corroborating evidence for the argument that as late as June 1920, Tatlin’s Tower was still called the Monument to the Revolution. For example, an article entitled “Die Neue monumentalskulptur in Russland,” which appeared in Der Ararat in March 1920, (correctly) described Tatlin’s monument as fulfilling Lenin’s plan for Monumental Propaganda. A review entitled “To the Clouds,” which appeared on June 29, 1920 in the weekly Zhizn’ iskusstva ( Life of Art), described the model of the “monument to the Great October Revolution” that the “artist V.E. Tatlin [had] just finished” as making a “grandiose impression.”36 Even Punin’s own article from 9 March 1919 which is often (incorrectly) assumed to be describing “Tatlin’s first ideas [...] for the Monument to the Third International,”37 provides evidence that contradicts the claim that 1919 was the year in which Tatlin began, at Narkompros’ behest, to design the project for a Monument to the Third International. Punin’s article “On Monuments in the New Style” presents a sustained argument against “figural sculpture” (“ultimately interesting to no one”) that sets the stage for a discussion of Tatlin’s novel Monument to the Revolution. A careful reading reveals that Punin refers neither to the Communist International and its bureaucratic structures nor to the monument’s function as Comintern headquarters. In fact, he seems to have only the vaguest sense of the monument’s functionality. For example, he notes that: “the Agitation Hall and other premises, [are] to be used for different purposes according to requirements.” And while he does mention the movie theater, the cloud projector, and the radio tower, these pieces of equipment are in no way linked to the Comintern or its mission. Instead, he says that the slogans to be generated in the Press Room would merely be “related to the day’s events.”38

There is of course no reason why Punin’s March 9 article would describe Tatlin’s Tower as a monument to the Third Communist International: after all, when the article went to press, the Third Communist International was barely a whisper. Although it is 27 possible that Tatlin and Punin saw the notices in Pravda about the inaugural Congress of the Third Communist International, which opened in Moscow on 4 March 1919, it is unlikely that the artist would have revised the plans that he had drawn up in 1918 for the sake of this infant movement. Instead, as we will see in the next section, the re-dedication of Tatlin’s Monument from the October Revolution to the Third International took place took place only at the beginning of July 1920, during the celebrations connected to the spectacular and well publicized opening of the Second Congress of the Third Communist International.

Second Safe Harbor: Petrograd SVOMAS/Academy of Arts

As we have seen, Tatlin’s association the Department of Fine Arts, and the network of modernist artists therein enabled him to enjoy a productive year in Moscow. During this time, from spring 1918 to summer 1919, Tatlin held down a teaching position at the Moscow Free Art Studios (SVOMAS), obtained administrative experience, sold some of his pre-1917 paintings to the State, and secured the commission of a lifetime from his colleagues. For the next three years, September 1919 until autumn of 1922, Tatlin’s base of operations—his second safe harbor—was his studio in the Petrograd Free Art Studios (SVOMAS).39 During this period, Tatlin earned a salary as a professor at the Petrograd Free Art Studios, continued to sell paintings to his friends on the IZO Purchasing Commission, and enjoyed an elevated degree of public and critical acclaim due to the visibility of his grandiose, and newly-rededicated, Monument to the Third International.

A number of factors contributed to Tatlin’s decision to relocate to Petrograd in summer of 1919. Although we do not have any specific document to corroborate this interpretation, it appears that Tatlin was affected by the dramatic staff cuts across Narkompros. Indeed, for all we know, he may have been among the 75 of IZO’s 110 employees who were laid off in August 1919.40 What is clear, however, is that as early as the spring of 1919, Tatlin, and many other individuals in the Russian art world began to experience marginalization and persecution because of their association with Futurism. For example, Iskusstvo Kommuny (Art of the Commune), the aforementioned Futurist organ, was shut down by Lunacharsky after 19 issues;41 and the Futurists were banned by the Petrograd Soviet from taking an organizational role in plans for mass festivals as of May 1919.42 Most germane to my argument is the fact that all Futurists were banned from creating monuments for the Plan for Monumental Propaganda, including those artists, like Tatlin, who were employed in the department of Narkompros charged with overseeing its implementation.43 Without this funding, the leaning tower that Tatlin had designed in direct response to Lenin’s April Decree seemed to lose its original raison d’etre.

Tatlin, and Dymshits-Tolstaia were two of the members of the Moscow IZO network who decided to abandon the increasingly hostile atmosphere of the Soviet capital.44 Upon arrival in Petrograd, however, the Muscovites quickly began to put together yet

28 another clique. Its titular leader was none other than Nikolai Punin, who thanks to a new round of administrative restructuring was now a board member of the Petrograd Free Art Studios (SVOMAS). It was Punin who most likely arranged for Tatlin to occupy a large studio on the premises of the school.45 Tatlin named his space the “Studio of Materials, Volume, and Construction” and it was conceived as a combination school, research center, and workshop where government commissions would be carried out.46 Once he had a salaried position at the Petrograd SVOMAS, a place to work, and students who could assist him in the construction of the model,47 Tatlin was able to finalize the plans for, and build a model of the “Monument to the October Revolution,” which he had only succeeded in imagining while employed at IZO. Tatlin and his team of students/assistants began to build the model for the Monument in March 1920, and the project retained its identity as such until July 1920 when the Monument to October became the Monument to the Third International.48 What impelled Tatlin to change the name, and thereby the purpose of this structure, was the Second Congress of the Third International, which took place both in Petrograd and Moscow from July 19, 1920 until early August 1920.During its first year of existence (March 1919–March 1920), the Third Communist International’s (Comintern) activities were intimately related to the Bolsheviks’ belief in “permanent revolution,” that is, the idea that the Russian Revolution would spark a series of Communist revolutions throughout the rest of the world, which would then herald the advent of the utopian workers’ paradise prophesied by Marx; consequently, they were generally restricted to revolutionary propaganda abroad. In an attempt to garner mass support for worldwide Communist Revolution, the opening festivities for the Second Congress were meant to rival the October anniversary festivals in their celebratory mood and popular appeal. For the entire month of July and into the first week in August, when the congress closed, practically all other events and concerns in Russia, including the ongoing civil war, were eclipsed in the media by reportage of the Comintern Congress. The week before the Congress opened was named ‘Week of the Third International,” which was to be a “week of agitation and propaganda about communism.” Teachers in academies were exhorted to “acquaint all students with the history of the International Union of proletariat of all countries.”49 On July 27, parades and demonstrations, which were attended by as many as 400,000 people, took place on Red Square in Moscow and in Uritsky Square (formerly Palace Square) in Petrograd. For each day of the Congress, Pravda dedicated the majority of news space to speeches, protocols, and reports on meetings, and for the duration of the congress, the paper also included special inserts entitled “Herald of the Second Congress of the Communist International.” According to Richard Pipes, the mood of the Congress was “euphoric” and in a state of “revolutionary delirium” Lenin, who presided over the Second Congress, sent Stalin the following cable:

“The situation in the Comintern is superb. I think that the revolution should be immediately exacerbated in Italy. My own view is that to this end one should Sovietize Hungary and perhaps Czechoslovakia and Romania.”

Gregorii Zinoviev, another Bolshevik leader, even boasted to the Second Congress that 29 the Comintern has become “fashionable.” 50

Tatlin, who was now living in Petrograd, the cradle of the October Revolution, and desperately seeking another source of funding to build his Monument to October, could not have helped but be keenly aware of the cultural significance of this particular political movement, or of its “fashionable” Second Congress, taking place in Petrograd on July 19, 1920. Tatlin, who had failed to realize his grandiose plan under the aegis of Lenin’s Plan for Monumental Propaganda, could see that the attention lavished on the Communist International offered yet another chance to obtain state support for his unfinished Monument, even if the actual funding for this project did not come directly from the Comintern itself. It is interesting to note that the Organizational Committee of the Second Comintern Congress did in fact sponsor a competition for a Monument to the Third International. The announcement specified that the monument should “personify those proletarian leaders who have fallen for the sake of the liberation of the world proletariat in the name of communism.”51

Judging by the fact that the Petrograd Soviet commissioned Isaac Brodsky (1884– 1939) and Boris Kustodiev (1878–1927), artists whose work can be characterized as “heroic realism” (a precursor to Socialist realism) to drive around Petrograd during the Second Congress and make sketches of the festivities,52 had Tatlin even bothered to enter his Monument into the competition—and he did not—he would probably not have won.53 It was the political clout of the Third International then that would have been especially attractive to the modernist artist, who sought to capitalize on this opportunity to realize his creative agenda, to embark on a project that he undoubtedly understood as being socially transformative, and to secure both financial and ideological support from an increasingly hostile cultural bureaucracy. The timing of Tatlin’s strategic decision to re-dedicate his Monument to the Third International, at the apogee of the Comintern’s visibility and activity, thus suggests that this tactical move was more than just the artist’s attempt to associate his work with the political trend of the moment—a way to ride this wave of euphoria to more commissions and financial stability.54 Rather, it was a way to express his “commitment to the all-transforming international socialist revolution”55 through a radically utopian architectural scheme; and a way to weather out the storm and to find his way back into favor. The Monument to the Third International became his lighthouse.

Initially, at least, the change of tack seemed to do the trick. The fall and winter of 1920–1921 saw the height of Tatlin’s success and prestige, which, as we have seen, was at least partly related to the protection of his new network which provided him with a safe harbor from which he was able to produce the model of this most visionary of projects. Tatlin’s model for the Monument to the Third International was completed in November 1920 and for about a month’s time it was on view in the Petrograd SVOMAS. In December 1920 it was dismantled, transported to Moscow, reassembled, and exhibited in the hall in which the Eighth Congress of the Soviets was taking place—a sign of official favor, if not formal approval. But the following summer and fall saw a shift in the attitudes of the Narkompros administration towards the “Futurists,” as well as

30 a serious attempt to increase state control over the kind of work that was to be carried out in art schools and studios. Lunacharsky’s short-lived support for Tatlin’s Monument was indicative of the shifting ideological landscape within the Soviet art apparatus. Scholars concur that Lunacharsky was favorably impressed with Tatlin’s drawings and model for the Monument to the Third International, and that he facilitated its transfer and reassembly in Moscow for the Congress of Soviets.56 However, indicative of the shifting ideological landscape, Lunacharsky’s putative support of Tatlin’s Monument was short-lived, for in 1922, in a published article entitled “Art as Ideology,” Lunacharsky vented his frustration with the scarcity of proletarian artists. The focus of his anger was Tatlin’s Monument to the Third International. Lunacharsky compared the Monument to the Eiffel Tower which is “true beauty in comparison with Tatlin’s twisted structure” and claimed that it “would be distressing not only for me if Moscow or Petrograd was decorated with a product of one of the most visible artists of the left trend.”57 The Futurists’ former boss now anticipated the death of Futurism itself: “It already stinks [...] it is only three days in the grave but it already stinks.”58 Lunacharsky’s desperate attempt even before 1922 to distance his agency from the Futurists did not forestall the complete reorganization of Narkompros, which was initiated by the Sovnarkom in November 1920. In order to redress the predominance of modernist professors in art schools, and to increase the Soviet government’s ideological control over fine arts education, Lenin decreed that all art studios were to be transferred out of the domain of IZO Narkompros and into that of the Department of Art Education, a newly-created bureaucratic organization, located within the Main Administration of Professional Education. This bureaucratic re-shuffling meant that the Moscow Free Art Studios (SVOMAS), where many of Russia’s modernists had found a safe harbor after the revolution and where Tatlin had taught before his move to Petrograd, were transformed into the Higher Art and Technical Schools (Vysshie Khudozhestvenno-tekhnicheskie masterskie, VKhUTEMAS); and that the Petrograd Free Art Studios, where Tatlin currently taught and where he had built a model of his Monument, were renamed the Petrograd Higher State Artistic and Technical Studios and transferred to the domain of the Petrograd Department of Professional Education. The reorganization of Narkompros was envisioned as a corrective to the perceived disorganization, absence of accountability, and lack of Communist influence that had characterized the agency since its founding. In the newly-restructured Narkompros, artists and other “bourgeois” specialists could still work and teach in State institutions, but they now had to do so under the control of Communists, who alone could determine the content of the curricula.59 Implicit in the creation of these units was the principle that all schools should be ideological training grounds.60 In other words, the new policy was a harbinger of the kind of total subjugation of art to political and ideological concerns that we usually associate with Stalinist “socialist realism.”61 In this atmosphere of increased hostility towards modernist artists in Soviet Russia, Tatlin’s second safe harbor came under new scrutiny. The reorganization of the Petrograd SVOMAS into the Higher Technical Studios meant that Tatlin’s students were forced to leave his studio, having been told to register in one of the new departments

that had a common, government-approved curriculum.62 Tatlin himself was regularly 31 harassed throughout the summer and fall of 1921 and there is evidence that he was even threatened with eviction from his own studio. In August 1921, Tatlin sent a letter of complaint to the Petrograd Department of the aforementioned Administration of Professional Education. In it he recounted how a commission composed of representatives of the Main Administration for Political Education, and the Union of Art Workers had come, unannounced, to conduct an inspection of his work space in the newly-renamed Petrograd Higher State Artistic and Technical Studios. Although he must have known exactly why the commission had appeared in his studio, Tatlin complained about not being informed about the purpose of the investigation. He then proceeded to defend his right to keep the studio by claiming that he needed this specially-equipped working space in order to build his Monument to Third International, which, he hastened to point out, was “commissioned by Narkompros in 1919.” In order to legitimize his claim to continued government support and to demonstrate his own importance, Tatlin added that he had been sent to the Third Comintern Conference in July 1921 for a personal conversation on the question of the monument.63 While there is no evidence that this conversation actually happened, a series of letters in the archives does attest to the ill intentions behind the commission’s repeated visits, as well as to Tatlin’s vigorous but increasingly more desperate efforts to protect his position and his studio.64

The documents also demonstrate that he was not going it alone. In fact, Tatlin’s ability to stave off his eviction for at least another year was directly related to the timely intervention of a seemingly unlikely ally—the Union of Art Workers (RABIS), a pro-Communist trade union founded in 1919. Originally formed to defend the interests of artists, RABIS was also responsible for exerting “Communist influence on cultural development.65 Their platform was “freedom of art” under the influence of revolutionary activity and the policy of the Communist Party.66 Although its more radical members did profess a rather blatant anti-Futurist bias—its main publication once described Malevich’s Suprematist project as “subjective individualism in an exotic intelligentsia-group”67—RABIS did allow Nikolai Punin to sit on its Ideological Commission and Sophia Dymshits-Tolstaia to be deputy director of the Fine Arts Subsection.68 It was Punin and Dymshits-Tolstaia who successfully petitioned the administration of the Petrograd Higher State Artistic and Technical Studios to allow Tatlin to stay on in his studio until August 1922.69 Tatlin’s spent his remaining time in the Petrograd Higher State Artistic and Technical Studios, between autumn of 1921 and summer of 1922, trying to create yet another safe harbor for himself. He proposed a new department within Petrograd VKhUTEMAS devoted to research of “new tendencies,” and even nominated himself for the position of Rector of the Academy of Arts.70 Neither of these suggestions, however, ever came to fruition.

Third Safe Harbor: Petrograd Museum of Painterly Culture/GINKhUK.

Tatlin’s quest for a special research studio in which he could supervise research and carry out his own creative agenda was only realized at the Petrograd Museum of Artistic

32 Culture (Muzei khudozhestvennoi kul’tury, or MKK), the beleaguered artist’s next safe harbor, the third in as many years. Originally planned as a branch of Moscow’s Museum of Artistic Culture, the Petrograd MKK had cultivated an independent identity and, more importantly, an independent operating budget. Under the skillful directorship of Andrij Taran (painter, active 1920–1929), the Petrograd Museum of Painterly Culture evolved into a multi-faceted art institution, dedicated to exhibitions, public education, and research.71 In 1923, Kazimir Malevich, Taran’s successor as director, initiated a major re-organization of the Museum, which was expanded to include a separate research wing where works of art were taken out of the public domain and used as research material in the various “laboratories.” A year later, the Museum became an Institute and in February 1924, it received official “state” status and a new name: State Institute of Artistic Culture (Gosudarstvennyi institut khudozhestvennoi kul’tury, or GINKhuK). Tatlin’s move to the Museum of Artistic Culture seems to have been a foregone conclusion. After all, as he was already part of an established network there: he had been a member of the Museum Commission since 1921,72 had staged his exhibition “New Tendencies in Art” in its galleries,73 and had known many of its employees including Malevich, Nikolai Lapshin (1888–1942, graphic artist, painter), Nikolai Tyrsa (1887–1942 draughtsman, painter), and Punin for almost ten years. Finally, the apartment in which he lived with his wife and young son were in the same building as the Museum.74 So when Mikhail Matiushin (1861–1934), Tatlin’s colleague (who had also been evicted from the Petrograd Higher State Artistic and Technical Studios by the Petrograd Department of the Administration of Professional Education) moved his own studio over to the Museum of Artistic Culture, Tatlin followed suit.75 For the next two years, between late 1922 and late 1924, Tatlin was able to earn a salary and to pursue his artistic goals. Just as his studio in the Academy of Arts was the safe harbor in which he could complete the model of his visionary Monument, so too was his “laboratory” at the MKK, from which he was able to realize this most modernist of theatrical events: a staged performance of Zangezi, a poem by the recently-deceased Futurist poet Velimir Khlebnikov (1885–1922), whose manuscripts, printed works, and drawings were exhibited at the MKK. The text of Zangezi was composed of trans-rational (zaum) language, bird sounds, as well as “astral” language, and the poem culminated in a war of the letters of the alphabet. It was performed against a backdrop of Tatlin’s counter reliefs and architectonic drawings.76 Although Zangezi showcased both men’s inclinations towards formal and verbal experimentation, and reordering the universe according to alogical principles, in his published description of the production, Tatlin adopted a proletarianized rhetoric: he characterizes his own role as making “Khlebnikov’s creative work accessible to the masses;” and justifies his choice of students rather than professional actors so as to “better mobilize young people untouched by the theater, so as to reveal Khlebnikov’s work as a revolutionary event.”77 During this two-year period, as Tatlin increasingly retreated into his “laboratory,” he learned to perfect the language of Bolshevism,78 both verbally and in terms of his creative output. By adopting the Bolshevik’s rhetoric of necessity, which stressed production as the key to post-civil war economic revival, he not only adopted the language

that would please his immediate superiors, but also reinvented himself as an artist: no 33 longer was he the architect of the Monument to the Third International, the master-builder of a technotopia, but rather an “artist-constructor,” a master of rationalized production, and a necessary specialist for the USSR.

The practice of artists sequestering themselves in “laboratories,” rather than teaching in art schools, was a wider phenomenon in early Soviet Russia. As Loren Graham has shown, by 1923 pedagogy was a carefully controlled and observed realm, infused with the spirit of socialism; research institutes, on the other hands, were maintained separately in order to avoid even the potential for the ideologically-suspect intelligentsia’s “bad influence upon Soviet youth.”79 Research institutes were tolerated as long as they produced something useful to the Soviet state, as we can see from the following directive from the Main Scientific Administration (Glavnauka), the state agency that oversaw the activities of all research institutions, including the MKK and its various research units:

“art institutions must focus on 1) developing and strengthening scientific-research institutions, having as their goal research […]in the area of theory and history of art, and in the creative process; 2) scholarly activity in the areas of art which satisfy the demands of the wide working masses; and 3) development of Marxist method in researching problems in the sphere of art.”80

As the head of MKK’s Department of Material Culture,81 the research lab in which the “artist-constructor” would implement some of his ideas for everyday objects, Tatlin was not only very much aware of the urgency of appearing “necessary” to the Republic, but also put it into practice in his everyday life as an artist and administrator.

As an artist, Tatlin channeled his creative energies into designing clothing, a man’s overcoat, and even a prototype for a stove. Although sequestered in his laboratory, Tatlin had every intention of putting his clothing designs into production, and even established contacts with several manufacturing concerns in Petrograd. So the image of Tatlin as a kind of monk in his cell doing what was necessary to survive must be tempered with the fact that he was fulfilling the productivist credo according to which artists realized their visions in the laboratories and factories rather than art studios. Although some scholars have dismissed this type of work as a retreat from true art, that is, as pure expediency, a concession to the demand that artists prove themselves useful to society, Christina Kiaer has effectively argued that such objects do indeed have modernist credentials. Rather than being “understood as a moment of descent of loss for the avant-garde,” therefore, Tatlin’s suits and stoves must be seen as “a conscious invention of an active material object through which the modernist principle of ‘truth to materials’[…] takes on social agency.”82

The kind of agency and self-conscious invention that Kiaer sees in the material objects that Tatlin produced during this period of his career can also be seen in the artist’s self-fashioning manifested by Tatlin’s increasing fluency in the language of Bolshevism. A clear example of “speaking Bolshevik” is Tatlin’s description of the activities that the head of the Department of Material Culture planned for fiscal year 1923. In the space

34 of two type-written pages, this document refers to the scientific “research methods” of Tatlin’s “laboratory,” its various “types of utilitarian construction of objects of everyday life,” as well as its mission to “establish new methods and types of utilitarian materials for the construction of objects of everyday life.”83 Another example in which Tatlin’s ability to “speak Bolshevik” is contained in the following lecture invitation, which he sent to a senior official in Glavnauka:

The lecture has as a goal to explain all aspects of the task of production in our country and also to find a place for the artist-constructor in production in connection with factories and general organization of the new life. Communists exclusively are invited to the speech, as well as individuals in charge of unions, syndicates, and members of factory administration. The Department of Material Culture asks you to attend this lecture […] or to send a party comrade as a replacement.

Writing in the name of the entire collective, the head of the Department signs off as follows: “With Comradely Greetings (S tovarishcheskim privetom), the Group of Material Culture.”84

The third and perhaps most interesting example of Tatlin’s use of Bolshevik-speak occurred soon after Lenin’s death in January 1924 and involved yet another renaming of the Monument to the Third International. According to the art critic Iakov Tugenkhol’d (1872–1928), when asked about the kind of monument that should be built to Lenin, Tatlin answered: “We should include everything that characterizes our late leader as an innovator and revolutionary in all areas of knowledge and human thought in general. It must have an enormous capacity, 200–300 telephones, a huge auditorium, and information office to let the world know of events in the Soviet republic, a powerful radio and so on...”85 Anyone familiar with Punin’s description of the Monument to the Third International (quoted above) cannot fail to see the obvious similarity between that structure and the monument with which Tatlin proposed to honor the recently-deceased founder of the Soviet Union. Although nothing ever came of Tatlin’s third and final attempt to re-dedicate his piece of monumental propaganda, the artist’s willingness to do so may reflect the fact that his position in MMK was rapidly deteriorating and that, once again, he was hoping to use his leaning lighthouse to guide him out of a difficult situation. This time, however, the threat appeared to emanate from within the very same institution where he had sought refuge.

On October 24, 1924, the Main Scientific Administration conducted an inspection of GINKhUK, as the recently-renamed Museum was now called. Apparently this inspection took place at the request of GINKhuK’s own director, Kazimir Malevich, who may have used this opportunity to get rid of the increasingly-disaffected and erratic Tatlin. The inspector, a certain Comrade Karpov, censured Tatlin’s department because of its physical isolation from the other departments and their workers, although he did concede that the “results of his work are altogether interesting” and even praised Tatlin’s designs for clothing and practical objects as displaying a high level of originality. Tatlin’s department contributed to Karpov’s conclusion that the newly-created research institute demonstrated “an objective, intellectual approach to the creative process, taking it out of the sphere of the emotional unconscious experience and transferring it to the sphere of 35 intellectual analysis”.86 However, less than two months later, in December 1924, Tatlin was under scrutiny once again by representatives of Glavnauka. The reason for this inspection was Tatlin’s flat refusal to present his plan for the remainder of the current academic year to his immediate supervisor, a clear case of bureaucratic insubordination. During the December 1924 inquiry,87 Tatlin did not even allow the inspectors into his studio, prompting the commission to conclude that Tatlin’s work had neither artistic nor scientific significance. They described Tatlin as “poorly educated” and possessing “insufficient special knowledge,” and went on to conclude that “his latest work has not been supported by any critic from the artistic or technical side; he is psychologically abnormal and disrupts the normal life of the Institute.” 88

Karpov also submitted a separate report specifically about Tatlin, in which he confirmed that the Department of Material Culture headed by Tatlin was completely isolated from other units in the organization and that despite the Glavnauka commissioner’s own attempts at peaceful resolution of the conflict between the director of the institute and one of his department heads, Tatlin continued to express open hostility towards Malevich. Karpov concluded that Tatlin was not only a paranoiac (a term describing a medical condition) but also anti-social (a more serious, politically-charged term of abuse).89 It is obvious from this brief summary of the case that it was Tatlin’s bizarre behavior, rather than his work, which prompted the negative review of the department head and his unit. For the only thing that had changed between October 1924 and December 1924 was the souring of relations between Tatlin and the other members of the GINKhUK family circle, headed by Malevich. Very soon after Glavnauka’s inquiry, Malevich apparently decided that this circle could be maintained only if Tatlin were expelled.90 And so, in December 1924, at the very end of the calendar year, Tatlin was officially fired from GINKhUK.91 Tatlin was replaced by a more politically-correct candidate named Tikhon Chernyshev (1882–1942), an artist who belonged to the Association of Artists of Revolutionary Russia (Assosiatsiia khudozhnikov revoliutsionnoi Rossii, AKhRR) and was a member of the Communist Party.92

Despite the fact that he was fired in December 1924, Tatlin remained at GINKhUK until the end of the fiscal year in June of 1925. During the course of those six months, he delivered six lectures (April and June) and exhibited some of his work at the GINKhUK exposition (May and June).93 In this same period, he was also commissioned to create a small model of his Monument to the Third International for the Soviet Section of the Exposition Internationale des Arts Décoratifs et Industriels in Paris in 1925; a commission which can be attributed as much to the organizing Committee’s desire to showcase bold modernist developments in the Soviet Union, as to the fact that two of Tatlin’s long time colleagues—David Shterenberg and Iakov Tugenkhol’d—were on the organizing committee.94 The artist complied with the patron’s wishes. There was no mention of the Monument to Lenin. Tatlin’s most famous work, which went through three different hypostases, had finally found its name.

Upon Tatlin’s departure from GINKhUK in early summer of 1925 Tatlin moved to Kiev to take up the position of ”teacher of formal technological disciplines” in the

36 theater-film-photo department of the Kiev Arts Institute, where he found himself in another circle that provided a safe harbor for him for two years. In Kiev, he reconnected with E. Ya Sahaidachni (1886–1961) whom he had known in pre-revolutionary Petrograd, and Andrij Taran the former director of the Museum of Artistic Culture who had been replaced by Malevich. Not surprisingly, Tatlin stayed in Kiev only two years, and in 1927 he moved to Moscow to take up a teaching position at the Moscow Higher Technical Institute, a safe harbor where he was able to elaborate his plans for another project, a flying bicycle called Letatlin (literally, “Flying-Tatlin”), his visionary flying apparatus,95 which may be seen as the artist’s tongue-in-cheek reference not only to his genealogy and early vocation, but also to his own nomadic existence. By focusing on Tatlin’s maneuverings in the early Soviet era, there is a risk that his Monument and other modernist projects are reduced to “functions” or careerist strategies.96 Rather, as I hope this interpretation makes clear, in Tatlin’s Monument coalesce multivalent aesthetic, symbolic, political and economic imperatives. Tatlin’s Monument to the Third International, while a masterful concept, drawing on a multitude of architectural, aesthetic and scientific traditions, was intended to be closely associated with Lenin’s plan for global armed conflict in the name of Communism. These aspects must be acknowledged, if not completely reconciled. To paraphrase Boris Groys’ formulation, the Monument’s greatness is inseparable from its complicity. 97 The Monument and Tatlin’s career then should be seen as emblematic of the Soviet moment in world history, precisely because of the conflation of idealism, violence, and impracticality; a moment in which modern artists were preoccupied with “our work in creating a new world.” 98 This discussion of Tatlin’s career in the early Soviet period offers an example that directly challenges the familiar image of Russian avant-garde artists as innocents who embraced a utopian version of Socialism only to have their hopes dashed by a repressive bureaucratic apparatus. Tatlin, the Flying Dutchman of Russian modernism, was not a passive victim merely trying to stay afloat in the turbulent ideological waters of early Soviet Russia. On the contrary, the naval cadet-turned-modernist artist was a stakeholder in the future of Soviet art: he successfully sought out places to work, and actively constructed a network of like-minded individuals to support his attempts to pursue his ambitious artistic agenda. From this perspective, Tatlin’s most famous work is as much a monument to this artist’s political acumen as to his commitment to participating in the Socialist project. The Monument’s changing names and functions reflect a restlessness bred not only by the ever-fluctuating landscape of the early Soviet bureaucracy, but also by the artist’s uncanny ability to change tack, the better to orient himself to the imperatives of his patrons within and without the Soviet cultural apparatus and, failing to do so, to locate a safe harbor in which to weather out the storm.
