Audience of Self / Audience of Reader

Lois More Overbeck

The notion of archives is ancient. As a designated place where records of value to a community or state are deposited, both for the purposes of preservation and legal surety, the intent of an archive is to secure property and continuity by making historical evidence available for public access in the future. The very idea of an archive carries complex assumptions about history, community, and value. The archives of Samuel Beckett are relatively young. It is less than sixty years since the “great extractor,” Jake Schwartz began trading in Beckett manuscripts.1 Only forty years ago, James Knowlson began to assemble the Samuel Beckett Archives, now formally established as the Beckett International Foundation at the University of Reading.Although rich in offerings, the collections of Beckett manuscripts at the Humanities Research Center at the University of Texas in Austin, Indiana University, Washington University in St. Louis, the University of Delaware, Ohio State University in Columbus, Syracuse University, Boston College, Harvard, The Library of Congress, Trinity College, Dublin, as well as IMEC in Caen, the Deutsches Literaturarchiv in Marbach, and the Akademie der Künste Archiv in Berlin are relatively new. Indeed these collections continue to grow as materials in private hands make their way, through gift or sale, to these archives and other depositories.

For Beckett scholars, the breadth of materials is the result of efforts to collect, to preserve, and to describe by, among others, James Knowlson, John Pilling, Mary Bryden, Breon Mitchell, Carlton Lake, Jane Maxwell, Matthijs Engelberts, and Everett Frost. It has been more than two decades since Beckett’s death in 1989. While we can say that the archives are nearly finite, they are not finished. Not only are collections scattered, but items known to have existed have been destroyed, for example Leslie Daiken’s class notes, acquired by Henry Wenning and sent to Beckett; and on the other hand, items said to be destroyed do still exist in part or by report, for example Lucia Joyce’s letters.4 Archives hold unpublished manuscripts and fragments thereof; there are series of drafts, some of them neatly arranged by Beckett himself, that invite comparative study. And there are letters, spanning the years of friendship, long correspondences reflecting the ebb and flow of relationships, just as even brief exchanges of letters provide a record of interaction, or interest, or reflection. Do these remains show Beckett wrestling with his texts? First to get it down, whether all in a sitting or, literally, “half a page on good days.”6Do the shards record the weighing of words, the shaping of sentences, the discovery of meaning or purpose in what has been drafted, or, as is often the case, the failure? Do these material remnants capture the process and experiment that happens only in the writing? And what of Beckett’s intrinsic need to see a play on stage in order to know if it works? Or the details of his adjustments, when it does not? An example can be found in the evolution, performance, and recording of the television play Quadrat. In the immense Süddeutscher Rundfunk sound stage in Stuttgart, Beckett worked for days with technicians, musicians, and dancers—trying to find, and then to integrate, the drum beat, the costumes, the colors, the lights, the movements. Then Beckett viewed the outtakes overnight. When he came to the discussion with the producer and technicians the following morning, all waited anxiously for his verdict. He was very quiet. Then he said: “I think I have an idea—Quadrat II—the same action 1000 years later, monotone.”6

The Beckett archives are exceptional because they include traces of the living record 723 of performances (photos, reviews, recordings, interviews, and anecdotes by the actors and directors). Yet even well-documented performances are singular, different every night. We know that the productions directed by Beckett varied markedly, so much that the 1975 production of Warten auf Godot in Berlin provoked one reviewer to suggest that “the older Beckett-as-director does not understand the younger—Beckettas-playwright.”7 Still, how fortunate we are that Beckett did direct or have a hand in several productions of the same work; not for the record of how a perfect performance should look, but for the experience that all performance finds its own measure.8

There is, in our notion of gathering it all, the expectation that all of this will “mount up.” And even as we echo the lines of Fin de partie, we know that Beckett has been there before us. He knew from his own experience how little the note-snatching can accomplish. As scholars of a certain generation, we all have our feet wet in modernism, and we know, as the novels Studs Lonigan and Ulysses amply demonstrate, that we will never capture the whole, of even one day. Applied to the Beckett archive, perhaps the best we can manage are rare glimpses of what it meant to Samuel Beckett to write.

Such glimpses come when we read letters to younger writers. In a letter to Aidan Higgins of 22 April, 1958, Beckett responds very specifically to the story “Killachter Meadow.” He closes with a bit of humbling advice for a writer whose ambition has not yet been met by accomplishment:

Work, work, writing for nothing and yourself, don’t make the silly mistake we all make of publishing too soon. What I have said is much too abstract and personal and black and white, that’s what comes of trying to get down to the root. It’s a terrible effort for me to write such stuff and I hope you disagree with it.9

To the young Israeli writer Matti Megged, Beckett writes on 21 November, 1960:

The material of experience is not the material of expression and I think the distress you feel, as a writer, comes from a tendency on your part to assimilate the two. . . I understand, I think no one better, the flight from experience to expression and I understand the necessary failure of both. But it is the flight from one order or disorder to an order or disorder of a different nature and the two failures are essentially dissimilar in kind. Thus life in failure can hardly be anything but dismal at the best, whereas there is nothing more exciting for the writer, or richer in unexploited expressive possibilities, than the failure to express. This is perhaps quite beside the point in your case, but I don’t think so. Forgive me if it seems to you irrelevant or impertinent.10

And to the Swiss-born French writer Robert Pinget, who found in Beckett an admiring reader and a supportive friend, Beckett writes on 25 May, 1966:

We are not Men of Letters. If we take such dire pains, it is not for the result but because that is the only way to keep going on this wretched planet. . . stop re-reading your writing and get back to work. We shall never any of us know what we are worth, and it is the last question we should be asking. I at any rate love what you have done and very much hope that you will carry on.11

724 Here are three letters from Samuel Beckett to three young writers, who are, by circumstance and style, very different from each other, and who also differ markedly from their mentor. The sincerity of Beckett’s wish to be of help, which in each case was solicited, perhaps resounds as clearly as the advice he proffers. What do we make of these testaments? As secondary readers, removed from the central difficulty that each of these letters addresses, we are overhearing a private conversation, one that come from a deeply felt personal place for the writer of the letter, and this in response to a need voiced by the other. We read such letters with a sense of privilege. What do we take away? Respect for all the writers involved. The difficulty of writing despite uncertainties. That the desire for confirmation is not the same thing as recognition. Our words, even the need to restate, can only and inevitably be reductive. The center that holds us is greater than this. When we talk about “Out of the Archive,” we should be aware that we are outside, ever and always. Even when the keepers let us in, they may caution that we can only consult an item once (to protect it from damage by handling) or protest that scans or photocopies will undo the preservation that is their primary responsibility. Beckett scholars are most fortunate to have an often fairly complete series of manuscripts, and these, occasionally, in one place, thanks to digitalization and cooperative arrangements between archives. Yet, even when we are seated before a manuscript or a letter written by Beckett, we are outside of archives—because we are readers. Letters are written to a specific person at a specific time. We are excluded from the shared context of a past and lived history, of the relationship between writer and the recipient. A footnote may attempt to bridge the gap between modern readers and an allusion to the past, but it cannot approximate the unspoken shared moment. And no doubt, documentation runs a poor second to “knowing”—the easy friendly banter where just a name or a place establishes intimacy. We are mere observers. Even as we join the ranks of Beckett scholars (Ackerley, Gontarski, Feldman, Nixon) who worked meticulously to detail the facts, the gap of time, place, and living keeps us removed and aware that the simulacrum is not the lived experience. That said, knowing our place as readers to be limited, we persist. From the late 1950s, Ruby Cohn read each Beckett work as it was published; she often wrote to Beckett with her questions (and received answers), met with Beckett in Paris, watched him direct in Berlin. After many years of reading and writing, Ruby Cohn chose to write still another book—this to “rationalize” the Beckett canon, to trace the beginning and the ending of its creative gestures into a portrait of the full oeuvre. The critical need is almost a taking stock, an assessment of accumulation, a gesture akin to putting one’s house in order. How well we understand the nagging need to see it all—as if it could ever be whole and entire. Yet in our assumptions about archives, we all carry such a modernist longing into every visit with our pencils and computers. Despite our sophisticated appreciation of archive as a bricolage, or what Beckett may have called a “zig-zag” affair, isn’t this desire for the whole and entire always what tempts us, and what we attempt to find?12 We may study a narrow bit of text, or we may search comparatively in multiple drafts

to find evidence to support a critical interpretation. Motivating our efforts is the as-725 sumption that some thing can be found, well-found, or even discovered. When we are a bit honest with ourselves, we slip back a notch or two and hope for illumination of the text. And of course, it is our own illumination of which we speak. As Jack MacGowran said of acting in Endgame: “Every night one plays this play, more and more emerges from it. From the time I became deeply acquainted with his work, it has given me an absolutely new sense of values.”13

With the co-editors of The Letters of Samuel Beckett, Martha Fehsenfeld, George Craig, and Dan Gunn, I share this experience. As we have consulted, transcribed, and prepared Beckett’s letters for publication, every day brings new light on our subject: something we had not known or didn’t weigh with the importance that a new angle of vision insists upon. After more than twenty-five years of work toward the publication of a selected edition of Beckett’s letters in four volumes, we are daily chastened by its scale, its proportions, the need to penetrate the handwriting, and the need to ask questions in as many ways as necessary to find a pathway to what is past (or no longer present). With all this positivistic effort, comes the humbling counterpoint: the need to change our minds, to bend to re-examination when new information challenges what we thought we knew, to re-order sequence, to reassess context. Discovery and insight are counterbalanced by the responsibility to get it as close to right as we can, while knowing that even this is ephemeral.

The need to narrate, to connect the moments, to find sequence in the episodes, to seek out the development of ideas, also tends to convince us of the very possibility, and thus it becomes “a snare and a delusion” (L, 1, 11). In Proust, Beckett wrote about “involuntary memory,” the explosive “immediate and total” restoration of a past object and the “Lazarus that it charmed.” Involuntary memory consumes “habit and all its works” and reveals “what the mock reality of experience never can and never will reveal—the real.” On the other hand, as Beckett writes, “voluntary memory” is “not memory, but the application of a concordance to the Old Testament of the individual.”

This is the uniform memory of intelligence; and it can be relied on to reproduce for our gratified inspection those impressions of the past that were consciously and intelligently formed. . . It represents the past in monochrome. The images it chooses are as arbitrary as those chosen by imagination, and are equally remote from reality.14

When Beckett talked with Joyce about David Hume, Joyce asked: “How could the idealist Hume write a history of England?” Beckett replied, “A history of representations.” Joyce had no response.15 Entwined as it is with memory and idealization, the impossibility of history is evident throughout Beckett’s oeuvre. If Lawrence Harvey observed the overtones of nostalgia in the idea of happiness in Beckett’s work (most notably in Happy Days), it is possible to see nostalgia as an integer of memory—in Krapp’s Last Tape, Endgame, Rockaby, Company, That Time: “Was that another time all that another time was there ever any other time but that time.”16

As literary scholars, we draw on voluntary memory as we consider artifacts of the past (documents, memoirs, oral histories, retold stories). At best, our remembering

726 from these materials is selective, verifying our collective and individual imaginations, reassembling the pieces, if not into a whole, then at least into a semblance of what might have been. No matter how many drafts we see, or how well we authenticate information or explore nuance, retracing patterns from the artifacts may merely impose our preconceptions. Inevitably we are regressing backwards, from the starting point of the finished work itself, not unlike the action of Harold Pinter’s play Betrayal.17 A series of snapshots may be linked by landscape, but they are ordered outside of the time of the event—even as an aide-memoire. As in Heraclitus and Joyce’s Anna Livia, the river that is time keeps pushing us out of the way of our certainties. If there is one constant throughout Beckett’s letters, it is that with experience and circumstance, he may change his mind. We know how central music was to Beckett. It afforded him pleasure when his brother Frank rented a piano for his rooms at Trinity College in 1931, and when, in 1967, he acquired a small Schimmel piano for Ussy, on which he could “wander and blunder among Haydn.”18 The pleasure of playing came from years of attention, the sort of repetition and listening that comes with practice and intensifies appreciation. Such innate habit is reflected in Beckett’s response to a painting by Emil Nolde: “Feel at once on terms with the picture, & that I want to spend a long time before it, play it over & over much like the record of a quartet.”19 As the letters show, Beckett was often moved by music. After hearing a Beethoven concert by the Berlin Symphonic Orchestra under the direction of Eugen Jochum, he writes to Thomas McGreevy on 18 January, 1937: “The horn passage in the Leonore was as I have always imagined and never heard it, inaccessibly distant, sylvan and autumn dusk” (L, 1, 430). But Beckett could also be quite opinionated about music making, as in the 1934 letter to his cousin Morris Sinclair describing the scene of Fürtwangler conducting Bach’s Suite for Orchestra and Schumann’s Fourth Symphony: “He has the charming modesty of letting himself be led by his brass-players, who blow as only beer-drinkers can, while making with his little left hand very daring gestures towards his first violins, who fortunately paid not the least attention to them” (L, 1, 182). Fürtwangler is also mentioned as a composer in a letter to McGreevy from Munich on 25 March, 1937. Beckett vividly evokes a concert of violin sonatas with Furtwängler at the piano:

It was fearful. They played the earliest Mozart & Beethoven they could find. . . then the brand new [GROSSE SONATE] that Furtwängler has been moulting over all winter. It was unspeakable & lasted over an hour. The maximum determination (to get it all off his chest in a modern manner) & the minimum ability, a frenzy of impotence, with reverberations from everyone from Berlioz to Bartok. My musical susceptibility seems all concentrated in my arse, which ached diabolically. (L, 1, 270).

We also know that Beckett’s musical sensibility was deeper than affective response or opinion. Music could achieve that to which words and writing could only aspire. For example, a portion of Beckett’s letter of 9 July 1937 to his German friend, Axel Kaun (later a dance critic and literary translator):

Is literature alone to be left behind on that old, foul road long ago abandoned by music 727 and painting? Is there something paralysingly sacred contained within the unnature of the word that does not belong to the elements of the other arts? Is there any reason why that terrifyingly arbitrary materiality of the word surface should not be dissolved, as for example the sound surface of Beethoven’s Seventh Symphony is devoured by huge black pauses, so that for pages on end we cannot perceive it as other than a dizzying path of sounds connecting unfathomable chasms of silence? (L, 1, 518–519)20

From music, and from his trained and listening ear, came Beckett’s insistence on silence as a counterpoint to words and action in his plays. From this sensibility, a sequence of notes, varied in pitch and duration, are shaped into phrases; as in music, in Beckett’s prose, these may repeat and develop within and between voices, their timing articulated with pauses.

If music suggested possibilities to his writing, in the early days at least, Beckett insisted that the expressive potential of words, music, and movement were distinct. He told Morris Sinclair in the summer of 1934:

Do not believe that ballet is music. It is precisely because music has a subordinate part in it that ballet annoys me. For serious music cannot be of use. To represent a piece of music in a particular way, by means of dancing, gestures, settings, costumes, etc., is to degrade it by reducing its value to mere anecdote.21

Similarly he wrote early in 1952 to the art critic Georges Duthuit: “I do not believe in collaboration between the arts, I want theatre reduced to its own means, speech and acting, without painting, without music, without embellishments. That is Protestantism if you like, we are what we are.”22

This is the attitude that prevails when an aspiring young French composer, Edouard Coester, approached Beckett in 1954 for permission to set music to En attendant Godot. Beckett replied:

I hardly know what to say to you about your project. I have already publicly expressed my opposition to any stage music (Werner Egk had thought of writing some). For me that would be an awful mistake. A very different case would be music inspired by the play and I would be greatly flattered by any venture in that direction. But in saying that I have in mind instrumental music, no voices. To be quite frank, I do not believe that the text of Godot could bear the extensions that any musical setting would inevitably give it. The piece as a dramatic whole, yes, but not the verbal detail. For what is at issue is a speaking whose function is not so much that of having a meaning as of putting up a struggle, poor I hope, against silence, and leading back to it. I find it hard to see it as an integral part of a sound-world.

But this drama which you seem to have felt so keenly, if you thought fit to translate it, however freely, into pure music, that would interest me a great deal and give me great pleasure. And then what about silence itself, is it not still waiting for its musician? (L, 2, 474–77)

728 Coester made another impassioned plea to which Beckett responded firmly: “You speak as a musician, I as a writer, I fear that our two positions are irreconcilable. I am too sensitive to music, and too well aware of what it does to texts to be able to allow mine to be exposed to it” (L, 2, 477–78). Yet, the rivers of life flow on. Following Molloy and Malone meurt, and before l’Innommable, Beckett turned to writing plays. After the false start of Eleutheria, En attendant Godot enjoyed both controversy and success. Beckett began his next play Fin de partie, but he became bogged down in it—literally: stuck under the weight of his own words. At this time, Deryk Mendel, an English-born dancer and mime artist working in France, asked several playwrights if they would write new material for his performing character, a silent clown named “Frollo.” The scenario that resulted was Acte sans paroles. In this series of movements a solitary figure is tempted to satisfy his human needs by reaching for such objects as a carafe of water when it is lowered from above on a rope; but gratification is always slightly out of reach. The discipline of choreographing a mime, a scenario that presented a body in the space of silence, released Beckett from the trap of words. From this emerged the discovery that physical movement alone could tell a story and convey feeling. Much of Clov’s weariness of routine is shown with mime in Fin de partie (Clov preparing the set at the opening of the play, situating and climbing his ladder at the windows, centering Hamm’s wheelchair—at his insistence—with exactness). We see mime again in Krapp’s Last Tape, in the banana dance and even in the embrace of the tape recorder, as Krapp rewinds it to replay the recorded memory of lost love. In Rockaby, in which speech is largely recorded, the defining movement is the back-and-forth oscillation of a mechanical rocking chair. One can trace a kinetic pattern, whether in the physical gestures of a character or the mechanical movement of tape-recorder or rocking chair or the pacing of Amy and May in Footfalls, to see how Beckett employs these physical rhythms to frame and extend the resonance of his words. Indeed, as they become fewer and more challenged by silence, how his words depend on such rhythms as a continuo. Beckett asked his cousin John Beckett to compose music for the mime Acte sans paroles. John Beckett worked so closely with Deryk Mendel that the result appears to have been of a piece. Insisting that the music was central to the mime, Samuel Beckett writes to the Dublin Theatre Festival on 7 January, 1957: “The music is not at all music of atmosphere, but tied up very precisely with the action of the mime.”23 He explains to Alan Simpson, Director of the Pike Theatre in Dublin, that the music “has to be composed in function of a stage, a player and accessories.”24 Here, music becomes a necessary element. Beckett wrote to the Danish translator Christian Ludvigsen, “I am afraid the mime without the music is out of the question.” Moreover, “even if you had the score or recording of its music you would be no better off, the problem being its coordination with the movements of the mime.” Lacking notes for choreography and a score or recording that “indicates the music-argument coordination,” Beckett advises Ludvigsen to “drop the idea of Act Without Words.” 25

When Beckett came to direct Endgame, he depended upon its inherent musicality, 729 as he had with Godot. He told Georges Belmont, that the play was a “cantata for two voices.”26 He asked that certain phrases which occur throughout the text be spoken in exactly the same way with the same tone, like a note of music played in an invariable way by the same instrument. Hamm’s story is a monologue with four voices, each with a distinctive attitude. Michael Haerdter, dramaturge with Beckett’s production of Endspiel in Berlin in 1967, wrote his observations of the rehearsal process:

It is now fully orchestrated; in some passages the instrument even makes music . . . [Beckett’s] verbal directing, based on coloration and accentuation of single words and phrases, increasingly becomes “musical” direction bringing the melody to a scene or an entire section . . . Musical terms are turning up more often, legato, andante, piano, scherzo.27

If musicality is a hallmark of Beckett’s directing process in the 1960s and 1970s, it is just before this period that Beckett gradually came to explore what music could bring to his work and to his words. When the BBC presented Patrick Magee’s readings from Molloy, Beckett agreed to musical passages. He wrote to its producer Donald McWhinnie after the broadcast with appreciation of the “extraordinary quality of Magee’s performance.” He was also pleased by John Beckett’s music, adding: “I had wondered a little if the text would take it. It not only did, it benefited . . . The points at which the music took over I thought very well chosen and its entry finely prepared by the voice.28 And two weeks later, also to McWhinnie, Beckett said: “John wrote that he would do the music differently if he did it again. But I thought it was right, in a spirit not of reinforcement but of otherness.”29

Perhaps it is this potential for otherness that helps to explain why Beckett comes to accept music with his words. The next year (21 March, 1958), Beckett allowed composer Marc Wilkinson to set Lucky’s speech from Waiting for Godot: “There is no objection on my part to your having set to music an extract from Godot or to your having it performed.”30 Clearly Beckett made a distinction between music composed on the basis of his words and an “adaptation” of a work that changed his vision of it, for, just a few weeks later, he wrote to Judith Schmidt at Grove Press to oppose a project for “Endgame as a musical drama.”31

Beckett’s compunction about allowing his words to be joined with music was altered when the proposal came from a composer in whom he had trust. Certainly that was the case with the setting of Krapp’s Last Tape by Marcel Mihalovici to whom, as Beckett wrote to Barney Rosset at Grove Press in 1959, he had given permission “to make a chamber opera out of Krapp’s Last Tape, for the RTF and for a theatre in Germany . . . He is a fine musician and a good friend.”32 Mihalovici’s wife was the pianist Monique Haas, a friend of Suzanne Beckett. On 12 February, 1961, Beckett reported on a rehearsal of a concert performance of Mihalovichi’s Krapp to Judith Schmidt at Grove Press: “The music is very fine—but of course not much left of the wearish old man and his voices. On the other hand much added.”33

Soon after Mihalovici’s operatic treatment of Krapp’s Last Tape, Beckett composed the radio play Words and Music. As characters, “Words” and “Music” confront

730 one another. Each attempts to control the other. Fusion is impossible, collaboration unsuccessful, both acclimation and compromise seem to suffer defeat. In the thrust and parry, Words attempted to compose a poem “but stumbled till they followed the shape music dictated. Melody and rhythm were, in the end, what made them ‘right’ and released the meanings they were seeking,” wrote Katharine Worth. She added, “Only Beckett could have made a whole play out of this notion.” When Beckett asked Worth to convey his appreciation to Humprhey Searle who had composed the music for this production, Beckett admitted: “Music always wins.”34 Although not the finale of Beckett’s exploration of music with his words, in Words and Music, Beckett comes close to fulfilling his desire to find a method of verbally exposing the limitations of words, the goal voiced in his 1937 letter to Axel Kaun:

Since we cannot dismiss [language] all at once, at least we do not want to leave anything undone that may contribute to its disrepute. To drill one hole after another into it until that which lurks behind, be it something or nothing, starts seeping through . . . At first, it can only be a matter of somehow inventing a method of verbally demonstrating this scornful attitude vis-à-vis the word. In this dissonance of instrument and usage perhaps one will already be able to sense a whispering of the end-music or of the silence underlying all. (L, 1, 514, 518–19)

Over time, through changing circumstance, and stimulated by acute listening, Beckett changes his attitude with regard to music with his words. Discovery leads to creative possibility, and, with it, we can witness evolution in the rapport between music and words in his texts. And why not? We would not find the work as compelling as it is, if the man and the artist had not continued to explore the periphery, the edges of the media in which he worked.

The archives of letters and manuscripts map the false starts, the hesitations, the changes, and even the re-conception of a work. En attendant Godot was published before its stage production, and republished afterward to reflect some of the changes that developed during rehearsal. Beckett went on to direct this play himself on several occasions and prepared detailed rehearsal notes, these published in the rehearsal diaries.36 As he gained more experience in theatrical production, Beckett came to feel that his work was not finished until he had worked with it in rehearsals and seen it into production. While this sense of hesitation may be akin to a poet who continues to rewrite his work over time, there is something more intrinsic in this insistence by Beckett. Although he often spoke of writing the voices he heard in his head, the rhythms of speech spoken within the articulated stage space established by the physicality of production was a dynamic that contributed to what the script and the performers created (see the records of those who have witnessed Beckett directing).37

Letters to translators and directors repeatedly show Beckett’s latitude with regard to productions of his work. In 1958, Beckett advised the Danish translator Christian Ludvigsen of Fin de partie to follow

the original French and not pay too much heed to the English version, which is often 731 unsatisfactory. When rehearsing the French production we cut the boy scene because we found it dragged and that all that really matters is that the possible existence of this boy should be registered. But in another production it might be possible to give the full scene as written. The song does not figure in the English text simply because I could not find any satisfactory equivalent. I should prefer it in.38

Five years later, also to Ludvigsen, Beckett wrote:

The words of Clov’s song are mine and the melody, which is little known, I found in an old collection of children’s songs. It was replaced in the New York production by a quatrain from the English 18th century poet Thompson [for Thomson]. Some old Danish song should meet the case, the important thing being that the words should have some bearing on the Hamm-Clov situation.39

These sorts of details emerge frequently within Beckett’s correspondence with a translator, and also in conversations and exchanges with actors and directors. What they show is that Samuel Beckett gradually became more open to changes in performance, as he recognized that the text “in play” was subject to variables that a writer cannot always anticipate.

Another letter to Ludvigsen, shows how conscious Beckett is of the nuances that arise in production.

The mental stage on which one moves when writing and the mental auditorium from which one watches it are very inadequate substitutes for the real thing. And yet without them it is impossible to write for the theatre. My experience is that the mental vision and consequent stage directions are valid on the whole but have often to be corrected, and even altered, in function not only of the real theatre space, but also of the performers. . .

The ideal would be to work knowing in advance these real conditions. I dream of going into a theatre with no text, or hardly any, and getting together with all concerned before really setting out to write. That is to say a situation when the author would not have a privileged status, as is the case when he arrives with a text already set, but would rightly function as a specialist of neither more nor less importance than the other specialists involved.40

What an invitation for interpretation—if only Beckett had stopped there. But this marvelous statement of theatrical aesthetics is followed, not much more than six months later, by another letter to Ludvigsen. Having invited Beckett to come to Denmark, in hopes that, with the actors there, Beckett might be able to realize this dream, Ludvigsen may have been disappointed to read Beckett’s response: “I have lost all zest for travel. I have also lost that theater dream of a text to be created in function of and collaboration with all elements concerned.”41

Beckett is less revisionist than human. There are times when his later opinion of an early work exceeds even his usual tendency to self-deprecation. To Jérôme Lindon, he writes in 1954:

For Mercier et Camier I am very sorry that you are taking it seriously. I really could not bear it if that text were released in my imitation lifetime. It can always take its place, if you really want it, in a volume to be entitled Posthumous Droppings, together with all the false starts for example (not to be confused with the texts for nothing) and those still to come (I already have another one well on the way). The idea of Watt already makes me go purple right down to the bones. What about keeping it too for the P.D.? (L, 2, 445–47)

Republication of More Pricks Than Kicks suffered from similar approach-avoidance. In May, 1964, Beckett reported to Henry Wenning that he had agreed to allow John Calder to publish More Pricks Than Kicks. “A clause allows me to delay publication for as long as I like, but I see no point in further shilly-shally and expect the book will appear next year.”42 A similar arrangement was made with Grove Press. Then, in the midst of reading galleys in October, 1964, Beckett wrote to Barney Rosset that he had “broken down half way through . . . I simply can’t bear it. It was a ghastly mistake on my part to imagine, not having looked at it for a quarter of a century, that this old shit was revivable. I’m terribly sorry, but I simply have to ask you to stop production. I return herewith advance on royalties.”43

In 1968, Beckett refused permission for C. G. Bjurström to include More Pricks Than Kicks among the short prose texts he was translating for publication in Swedish, saying that it “deserves only oblivion.”44 Only in March, 1970, did Beckett accede to his publishers and his readers. To Kay Boyle who had written to ask about a copy of the collection, Beckett wrote that he had finally “capitulated” and allowed republication of More Pricks Than Kicks.45 Of course such hesitation was not without precedent. Almost invariably when Beckett gave permission for republication of his early work, poems or stories, it was with the proviso that their original publication and its date be given. He understood that his early work was just that, and that he had moved beyond it. Does Beckett’s own opinion of his early work change its value? Do we re-order the importance of the early work? Or do we take his view in a broader perspective.

Beckett told Samuel Putnam in 1930 that he would “get over Joyce before I die” (L, 1, 108), and later said to the poet Nick Rawson that “it had taken almost a dozen years to forgo Joyce’s influence.”46 What is more central is evident in what Beckett told John Gruen: “I can only say that Joyce had a moral effect on me—he made me realise artistic integrity.”47 We hear an echo in Beckett’s advice to Raymond Federman: “Whatever you write never compromise, and if you plan to write for money or for fame, do something else.”48 Over the course of nearly sixty years, this integrity remains.

Even in lengthy correspondences that reflect a long and lasting friendship, there are a myriad of voices in the letters: self-deprecation, flights of fancy, confessional tones, dutiful letters where events—not feelings—are the matter. To Niall Montgomery, the Dublin writer and architect, Beckett writes with a calculated Irish lilt and deliberately plays with Joycean rhythms. To Barbara Bray, a confidante from the early 1960s onwards, Beckett can move without preamble about his writings in progress (because she has read many of the drafts), but he can also write mechanically out of his diary of appointments or give a list of the mail he has received, simply because she has inquired, and because he has nothing else he cares to say. As might be expected, one man has many voices, writing out of circumstances and of moods; he can be starting to write or be stuck in 733 it, or he might prefer to dig holes in his garden and indulge in close observation of the birds in the hedges. One voice can never be taken as if all.

Nor can it always be believed. Quite often the unreliable narrator of memory is exposed by the record of archive. And yet, memory may prove the far richer, if more precarious vein. The best an archive can do is to preserve the exoskeleton of what went before. All of this is more than we had, more than we can usefully explore, even together. The archive, for all its provocative depth, will never reveal a complete narrative of artistic process, we will never have the ur-manuscript, but only a record of a phase, or a series of phases. One is reminded of a “flip book” of images that become animated when the pages are turned rapidly; clever though this is, a cartoon representation cannot convey essence.

Moreover, our post-modern psyche may well question “essence,” even as Beckett himself did. Beckett said to Charles Juliet, “The fall of a leaf and the fall of Satan, it’s all the same.” He continued: “But how to express it? There is no personal pronoun . . . I, he, we . . . nothing really works . . . One must stand where there is no pronoun, no solution, no reaction, no tenable position . . . That’s what makes work so diabolically difficult.”49 And to John Gruen, “I can only estimate my work from within. If my work has any meaning at all, it is due more to ignorance, inability, and an intuitive despair than to any individual strength.”50

At the Modern Language Association meetings in 1979, Beckett scholars gathered in San Francisco, and a large panel of Richard Admussen, Enoch Brater, Ruby Cohn, Martha Fehsenfeld, S. E. Gontarski, Dougald MacMillan, James Knowlson, John Pilling, and Hersh Zeifman convened a discussion about the work to be done in Beckett studies. Already advanced in their own contributions to Beckett scholarship, these colleagues issued an open invitation to continue and enlarge this effort. The generosity of this presentation marked the communal spirit in Beckett scholarship that is often witnessed in archives—not only do we help each other and look forward to our conversations, but we seek out opportunities to collaborate. Lest we overstate, as the Samuel Beckett: Out of the Archive conference has shown, we also acknowledge our differences, but the man and his writing are an overriding passion—not just a good book topic but an invested passion that drives most of us to write several books and initiate new projects. This is possible only because we can continue to return to a seemingly inexhaustible well: the work itself. Beckett’s work never seems to ring exactly the same notes, no matter how often it is reread or replayed. This is more important now that the web can relay electronic texts and make it possible for archives to be virtual.

Seamus Heaney writes about history as bog from which one can uncover the past that is embalmed and preserved in it.51 What re-membering can we do out of the bog of history? What can we take out of the archives? The Beckett archives invite exploration and evaluation. They allow scholars to shape critical ideas and test them against evidence that a series of drafts can provide (as long as we acknowledge that there are gaps in this record). They encourage us to revisit our conclusions. We cannot long hold our assumptions unassailed any more than the modernist ambition to capture “a day in the life of” can represent the truth of any moment.

734 We know by now the legions of productions of Waiting for Godot: with women, with prisoners, with puppets, for children, for desert nomads (to whom Godot represented waiting for water), and even, thanks to George Tabori, in rehearsal. There has been resistance and reaction from the Estate of Samuel Beckett, as it has been variously constituted over the years. It is common lore that Beckett exercised absolute control over his texts. Yet this is challenged by Beckett himself as the transmigrations of performance shifted the centrality of this or that line. We can see in the hats of Godot as portrayed in the first Polish production (where the boy had a wide-brimmed straw hat) and that produced in South Africa (where the hats were of the people), that local situations (from actors and stage space to politics and history, not to mention the personal stamp of directors) affect the whole that constitutes Waiting for Godot—for which the text is merely the beginning. Archives preserve records of value to a community because that community has felt that these values had a place in the future, and even, perhaps, that its value could shape the future. The problematic is both in the way we do our research in “primary” materials (itself a value statement), and in the ways we apply our research. What does our response to the texts contribute to the record that is preserved? If our re-membering imposes the traces of our collective memory, it makes histoire out of the shards. On the other hand, if uses the archive of oral history, letters, comparative drafts, and other documents to pose new questions of the work, it makes inquiry a living thing. We have a responsibility to be ambitious in our questions and critical of our conclusions. Our minds can only approximate the shape, not define it.52 But this should never keep us from asking the questions nor from asserting the values that have resonance for us—and for the future.
