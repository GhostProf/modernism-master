The Great War, Pornography, and the Transformation of Modern Male Subjectivity

Carolyn Dean

The vagueness of nineteenth-century criteria for pornography is infamous. The British jurist Alexander Cockburn, for example, wrote that pornography "deprave[s] and corrupt[s] those whose minds are open to immoral influences," and although his nebulous statement gave rise to some debate about the dangers of such imprecise definition, no sources suggest that contemporaries had any doubt about what constituted pornography. 1 Though it is true that obscenity laws in England, France, and Germany were invoked to prosecute pamphlets about contraception, cheap sexually explicit texts, as well as politically suspect writing, prosecution was typically conducted with genuine confidence in the solidity of the pornography concept. 2 It was only in the aftermath of World War I that charges against "pornographic" work became increasingly inconsistent, incoherent, and enacted for the most part with great reluctance. 3 In a dramatic epistemological and rhetorical shift, critics began to use the term "pornography" to designate less an identifiable category of texts and images, more the attenuation or dissolution of semantic categories that had once been clearly demarcated. The appellation "pornographic" no longer served to define so-called immoral material, but to re the social as a body whose desire exceeded conventional boundaries between morality and immorality. This important transformation in the status of pornography usually goes unremarked, and yet the catechrestic use of "pornography" to which it gave rise informs nearly all current accounts of what pornography represents.

Indeed, commentators of different ideological persuasions transform pornography's unclear boundaries into the theoretical grounds on which to oppose censorship. Because pornography cannot be clearly defined, many American liberals insist that censors might wittingly or unwittingly target worthy ideas that they believe are pornographic. 4 In a more affirmative evaluation of pornography, anti-censorship feminists argue that the term designates material that refuses to define human desire in fixed terms, and will be the target of censors committed to destroying social forces (including women's, lesbian, and gay liberation) that challenge the dominance of normative heterosexuality. 5 In contrast, those who are most committed to pornography's abolition reject the assertion that pornography is an elastic category of meaning. Feminists opposed to pornography, most notably represented by legal scholar Catharine MacKinnon, argue that pornography is so central to the maintenance of men's power that arguments about its elasticity are merely ruses the justice system employs to keep pornography legal. MacKinnon defines pornography as a form of gender exploitation, and gender exploitation as a form of pornography. The persuasive power of her analysis, however, lies in the rhythmic repetition of this tautology rather than in any precise definition of the pornographic. 6

Like these feminists, fundamentalist Christians (from a different vantage point) extend the notion of pornography to include an array of materials so vast and divergent that it renders their definition equally imprecise. Because pornography means everything, it means nothing in particular, which may account for the rather dramatic slippage that pairs "pornography" with images of lynching, sexuality, or even environmental pollution. Such images share only one feature: they are "pornographic," meaning, tautologically, that they are racist or sexist, and ratively, that they contaminate the air we breathe.

Debates about whether or not to censor pornography have thus become occasions to assert, in various ways, the elasticity of its meaning. But to the extent that elasticity defines its socio-cultural power, pornography is curiously emptied of any concrete meaning: its elasticity cannot therefore be particular to anything substantive about pornography itself. If we focus less on the explicit social agendas of different debates and address the ideological structure that makes those debates possible, we might speculate about how this construction of pornography as a conceptually indeterminate category developed.

Some scholars have suggested that pornography violates the integrity of the human body by stripping it of the abstract rights invested in it since the eighteenth century. In this view, the concept of the pornographic refers above all to instances in images or texts when a body is treated as if it were merely a body, a "thing" to be used. Frances Ferguson defines the Marquis de Sade's materialist reduction of the body to its use-value, rather than its rights, as the origin of modern pornography. 7 This analysis provides a unified base for the very different arguments made by censorship and anti-censorship advocates alike: that pornography represents the degradation of women's and men's bodies (anti-pornography feminists); that it denies human spirituality by denying the integrity of bodies (fundamentalists); that it must be defended in case we mistake real "art" for unworthy "pornography" (liberals); that it shatters a fixed concept of the body's desires (anti-censorship feminists). These arguments depend for their coherence on the idea that pornography is an assault on human integrity and rational subjectivity for better or (usually) for worse.

Ferguson's argument, however useful, retrospectively endows Sade's work with meanings that were not invented until the early twentieth century. These meanings, I shall argue, first emerge in the wake of the Great War, when social commentators and analysts began to postulate a significant link between the wartime proliferation of pornography and the unprecedented violence of modern combat. Only then did pornography and violence became continuous descriptions of assaults on the integrity of the human body, establishing a sense of continuity that remains essential to the idea that pornography is a mobile category of meaning with no fixed referents. It is not a coincidence, in other words, that during the years since 1920 non-violent heterosexual "pornography" has gradually been exempted from obscenity prosecution in the United States and Western Europe. Nor is it surprising that anti-pornography feminist activists believe that pornography is violence against women. 8 Both are reflexes of the massive redefinition of pornography sparked by the Great War.

In the mid- to late nineteenth century, cultural elites defined pornography as material that stimulated or provoked sexual sensations and acts that were antisocial, even dangerous, especially in the morally weak or unformed (i.e. children, women, working-class men). Pornography, by stimulating nerves already overwrought, compromised the rational will, blurring the boundaries between fantasy and reality, and reduced human passion to sexual instincts; in so doing it transformed manly men into effetes enslaved by their senses, threatening the reproductive heterosexuality that subtended class and gender hierarchies. By the end of the Great War, however, this kind of rhetoric had shifted. Pornography's putative degradation of the human body no longer signified undisciplined sexual pleasure, but represented discipline as a form of pleasure. My focus is on how this shift occurred, its repercussions, and, most importantly, how it transformed the concept of rational subjectivity that depends on a clear distinction between compulsion and will, pleasure and discipline, for its very stability. By transforming male bodies into "pornographic" spectacles, the war unravelled conventional boundaries between the moral and immoral, a process that also dissolved gender distinctions that those boundaries subtended and thereby precipitated a reinscription of masculinity and femininity in new terms.

While it is true that eroticized violence was a predominant theme in underground pornography in England and North America during the nineteenth century, contemporaries generally viewed it as a manifestation of its authors' and readers' weakness of moral will; its violence was one among many examples of the human susceptibility to sin which was evident in all pornographic material. 9 After the Great War, however, there is a new continuity between the thematics of war and pornography, in which sexualized violence becomes the primary example of human moral failure. Already before the war, in 1905, Sigmund Freud had suggested that "the history of human civilization shows beyond any doubt that there is an intimate connection between cruelty and the sexual instinct." 10 But it was during the interwar years that most critics and medical men insisted on the primacy of "sexual emotion," repeatedly stressing "the narrow connection between jouissance [pleasure] and suffering." 11 Although this discourse was partly a consequence of the war--one thinks of Freud's 1920 Beyond the Pleasure Principle--it also constituted the terms within which the war was narrativized. For a wide range of commentators, war became an extension of sexuality and sexuality an extension of the violence intrinsic in warfare, a connection often established by equating war with sadomasochistic sexuality. In 1933, for example, Dr. Paul Englisch urged a direct link between the Great War and flagellation pornography: "Whereas before the war of 1914 German pornographers focused on normal sexual relations, in its aftermath pornography about sexual perversion flourished. Long years in the trenches stirred men's sadistic instincts and their taste for horror." 12

Englisch's remarks need to be set within the context of more generalized myths about modern war, in which violence and the spiritual cleansing of the male body are oddly continuous: violence, paradoxically, purges the suffering intrinsic in violence. As Paul Gautier argued in 1919, war is a "natural" form of destruction aimed at social regeneration and "the happiness of all." It is a process that repairs "seams in the social fabric" by awakening "moral force and courage": war, which is also a sin, is a form of "punishment, expiation, and suffering," but a "purifying suffering [douleur purificatrice]" and hence a sin that also cleanses the social body of sin. 13 Gautier was only reiterating a long romantic tradition in this construction of patriotism as willful self-sacrifice, a tradition repeatedly invoked by journalists and novelists throughout the war's duration. Governments, as historians have demonstrated, likewise propagated the image of the brave soldier whose soul goes to heaven, his spiritual wounds healed by the rative tears of the nation. War-induced suffering purges the body of sin, and that suffering itself is transcended through national mourning.

But the Great War provoked a reassessment of this myth. In 1917, the critic Edmond Haraucourt wrote a report, commissioned by the government, about pornography laws and the effects of "demoralizing" literature and images in a far more critical vein. He argued that the Great War had transformed the willful and noble sacrifice of men's bodies into the love of death. In this account, patriotism no longer served to heal wounds, but became a pretext for inflicting more wounds, more eviscerations: "Vers l'amour" now means "pour la mort." He goes on: "At the end of this long alert which unleashed violent instincts, we can expect to find those instincts alive and well. . . . We will continue to kill a great deal after the war." For Haraucourt, pornography instantiates this never-ending cycle of death and destruction evacuated of transcendent meaning, and deprives this myth--whereby the soldier's suffering cleanses the national body of sins--of narrative closure. Before the war, "[pornographers] troubled morality; during the war, they did not respect our mourning, and after the war, they constitute a threat to the motherland." Although he never articulates a clear relationship between the war and pornography, he believes that pornography has become one of the primary vehicles through which the "contagion" of war, the "attraction to death," will be disseminated. Depicting human life as a struggle between love and death, Haraucourt argues that the war has signalled the triumph of death and transformed sexual desire into longing "for death." Pornography both represents and produces a national psyche in which the supreme sacrifice is no longer continuous with the body's cleansing but with its degradation, and acts of will have become inseparable from psychic compulsion. 14

Moreover, writers again and again red women as emblems of the war's sexual sadism. In 1921 Marcello Fabri represented the war as a seductive female vampire who drains male bodies of their virility, and he noted a correlation between the "thirst for blood," sexual perversion, and pornography. 15 According to Fabri, patriotic self-sacrifice is no longer a dignified, because freely chosen, gesture, but a pleasurable, irresistible, virility-sapping perversion. Charles-Noel Renard also allegorizes the war as a pack of vampire lesbians whom men find irresistible. 16

There are numerous interwar novels and commentaries in which women are surrogates for war, and the sadistic and vampiristic qualities ascribed to women in them draw upon conventional tropes. But many critics describe soldiers--and men's role in warfare more generally--in less conventional terms. They no longer equate self-sacrifice with spiritual cleansing, but with spiritual degradation; the body has become enslaved by its own instincts, or, more specifically, by a compulsive death-drive. Men have learned to obey their instincts unencumbered by the dictates of moral will. 17 The war's degradation of men's dignity is red in the putatively emasculating and sexually pathological pleasure of humiliating and being humiliated, of exposing and being exposed. The war, in this view, had turned into a pretext for the satisfaction of other pleasures.

In a 1921 work graced with a preface by the famous Swiss sexologist Auguste Forel, André Lorulot compared the Great War's "barbarism" to the sadistic pleasures that Spanish inquisitors had taken in performing their task. He conjured up predictable anti-clerical images of debauched, perverted priests, but in a later work he made his real point more explicit. In a book about German barbarism that is essentially a litany of anecdotes about flagellation and sexual perversions, he considers the question of how punishment could produce sexual feelings so powerful that men and women "passionately sought them out." Lorulot finally associates sadomasochism with pathological politics, and specifically with Hitler's rise to power. 18 Jean de Mezerette, in a similar and by then predictable theme, will re Hitler and his entourage as effeminate, homosexual, sadomasochistic, and Jewish (although he deleted all references to Jews in the 1945 edition). 19

The magazine editor Georges Anquetil's 1925 Satan Conduit le bal (Satan Leads the Dance) similarly equates the war with sadomasochistic sexuality. Trench journalists had already described the spectacle of dismembered corpses in macabre detail, but Anquetil implicitly eroticizes such images by juxtaposing flagellation scenes in brothels with anecdotes of war atrocities. 20 The war is turned into a transnational brothel, another eroticized mise-en-scène in which the body's private pleasure and pain become public affairs. In short, he represents "depraved morality" as an eroticized quest for death whose most privileged embodiment is sadomasochistic spectacle: the body bound, unclothed, mutilated, humiliated. 21 In the anti-German articles he penned in 1917, Paul Voivenel had argued that sadomasochism directed the German war effort on the grounds that German soldiers took masochistic pleasure in "waxing the boots of the State." 22 Sadomasochism implies the submission of the individual to the State and the collapse of private and public, symbolized again, in Lorulot's and Anquetil's many anecdotes, by the constrained, even wounded body, flushed with pleasure in spite of itself. The novelist Louis Dumur's antihero describes an assassination that makes his companions' faces light up with a "pure and evident pleasure," and confesses that the sadistic rape and murder that constitute the center of the novel "disgusted and excited me at the same time." 23

Critics increasingly associated corporeal degradation with the war's unprecedented destruction of bodies, and they conceived narratives about German war atrocities as pedagogical tools meant to instruct civilians about the horrors of war. But these narratives worked for and against their own ideological (nationalist) purposes. In their frequent efforts to portray German militarism as the pathological (because sadomasochistic) desire for discipline, they implicitly question the motives of all military discipline. The Germans' sadomasochism implies that patriotic self-sacrifice might have other, more suspect motives. The war, in such accounts, appears to transform all soldiers into sexual perverts, permanently contaminating postwar culture as well. Although the "thirst for blood" has existed in all places and all times, wrote Marcello Fabri, the soldier of this war has demonstrated an unparalleled desire to "jouir-avant-la-mort." The desire has proved contagious: "no longer content to suck at wounds like a vampire [the people] demand more victims to pollute, and this ignominious spectacle is still not enough to satisfy their senses." 24 Now, Renard writes, in this "feminine" Republic, "all begins and ends in sexual sensation." 25 Writers who believe that the war was motivated by perversion rather than patriotism indict the French government for legitimating the war's unparalleled, frenzied sadism. They no longer criticize warfare itself, but the effects of this war, especially its sexual contamination of postwar French culture. 26

These accounts call into question the distinction between "normal" sexual instinct and perversion, insofar as they leave it unclear whether sadomasochistic sexuality (and hence a propensity to torture and rape) is a natural inclination of human beings freed from social stigmas, or the perverted product of industrialized warfare. For Lorulot and Anquetil, the war is merely a vehicle for the acting out of sadomasochistic pleasure, even as they characterize that pleasure as the pathological product of war. In 1918, Voivenel and another doctor, Louis Huot, sought to explain soldiers' psychology as the ebb and flow of sexual instinct. At the beginning of the war, when young European men marched blindly and bravely off to the front in the name of patriotism, "the hypnotism of destruction [was] comparable to a rooster hypnotized by sexual instinct." 27 Yet they also claimed that the war was responsible for transforming men into machines of destruction. Haraucourt similarly argued that the war both "unleashed violent instincts" and produced them.

In perhaps the most dramatic illustration of this paradox, debates among medical men about the effects of war on soldiers suggested that barbaric acts manifested an "instinct of preservation" in face of fear. They transformed barbarism into the body's natural quest for survival. The shock of so much violence, the effect of being "a plaything" of overwhelming forces, produced irrational, unmanly behavior that nevertheless had a rational and reassuring explanation. At the same time, doctors presumed that barbarism--here the male body's instinctive defense against unnatural sensations and feelings of powerlessness--was also psychopathological. 28 Hence the best soldiers (with some exceptions) turned out to be alcoholics and perverts because their "impulsiveness," "taste for adventure," and "morbid courage"--their so-called social defects--transformed them into heroic soldiers. 29 Because psychopathology is a symptom of war, Dr. René Charpentier argued, the best soldiers are often psychopaths. He recommended that "in times of war, we not systematically exclude from the army certain men we would reject in peacetime . . . [their] psychopathologies are, at least temporarily, compatible with national defense." 30

If psychopathology was compatible with national defense, then nationalism-- soldiers' willingness to die for their country--no longer always required self- discipline. The soldiers' self-sacrifice was not necessarily an act of supreme self-restraint. This construction of the soldiers' patriotism compromised the distinction between a man's free will and his degrading concession to instinctual pleasures. Because writers more generally redefined military discipline itself as a form of perverse (indeed, psychopathological) pleasure, they so completely conflated patriotic self-sacrifice and sadomasochistic desire that they portrayed nationalist impulses as sadomasochistic ones, and rendered the sacred national body indistinct from a profane, contaminated one.

And yet these same writers sought through these sorts of analyses to restore national integrity. Such analyses had recent precedents: in 1903, for example, in defense of Emile Zola, Ernest Charles noted that readers' saturation with repulsive texts would regenerate literature. 31 Zola himself believed that in order to cleanse the social body, a writer was obliged to expose and describe the causes of its impurity. Writers used this argument as the paradoxical foundation of postwar regeneration. Louis Dumur's defense against censorship was that his book about the war "told the truth." His editor claimed he had received hundreds of letters protesting the sadistic rape scene of an aristocratic French woman by German generals; they accused him of being "a pornographer." In his defense, Dumur claimed that the Germans counted on the silence of thousands of French women who refused to speak out from a sense of shame and modesty [par pudeur]. 32 Dumur did not denounce war but the German people--the book is Dumur's rewriting of the stories told to him by a German captain at a POW camp. His aim was not to depict the horrors of war or to call the concept of women's pudeur into question, but to demonstrate that the atrocities perpetrated by the Germans were real. He argued that his admittedly fictional account of the war would restore the French to their senses; the pudeur of ashamed women, like the pudeur of a nation raped and pillaged, had to be violated in order to be restored.

Similarly, Georges Anquetil, whose book is a seven hundred page compendium of the most ghastly war atrocities juxtaposed with their civilian analogues, asked "Am I the only one to denounce the perversion all around us?" He goes on to "cite and resume all the unimaginable series of savageries, of sadism, of cruelty, and of pederasty . . . during the war," in order to make them imaginable and hence unimaginable in the future.

Haraucourt also argued that literature that simply repressed what he called the natural links between sex and war did not restore discipline, but encouraged degeneration. Books, he argued, should no longer be censored according to abstract formulations of good and evil, but rather, according to changing social and cultural mores which themselves determined which books "cool" passions. Haraucourt's aim of cooling passions was predictable. But the way he proposed to achieve it--a flexible censorship grounded in loosely defined mores rather than abstract moral laws--derived from his conviction that in the Great War military discipline had heated rather than cooled passions. Men had been as excited by death as they were because interdictions--in the form of overzealous censorship and social taboos--against the "literature of love" forced writers to focus on "the territory of death"--"and they threw themselves into this territory with passion. Fear was the supreme thrill." 33

These commentaries thus represent perversion (or suggest the lifting of taboos against many erotic works) in order to prevent it, dismembering bodies in order to restore their integrity. Material which tells the truth about the war does not encourage the violent sexuality inherent in human nature, but, oddly, exposes sexual violence in order to restore integral, phallic bodies. The sexuality that was indissociable from the degradation of bodies is now indissociable from their redemption. As Anquetil, Dumur, Lorulot and others saw it, truth-telling renews patriotism. Such writing is labelled pornographic only by those men responsible for the war--those with an investment in lying, including perverts, corrupt politicians, and men out for commercial gain. These critics thus reproduced the voyeuristic, sadomasochistic pleasure they denounce, but did so in the interest of national and moral regeneration. 34 It is perhaps no coincidence that during the interwar years, Sade began to be revered as a moralist who revealed our inhumanity to ourselves. That is, Sade--according to a literary critical tradition initiated by the surrealists and most forcefully expressed by Jean Paulhan--sought to repel his readers in order to show them the error of their ways. 35

In their effort to distinguish their work from pornography, however, these writers confused the issue of what pornography was. Indeed, Lorulot's and Anquetil's documentary and high moral style recalls the sensationalism of novels replete with sadomasochistic content that have no such pretensions: their writings detail the body's humiliation, how it is bound, and where and how deep its wounds are. Commentators transformed this confusion into the central meaning of pornography, thereby denying pornography its status as a specific category of texts and images. They used pornography instead to refer to a wide variety of material whose only common attribute, according to them, was its refusal to take a stand, to proclaim its meaning.

The pseudonymous Balkis, whom Anquetil includes in a long chapter euphemistically titled "Nos Moeurs d'après les romanciers" (Our Mores According to Novelists), quite literally expressed this refusal of singular meaning. Balkis' "pornographic" work Personne (No One) is an unnamed young woman's diary, filled mostly with her sexual experiences and her reflections about them. In one part of the "diary" its author muses: "'Personne' represents me, perhaps, since I have never taken up a single posture [posture] but a hundred of them, always leaving open the possibility for others. No one, because being no one, I am necessarily someone. . . . No one, since, changing form so easily, each form once sketched is but the shadow of a form." 36 The "no one" whose diary might be anyone's, paradoxically desires "cerebral purification" by humiliating others or being humiliated. 37 Balkis not only links sexual "postures" to the loss of self-definition, but also yokes sexuality to the desire for self-purification; he thus--in an apparently Catholic moment--conflates self-loss and purity and compares "personne's" pure self-loss to the mentality of a general in the Great War.

Anquetil denounces this desire for self-shattering purification when he criticizes the book on the grounds that its author is perverted and sadistic. In his view, the book is pornographic because it is really about the possibilities of self-multiplication and not about self-transcendence. In the part he chooses to excerpt, "personne" takes on a variety of different roles, including Salome, and becomes "a woman just like all women--no one." "La petite fille" who becomes a woman in the course of the diary "has become a garçonne," meaning a nobody, or someone who doesn't know who she is. 38 Like pornography, the discourse about purification is only a pretext for having more sex, much in the same way that the war became a pretext for more stimulation. Anquetil draws our attention to this dissimulation when he singles out "personne's" playing the part of Salome; that is the moment when she most explicitly positions herself as "no one," as an actress capable of playing multiple roles whose only identity is her assumption of other identities. All the "pornographic novels of our times" enact this self-multiplication, which in Anquetil's view constitutes a misrepresentation: pornography is an act, a lie, filth passing for purity, the pervert passing for a soldier.

It might be useful to think of pornography not in terms of any specific content, but rather, as an instantiation of Anquetil's implicit claim that representation in postwar culture must always be inadequate to its object, that pornography's meaning is precisely its elusiveness, its refusal to "mean" anything in particular. Just before and certainly after the Great War, critics conceded again and again that pornography was not what it used to be, meaning that pornography was no longer contained and identifiable. Adolphe Thery wrote that at another time consumers of pornography "themselves [had] recognized that material which crudely addressed the senses . . . [or] 'obscene' work . . . must be hidden." Today, however, "pornography is everywhere, and no place, however sacred it is, remains completely closed, because [pornography] is a supple, rich, intelligent enemy who hesitates before nothing . . . " 39 Writing in La Vie Parisienne on "The Progress of Pornography," another critic wrote that there is "no longer any vice that has not become the object of a specialized monograph," and that those monographs can be found everywhere. 40 Anti-pornography forces in fact gave up the struggle against immoral texts and acts themselves to battle instead the "contagion of immorality," "the continual incitement to immorality." 41

After the war, then, pornography defined material that no longer remained behind the closed doors of rich men's studies and infiltrated locales where it had no business being. 42 No longer hidden, pornography nevertheless eluded identification. Moreover, the tension between pornography's apparent visibility and its surreptitious infiltration of space--Thery wrote that it enters the sanctity of the home "under a benign appearance"--produced the narrative structure of an "open secret" in which the increasing visibility of pornography intensified cultural fantasies about (and surveillance of) its protean character. 43 In marked contrast to the late nineteenth century, when commentators blamed immoral authors for pornography, now pornography was perfidious because it represented a chain of "incitements" whose origin was mysterious even as their effects were apparent to everyone.

Certainly some writers blamed pornographers for trying to conceal their "real" message from readers: Louis Bethleem claimed that pornographers were mercenaries who hid the message of their work under the cover of characters who seemed to have "grandes ames." 44 In two different commentaries on Victor Margueritte's scandalous La Garçonne, one critic wrote that "what is even more comical is to see all this pornography carefully woven, detailed, nuanced, pretending to moralize and contemplate social vices." 45 And Gustave Tery noted that La Garçonne was pornographic because it was "dishonest" and "treasonous." 46 But this approach to the problem already implied that pornography was something that could be concealed, that could conceal, as it unfolded before your eyes. Indeed, in most accounts pornographic authors counted for little because they had become agents of contamination, symptoms of an epidemic that spared no one. Pornography took on a life of its own; it appeared in different guises--Bethleem was upset because pornography passed for literature--and was defined, like the diarist personne, only in terms of its ability to elude definition.

Hence Georges Fonsegrive, writing in 1911, claimed that pornography was like a prostitute: it pretends to love without loving, "its expressions are those of a body emptied of its soul, expressions without meaning or purpose, expressions that define the singularity of pornography." He suggested that pornography was dangerous because it was no longer recognizable. 47 Again, Anquetil best captured (and produced) pornography's protean quality in his condemnation of a poster inviting Parisians to a "ceremony" in honor of the war-dead. The poster's title, Monuments aux morts (Monuments to the Dead), was misleading. In fact, when you read the fine print, it suggested you join a group of party-goers to spend the evening dancing, or rather, two-stepping like soldiers. Anquetil denounced such irreverence. He did not use the incident, however, to condemn immoral youth, but to denounce a pornographic culture in which it was possible for young people to conflate exhausting dancing and exhausting combat. 48 Pornography, then, is not just immoral, but represents the impossibility of determining the distinction between immorality and morality. In the interwar years, writers at once transformed pornography into an elastic category of meaning and gave it a negative valence.

All the writers I have discussed were emphatic about the patriotic intent of their works and certain that their patriotism was self-evident to readers not duped by politicians' lies or emasculated by abnormal desires and perceptions. By contrast, for them, pornography expressed the loss of the boundary between free will and compulsion and hence the loss of the singular, redemptive meaning once ascribed to patriotic self-sacrifice. 49 It is still not clear, however, what distinguishes their work from the work they call pornographic: Anquetil's and others' invocation of sexual activity and of eroticized violence in particular is, as I have suggested, not clearly differentiated from similar invocations in so-called pornography. Anquetil himself accuses Louis Dumur of exploiting the war for mercenary gain, whereas Dumur defended himself--as we have seen--against all accusations of pornography.

This elasticity explains, I think, the now extraordinary descriptive breadth of the "pornographic"--no longer confined to "pornography," a clearly identifiable category of material against which one could legislate. As documents from police archives demonstrate, the description and prosecution of pornographic material in France after the Great War was inconsistent, incoherent, and aimed at an increasingly wider variety of material. 50 At the same time, many writers' efforts to draw the boundary between redemptive literature and pornography in new terms indicates that they defined pornography's negative valence in terms of its perceived challenge to clear dichotomies of truth and falsehood, morality and immorality, and masculinity and femininity. Any analysis of changing definitions of the pornographic thus suggests not only the centrality of sexuality to new definitions of subjectivity, but marks the transformation of the terms in which rational subjectivity has been constructed.

In a fascinating essay, Karen Halttunen argues that eroticized violence emerged as the underside of the new late eighteenth-century culture of sentimentalism: as violence became increasingly taboo it emerged in eroticized, pornographic form. Both arguments presume that "the pornography of pain" ultimately reflects a moral failure or immoral or pathological desire (in Halttunen, it represents the necessity of transgressing new cultural taboos); McLintock argues correctly that late Victorian scientists "demonized S/M as the psychopathology of the atavistic individual, a blood flaw and stigma of the flesh." (She does not address pornography per se.) See Anne McLintock, Imperial Leather: Race, Gender and Sexuality in the Colonial Context (New York: Routledge, 1995), 142-43; Karen Halttunen, "Humanitarianism and the Pornography of Pain in Anglo-American Culture" American Historical Review 2 (April 1995): 303-34.

In this essay, I am interested in a different historical moment in which pornography's meaning becomes indeterminate.
