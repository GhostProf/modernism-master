Seeing into Modernity: Walter Sickert's Music Hall Scenes, c. 1887-1907, and English Modernism *

David Peters Corbett

res

1

The French tradition beginning with Baudelaire's writings on art and the work of Manet has seemed to generations of scholars to be the most compelling modernist work there is. In the earliest historiography of modern painting this art was conceived as possessing an inherent drive towards abstraction, the full realization of which was understood to occur only with the passing of the modernist baton to New York in 1945. The dominant concern of modernist art was said to be the pursuit of a pure painting, a preoccupation with pigment and the surface of the canvas, and a consequent denigration of the importance of subject matter. Associated above all with Clement Greenberg and Alfred Barr, this style of thinking has been widely criticized from the 1970s onwards and largely replaced by an alternative reading which privileges engagement with social modernity. 1 Stemming from the thought of Walter Benjamin, a very specific reading of Baudelaire, and, most immediately, from the popularity of T. J. Clark's powerful analysis of Impressionism in The Painting of Modern Life, this approach defines a modernism in painting that is engaged with the society of the spectacle and the social consequences of modernity. 2

However different these accounts may be, they share a fascination with the art and thought of France as central to the project of understanding modernism, and hence provide a norm upon which any definitions must explicitly or implicitly be built. 3 One consequence of such a situation is historiographical hesitancy about the status of modern visual art made in other places and under other circumstances. In the case of English art, the evident differences from French norms have obscured the possibility of evaluating the modernity of work produced from the mid-nineteenth century onwards. 4 With very few exceptions, such as that of Vorticism around 1914 or the revivified continental abstractionism of Ben Nicholson in the 1930s, English art has seemed continually at odds with ideas of modernism derived from France, whether it is conceived as abstraction or as socially responsive. An artist like Walter Sickert (1860-1942) is problematic in this way. Evidently occupied with the subjects of modern life, he is also committed to exploring the idea of a purely formal painting. But in neither aspect is he readily imaginable within French norms except through interpretative violence.

The question of the modernity of late nineteenth century English art has only recently begun to receive attention. Debate has focused around two issues: the possibility of understanding the ways in which the art produced in England in these years responded to cultural modernity in Britain; and how far it can be related to modernist art made elsewhere. 5 These questions open up the possibility of reading the modernism of an artist like Sickert within the historical circumstances and concerns which formed his art. But Sickert's modernity continues to be judged by the degree to which he can be brought within the definitions of French modernism. In 1970 Wendy Baron concentrated on Sickert's interest in formal matters and argued that his work can be understood as the accommodation of a succession of French styles via Manet, Whistler, and Impressionism to an earlier, "pre-Impressionist" French tradition which she characterized as "the climax of Sickert's history." 6 More recently art historians have begun to explicate Sickert's art by adapting the concerns which the social history of art finds in French modernism. Anna Gruetzner Robins, the foremost commentator in this vein, has suggested that we must understand Sickert as a flâneur, integrating him into an interpretation of "modernist strategies and modern life" which derives from the preoccupations of current readings of French experience. 7

These commentators seem to share a certainty that the developments to be found in France are ineluctably prior to any which could have taken place in England. If Sickert's modernism is taken to lie in his technical practice, and yet he seems to have rejected the procedures of Impressionism, then it is inevitable that he found the "method . . . which he considered to be ideal" in another version of French painting, even if this time "of pre-Impressionist origin." If the interest is in the social modernity of his subject matter, then it seems equally inevitable that we should turn to powerful concepts such as the spectacle, the flâneur, and ideas of urban leisure evolved in scholarship on the social history of French modernism, to better understand Sickert's work. Yet it is no more clear that we can use definitions of cultural modernity predicated upon French history to improve our grasp of English art than that we should refer any technical innovations to French models. The circumstances of French and English modernity are distinct and modes of representation in the two countries are culturally specific. To translate terms and concepts evolved to account for one type of art to describe work produced elsewhere is to court misreading.

In this essay I pursue a rather different argument. Taking the example of the important music hall scenes he produced from the late 1880s onwards, I suggest that we can discern in Sickert's work a critique of the capacity of painting to comprehend modern experience, even as he deploys it himself in an effort to do so. Sickert inherits from this two mentors, Whistler and Degas, contrasting approaches to the representation of modernity, broadly equivalent to the formalist and the social definitions I have discussed. Both are present in his work. But their passage through English culture in the form of Symbolist and Realist modes of painting, makes them available to Sickert in ways that proved conducive to critique. By looking closely at the music hall paintings we can observe both his attempts to enact the modernist imperative to define and understand modern life and his articulation of the difficulty of attempting to do so through what he called the "grossly material" substance of paint. 8 In the process he both asserts and questions the fascination of modernist painting with pure visuality and with the subject matter of modernity. I will argue that this dialectic is formed within the specificity of English modernity.

2

Sickert's music hall paintings fall into several distinct chronological periods. 9 They first appear in his oeuvre around 1887, and Sickert produced some of his most important paintings and graphic works of the London halls in the years up to 1889. This first period includes the sequence of paintings which I shall discuss below, in which mirrors play an important compositional role. Returning to this theme between 1894 and 1898, Sickert produced a number of studies concentrating mostly on the Bedford Music Hall in Camden Town. The majority of these paintings take as their subject various descriptions of the audience. This phase of his work seems to have been brought to a close when Sickert departed for Dieppe at the end of 1898. He was based abroad for seven years and returned to London to live and work during 1905, a move which may have inspired his next sequence of music hall subjects. In 1906-1907, starting with Noctes Ambrosianae (1906, Nottingham Museum and Art Gallery), Sickert produced studies of the audiences seen in halls in London and Paris. After this, and with very few exceptions, Sickert abandoned themes related to the halls.

The early music hall scenes have been identified with Sickert's transfer of loyalties from Whistler to Degas during the 1880s. Their importance in this way is indicated by Wendy Baron, who ascribes to them Sickert's main influence from the French tradition concerned with content rather than technique. According to Baron:

In spite of Sickert's denial of any French influence behind his early music-hall paintings their compositional arrangements were certainly derived from Degas's theater and ballet pictures; moreover, Degas's cafés-concerts, with their harsh lighting and robustly characterized chanteuses must have contributed towards Sickert's initial inspiration to tackle their nearest English equivalent, the cockney music-hall. 10

She goes on to account for this influence in formal terms of composition and technical procedure. More recently, T. J. Clark in The Painting of Modern Life devotes some close examination to the cafés-concerts and the attention Degas paid them in the 1870s. Clark sees the commodified leisure of the urban Parisians as a fertile soil for the complexities of definition and enactment of the new class identities modernity brought in its wake. Clark's café-concert "produced the popular" as a sounding board against which the uncertainties of class could be made to resonate and which it "mitigated . . . by making a spectacle of them." 11 For at least some of its audience, Clark argues, "it seems . . . that the masquerade [of the performance] confirmed their sense of the class as pure contingency, a matter of endless shifting and exchange, for society was pictured in the café-concert as a series of petty transformation scenes, in which everyone suffered the popular sea-change'. 12 In responding to these strange circumstances, Degas's paintings give an image to this sense that the audience "were the illusion (Painting of Modern Life, 224)."

Intriguingly, in making this argument Clark feels the need, virtually for the only time in his writings, to draw a standard of comparison from the circumstances of English modernity. He cites T. S. Eliot's 1923 obituary essay on the music hall artist Marie Lloyd. Clark quotes at length and it will be worth following what holds his attention so strongly. Eliot observes in his text that Lloyd had in her time "far the strongest hold on popular affection" and attributes this to "her understanding of the people and sympathy with them, and the people's recognition of the fact that she embodied the virtues which they genuinely most respected in private life." Eliot concludes:

I have called her the expressive re of the lower classes. There is no such expressive re for any other class. The middle classes have no such idol: the middle classes are morally corrupt. That is to say, their own life fails to find a Marie Lloyd to express it; nor have they any independent virtues which might give them as a conscious class any dignity. (Painting of Modern Life, 216-17)

This is, in other words, an argument about the representation of bourgeois modernity in English culture. In some way which neither Eliot nor Clark explore, the difficulties of evolving a set of languages through which modernity of experience could be represented is rooted, or perhaps especially telling, in the English experience. Clark returns to Eliot's claim to clinch his own argument.

To put Eliot's verdict in a more limited form: the middle class in the later nineteenth century, and even the early years of the twentieth, had not yet invented an imaginary of its own fate . . . for the time being it was obliged to feed on the values and idioms of those classes it wished to dominate; and doing so involved it in making the idioms part of a further system, in which the popular was expropriated from those who produced it--made over into a separate realm of images which were given back, duly refurbished, to the "people" thus safely defined. (Painting of Modern Life, 229-230)

As a result, Cark says, "the material called popular" was hotly contested, needing to be "continually renewed and recast, lest its working-class users find ways of giving it back its original consistency--making it assume, for example, the form called proletarian" (Painting of Modern Life, 230).

One might well argue that Sickert's music hall scenes are indices of the same modern processes that Clark discerns in the cafés-concerts. Sickert's colonization of the popular forms of the music hall, his forceful use of the bodies of working-class models and the gestures of working-class entertainment as markers of modern experience, might seem to amount to the same thing as Clark's cafés-concerts, the capture of powerful representations of modernity as a means of cloaking the nakedness of the bourgeois imaginary. It's a good argument, and I can readily imagine someone giving it an outing on Sickert's music hall pictures. But I want instead to suggest that the meanings Sickert took from Degas would not be adequately described by this reading. Degas, for Sickert, is part of a complex of possible relations to modernity which are best described as deriving from the patterns of reception of continental influences in England. They are bound up with the ways in which Symbolism and Realism as alternative and competing descriptions of modernity were alive for Sickert--not least within the process of the compositions of his paintings; and not least when they were named respectively Whistler and Degas--and the ways in which they were worked through as part of the interest in making painting a practice suited to the representation and investigation of modern life within specifically British conditions and circumstances. To clarify this I need to say something about how these relations were defined by the time that Sickert began his music hall sequences in the 1880s, and particularly about the role of Symbolism in that unfolding process.

3

In 1889 Sickert wrote an introduction to the catalogue of the "London Impressionists" show at the Goupil Gallery in which he showed several music hall pictures. 13 What Sickert said there has had a bad press from art historians, who have seen it as an instance of bad faith or a deliberate attempt to throw the readers off the scent of his relationship with continental Impressionism. The problem is that Sickert writes about a collection of paintings which have a shared aim to depict the contemporary scene as if they were perpetuating the protocols and concerns of Symbolism. In the catalogue he defined Impressionism like this:

Essentially and firstly it is not realism. It has no wish to record anything merely because it exists . . . It accepts, as the aim of the picture, what Edgar Allen Poe asserts to be the sole legitimate province of the poem, beauty. It is . . . strong in the belief that for those who live in the most wonderful city in the world, the most fruitful course of study lies in the persistent effort to render the magic and poetry which they daily see around them. 14

This has often seemed disconcerting to later readers. 15 Sickert's justification for the subject matter of the show is not social realism or "the painting of modern life." Instead, he couches his work in the language of symbolism by using the terms "beauty," "magic" and "poetry." And by evoking Poe he calls upon one of the arch-symbolists of the English-language tradition. How are we then to reconcile this artist's own account of his work with the ambitions which seem to be embodied in works shown at the Goupil, such as Sidney Starr's The City Atlas (1889, National Gallery of Canada) or Sickert's own The Butcher's Shop (1884, York City Art Gallery), let alone with his music hall paintings? The catalogue introduction seems to suggest a different and inappropriate principle of meaning for images which strike us as plainly about social realism.

It would be a mistake to place too much interpretative weight on Sickert's introduction. Nevertheless, I think that we can largely accept what he says here. The fact is that it is possible to read this strand of British Impressionism as profoundly aware of the ambitions of Symbolism and influenced by them in its project to represent the modern city. 16 These paintings have a central concern with describing the realities of experience as they obtained within the modernized environment of London's new boundaries. 17 The modern city is presented as a legitimate slice of reality on which to expend the analytical and registering powers of painting. 18 The perception of the London Impressionists show as centrally concerned with the requirements of this recording is accurate enough. But it is also the "magic and poetry" and the "beauty" of this material which is to be evoked and these are Symbolist ambitions, that Wendy Baron quite rightly refers directly to Whistler's Ten O'Clock Lecture.

The last decades of the nineteenth century, those in which Symbolism was emerging within the fluid relationships of the British art world, saw the beginning of an unprecedented modernization of the conditions of artistic production which continued into the twentieth century. Rapidly changing, requiring ever-greater professionalization from practitioners, this modernizing process enforced the commodification of the work and the transformation of art practice into capitalist production which is so marked a feature of British experience--as of French--from the Pre-Raphaelites to Vorticism and beyond. 19

Under these circumstances the most urgent impulses of British art were those which derived from the experience of modernity. The common preoccupations were not only about the evolution of new modes and idioms of visual enquiry, but also about what needed to be enquired into, whether modern life or some promise of alternative value. What emerged was in part a new definition of practice which emphasized the function of the work of art as revelation. Capital's professionalized publicity machines and the strutting artist-entrepreneurs, of whom Whistler, "his pockets bulging with newspaper articles" is only the most obvious example, often made art out of a belief in its capacity to transcend this lived modernity. 20 The values of art were understood to be those which the materialism of contemporary life had put aside. It was to be prized for its access to concealed, but fundamental, truths, to a reality which the frenetic, mobile, surface of modernity merely disguised. Modern life was something which it was the task of the artist to endure, overcome and discard in order to reveal the truths beneath.

In the formation of this set of concepts, the induction of French Symbolisme into Britain, through channels such as Charles Ricketts's periodical of the 1880s and 1890s, The Dial, and Whistler's close relationship to Mallarmé and his circle, plays an important role. 21 Coming out of the pressures of modernity in the late nineteenth century on the art world, British Symbolism's central concern was the search for a representation which would access those aspects of experience and reality which the dominant materialism of industrial capitalism was felt to elide or cover over. Its most urgent desires were those which aim to strip away the veil of matter and to suggest or evoke something of greater truth beneath.

This did not mean that such art abandoned an interest in modern experience. Far from it. Whistler's nocturnes have been cited as "central statements of the Symbolist position" because of their artificiality and frank distance from reality. 22 A painting like Nocturne: Blue and Silver--Chelsea (1871, Tate Gallery) elides the contemporary elements of the scene in favor of a painterly self-sufficiency and the evocation of what Whistler called "poetry" and "palaces in the night." 23 Nonetheless Blue and Silver remains a contemporary scene, recognizable as a view across the Thames towards Chelsea. The motif it depicts is an industrial, quintessentially modern one. 24 In a parallel way, the work of G. F. Watts exemplifies modes of Victorian painting that repudiate the claims of Realism, yet he conceived it as offering a direct relation to contemporary experience. In Mammon: Dedicated to his Worshippers (1884-1885, Tate Gallery), Watts depicts modern society's self-destructive embrace of idea that "prosperity has become our real god." 25 His re of Death pressing remorselessly across the threshold of a Victorian household in Love and Death (c. 1874-1877, Whitworth Art Gallery, Manchester) likewise aims to bring questions about the meaning of existence forcefully into Watts's contemporary world.

These paintings derive from a concern with the character of modern experience, but deal with it by attempting to find principles through which a more authentic meaning, a deeper truth, can be brought to bear. Symbolism seeks a representation which will access those aspects of existence and reality which it believes the dominant materialism of its culture covers over. Modernity's surface, an account of its appearance and optical character, is repudiated in favor of the 'beauty' which may be found within it. But this act of revelation remains a way of responding to the contemporary: Watts's symbols of terror and sterility and Whistler's transformation of the London scene are equally ways of attributing significance to the contemporary themes and sites from which they begin. Symbolist representation in this sense is committed to an analysis and understanding of modern experience, but distrusts a surface account of the visual world as a means of achieving it. Symbolism proposes a deeper investigation and revelation of the conditions of experience than the surface can provide.

4

The advent of an art explicitly concerned with contemporary life and subjects in Britain is in part the result of a shift from this Symbolist ambition to make sense of the contemporary world by uncovering abstract or generalized truths which can substitute for it to an ambition to comprehend the modern on its own terms. In the history of that ambition artists like Sickert were also the heirs to a mode of painting opposed to Symbolism, one which attempted to transcribe and understand the character of modern life precisely by cultivating an art of the surface. For, by the end of the century, not only was there a wing of British painting composed of Aesthetes, Decadents, Pre-Raphaelites and others--artists concerned with the inherent or essential meaning of experience and hence loosely 'Symbolist'--but there was also a broad spectrum of 'Realist' painters whose aim it was to describe the appearance of the world in their art, whether as plein-air painters, Impressionists or naturalists. Whereas Symbolist practice understands the character of modern life by searching for its essential 'beauty' beneath the surface, Realist practice in all its variety pins down experience by replaying its visual, often its optical, appearance rather than by attempting to conjure its essence. In other words, Realist attention is directed towards an account of the forms and surfaces of the world, painting modern life through the kind of description the analysis of appearance could offer. 26

Both Realists and Symbolists are divisible into a multitude of sub-groups, and many artists have characteristics that belong to apparently opposing camps, so that these options are in fact best understood as points on a continuous range of possibilities. Individual artists frequently had connections to both ends of the spectrum, and many produced work which is best understood as bringing together apparently opposed ambitions and strategies. Both were of crucial importance for the development of Sickert's account of modern experience in his art. 27 Leading 'London Impressionists' exhibitors in 1889 like Philip Wilson Steer accordingly show a pattern of movement between the recording of surface and this delving for universal truth. Girls Running, Walberswick Pier (c. 1889-1894, Tate Gallery) or A Summer's Evening (c. 1887-1888, private collection) combine in their fabric the dual inheritance of Symbolism and Realism. A Summer's Evening yokes an overt reference to the universalized Symbolism of Puvis de Chavannes to experimental paint marks which push forward the plein-air and recording ambitions of much English Impressionism. 28 Steer's most interesting works are hybrid, combining the ambitions of Impressionism to transcribe and register the surface realities of the world with the Symbolists' aspirations to universalized truths to produce an art which aims to uncover and reveal the realities of modern experience.

Like Steer, Sickert's explicit interest in revealing modernity's character by uncovering the surface to lay bare the essential realities, was deeply indebted to the ambitions of Symbolism to uncover the hidden truths of experience. But, also like Steer, his attention to the surface character of that life as a sign of its authenticity and nature were indebted to Realism. The apparent ambiguity in Sickert's artistic loyalties was the occasion of a great deal of comment in the art press. 29 Sickert's critics see in his work an example of modernity in English painting because they recognize a very modern kind of puzzle, a great technical ability and sophistication put at the service of what seems to them to be unequivocally sordid and ugly subject matter. On the one hand, Sickert is for his critics the proponent of an "extremist Realism," a recorder of the surface of reality, whose "eye is a purely mechanical contrivance for conveying images" which "seems to strip his subjects of all the[ir] aura." 30 On the other hand, he is a painter who "like Mallarmé . . . gives you a fragment here and a fragment there, enough to prove the reality of his vision, but not enough to render it visible to the world," or who shows "how . . . a subject may be beautified by the touch of mystery that results from a dextrous suggestion of the unseen." 31 These critics understand that Sickert has a visual project, sustained over a number of years and a great many works, but they can only understand the content of his paintings as evidence of a perplexing desire to be 'modern' at all costs. 32 These uncertainties are, I think, about the ways in which continental influences coming into Britain are to be understood as affecting art practice via their intersection with domestic traditions rather than as if they were straightforwardly transferrable as defining concepts from Paris to London. Symbolism and Realism were for Sickert vital players and signs in the ambition to paint the modern world he encountered. But their roles within that arena were ones defined by the complexities of the indigenous histories which absorbed French and other continental influences and remade them into forms which reflected domestic concerns.

In Sickert's case, this dual investment in the aims of Symbolism and Realism gives rise to a further possibility. Sickert understood the Symbolist painter and the Realist painter as sharing a similar goal: both attempt to understand the world through the agency of the visual, though they each interpret the character of this world in different ways. Ideas of the visual seemed to underlie both the optical realism of Impressionist practices and the investigation of hidden meaning which would support the project of Symbolist artists. 33 The visual is imagined as describing both the world as surface, as appearance, as what optically registers as its characteristics and organization; and the visionary, the province of the seers and prophets so beloved of Symbolism: that which lies beneath the surface and in which reality inheres. The visual bounces back from the surface, and it penetrates into the depths. Contemporaries understood the visual as recording the reality of experience because it registers what is really there; and as recording the reality of experience because seeing beyond what appears on the surface into the depths of meaning.

The surface thus became a site of contestation between the Symbolist and Realist modes of representation. The visual seemed to treat the surface as the only authentic account of the world, but also contends that perceptions beneath the surface were what counted in the representation of reality. The possibility accordingly arose for these artists that paint, the substance of the visual itself, could accurately register surface reality or uncover the depths of meaning in a revelatory transcription of essence onto the surface of the canvas. At a moment when the authority of language to register the world was increasingly in doubt, the promise of an unmediated access to reality through the visual media of paint and its graphic analogues became truly attractive. 34 But to say this is already to raise the principal difficulty, for paint is unquestionably material, a densely physical medium which inserts its own creation of the world into the ideal space of an unmediated presentation of reality.

This nature of paint allows it to match the opposition of Symbolism and Realism, depth and surface, with a third set of terms and to act, for Sickert, as a critical tool in opening up the implications of the modes of representation promulgated in Symbolism and Realism. Sickert does this by problematizing the signs which paint offers as a route to comprehension of the world. On the one hand, there is a neat sequence of connections to be posited which runs from the real via the visible forms and surfaces of the world to the surface of the painting which embodies the visual transcription of those forms and surfaces. On the other, there is the surface of the painting itself, a surface which intrudes into the fantasy of an unmediated transcription of reality, a blatantly fictional skin of paint or cluster of marks which constantly threatens to reduce itself to its own materiality and consequently to yield up its presumptive rights to be the world which it describes. The interplay between fictionality and reality which the painted surface enacts allows the contest between a form of understanding which wishes to see truth in the visible forms and surfaces of the world and one which wishes to see truth only in what is invisible, beneath or behind those forms and surfaces, to be articulated and explored within the physical and material constitution of paintings themselves. 35

Themes of the visual--looking, transcription, the exigencies of the medium of paint or some other graphic process--are all important in Sickert's art because they strive to articulate these issues within the works. 36 The visual seems to Sickert to be both the necessary medium of understanding and one which, by the very fact of its materiality, is compromised because it carries inherent difficulties of analysis within it. This gives rise to a critical project within Sickert's oeuvre, one which deploys the diagnostic possibilities of the visual to understand and determine the realities of contemporary experience while self-reflexively attempting to understand the limitations and range of those possibilities. These preoccupations are discernable in the thematization of the visual and the act of looking which occurs in Sickert's music hall works. It is not just "poetry and magic" which are derived in these works, the deep meaning of the modern, but also the adequacy of the visual as a tool for uncovering that meaning. In effect Sickert's use of the "grossly material" medium of paint tests the adequacy of painting as an investigative instrument for the apprehension of the realities of modern experience.

5

The music hall paintings use the scenes of contemporary entertainment in the halls to stand for the character and meaning of the modern. Sickert seems to have felt that the authenticity and reality of modern experience that he wished to reveal to his audience could best be suggested in the rough and tumble music halls that were mainly located in outlying London districts. 37 Sickert sets the dialectic between essence and surface in motion by drawing attention to the theatricality and the falsity of the stage as a surface in his depiction of his music hall scenes. The ramifying visual complexities and nested images of paintings like Little Dot Hetherington at the Bedford Music Hall ( 1) or Vesta Victoria at the Old Bedford ( 2), are Sickert's meditations on this [Begin Page 296] dialectic between the representation of the material surface of experience and what lies beneath them. In Vesta Victoria and Little Dot Hetherington, space is misleading: what we initially take to be the stage is revealed to be a reflection, and the direction of the singer's gestures have to be painfully reconstructed from reversed information. 38 The first experience of encountering an image like Vesta Victoria is often puzzling. What is being depicted here? And what can we make of the relationships between its parts? The eye persistently attempts to read the left-hand side of the picture as a hollow space that allows us to see into the hemisphere of the box. But the presence of the audience in the gallery above therefore becomes inexplicable, a problem compounded by Sickert's abstraction and flattening of the vertical divider between the audience and the stage which here seems flush with the picture plane and parallel to it. Lower down, Sickert offsets the increasing clarity of this vertical element by juxtaposing it with an incoherent passage composed of fields of near-formless color. It is only when we reach the very bottom of the canvas that the vertical element fully crystallizes into the beaded frame of the mirror, at which point the spatial complexities of the painting at last become clear. In short, Sickert contrasts represented space with the flatness of the picture surface in order to confuse our perceptions of the events he depicts.

Recession, too, challenges our understanding. In The Sisters Lloyd (c. 1888-89, Government Art Collection), or Little Dot, our perception of the backdrop has to be laboriously wrestled clear of the perspective space. We are reminded that we cannot trust the surfaces of appearance because of the inherent theatricality, and hence unreality, of the performance depicted. Sickert also uses mirrors, backdrops and other theatrical devices to draw our attention to the instability of representation and appearance. 39 The P. S. Wings in the O. P. Mirror shows the performer in the off-prompt mirror reflected behind the watching audience, whose fascinated attention to the stage therefore appears to us at first misdirected ( 3). We see the act of vision, of looking, of surrendered self-consciousness, as well as the performance which attracts it, but now the two are disjunctive and ill-matched. Appearance, analysis and display, the movements of the surface and of its meaning, come to us through Sickert's painting, confused and in an alienated relationship to each other. It is as if the lines of relationship which connect appearance with the meaning of events are disrupted and difficult to access. Surface is revealed as inadequate and deceptive. "Poetry and magic" arise from the gap between the surface appearance, the act of looking, and the reality of what is seen, as they are imaged in the commercial halls where the essence of modern experience is performed through the surface of entertainment.

This might be regarded as stage one of Sickert's argument in paint. In paintings such as Gatti's Hungerford Palace of Varieties: Second Turn of Katie Lawrence, Sickert advances his argument further through the extended use of facture and depicted space to problematize visual meaning ( 4). 40 In Gatti's, Katie Lawrence is standing full square in front of a backdrop curtain which has an elaborate architectural space painted on it. 41 There is none of the overt spatial obscurity which dominates Vesta Victoria. Having set himself the challenge that this poses, Sickert manipulates perception and [Begin Page 298] space, surface and depth by exploiting the ambiguities that the backdrop curtain suggests. 42 As in Little Dot and The Sisters Lloyd, the tromp l'oeil effect of the backdrop confuses us. It is difficult to decide whether what we are looking at is intended to represent real or painted depth. The backdrop is depicted in such a way that the areas between the columns at the 'sides' are to be imagined as mirrors. 43 On the right, the distinction between the backdrop and the 'real' space is defined by the curtain (although this is only a darker maroon compared to the lighter maroon, plum and dark pink dominant in much of the backdrop, a similarity which itself suggests more confusion or blending of real and fictional spaces, perhaps even a confusion of identity). Although the pelmet of the curtain is visible across the whole of the top of the picture, it is much harder to make out what is happening on the left hand side. The backdrop appears to extend beyond the edge of the canvas, but this does not make visual sense as it becomes vertiginously difficult to disentangle real from fictional space. The thin strip of light color at the bottom of the backdrop marking the gap between it and the floor does not extend beyond the hats of the audience at the far left, so the final section lacks this key marker of difference. Immediately above the space on the left where this gap would be is a strictly illegible patch of paint in light and dark tan colors which has been simply scrubbed onto the surface of the canvas. This patch is strategically placed to allow the design of the backdrop to appear to be real space and to emphasize the effect of the perspective recession painted onto it. With this arrangement, it is almost impossible to read this left hand section of the backdrop as flat. Only the hint of the swag of the curtain at top left allows us, if we notice it, to key in the proper relationships of depth and reality. At the far right, above the round plate-shaped hat of the watching woman, there is another similar area of illegibility. Although it doesn't have the same deliberately disturbing effect as that on the left, this area also disrupts expectations of clearly differentiated space.

Sickert also used specialized painting techniques to extend this confusion of reality and fictionality into other areas of the canvas. A thin and watery application of yellow paint describing the low railings across the front of the stage functions representatively rather than mimetically. This application of yellow paint changes with the pressure of the hand and the fullness of the brush. Such is the mark of this medium: it cannot claim a direct transcription of the world but rather asserts its physicality in constructing its account of the world. In a similar spirit, some of the heads at bottom right of the painting are outlined in a heavy black line. Sporadically across the surface (for instance in the woman's hat and hair in the foreground) there are lines of bright red paint which seem at first to be the remnants of lines at right-angles to each other and resemble the painter's squaring up, the network of gridlines used to accurately transfer the information from preparatory drawings to the canvas. Some of the lines are tight and appear to be ruled, others are blotted. This gives a random accent to the surface which provides visual interest, but it is certain that these grid lines were applied after the main stages of painting. 44 They are visible even where the main body of the paint is wet on wet, and it's clear that in some areas the red paint has been brushed dry over dried wet on wet paint. 45 Again, the woman's hat was described using liquid paint, and the thinly drawn red lines could only have been added afterwards. Like the patches of illegibility which paint as a material is allowed to describe at the two sides of the canvas, these dialectical relationships of paint surface and descriptive technique function to draw attention to the character of paint as a medium. 46 Paint in Sickert's music hall scenes is avowedly fictional, a medium of apprehension and understanding rather than a transparent transcription. It is precisely this fictionality, the metaphorical opacity of the medium, which is acknowledged through the literally opaque and declaratively material character of the paint itself. 47

These strategies draw attention to the paradoxes of the visual surfaces of these scenes. A mere description of contemporary life will not do. Sickert embarks upon an Impressionistic investigation that transcribes the surface appearances of modern life, but he doesn't stop there. The music hall becomes what W. J. T. Mitchell has called a "hypericon," or an image that seeks to investigate "the act of picturing" or "imagine the activity of imagination, re the practice of ration." 48 The music hall serves as a workshop for Sickert's investigation of how painting renders meaning in relation to the desire for knowledge about the world. This investigation questions the access to reality which paint affords. By exploiting the paradoxes and ambiguities clustered within representations of the 'sordid' music halls of London, Sickert questions the feasibility of communicating a deep understanding of modernity in paint. The visual surface of the painted canvas is the tool he uses for this enquiry.

This relationship to the visual also accounts for the interest so many of Sickert's music hall paintings and drawings take in the audience. The various versions of The Gallery of the Old Bedford ( 5) or the 1906-1909 series--which includes Noctes Ambrosianae, The Old Middlesex (c. 1906-1907, The Beaverbrook Art Gallery, Fredericton, New Brunswick), with its bird's-eye view down onto the spectators in the stalls, and the Gaiété Montparnasse (c. 1907, private collection), which takes the opposite viewpoint, sharply upwards from the stalls into the gallery--all dwell on the watching audience. In these works the loss of the self in the act of spectatorship signals a direct involvement with the world, the absence of mediation which Realism claimed for itself. The rapture of the audience as they watch, craning, leaning and gawping down, suggests a complete self-identification and involvement of the spectator with the spectacle via the transparent medium of transcription. But the observation of the audience in these works also allows Sickert to question and explore the adequacy of that account of visuality.

In one of the climatic paintings of the Camden Town period, The Studio: Painting of a Nude, Sickert foregrounds visuality as a thematic concern ( 6). 49 The Studio places the nude re which serves as the center of attention in the Camden Town series into a more complex spatial setting and introduces the explicit re of the painter observing his model in a mirror. 50 The real and implied multiplication of images of a passive nude re suggest that she functions solely as an object of observation. The important action is the painter's translation of the nude body into the medium of paint. Likewise, the rapture of spectatorship, the loss and dissolution of the self in the parade of tawdry action on the Music Hall stage can be brought back into the realm of comprehension by the pressure of the analysis the painter brings to bear upon it. It is the painter's vision which renders the nude and the audience, the music hall and its performers, meaningful and exemplary of modernity and reality. The music hall audience, like the nude, is itself the object of observation in the painter's vision. The meaning of these events seems to be that they are observed and therefore brought into the context of knowledge. In both types of subject matter Sickert's painter's eye can assess, discriminate, classify and analyze the shifting social practices of modernity.

The audience of rapt watchers stands for a complete identification of the spectator with the thing observed the imagined abolition of the medium of representation. But it is also the opportunity for Sickert to create a conceptual laboratory which will allowed him to investigate that situation and hence to assess the position of the painter as well as of paint. The painter sees not only the world but also those who look at it; he sees not only reality but its watchers, too. This serves to assert Sickert's claims for the authority of the painter as the finest example of the visual and its power, the purest vision, and the purest instance of visuality at work. But the fictionality of the music hall performance reminds us that the revelations offered to us by the visual (and hence the painter) are in fact inadequate. Sickert's play with mirrors and his insistence on the contested nature of visual knowledge in painting increase this effect and draw our attention to it. Thus the audience is the image of the direct engagement with the world that visuality claims it can deliver, but the location of this example is within circumstances which are clearly fictional. The audience's rapture is provoked not by immediate reality but by theatrical performance, a fiction which deceives them and their fascinated observation. Depth frustrates surface here. The artist's observation of the self-deception of the audience gives Sickert the space he needs to consider the limitations of the claims of the visual. The artist as observer still retains some authority, but the presence of the audience implies a critique of visuality that suggests that the artist, too, is compromised.

The drawing Sickert made of the drop curtain at the Gaiété, Montparnasse sometime between 1906-1907 displays this compromise in a summary form ( 7). The curtain is, first of all, a surface; its smooth fall obscures the arrangement or rearrangement of objects and events behind it. The curtain here represents the flat surface of the canvas or paper. Like those surfaces, it is marked and scored with a multiplicity of visual and verbal signs. These signs are both representational in the sense that they function as drawings and icons of the advertisements and conventional in the sense that they are linear elements which divide the screen into boxes and hence into discrete commercial spaces. But the marks that describe the curtain and the signs for which it is the material vehicle are identical to those that describe the surrounding spaces in the drawing. This suggests that the semiotics of modernity implied in this re of the curtain cannot be separated from the social space where these meanings are realized. Despite this paradox, Sickert's attention is firmly focused on the curtain itself and the messages it flashes out at the audience beneath it.

Here, Sickert thematizes the surface as a subject in its own right. In Camden Town period paintings such as The Studio: Painting of a Nude, the surface represents the imposition of meaning on the world by the controlling mark of the painter's hand. Even the nugatory or trivial can be become meaningful once a particular quality of attention is paid to it by the central self of the painter who wields the brush or pencil as an instrument of knowledge about and definition of the world. Sickert used painting techniques to draw our attention to the overt presence of the materiality of the paintwork such that the surface itself functions as a sign of his authorial knowledge. The surface is the site where that process of definition takes place. And here we have a surface on which particular sorts of modern meaning are frankly declared. What is thematized is therefore the process or fact of surface as meaning. The sort of meaning it offers is, of course, another matter. The modernity of the advertisements makes them also the subject of Sickert's attention. They are what he understands and renders comprehensible through the practice of his art. By the process of drawing them, the advertisements become known, are brought into the orbit of comprehension, but they also become that process. Their superficiality (in the literal sense), flatness, reduction of the signs of meaning to a few gestural formulations, suggests the problems attendant on knowing the world through representation. What is happening here is that Sickert's own practice, his own use of surface and materiality to render modern life in a form of knowledge, is seen as inadequate, as a reduction of the fullness of the world into a flattened surface, into representation. What presents itself as informational, as knowledge or as reportage about the world is revealed as discourse, as invested or interested. The view of reality which the surface encapsulates and promotes is a partial rather than a full account. It is compromised and bound into the systems it seeks to explicate.

6

I have taken a rather different view of Sickert's music hall scenes than that one might derive from a reading dependent on either the formalist or the social-historical readings of modernism. In particular, I have argued that Sickert's work at this period resists the charge one might muster from a reading of Clark via Eliot. This work, in fact, is precisely about the representation of English bourgeois modernity which Eliot notes as the central issue in his discussion of Marie Lloyd. Clark sees French depictions of the cafés-concerts as involved in the lack of 'an imagery' adequate to the representation of middle-class experience. Sickert's work depends on a similar problematic absence of representation and the concomitant need to bring it into existence to clarify and describe the experience of modernity. But if Sickert attempts to make use of the forms of working-class entertainment to image and assuage a middle-class homelessness in modernity, then he also subjects that project to critical attention. In undertaking this critical project, Sickert makes use of paint, the visual itself as the principal means of inquiry and criticism. I am not suggesting that this is better or worse than the monuments of French modernism, simply different. In other words, if we want to progress in the ambition to revise our understanding of modernities outside the French experience then we need to tackle them from within.
