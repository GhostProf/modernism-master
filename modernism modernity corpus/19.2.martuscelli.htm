Between Modernism and Surrealism: Dada in Portugal

Tania Martuscelli

In his anthology Textos de afirmação e de combate do movimento surrealista mundial (loosely translated: “Texts of affirmation and opposition of the worldwide surrealist movement”), Mário Cesariny, perhaps Portugal’s most acclaimed surrealist artist, sets out to do just what his title suggests: to reconstruct the history of the surrealist movement as it manifests itself around the world. Although Cesariny confined himself in his subtitle to the years 1924–1976, he actually begins his anthology with a series of dadaist texts dated from the early 1910s, when the movement began. He starts with an excerpt of Tzara’s Seven Dada Manifestos (1916), an excerpt of Hugo Ball’s Fight Out of Time (1916) followed by his Phonetic Poems (1917), and includes an excerpt of Marcel Duchamp’s Ninety-three Documents from 1911 to 1915. Next, Erick Satie’s Venomous Obstacles (1914) makes an appearance, followed by poems by Pierre Albert-Birot (1916), letters from Jacques Vaché to Breton and Aragon (from 1917 to 1918), and Picabia’s excerpt of the periodical 391, among other texts and authors, until dada reaches its zenith—or, its culmination, as it were—with Breton’s Drop Everything (1922), and a final excerpt of Benjamin Peret’s Les parasites voyagent (1923).

Cesariny argues for a strong connection between dada and surrealism. In his preface, he calls attention to the “massive adherence of the dadaists to the new movement,”1 and stresses that, “None of the great writers, of the great painters who came from dada to surrealism (such as Ernst, Arp, Tzara, Péret, Man Ray, Duchamp) changes a line, painted or written, to the anti-speech speech that was being declared since the emergence of dada in Zurich, in 1916” (11).2

It is clear that the Portuguese poet is not alone when he includes the dadaist experience as an integral part of surrealism, whether in its affirmation or its negation of the movement. Marcel Raymond, for instance, goes beyond this assertion in From Baudelaire to Surrealism, where he weaves together the many different -isms to create a comprehensive history detailing the interconnectedness of these movements in France since the nineteenth century. In fact, when analyzing Portuguese literature, it is common to see dada’s presence in both symbolist and surrealist-influenced art, making surrealism the ultimate dada experience. In another affirmation, dating from 279 1966, Cesariny proclaimed that “dada was a baptism, a late one, after the child’s birth,”3 arguing that “In the cases of Arp, Schwitters, Man Ray, Richter, and many others, we do not see a contradiction between their triumphant dada periods and the works that they did afterwards, whether these took on a surrealist bent or not” (109).4

The first Portuguese avant-garde movement, modernismo, also referred to as the “first modernism,” was born of the hands of Fernando Pessoa, Mário de Sá-Carneiro, and José de Almada Negreiros in 1915. While there were certainly others responsible for the emergence of modernismo, these three men remain the most recognized and studied artists to have partaken in it. Together, they published two volumes of the periodical Orpheu, and also put together a third volume that was not published at the time because Mário de Sá-Carneiro’s father refused to continue funding the project. Yet this short-lived avant-garde periodical, edited mainly by young artists and surviving for only one year, left an indelible mark on the history of Portuguese literature. It caused “a public reaction of refusal and insult,” as Fernando Cabral Martins states in the foreword of a 1989 facsimile of Orpheu, which included a draft of the publication’s third volume.5 This “public reaction” would become the essence, or even the trademark of avant-garde literature’s reception at the beginning of the twentieth century.

Still according to Cabral Martins, in 1965, José de Almada Negreiros published Orpheu 1915–1965, underlining the fact that the periodical was the first Portuguese “encounter of poetry with painting.”6 It is, indeed, the encounter of images and words resulting in that “public reaction of refusal and insult” that is often associated with dada. On the other hand, some critics try to avoid the assumption of a “dadaism avant la lettre,” or, more commonly used, a “surrealism avant la lettre.” Due to the fact that Orpheu was published in 1915, critics usually cite both Apolinaire’s Calligrammes and Mallarmé’s A Throw of the Dice as an influence.

Not unlike the dada artists who morphed into surrealists, most of the poets and painters who collaborated in Orpheu organized a conference in 1917 where they introduced futurism to Portugal. The manifests, poems and paintings presented at this conference were published in one volume under the title Portugal Futurista. The writings of Mário de Sá-Carneiro, who had committed suicide in 1916, were presented at the controversial conference, and included in its subsequent publication, which survived only a few days before being censored. Sá-Carneiro had been living in Paris, and he was certainly one of the fathers of Futurism in Portugal.

Interestingly, however, while Futurism was an umbrella movement for several other Portuguese –isms, it was the one that would align its artists with a larger European movement most effectively. The Portuguese critic Carlos D’Alge notes that Marinetti’s suggestions “were not as decisive to the Orpheu generation, and they would rather turn to Fernando Pessoa’s theory about ‘sensationism’, as they previously did to his ‘paulism’ and ‘intersectionism.’”7 One could say that Fernando Pessoa’s “paulism” is as comparable to a European decadentism, as his “intersectionism” is comparable to cubism, and his “sensationism” to Futurism, but, as always, uniquely adapted to the Portuguese scene. Vítor Aguiar e Silva emphasizes that Pessoa was interested in

280 developing and expressing the aesthetics of a Portuguese modernity that would have an international dimension and resonance, a goal underscored by the fact that he persistently wrote texts in English in order to spread the sensationist movement. Aguiar e Silva goes even further, stating that Pessoa only accepted the comparison between Marinetti’s Futurism and his intersectionism to draw the attention of Frank Palmer, a British publisher, to Orpheu. Pessoa’s proposal to Mr. Palmer was to translate the Portuguese periodical into English. In his own words: “. . . I am sending, registered, a number of [the] review, Orpheu, of which I am part-editor. It is a review of all kinds of advanced literature, from a quasi-Futurism to what we here call intersectionism.”8 In fact, those Portuguese-specific -isms—which did not necessarily lack in European influence—were adopted by this generation of artists. It is important to note that, during the 1910s, the Portuguese political and historic situation was certainly not at its best. While the monarchy had come to an end, the young republican regime nonetheless found itself struggling to hold its ground against those who wanted the kingdom back, and others who favored a military coup d’état, which would eventually take place in 1926. The influences of the international political and ideological crisis effectively resulted in a tug-of-war between divergent trends in Portuguese literature. When the Orpheu group decided to adopt Italian Futurism, this idea was accepted by Fernando Pessoa, but with some restrictions. Interestingly enough, only his heteronym, Álvaro de Campos, adopted Marinetti’s ideas. Aguiar e Silva quotes a letter that Pessoa sent to a friend, explaining that Sá-Carneiro was the one truly excited about the Italian movement, while he himself was a Futurist in solely the sense of “leaving everything for tomorrow” (22).9 The Portuguese critic also points out the fact that in Portugal Futurista, Fernando Pessoa, writing as himself, publishes “poems in which not only the style but also the theme are scandalously anti-Futurist”(24).10 Cesariny states in As Mãos na Água, a Cabeça no Mar that “if the dada movement had been anticipated a few years before its birth in Zurich a much bigger and much longer-lasting favor would have been done to the poets and painters of ‘Orpheu’ than the Futurism ever offered” (262).11 He was certainly referring to the fact that, as with dada, the poets of Orpheu were the first to introduce to Portugal a synthesis of poetry and painting, the idea of contradiction, confusion (in the body of the poem, and in its format and theme), as well as the idea of controversy. As Tzara suggests: “By its very nature dadaism expresses confusion, proclaims links between opposites, negates the principle of non-contradiction, assuring us that yes=no.”12 Despite not being a Futurist himself, Fernando Pessoa published in Portugal Futurista his “Interlude Fictions” (Ficções do Interlúdio), comprised of five poems, one of them titled “Saudade Dada” (“Dada nostalgia”); partially translated by Francisco Cota Fagundes as “Longing”:

During still blond, beautiful times Clorindas and Belindas, tenderly Play the game of forfeits. Those who came to see from the porch From where they hear them coming and laughing Stare at the cold space

“And there are misty disenchantments of the enchantment of the thoughts In the sluggish saints of the recesses of the saintly corners of the convents . . . Penitents in tears, lingering, legion Who enchant the attentive winds.”13

Francisco Cota Fagundes and Aguiar e Silva would both agree that Pessoa’s excerpt embodies something closer to a symbolism congruent with Verlaine’s “Song of Autumn,” than actual dadaism. According to Fagundes, this poem traces a lineage to Verlaine and symbolism because of Pessoa’s attempt to “[imitate] the acoustic quality of music” through “alliteration, assonance, rhyme, as well as other devices of sound repetition” (135). As Verlaine emphasizes in his well-known Art Poétique, music comes first among the arts. Pessoa’s poem is endowed with a musical quality through a remarkable rhythm formed by both alliteration and free verse. Words are not arranged so as to shape coherent ideas so much as to form a melodic framework for the poem. In terms of poetic sensation, the melancholy and mysterious ambience staged by his unusual metaphors lead the reader to think more of symbolism than dadaist experimentalism.

It is also worth noting that symbolism plays an essential role in Portuguese modernismo, due to the fact that some modernists cultivated a kind of adoration for symbolist poets. In addition to emulating them in their own poems, the modernists also published texts by symbolist authors in their journals. According to Fernando Guimarães, Pessoa’s “Saudade Dada,” more than an inspiration, is an echo (or pastiche) of a Portuguese symbolist poem by Eugénio de Castro, from Oaristos, that begins with “Na messe, que enlourece, estremece a quermesse,” (loosely translated as “The carnival shakes the harvest that turns blond”) (805).14 The sound repetition, more than the theme of the poem itself is what “echoes” in “Saudade Dada.” This technique is not only in tune with Verlaine’s Art Poétique, but also brings to mind the dada technique of sound-poem. The poem’s title shows that Pessoa was in tune with the European vanguard movements, though not exactly affiliated to them—resulting in a poem supposedly inspired by dada (in that the poet is longing for dada), but clearly influenced by symbolism.

Eduardo Prado Coelho draws a comparison between the “first” and “second” stages of modernismo in Portugal (Orpheu and Presença) in relation to dadaism and surrealism, demonstrating that, despite the fact that the first and second modernismos were contemporary to dada and Breton’s manifestos, respectively, both dadaism and surrealism were more evident in Orpheu than in Presença. Orpheu (1915) was certainly more controversial due to its confrontational aspect, and surprised the public by launching the first vanguard movement in Portugal. Presença appeared more than a decade later, in 1927, announcing its support of a literatura viva, or a “living literature”—one 283 characterized by a more intuitive writing, free from academic standards. Presença served as a vehicle for several literary trends in Portugal. While at times these trends were in complete opposition to one another, the journal maintained an independent and neutral position. In its pages, one could find Futurist texts by Almada Negreiros side by side with neo-realist poems. The first editor of the journal, José Régio, who argued for the concept of literatura viva, for instance, was a Catholic writer. Prado Coelho notes, however, that

Orpheu contested art, while at the same time making use of it, following the nihilist model of some new aesthetic trends. Not even the “delay” of Presença can be measured by the fact that Régio questions the “enchantment by the rare and by the unforeseen” as an automatic guarantee of a healthy modernity . . . even Breton, by cutting relations with Tzara and dadaism, rejected all the vanguard that was limited to the pure exploitation of novelty and the free destruction of art. However, while Presença developed from the consolidation of literary strongholds, surrealism was transporting art to its exterior, following modernity specifically, and this is what Orpheu most evidently proclaimed among us.15

The official launch of a Portuguese surrealist movement, including what would become two divergent surrealist groups in Lisbon, took place in art exhibits and conferences in 1947 and 1948. Bearing this in mind, it is curious to note that when Mário Cesariny organized his anthology of worldwide surrealist movements, he considered both dadaism and surrealism to have had, to a certain extent, an imminent presence in Portugal from 1916 (or 1924) until 1976, the year when he finished his book, though not necessarily the year the movements “died.” The organizer of the anthology goes against the argument common among critics, which expounds the “impossibility of the vanguards,” as a given during the post-modern, or post-war era. Perfecto Cuadrado quotes Renato Poggioli, author of The Theory of the Avant-Garde, stating that around the 1960s the concepts of movement (as a collective and dynamic project) and of vanguard (as a revolutionary act) were old-fashioned. According to the Spanish critic, what existed or what was said to exist was the “comfortable negotiation with their inexhaustible plunders.”16

Dada, nonetheless, proved to be alive, or perhaps, more accurately, reborn, among the Portuguese artists. In 1935, António Pedro co-signed the Dimensionist Manifesto in Paris, together with Harp, Duchamp, Kandinski, Miró, Picabia, Delaunay, and others. If, on the one hand, these artists confirmed that Futurism and cubism are the “unconscious origins”17 of the movement in their manifesto, on the other hand, Dutra Faria writes that “Futurism, cubism, dadaism, surrealism—were four rivers; four rivers that were torrentially flowing, roaring and destroying . . . Dimensionism is an encounter of those rivers. Put together, the waters rose, and they calmed down.”18

In the 1960s, a post-surrealist, post-war, experimentalist movement, known as Poesia 61 (Poetry 1961), emphasized the influence of different “roots,” or -isms, if you will, namely to praise the renewal of traditional Portuguese poetry together with surrealism, and to keep a tentative distance between the concepts and models of the

284 poet Fernando Pessoa and his heteronyms. Dada, or neo-dada, if we aim to consider the international scene, also formed part of this group’s inspiration. Take, for example, “Silly Sonnet I” (“Soneto Pateta I”), by Salette Tavares, which she considered to be “a dada manifesto”:19

Who crowned the word with a bugle horn who put the tempest in the pen who said poet?

A handbell tear

makes of the grayish female donkey this violet here.20

This poem by Tavares, much like Pessoa’s poem “Longing” (for dada), brings to light the intimate relationship between word and sound through its use of alliteration. At this moment in time (the early 1960s) these characteristics, once tied to Verlaine and symbolism, are more closely associated with Brazilian concrete poetry. Another observation ought to be made here: it is the use of sound repetition (or “sound-poem”), and the ultimate abstraction with an imaginative and unconventional word association, that recalls dada the most. These are the things that characterize dada in Portuguese poetry. It was only in the late 1940s and early 1950s, with the establishment of the Portuguese surrealist movement, that artists frequently adopted dada’s often-quoted formula for poetry building: the “cut-up”, in which the artist was encouraged to cut out words from a newspaper, shake them inside a bag, and place them on the paper at random.

Later in 1972, the Portuguese newspaper, A Capital, became the very first media outlet to publish a translation of the manifest Dada Excites Everything, dating from 1921, and Raul Hausmann’s “Pamphlet Against the Weimar View of Life” from 1919, in its literary pages. In 1977, Mário Cesariny published his anthology of surrealist movements around the world starting his work with a series of dadist texts. At the same time that he provided “closure” for the vanguard movements by painting a picture that balanced what they were with what they were not, he also opened several doors to discussion of the influence and immanence of the movement throughout the world in the late 1970s. We should remember that it all began with dada.21
