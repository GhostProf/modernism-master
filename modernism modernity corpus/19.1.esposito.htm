In “the shadow of the winged machine...”: The Esposizione dell’aeronautica Italiana and the Ascension of Myth in the Slipstream of Modernity

Fernando Esposito

In June 1934, in the year XII of the fascist era, the Milanese Palazzo dell’Arte reopened its halls to the public. Until January, the palazzo housed the Esposizione dell’aeronautica Italiana (EAI), an exhibition dedicated to

all the contributions of the genius, the heroism and the work of the Italians to the solution of the problems of flight, to the achievement of memorable deeds in war and peace, and to the construction and uses of a means of transportation, for whom destiny has conceded an ever broadening development.1

According to one of the popular monthly aviation periodicals published by Italo Balbo’s Aeronautical Ministry, L’Ala d’Italia, in approximately six months over one million visitors “had wandered through the rooms of the Palazzo dell’Arte and dwelled in its halls to relive the story of the Italian eagles and the grand poem of the proud human conquest.”2 By order of the Duce, the duration of the exhibition had been extended so that further visitors were able to undertake a “pilgrimage of love” and to admire the marvels brought forth by “the ingeniousness and the capacity of the Italian people” that were displayed on the two exhibition floors of the recently erected structure.

The idea for the exhibition began with Marcello Visconti di Modrone, the Milanese podestà. He entrusted Colonel Francesco Cutry from the historical division of the Aeronautical Ministry, Carlo A. Felice, and the architect Giuseppe Pagano

Some of the most renowned architects and designers of the Italian modernist movement were to contribute to the exhibition.3 On the entrance side, for example, the building’s façade was adorned by a composition designed by the painter Erbeto Carboni: a stylized fascio littorio overshadowed a map of the world flanked by a dynamic swarm of airplanes that “symbolized the primacy of the Italian wing.”4 The curators wanted to overwhelm the visitor right from the start. Once visitors entered the building, a typically resolute-faced Mussolini glared down upon them from the far side of the entrance hall.5 The caption that underlined Mussolini’s–and accordingly all of Italy’s–determination read: “This wing that has resumed its flight won’t be broken again.”

Visitors proceeded into an atrium (2) from which they could already catch a better glimpse of the highlight on the first floor. It was the SVA-airplane of the Squadra di San Marco (15) with which Gabriele D’Annunzio had dropped his cynical leaflet-bombs on the Austro-Hungarian capital of Vienna on August 9th, 1918. The imagery was obvious: the relic lay at the foot of the steps and the ascent that followed led upwards, not only to the second floor of the exhibition, but to the regenerated Italy that fascism had brought forth.

Mussolini’s Aviatik-airplane (15/2) was at the top of the steps. He had crashed it at Arcore—what subtle irony of history—in early March 1921 while practicing for his flight license. What followed was a tale of successes, astonishing feats, the fulfillment of a glorious destiny. The second floor exhibited a wide range of achievements from the fascist reign. It encompassed the airports that had been built (16), the study of aerodynamics (17) and meteorology (21), civil aviation and air mail (28), and the Italian records in speed and flown distances (19 and 26), as well as mass flights (20). Of these, the Crociera aerea del Decennale (22), Italo Balbo and his “winged century’s” mass flight from Orbetello to Chicago, was displayed most prominently.6

Like other fascist exhibitions, the EAI was designed to convey the spirit of fascism 141 and the fascist order to Italian society.7 An exhibition on aviation was an apt means to this end, as aviation was a popular symbol of modern technological mastery and thus a crowd puller. In particular, aviation symbolized dynamic, upward motion, the new departure that was decisive for fascism’s self-conception and for the image it presented to the masses. In fact, as well as aviation being legible as a metaphor for fascism, the EAI itself can be seen as a paradigm of the fascist order that was “inscribed” into Italian society.8 In the following pages, I will highlight the mythical structure of this order and elucidate its nexus to modernity.

The layout of the exhibition was teleological. While the route was designed as the “path taken by the Italian genius on the difficult climb towards the millenarian human aspiration, the conquest of the skies,” the true telos was the fascist society, or rather gemeinschaft itself.9 On the first floor, visitors were guided through the eventful first decades of flight that were expanded to include significant Italian “precursors” (3) such as Leonardo da Vinci and the inventor Enrico Forlanini (4). The chronologically-arranged tour led past the “first flights” (5), to the hall dedicated to aviation in the Italo-Turkish war of 1911 (6), where Italians were the first to use aviation militarily. After passing

142 through the “sanctuary” constructed for the 14 dead and 12 living pilots that bore the medaglia d’oro (8), visitors entered the “D’Annunzio hall” (9). The word “hall,” sala, seems an overstatement: I am tempted to see the comparatively small amount of space devoted to D’Annunzio as reflecting Mussolini’s jealousy of his former rival. In the turmoil of the post-war years, D’Annunzio—the celebrated poet, irredentist agitator, aerial hero and commandante of the Fiumian adventure10—had seemed the ideal and sole leader capable of unifying the heterogeneous veterans’, syndicalist, futurist, and nationalist movements that lay at the root of nascent fascism.11 D’Annunzio was not only the “first Duce,” as Michael Leeden has called him, he was also the original “primo pilota.”12 Central elements of the fascist aviation narrative originated in his speeches, writings, and deeds.13 With a nod to the British journalist Sisley Huddleston, one could say that the exhibition accurately portrayed him as the “John the Baptist” of fascism.14 D’Annunzio’s major role in the Italian aerial history of the First World War, but also his many contributions to the articulation and mediation of the flight experience itself and to the genesis of the nexus between fascism and aviation, could only be downplayed, not omitted, at the EAI.15 Yet ironically, the “D’Annunzio hall” was a dead end. Visitors had to return to the “sanctuary” and pass through the “Corridor of Arms” (10) to finally enter the “Aviation in the Great War hall” (11). This was designed by the erstwhile futurist Mario Sironi, and it exhibited five planes, including a huge Caproni-bomber and the sorry remains of Italy’s leading ace Francesco Baracca’s fighter plane.16 The following room (13) covered “Aviation and Fascism from 1919 to 1922 and also encompassed the “Fiumian Wing.” The tenor of this part of the exhibition was that the climax of the First World War, in which the heroism of Italian aviators and their spirit of self-sacrifice had led from one victory to another, had been followed by a deep abyss. Because of the impotence of the liberal government and their unwillingness to fight, the Italian wing was broken. There were no “pilots” among the liberals, and only the fascists kept the aviation spirit alive. The catalogue read as follows:

Fascism spreads! It is the expression of the rebellion against the weaknesses and the cowardice of all those that are disposed to all kinds of settlements; [...] expression of the will that the country must be sent underway [...] Mussolini, the creator of this new faith, the leading spirit, condottiere and head harmonizes and channels all the enthusiasm and all the fever, he understands the exasperation of the aviation fanatics, and since he is also a fanatic he is extremely saddened by the state of the Italian Wing; he encourages, spurs, drives on, urges and cautions severely but justly; he is not alone, but becomes the comprehensive expression of the aviation revolt, of the will to rebuild, and he flies, flies, and flies to bear witness to a faith and to inspire the spirit of his followers that are becoming a great phalanx.17

In the years that followed the “abyss,” the topos of Mussolini as pilot would be among the many prominent images that fascist propaganda would spread of the Duce.18 In his book Mussolini aviatore Guido Mattioli clarified the meaning of the aviation metaphor:

Who ventures in aerial navigation faces a new way of educating the spirit. That is why 143 Benito Mussolini took up flying so soon. [...] History’s big games are won with moral and physical courage. He who is fearful doesn’t act, doesn’t bring forth matters of fact, doesn’t create anything. He who is scared doesn’t fly, can’t fly. [...] No machine requires more concentration of the human spirit and of man’s will than the airplane to function perfectly. The pilot really knows what it means to govern and steer. This is the reason for the seemingly necessary and intimate spiritual connection between Fascism and aviation. Every aviator would be a born fascist.19

It was this link between fascism and aviation that the exhibition tried to “engraft” on visitors. The fascists didn’t only want to benefit from the popularity of aviation, of its esteemed image as a “modern wonder.”20 The analogy drawn between fascism and flight was deeper and in itself twofold.

The first floor culminated in the Hall of Icarus–a cylindrical room two stories high. According to the catalogue, it was allegedly the only room in the exhibition with symbolic character.21 A blue spiral that rose to the ceiling stood at its center. The meaning conveyed by the room was unequivocal and was affirmed by one of D’Annunzio’s dictums, which adorned the wall below the Icarus statue:

A limit to man’s powers? No limit to power exists. A limit to courage? No limit to courage exists. A limit to hardship? No limit to hardship exists. I declare that the ne plus ultra is the most outrageous blasphemy [ever pronounced] against God and against man.22

144 The swaggering was as typical for D’Annunzio as the references to antiquity. Ne plus ultra may have been inscribed on the Pillars of Hercules, i.e. at the Strait of Gibraltar that had once marked the boundary of the ancient world and warned travelers not to venture beyond.23 D’Annunzio most probably referred to the 26th canto of Dante’s Inferno, where Ulysses is punished for his Promethean spirit and his will to transgress the edge of the known earth. The message was straightforward: not even the sky was the limit for fascism. Fascism had reawakened the Promethean and Icarian spirit of the Italians and fascist new man strove beyond common boundaries and constantly aimed higher: Excelsior! For his part, D’Annunzio had worked on this version of the Nietzschean Übermensch since his own first flight as a passenger at the airshow in Brescia, in 1909. The central elements and topoi of the bellicose aviatic narrative that would eventually structure the fascist myth of the new man—that the exhibition as well as other popular media translated for a broader tier of society—were already present in his novel Forse che sì, forse che no (January 1910). Together with the topos of plus ultra, the Icarian Übermensch encompassed technological mastery; the fusion of man and machine; the intoxication with and the idolization of speed, dynamism, youth, masculinity; and the corresponding virile and misogynous attributes. Yet the primary characteristic of the new aristocracy of supermen symbolized by the D’Annunzian Icarus was the defiance of death and the will to sacrifice. D’Annunzio—as well as the Futurists—had cultivated these topoi years before the First World War. Yet the context of the war proved decisive for its meaning, as well as for the success of the aviatic narrative. Symbolizing the Nietzschean Excelsior, Icarus embodied the essence of the modern zeitgeist. However, it was his fall that predestined him to become a figure paradigmatic of the nascent fascist palingenetic myth.24 Icarus’s fall was not taken as a consequence of his hubris, but was reinterpreted: Icarus was an essential sacrifice on the “altar of the fatherland,” and his death was a necessary precondition for a new man to be born and a regenerated Italy to arise. Had it not been for the so-called vittoria mutilata, the “mutilated victory,” and for D’Annunzio’s ability to skillfully interweave his vision with existing interpretative patterns, the vision of the Icarian Übermensch would probably have remained confined to small literary and intellectual circles. Yet by interlacing his elitist image of a new man with predominantly religious commonplaces, D’Annunzio theoretically enabled even the illiterate foot soldier to relate to the narrative and make some kind of sense of it. D’Annunzio’s language was permeated with Catholic motifs, and his speeches frequently became prayers, as is evident in the speech in the Corriere della Sera published on the 26th of March, 1918. It was titled The Faith in Italian Aviation, and in fact D’Annunzio had given this “sermon” the day before at a fundraising banquet for the Italian Air Force at the Milanese Scala:

See, the war passes on from one element to another, from the earth and the water it has risen to the skies. Nike flies as in the myth, but not with two wings, rather she flies with thousands and thousands and thousand more wings.

D’Annunzio continues by calling attention to donations made by other cities. These 145 weren’t petty gifts; rather they were:

„[...] testimonies to faith. The most humble donation is an act of faith in that weapon, that alone can win the war. [...] These heroes know it. The living know it, the dead know it. The shadow of the winged machine is similar to the shadow of the wood of sacrifice and salvation. I recognized this on a day that has now long passed, on a day in that other war, on the grim field of Gonars, that resembled a levelled Calvary, as I beheld the plane that Oreste Salomone conducted, with its mournful burden and all sprinkled in blood, then the likeness appeared to me. Its two transverse wings between the prow and the rudders formed the bloody cross. […] ‚O, Wing of Italy, you are my faith, is confessed by those of us who have been lacerated, who have been crushed, who have been an earthbound holocaust just to be abducted into the sky again by the spirit of fire.25

To make sense of the war in the air, as well as of the agony in the trenches, D’Annunzio depicted the soldier as a suffering Christ, the aviator as a holocaust: as a burnt offering, as an oblation, and a crucified saviour.26 These were the central elements and images of the “cult of the fallen soldier” that subsequently became a political religion and mythical order founded on nationhood.27 After the war was over, D’Annunzio also integrated the spoils which the Entente had “denied” Italy into this narrative. The liberal order and “the West” were discredited, and a new and greater Italy, la più grande Italia, had to ensure that the hecatombs offered to the gods would not be in vain.28 The figure of the D’Annunzian Icarian and Christian hybrid was advanced by the credibility D’Annunzio had attained through his daring feats in the war and during the occupation of Fiume, as well as by the media that conveyed them and promoted him. But mainly, it was the First World War itself that promoted these ideas and extended the sphere of their impact.

The war had not only threatened the existing order but had virtually swept it aside. The “world of yesterday” that had already been corroded at the Fin de siècle now crumbled.29 The dissembling of the existing order was a result of social, economic, technological, and cultural upheaval that had found its outburst in the clash of nation states. The failure of traditional interpretative patterns and of the grand narrative of progress to make sense of the present, or to envision a stable future, catalysed the radicalized “quest for order.”30 The omnipresence of death, turmoil, and chaos created a disposition for all kinds of narratives of redemption and purification, of salvation, rebirth, and regeneration, which gave action in general and suffering in particular a direction and a meaning. And thus the societies and individuals in war-stricken Europe—especially those that were defeated and whose war was therefore exempt from meaning—reacted by trying to “abolish history” and to “renew time.”31 They tried to destroy and to symbolically transcend senseless, profane time by incorporating the historical events in a super-historical context, or nomos.32 The Christianized Icarian and palingenetic narrative that the EAI spatialized was a means to this end. It offered visitors a way of understanding the reality that had emerged during the war, a symbolic solution to the urgent questions that resulted from “defeat” or rather “mutilated victory,” a new order of history and a vision of the future.

146 The liberal order, the decadence, the and impotence that had impeded the Italian ascent was symbolically abolished on the first floor of the EAI. In contrast, Mussolini’s airplane at the top of the flight of stairs embodied the new Italy that had arisen from the shambles of the old world or, for that matter, from the ashes, like a phoenix. The whole second floor culminated in the hall dedicated to Balbo’s Crociera del Decennale. Balbo’s mass flight across the Atlantic was an expression of the collective spirit that fascism aimed to nourish in all Italians. As the catalogue implied, it reified the reborn Italy:

Thus the Crociera del Decennale does more than conclude an era of modern aviation. She is one of the brightest undertakings of an Italy that has again become a Nation of heroes and scientists, of workers and artists, of men of thought and of action. The great, glorious ‘I. Balb’ [Balbo’s seaplane] that the crowd admires isn’t only a perfect machine all of whose components were built on Italian shop floors […]. […] It [the seaplane] is a piece of the Fatherland that has been cast about the world to demonstrate what stuff the Fatherland is made of.33

Balbo’s adventure had in fact been a major propagandistic success for the regime abroad. In Chicago, a street was named after Balbo; President Roosevelt received him at the White House; and the city of New York arranged a ticker tape parade for the fascist heroes on July 21, 1933. On the evening of that same day, Balbo greeted approximately 60,000 Italian-Americans at Madison Square Garden and greeted them from the Duce:34

Be proud of being Italian […] because you are the love and the pride of the Duce, you that are faithful and fertile, you that have the spirit and the patience of the builders of Rome! Mussolini has ended the time of humiliation: to be Italian is an honorable title. Fifty million Italians carry on marching the ways of the earth, of the seas and of the skies: yet they do so under the pennant of Rome and under the command of a great leader: Italy is no longer plebeian but an army of civilisation underway in the world.35

The reciprocal acclamation concluded with the fascist hymn to youth and the “spring of beauty” that Mussoini had allegedly brought about, Giovinezza. According to Balbo, the audience inside and the thousands crowded outside joined in, and via radio waves, the melody “spread till the bright shores of Italy.” This was the mythical communion and community of Italians that fascism strove to erect and that the exhibition attempted to inscribe into its visitors. In an article covering the importance of “Aviation and Art” at the exhibition, Vincenzo Constantini stated that commemorative exhibitions of this sort and especially the artwork displayed enabled the visitor “to live in the spirit” of what was exhibited:

[...] in the end [these exhibitions] are more interesting than those which accumulate the usual ‘still lifes’ or ‘bathers.’ Why? The ‘commemorative exhibitions’ engraft the artistic fantasy into social life. [...] in these exhibitions the aesthetic contemplation descends into the active life of the nation, it gives itself to the people, that runs in masses to visit a production that is of interest to daily life.36

Yet it wasn’t only aesthetic contemplation that “descended into the active life of the 147 nation” by means of the exhibitions. A new order of history and time overarched the “abyss” between the “glory” of ancient Rome and the reborn fascist New Italy. It is this destruction of history and profane time, the subsequent reordering of time, and the incorporation of historical events in a super-historical context that the exhibition attempted to engraft into the visitors—and that also seems to be one of the central characteristics, both of fascism as an expression of the radicalized quest for order that resulted from the war, and of mythical modernity in general. What is mythical modernity, and how can this apparent oxymoron be understood?

At the risk of oversimplifying a complex phenomenon, taking Zygmunt Bauman’s theory of modernity as a starting point, we can characterize modernity as a specific mindset and ethos that is grounded on the consciousness of order as task and the subsequent quest for order.37 This modern mindset spread in reaction to the accelerating rate of change and to the resulting perception that “all that is solid melts into air [and that] all that is holy is profaned.”38 This liquefaction and profanation leads to the search for an order that is grounded on truly “solid” and “holy” entities/principles. Hence mythical modernity can be understood as an attempt to semantically restructure, mentally remap, and reorder the world in accordance with what is ahistorical, absolutized, and thus “holy.” The mythical order was modern because it was borne out of the understanding of order as task. It was mythical because it legitimized the constitution of society by anchoring its nomos in a supreme and allegedly uncontested sacred entity or value.39

In contrast to the normative usage of the word myth, which usually implies a false story and as such the opposite of reason qua logos, philosophical and ethnological theories of myth define it as a linguistic social practice.40 Mythical narratives legitimize a natural or cultural phenomenon by connecting it to the sphere of the sacred; they “naturalize” history, as Roland Barthes has shown.41 The sacred is a value or an entity that is intersubjectively uncontested. The mercilessness of the conflicts between the modern ideologies resulted from an underlying fundamental contradiction: on the one hand, a common need for an intersubjectively uncontested “solid”; on the other, a contestation of the concepts and candidates for the role of the sacred that stood at the core of each of the modern ideologies, be it the nation, the Aryan race, the proletariat, or reason.

D’Annunzio and the EAI legitimized suffering, sacrifice, death, and killing in the war by connecting them to the rebirth and regeneration of the sanctified nation. Both divinized aviators and their machines by portraying them as saviors and true expressions of that very same sacred nation. Neither the analogy between “the shadow of winged machine” and “the wood of sacrifice and salvation,” nor the essential message of the exhibition—the fall and ascension of the nation—should be seen as mere artistic hyperboles. They should rather be understood as earnest attempts to order both a chaotic world and a time that seemed to be out of joint by constituting a commonly accessible meaning and a Gemeinschaft, an organic society, on the grounds of a sacralized nation. The resulting order can be seen as a mythical solution to the quest for

148 order. D’Annunzio’s literary works and his speeches, as well as the EAI, were not only outstanding examples of the sacralization of politics that characterized fascism. They were also examples of the aesthetization of politics, which, according to Benjamin, was fascism’s aim.42 This (re)merger of the sacred and the aesthetic with the scope of molding a Gemeinschaft is also voiced most accurately through the concept of myth. The mythical solution to the quest for order seems a logical consequence both of a reality that was perceived of as a “state of emergency” and of a human condition that was experienced as “transcendental homelessness”.43 This state of emergency as well as the transcendental homelessness resulted from the “death of God”: i.e., the contestation and thus profanation of “all that is holy,” not in the least by means of historicist thinking. As Heidegger has shown, Nietzsche’s “God is dead” didn’t only refer to the Christian God, it referred to the dissolution of the supernatural and the metaphysical in general, and as such the discovery of the contingency of all orders and all that underlay them.44 In hindsight, it is possible to equate the discovery of the contingency of the existing order with the discovery of the impossibility of order in general. Yet the contemporary reaction to the dissolution of both the order of the ancien régime and of the liberal order in the course of the First World War was not the abandonment of order altogether, but rather the frantic quest for alternative orders: alternative orders that were anchored in something truly “absolute” or truly “sacred,” i.e., definitely, intersubjectively incontestable. As Zygmunt Bauman asserts:

[…] all this [liquefaction] was to be done not in order to do away with the solids once and for all and make the brave new world free of them forever, but to clear the site for new and improved solids; to replace the inherited set of deficient and defective solids with another set, which was much improved and preferably perfect, and for that reason no longer alterable. […] the ‘found solids’ were resented, condemned and earmarked for liquefaction for the reason that they were already rusty, mushy, coming apart at the seams and altogether unreliable. Modern times found the pre-modern solids in a fairly advanced stage of disintegration; and one of the most powerful motives behind the urge to melt them was the wish to discover or invent solids of–for a change–lasting solidity, a solidity which one could trust and rely upon and which would make the world predictable and therefore manageable.45

During the fin de siécle and especially following the First World War, the “solids” —now the ones upon which the liberal order was constructed—again appeared “in a fairly advanced stage of disintegration.” They were liquefied and replaced by allegedly reliable “solids,” such as the nation or the Volk, in the fascist case, or the proletariat, in the Bolshevist case. Such “truly” reliable solids, i.e. absolute, eternal and sacred entities, were necessarily ahistorical and thus mythical. An order grounded on just such mythical solids gained its legitimation either from “science,” thereby repressing its mythical origins, or was consciously proclaimed and preached as myth, relying on the belief that real truths were organic and holistic—exactly the opposite of scientific. Both versions were present in European fascism, and they were often mixed to different degrees.

The point that should be stressed is that this paradoxical as well as aporetical situation is, I believe, a defining characteristic of modernities in general. The modern ethos or mindset dismisses myth and longs for it nonetheless. It is capable of delegitimizing, 149 deconstructing, and destroying myths but is not able to live without them. These two states of mind were not successive; they were coexistent. To perceive reality as a “state of emergency,” as chaos and as the absence of order, means that “the problem of order” is already present; or as Bauman puts it, “has already come out of hiding, out of nonexistence, out of silence.”46 Accordingly, the modern mindset seeks to overcome the state of emergency by finding a solution to the “problem of order.” Yet the solution can only repress or sublimate the problem of order, not dispose of it once and for all. The aporia results from the fact that the discovery of the contingency of one specific order is a point of no return; or, as Nietzsche put it, referring to his own philosophy on the day he broke down in Turin, “After you had discovered me, it was no feat to find me; the difficulty now is to lose me...”47

This is in fact the crucial point in regard to the concept of mythical modernity: modern consciousness searches for an order that is grounded in an absolute, but this search itself is the product of a consciousness that is skeptical of absolutes, and of the myths that order the world in relation to absolutes. This longing for myth is simultaneous with the incapacity to live in a mythically ordered world, and not, as the Enlightenment and modernization theory has had us believe, chronologically posterior. The “Gleichzeitigkeit des Ungleichzeitigen,” i.e. the contemporaneity of the incontemporaneous, is itself a legitimating myth of the Enlightenment which, as Adorno and Horkheimer state, reverts back to mythology.48 And thus myth— commonly branded as the “other” of modernity, as the precursor of the modern and of logos—becomes complementary of modernity. Ironically, through the transformation of our perspective on modernity, fascism—the former paradigm of the unmodern, or, as Jeffrey Herf calles it, of “reactionary modernism” —actually becomes a possible archetype of the modern.49
